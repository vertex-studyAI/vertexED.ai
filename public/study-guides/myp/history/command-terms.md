# MYP History — Command Terms Reference

**Based on**: 13 sessions (N16–M25)
**Last updated**: 2026-04-19

This file gives a complete reference for every command term used across all 13 MYP History sessions. For each term: the official MYP definition, what the mark scheme actually rewards at each level, what to avoid, and which sessions used it.

---

## How to use this file

Every MYP History question is anchored to a command term that tells you exactly what depth is required. The mark scheme descriptors map directly onto these terms — so if a question says "Explain", the 4-mark mark scheme escalates as State → Outline → Describe → Explain. Understanding the terms is the single most reliable way to target the right mark band.

---

## STATE

**MYP definition**: Give a specific name, value or other brief answer without explanation or calculation.

**Typical marks**: 1

**What to write**: One sentence. A specific fact, name, date, or characteristic. No elaboration required or rewarded.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- "The source is from the National Geographic." (origin of a source)
- "The suffragette movement got women the right to vote." (change caused by a protest movement)
- "ABC News" / "Gene Johnson" / "Associated Press" (acceptable answers to a State the origin question)

**What examiners reward**: Specificity. "A newspaper" is often rejected as too vague; "A newspaper article from the time" may be accepted; "ABC News" or "an MYP history teacher" (with role) is the target level.

**What to avoid**:
- "To inform an audience" as a purpose — explicitly rejected in every session as too vague. Must say *what* is being informed about.
- Vague origins like "Instagram" alone (N20) — the creator's role/identity is required.
- Multi-sentence elaboration — this wastes time and earns no extra credit.

**Sessions**: Every session (appears in virtually every paper as a sub-component of longer source analysis questions).

---

## LIST

**MYP definition**: Give a sequence of brief answers with no explanation.

**Typical marks**: 1 per item, up to 2–3 marks total.

**What to write**: Name each item clearly. No explanation, reasoning, or connective language needed.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- Diaries / speeches / letters / interviews with eyewitnesses (M17 — list two primary sources)
- Memoirs / government archives / newspaper articles / propaganda posters (N24 — list two additional primary sources)
- Any valid source type, method, or piece of information

**What examiners reward**: Validity and relevance. Any accurate item that would genuinely help the investigation is accepted. Examiners consistently state "accept any other valid response."

**What to avoid**:
- Overly generic responses: "Newspapers" without further specificity is rejected in several sessions (N22, M22). Must be something like "Newspaper articles from the time on the topic."
- Explaining the items — this is List, not Explain. Explanations are neither required nor rewarded.

**Sessions**: N16 (Q2d, Q2e), M17 (Q1h, Q1i, Q1j), M22 (Q2g), N24 (Q2d), M25 (Q1a).

## IDENTIFY

**MYP definition**: Provide an answer from a number of possibilities. Recognise and state briefly a distinguishing fact or feature.

**Typical marks**: 1–2

**What to write**: Name the answer, or choose the correct option. One sentence maximum. No explanation needed unless the question asks you to both identify AND something else.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- "Identify two additional people you would have interviewed" — Survivors of Chernobyl / historian of Soviet history / government official (N19 Q5a, 1 mark each)
- "Identify two examples of cultural change in Source A" — new techniques in art / rediscovered Ancient Greece and Rome (N21 Q4)
- "Identify Source B and Source C as primary or secondary" — B = Primary, C = Secondary (M23 Q6a, 1 mark each)
- "Identify one primary source / one secondary source" (N23, M24 Q4c/d — 1 mark each)

**What examiners reward**: Accurate identification of a valid item or classification. "Accept any valid response" is standard.

**What to avoid**:
- For source identification (primary vs. secondary): confusing sources created at the time of the event (primary) with those created later (secondary).
- On "Identify two examples" questions: repeating the same point in different words — both points must be distinct.

**Cap rule (M23 Q6a)**: Note that *verbatim quotes from the source do not count as additional detail* in a sub-question that follows Identify.

**Sessions**: N16 (Q2c), N19 (Q5a), M22 (Q2g), M23 (Q6a), N23 (Q4c/d), M24 (Q4c/d), M25 (implicit in source selection).

## SELECT

**MYP definition**: Choose from a list or group.

**Typical marks**: 1

**What to write**: Choose the correct option from those given. No explanation needed.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- "Select the related concept most appropriate to the topic" — correct answer: **Significance** (N20 Q1a)
- "Select whether the following statement is true or false" — correct answer: **True** (N20 Q1b)
- "Select true or false: Globalization reduced inequality in Latin America" — correct answer: **False** (M24B Q6a)
- "Select true or false: The United States benefited economically from migration" — correct answer: **True** (M24B Q6b)

**What examiners reward**: The single correct selection.

**What to avoid**: Changing your answer or providing reasoning — only the selection is marked.

**Sessions**: N20 (Q1a, Q1b), M24B (Q6a, Q6b).

## OUTLINE

**MYP definition**: Give a brief account or summary.

**Typical marks**: 2

**What to write**: Identify + brief elaboration with context. Typically one sentence with two clauses, or two short sentences. Enough to show understanding beyond bare identification but not a full explanation.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 1 mark: "The women's liberation movement wanted equal rights for women." (N16 — this is just identification, not an outline)
- 2 marks: "The women's liberation movement in the 1970s in many countries tried to gain more rights and respect for women in society, but especially in the workplace." (N16 Q1a — this is a proper outline)
- 1 mark: "The UN was set up after World War II." (M21 — just identification)
- 2 marks: "The UN was set up in order to prevent future wars and conflicts to promote world peace." (M21 Q1 — elaboration added)

**What examiners reward**: The combination of identification AND brief detail/context. Without the brief elaboration, the response is capped at 1 mark (which counts as just "states" in mark scheme language, even when the command term is Outline).

**What to avoid**:
- Naming something without any elaboration — this scores 1 mark, not 2.
- Providing reasoning or cause-effect analysis — that is Explain territory; Outline just needs brief factual detail.
- For source analysis (Outline purpose/value): the mark scheme expects the *what* and *for whom*, not the *why it is valuable*.

**Used in every session** across multiple questions. Key appearances: knowledge recall (Q1-level), source purpose, source value, investigation strengths/limitations.

## DESCRIBE

**MYP definition**: Give a detailed account or picture of a situation, event, pattern or process.

**Typical marks**: 3–4

**What to write**: Detail without comprehensive reasoning. A short paragraph that covers the event, situation, or characteristic with specifics. More than Outline but stops short of explaining *why* things happened or happened the way they did.

**Mark scheme examples**:
- "The British Empire dominated the people of India with its military control, violently suppressing uprisings like the Amritsar Massacre. The system of government and the laws implemented also influenced the Indian population." (M21 Q2 — 3 marks, describing how empire influenced people)
- "Advances in medicine changed the way societies dealt with epidemics, going from a religious approach to a scientific approach. In the 14th century, people and doctors didn't understand where the disease came from. In the 20th century, doctors understood diseases so used disinfectant and antibiotics." (M22 Q6 — 3 marks)
- "A primary source is one that comes from the time period; it can be a newspaper article from the time or diary of a person living in that period. A secondary source is produced after the event took place." (M17 Q1g — 3/4 marks)

**What examiners reward**: Specific, accurate detail. Named examples (the Amritsar Massacre; germ theory / Pasteur and Koch; James Watt's steam engine) push responses into the top of the Describe band. Detail *without* causal reasoning is the ceiling.

**What to avoid**:
- Short sentences that merely repeat the outline — must add specificity.
- Over-explaining cause and effect — that tips into Explain territory and may be mis-matched to the command term.

**Sessions**: M17 (Q1g), M22 (Q5a, Q5b, Q6), N24 (Q1, Q2c), M25 (Q7a, Q7b).

## EXPLAIN

**MYP definition**: Give a detailed account including reasons or causes.

**Typical marks**: 4 marks for short Explain; 6–10 marks for extended Explain (Criterion A in creative tasks).

**What to write**: Detail + cause-and-effect reasoning. A paragraph that answers *why* or *how*, using "because", "as a result", "this led to", "therefore" language. Examples must be accurate and support the reasoning chain.

**Mark scheme escalation (typical 4-mark Explain)**:
| Marks | Depth |
|-------|-------|
| 1 | States one fact/characteristic (one sentence) |
| 2 | Outlines — states with brief elaboration |
| 3 | Describes — detail without reasoning |
| 4 | Explains — detail + cause-effect reasoning, "because…", consequence identified |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 4 marks (N16 Q1c): "Although it has not been a complete success, there has been much improvement over the past 50 years in achieving more rights, respect and equal pay for women in society. **This was because of their constant struggle to change the attitudes of society.**"
- 4 marks (M21 Q2): "The British Empire dominated India with military control… Eventually this dominance **influenced the growth of a nationalist movement that aimed to challenge Britain and be independent from British rule**."
- 4 marks (N20 Q2): "The research question is appropriate because it provides an **overall understanding** of the invention of the microscope, such as why it was needed and will help the research team focus their investigation." (Explain strength of an investigative process)

**What examiners reward**: The *because* — the causal link. Named examples that are accurate and deploy reasoning (not just listed). At the top of a 4-mark range, one well-explained point is enough.

**What to avoid**:
- Listing multiple facts without connecting them causally — this is Describe, not Explain.
- Vague language: "it was important because it helped people" is typically 1–2 marks; the reasoning must be specific.

**Sessions**: Every session. Key appearances: Q1-level short answer, research methodology (source usefulness Q3e/Q4c/Q4d), creative tasks (Criterion A for essays/speeches/scripts), source analysis value/limitation (Q6c/Q8a/Q8b etc.).

## FORMULATE

**MYP definition**: Express precisely and systematically the relevant concept(s) or argument(s).

**Typical marks**: 2

**What to write**: A research question (or sub-question) that is both **CLEAR** (intention easily understood) AND **FOCUSED** (specific enough — names a person, place, time period, or specific topic).

**Mark scheme escalation**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Rephrases the SoI (too vague or simply restates it) |
| 1 | RQ is clear OR focused but not both |
| 2 | RQ is both clear AND focused and connected to the SoI |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 1 mark: "To what extent has nuclear energy impact health positively?" — clear intention but lacks focus (no country/time specified) (N19 Q3a)
- 2 marks: "To what extent has nuclear energy **improved the health of people in Japan**?" — clear AND focused (specific country) (N19 Q3a)
- 1 mark: "How did the economic conditions [of Germany] create support for Nazism?" — clear but not focused (no time specified) (N24 Q2a)
- 2 marks: "How did the economic conditions **of the 1930s** create support for Nazism?" — clear AND focused (specific decade) (N24 Q2a)
- 0 marks: "To what extent have global interactions caused significant impacts on the identities and relationships of stakeholders?" — this merely rephrases the SoI (M21)

**What examiners reward**: Specificity. A 2-mark RQ must be answerable with specific evidence — it should narrow the investigation to a named person, country, time period, or clearly defined aspect.

**What to avoid**:
- Rephrasing the Statement of Inquiry = 0 marks, no exceptions.
- Broad, unanswerable questions ("How has science helped people throughout history?") = 1 mark at most.
- Questions so specific they cannot be researched without privileged access ("What was Karl Marx thinking when he wrote Das Kapital?") — must be researchable.

**A sub-question (Formulate a sub-question)**: Same rules apply. Must be narrower than the RQ, reference the given concept (can be implicit), and be both clear and focused (M17 Q1e/Q1f, M22 Q2e).

**Sessions**: Every session (Q2a/Q3a/Q4a). Key appearances: N16, M17, N19, N20, M21, N21, M22, N22, M23, N23, M24, N24, M25.

## JUSTIFY

**MYP definition**: Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion.

**Typical marks**: 4

**What to write**: Reasons AND reasoning for why the answer/conclusion holds. Not just describing what the RQ is about, but arguing *why* it is worth investigating in relation to the SoI.

**Mark scheme escalation (Justify the relevance of your RQ to the SoI)**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | States the relevance (single sentence, simple connection) |
| 2 | Outlines the relevance (brief detail on how the RQ connects to the SoI) |
| 3 | Describes the relevance (argument considered in detail without full reasoning) |
| 4 | Justifies the relevance (argument + reasoning for why the RQ is relevant to the SoI) |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 4 marks (N19 Q3b): "It is important to study nuclear energy because it has been a significant innovation in history. It produces less pollution than other energy sources, such as fossil fuels, but it can pose a danger to society because of its radioactivity. By investigating how Japan uses nuclear energy and analysing the impact it has had on the people, **we can find valid evidence to support the statement of inquiry**."
- 4 marks (M21 Q3b): "My research question deals with the change in relationship between two superpowers as a result of their interactions after WWII… **This will help me determine how significant their interactions were to the start of the Cold War and is a good example of how global interactions can impact relationships.**"
- 4 marks (M23 Q4b): "It focuses on one leader who has used national identity and the significance it had in helping him obtain power… **By investigating Hitler's use of nationalism in his rise to power we can find out the uses to which national identity can be put if taken to their extreme.**"

**What examiners reward**: A forward-looking argument — explaining what the investigation *will reveal* and why that is relevant to the SoI. The 4th mark is consistently awarded for a *because* or *by investigating X we can find out Y* construction.

**What to avoid**:
- Simply describing what the RQ covers — this is Describe/Outline level (2–3 marks).
- Circular reasoning: "The RQ is relevant because it is about the same topic as the SoI" — this is 1 mark (States).

**Also used as command term for source-based arguments** (M23 Q6c — "justify the argument that Germany is most responsible for WWI using only Sources B and C"):
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Implicit reference to source(s), limited justification |
| 2 | References at least one source explicitly |
| 3 | Clear argument referencing both sources |
| 4 | Detailed argument clearly referencing both sources |

**Sessions**: Every session (Justify RQ relevance appears in all 13). Also M23 (Q6c), M24B (Q7).

## EVALUATE

**MYP definition**: Make an appraisal by weighing up the strengths and limitations.

**Typical marks**: 8

**What to write**: Strengths + Limitations + Overall appraisal. These are always assessed as three separate sub-strands in the mark scheme.

**Universal structure** (confirmed across all 13 sessions):
- **Strengths** (0–3 marks): State (1) → Outline (2) → Explain with reasoning (3)
- **Limitations** (0–3 marks): State (1) → Outline (2) → Explain with reasoning (3)
- **Appraisal** (0–2 marks): Brief overall judgment (1) → Weighs both sides with overall conclusion (2)

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 3-mark strength: "The research question is appropriate to the SoI. It focuses on European empires and asking for the main reasons for their expansion by investigating key events from the 19th century. **This means the investigation should produce valid results supported by evidence based on several factors from a variety of European empires.**" (M21 Q4)
- 3-mark limitation: "The second and third sub-questions are off topic. They compare Argentina with other countries rather than focusing on Argentina only. **This could lead students to spending too much time on the independence process without linking it to present national identity.**" (M23 Q2)
- 2-mark appraisal: "Overall, the sources used are credible but **because of the way the RQ is phrased, the investigation is too biased.**" (N24 Q3)

**Cap rules**:
- Strengths only or limitations only: capped at 3 marks (cannot access the full 8)
- To reach 7–8, both strengths AND limitations must be addressed, with an appraisal

**Special note on N16 (12-mark Evaluate)**:
N16 Q3 used a 12-mark Evaluate worth double the usual. The bands were: Limited (1–3) → Limited + weaknesses (4–6) → Describes + argument (7–9) → Thorough + detailed argument (10–12). The three-strand structure still applies.

**Key principle from mark schemes**: "The quality of the response is what is rewarded, not the number of strengths/limitations referred to." — One well-explained strength beats five vague ones.

**What examiners reward**: Reasoning. An explained strength/limitation must include *because* or *therefore* — a consequence that shows *why* this is a strength or limitation.

**What to avoid**:
- Listing multiple vague strengths/limitations without elaboration — quality over quantity.
- Appraisal that is just a restatement of the question or a meaningless positive ("Overall, the investigation is good") — must weigh both sides.
- Ignoring the appraisal — without it, the maximum achievable is 6 marks.

**Suggesting alternative methods** = counts as identifying a limitation (confirmed in N22, M23, N23, M24, M25 mark schemes).

**Sessions**: Every session. N16 (Q3, 12 marks), M17 (Q2, 12 marks), N19 (Q4, 8 marks), N20 (Q3, 8 marks), M21 (Q4, 8 marks), N21 (Q2, 8 marks), M22 (Q1, 8 marks), N22 (Q2, 8 marks), M23 (Q2, 8 marks), N23 (Q2, 8 marks), M24 (Q2, 8 marks), N24 (Q3, 8 marks), M25 (Q3, 8 marks).

## ANALYSE

**MYP definition**: Break down in order to bring out the essential elements or structure. To identify parts and relationships, and to interpret information to reach conclusions.

**Typical marks**: 4–6 (single source); 6 (dual source, two questions)

**What to write**: Break down the source/situation into its components and interpret what they mean. For source analysis: identify what the source shows, what it implies, and draw interpretive conclusions. For perspective analysis: take the viewpoint of a named individual or group and argue both sides.

**Mark scheme escalation (6-mark Analyse)**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | States the message/analysis |
| 2–3 | Outlines — identifies with some detail |
| 4–5 | Provides analysis — breaks down components with interpretation |
| 6 | Provides detailed analysis — thorough breakdown with interpretive conclusions |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 5 marks (N19 Q7): "The message is that the USA feels superior to Cuba. The cartoon depicts Cuba as a child playing in the sand. He doesn't seem happy to hear Uncle Sam lecturing him. Uncle Sam, much more mature, looks at the boy from above and watches him closely. The text in the sub-caption saying 'I will keep a father's eye on you' **suggests that the US is letting Cuba know who is in control.**"
- 4 marks (N24 Q6 — dual source analysis): "Unlike Mandela, who led through peaceful ways, Stalin was a violent dictator. As we can see in Source B, Stalin got rid of anyone who opposed him by either killing them or imprisoning them. Mandela focused on unifying the country through rugby. **We can see that Mandela was focused on making the lives of all South Africans better. However, Stalin's totalitarian regime was clearly more focused on his own power** rather than what was good for the people."
- 6 marks (N23 Q7): "In Source B the author shows how Kwame Nkrumah has taken a political approach and used basic tenets of nationhood… While in Source C the artist Wiz Kudowor uses traditional African symbolism… **This demonstrates how symbolism is significant in expressing national identity**."

**What examiners reward**: Interpretation, not just description. The key word is *suggests* or *implies* — going beyond what the source says to what it *means*. For dual-source analysis, evidence from both sources is required; only one source used typically caps at half the available marks.

**What to avoid**:
- Describing the content of the source without interpreting its message or meaning.
- Single-source analysis when both sources are required — for Q7 in N23/M24 (analyse two sources), only analysing one caps the mark at 3.

**Sessions**: N16 (Q5, Q6), M17 (Q7 partial), N19 (Q7), N20 (Q7), M21 (Q7 partial), N23 (Q7), M24A (Q7), N24 (Q6), M25 (implicit in essay).

## CONTRAST

**MYP definition**: Give an account of the differences between two or more items or situations, referring to both/all of them throughout.

**Typical marks**: 3–4

**What to write**: Differences only. Similarities are not rewarded and should not be included.

**Mark scheme escalation (3-mark Contrast)**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | States a difference |
| 2 | Outlines a difference — names both sources with brief detail on one difference |
| 3 | Describes differences in detail — both sources referenced with specific evidence |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 2 marks (N22 Q6): "Source A shows how chlorine has **improved the lives of millions of people**, while Source B shows how explosives have **made many people's lives worse**."
- 3 marks (N22 Q6): Above + "Source A shows how chlorine has helped through the manufacture of products such as contact lenses, while Source B shows how explosives have caused death and destruction due to an enormous amount of casualties."
- 1 mark (M25 Q6b): "Source A argues the washing machine was a significant invention and Source B argues that it wasn't."
- 4 marks (M25 Q6b): Full response contrasting Source A (washing machine saved time) with Source B (washing machine only changed hygiene habits; frozen dinner was more significant).
- 3 marks (M24B Q9): "Source A has a global perspective on positive and negative consequences while Source C refers to the negative consequences on one specific country."

**Cap rule**: No marks for similarities. If a student provides similarities, examiners simply ignore them — but including them does not lose marks.

**What examiners reward**: Specific evidence from each source that illustrates the contrast. Naming the sources (Source A vs. Source B) and providing specific quotes or referenced content.

**What to avoid**:
- Writing about similarities — these receive 0 marks for the Contrast command term.
- Generic contrasts without source evidence: "One is positive and one is negative" without specific reference = 1 mark.

**Sessions**: N22 (Q6, 3 marks), M24B (Q9, 3 marks), M25 (Q6b, 4 marks).

## COMPARE AND CONTRAST

**MYP definition**: Give an account of similarities and differences between two or more items, referring to both throughout.

**Typical marks**: 6–8

**What to write**: Similarities AND differences. Both are required to access the full mark range.

**Mark scheme escalation (6-mark Compare and Contrast)**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1–2 | Limited — addresses similarities or differences only |
| 3–4 | Satisfactory — addresses both but with limited detail |
| 5–6 | Detailed — thorough coverage of both similarities and differences with specific evidence |

**Mark scheme escalation (8-mark Compare and Contrast)**:
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1–2 | Limited account of similarities or differences |
| 3–4 | Satisfactory — addresses one side well |
| 5–6 | Detailed — covers both with specific evidence from each source |
| 7–8 | Thorough — comprehensive coverage of both with interpretive synthesis |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 6 marks (N16 Q7): "Source C — Peace Corps, peaceful, voluntary, education/food/health focus; Source D — blockade of Cuba, militaristic, threatened to sink ships. Both show US influence over public opinion at the same historical period." (Full compare and contrast)
- 7 marks (M17 Q6): "Both sources refer to the potential of science to make life better... Source C is much more sceptical of technological innovation, showing how destructive it could be. It offers a warning rather than an advertisement. Source B focuses on science for the benefit of the daily life of the family; Source C focuses on the implications of science for humanity as a whole."
- 6 marks (M21 Q7): "Both sources are primary sources and provide a visual to give the student direct accounts of the events. However, Source C gives a commentary with images providing detail on WTO protests. Source D does not show the impacts. The sources differ in terms of methods: Source C shows violence against police and businesses, Source D suggests the civil rights march was more peaceful. **This allows the student to understand that protests occur for many different reasons.**"

**Cap rules**:
- Similarities only or differences only: capped at 4 marks (out of 6) or 4 marks (out of 8).
- At 7–8 (out of 8): specific details from both sources must be used; response must be thorough.

**What examiners reward**: Use of evidence from both sources to support both similarities and differences. The key at top band: the response must *use both sources explicitly* throughout, not just mention them at the start.

**What to avoid**:
- Comparing only or contrasting only — capped at 4 regardless of quality.
- Generic statements without source-specific evidence.
- Discussing only the sources' reliability (OPVL-style analysis) when Compare and Contrast asks about their *content* in relation to a theme.

**Sessions**: N16 (Q7, 6 marks), M17 (Q6, 8 marks), M21 (Q7, 6 marks).

## DISCUSS

**MYP definition**: Offer a considered and balanced review that includes a range of arguments, factors or hypotheses. Opinions or conclusions should be presented clearly and supported by appropriate evidence.

**Typical marks**: 24–30 (always the extended essay)

**What to write**: A balanced essay with at least two perspectives, named historical examples for each, and a synthesising conclusion that takes a clear position. Introduction + body + conclusion in separate paragraphs.

**Mark scheme structure** (Criterion D component, typically 8 marks):
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1–2 | One perspective, summarises information superficially |
| 3–4 | Two perspectives outlined, some development, limited conclusion |
| 5–6 | Two perspectives analysed in detail, synthesis present |
| 7–8 | Two perspectives thoroughly developed, synthesis convincing, balanced, effective examples |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 7 marks (M17 Q7 — "Pioneers are influenced most by the period in which they live"): "Henry Ford was a product of his time. Living in a country that believed in individual ownership and innovation, the conditions were right… But Ford took advantage of the opportunities created in his time and helped change the way that the US economy worked, not only in his day, but throughout the 20th century." — Two perspectives present (period shaped Ford AND Ford shaped the period); synthesis convincing.
- 7 marks (N20 Q8): Full causal argument on Britain's industrialization — technological, economic, and social factors considered with specific named examples; conclusion synthesises the interplay of factors.

**What examiners reward**: Balance. Both sides of the argument must be substantiated with named examples. The conclusion must synthesise (combine both perspectives into a judgment) not just restate them.

**What to avoid**:
- One-sided essay — capped at 3–4 for the Criterion D component.
- Conclusion that only summarises (restates what was said) rather than synthesises (takes a position based on the evidence).
- Only listing examples without analysis — must argue *why* they support the perspective.

**Sessions**: N16 (Q8, 30 marks), M17 (Q7, 30 marks), N20 (Q8, 24 marks), N23 (Q8, 24 marks), M24A (Q8, 24 marks), M25 — Discuss command not used; "To what extent" is the essay command in most sessions from N19 onward.

## TO WHAT EXTENT

**MYP definition**: Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument or concept. Opinions and conclusions should be presented clearly and supported with appropriate evidence and sound argument.

**Typical marks**: 8–24 (used for mid-length argumentative questions and the big essay)

**What to write**: Take a clear position on the question. Argue one side with evidence, acknowledge the counter-argument, then conclude with a nuanced judgment about *how much* you agree (extent).

**Key difference from Discuss**: "To what extent" requires you to *take a position* and argue for it, while acknowledging the other side. Discuss is more balanced (equal weight both sides); To what extent is more directional (argue a position, acknowledge the counter).

**Mark scheme structure** (Criterion D/A for 24-mark essay):
| Marks | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1–2 | One perspective only, superficial analysis |
| 3–4 | Two perspectives, limited synthesis |
| 5–6 | Two perspectives developed, synthesis present but not fully convincing |
| 7–8 | Two perspectives thoroughly developed, clear position, effective synthesis in conclusion |

**Mark scheme examples**:
- 7 marks (M17 Q5 — "Innovation comes from individuals responding to crises"): "Many innovations have resulted from a need for security or survival. The space race came about during the Cold War when the US and USSR were fighting a global battle of ideology. However, space exploration today is mostly done out of curiosity. **To a large extent I agree with the claim — many innovations result from crisis, but this is not the only driver.**"
- 7 marks (N19 Q9 — "The world is more peaceful under a single great power"): Full argument from Cold War proxy wars through US post-Cold War dominance, acknowledging: *"stability does not necessarily mean peace: countries may be forced to accept rules from more powerful countries even if it does not benefit them as much."*
- 7 marks (M22 Q7 — "Scientific innovation has always brought positive change to health"): Counter-perspective on nuclear weapons; synthesis: *"innovations have generally improved health but with significant exceptions that should not be ignored."*

**What examiners reward**: A clear, argued position with named evidence for the main argument AND a substantive counter-argument. The conclusion must go beyond restating the evidence — it should synthesise both perspectives into a nuanced judgment.

**What to avoid**:
- Only arguing one side — caps Criterion D at 3–4.
- Vague conclusions: "Overall, it depends on what you mean by…" is not synthesis; must commit to a position.
- Forgetting the required elements (e.g., the question says "consider at least two other factors" — omitting this is penalised).

**Sessions**: M17 (Q5, 8 marks), N19 (Q9, 24 marks), M21 (Q8, 24 marks), N21 (Q7, 24 marks), M22 (Q7, 24 marks), N22 (Q8, 24 marks), M24 (Q8 Var A / Q10 Var B, 24–29 marks), N24 (Q7, 24 marks), M25 (Q8, 24 marks).

## SUMMARIZE

**MYP definition**: Abstract a general theme or major point(s).

**Typical marks**: Used as a mark scheme descriptor rather than a direct command term in questions. Appears in mark scheme band descriptors at the 1–2 mark level for Discuss and To what extent essays ("summarises information to a limited extent").

**Sessions**: Not a direct question command term across these 13 sessions; appears only in mark scheme language.

---

## SYNTHESIZE

**MYP definition**: Combine different ideas in order to create new understanding.

**Typical marks**: Used as a mark scheme descriptor for the top bands (6–8 marks) of Criterion D in essay questions. Not usually a direct question command term.

**What examiners mean by "synthesis"**: The conclusion or argument goes beyond listing two perspectives separately — it combines them into a judgment that is *more than the sum of its parts*. A synthesis in a Discuss essay might say: "While the period shaped Ford's opportunities, it was Ford's specific response to those opportunities that made the difference — neither factor alone explains his significance." This is synthesis because it creates a new understanding by combining both perspectives.

**Where synthesis is required**: At 6–8 marks for Criterion D in all extended essays. In N22 and N23, the mark scheme explicitly states: "The synthesis (combining ideas) could appear in the main body of the essay or in the conclusion."

**Sessions**: Referenced as a band descriptor in all extended essay mark schemes (N19 onward). Also appears as a direct term in M24 official command term list.

## Mark scheme progressions — quick reference

The mark schemes consistently use the same language hierarchy. Understanding this hierarchy unlocks every question:

| Mark scheme word | What it means | Typical marks |
|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| Does not reach standard | Response not relevant or empty | 0 |
| States | One sentence, a specific fact or name | 1 |
| Identifies | Names something from a list or source | 1 |
| Outlines | States + brief elaboration (one sentence with two clauses) | 2 |
| Describes | Detail without comprehensive reasoning (short paragraph) | 3 |
| Explains | Detail + cause-effect reasoning ("because…") | 4 |
| Justifies | Argument + reasoning for why the conclusion holds | 4 |
| Analyses | Breaks down components, interprets relationships | 4–6 |
| Evaluates | Weighs strengths and limitations with overall appraisal | 8 |
| Synthesises | Combines perspectives into new understanding | Top essay band |
| Thoroughly explains | Full causal chain, accurate named examples, appropriate terminology throughout | Top band of extended questions |

## Common examiner phrases decoded

| Mark scheme phrase | What it means for you |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| "Quality and depth is more important than length" | One well-developed point beats three vague ones every time |
| "Accept any valid response" | Your own historical examples are fine; the mark scheme list is not exhaustive |
| "Spelling errors must not impact marks" | Typos and misspellings are ignored — focus on content and reasoning |
| "Only one strength/limitation needs to be mentioned" | Do not list five weak points; develop one strong point fully |
| "Suggesting alternative methods can be interpreted as addressing limitations" | Saying "they should have also used X" counts as identifying a limitation |
| "Best-fit model" | If you meet most but not all of a band's criteria, you still get that band |
| "Does not need to be a concluding statement" | The appraisal (Evaluate) can appear anywhere in the response |
| "The student may have demonstrated an excellent understanding, however this does not mean the response must be perfect" | Full marks are achievable without covering every possible angle |
| "A verbatim quote from the source cannot be considered as additional detail" | Copying the source word-for-word earns no extra credit (M23 Q6b) |
