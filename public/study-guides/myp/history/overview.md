## Exam format summary

The MYP History on-screen examination is a single paper of approximately 70–82 marks across 8–13 questions. All questions are compulsory. Responses are typed into the on-screen system. Visual/interactive stimuli (videos, slideshows, notebooks) are embedded.

## Criterion A — Knowing and understanding

**Where it appears**: Every paper. Short-answer recall questions (2–4 marks), extended writing tasks (6–10 marks), and essays.

**Mark-scheme progression**:
- 1 mark: Student **states** a fact or example (one sentence)
- 2 marks: Student **outlines** — identifies with brief elaboration
- 3 marks: Student **describes** — detail without comprehensive reasoning
- 4 marks: Student **explains** — detail + cause/effect reasoning
- For higher-mark bands (6–10): same logic scaled up
  - 1–2: Limited (outline level)
  - 3–5: Adequate (description level)
  - 6–7: Substantial (explanation with accurate examples)
  - 8–10: Detailed (thorough explanation, causal chain, appropriate terminology)

**What examiners penalise**:
- Bare identification with no elaboration
- Missing causal reasoning at the explain/thorough levels
- Vague or inaccurate terminology

## Criterion B — Investigating

**Where it appears**: Every paper. Usually 12–20 marks across 5–7 sub-questions.

**Standard structure** (appears in virtually every session):
1. **Formulate** a research question (2 marks) — must be both *clear* AND *focused*; just rephrasing the SoI = 0 marks
2. **Justify** the relevance of the RQ to the SoI (4 marks) — escalates from state → outline → describe → justify
3. **State** or **List** primary and secondary sources (1–3 marks each)
4. **Explain** source usefulness / why a method was chosen (3–4 marks)
5. **Evaluate** an investigative process (8 marks) — always marked as Strengths (0–3) + Limitations (0–3) + Appraisal (0–2)

**Key Criterion B rules**:
- Research question must be both **clear** (intention easily understood) AND **focused** (specific enough — named person, place, or time); one without the other = 1 mark
- Rephrasing the statement of inquiry = 0 marks for the RQ
- Evaluate (8 marks): strengths-only or limitations-only caps at 3 marks; combining both + appraisal needed for 7–8
- Suggesting alternative methods counts as identifying a limitation
- Quality over quantity — one well-explained strength beats three stated ones

## Criterion C — Communicating

**Where it appears**: Extended writing tasks (essays, speeches, articles, presentations, podcast scripts, social media responses, emails, biographies, journal entries, tour guide scripts).

**Sub-criteria always assessed together**:

**C1 — Format** (0–2 marks for short tasks; up to 3 for essays)
- Tests whether the student has used the correct format for the task type
- For essays: introduction + body + conclusion as *separate paragraphs* = 2 marks; missing one element = 1 mark; no elements = 0
- For creative tasks: presence of required structural features (e.g. title + sub-headings; greeting + sign-off; hashtag + salutation)

**C2 — Communicating information and ideas** (0–3 to 0–6 marks)
- Tests style and language appropriateness for the given format and audience
- Rarely appropriate (too informal or too formal for context) = 1–2
- Occasionally appropriate = 3–4
- Consistently appropriate = 5–6
- Emotive language, rhetorical questions, appropriate register all rewarded

**C3 — Organizational structure** (0–3 to 0–8 marks)
- Tests logical flow and paragraph/section transitions
- Rarely → Occasionally → Frequently → Consistently effective
- Must use effective transitions between ideas; paragraphs must build on each other

## Criterion D — Thinking critically

**Where it appears**: Source analysis questions (6–8 marks) and the big essay (24 marks).

**Source analysis patterns** (appears in every paper):
- **State origin** (1 mark): Who/what created the source; must be specific
- **State purpose** (1–2 marks): What the source is trying to do; "to inform an audience" is always rejected as too vague
- **Explain value/limitation** (3–4 marks): Must reference origin *or* purpose; escalates from state → outline → describe → explain
- **Compare/contrast** or **Analyse** sources (4–8 marks): Must use both sources; similarities-only or differences-only caps at 4/6

**Essay (24 marks)**: Almost always a "To what extent" or "Discuss" question. Requires:
- At least 2 perspectives (positive AND negative; or two competing factors)
- At least one named historical example
- A conclusion synthesising both perspectives
- Top band (7–8): both perspectives balanced, synthesis convincing, examples accurate and effective

## Mark scheme universal rules

1. **Best-fit model**: Examiners choose the level that *overall* best matches the work. Not every aspect needs to be met. Partial performance across two bands resolves to the better-fitting band.
2. **Mark positively**: Reward what is correct; do not deduct for errors or irrelevant material.
3. **Spelling errors never penalised**: Explicitly stated across all sessions from M21 onwards.
4. **Quality over quantity**: One well-explained strength beats five vague ones. The mark scheme says this explicitly for every Evaluate question.
5. **Open content tolerance**: "Accept any valid response" appears for List and State questions; students are not restricted to the mark scheme's examples.
6. **Cap rules**:
   - Evaluate strengths-only or limitations-only: capped at 3 marks
   - Compare/contrast with only similarities or only differences: capped at 4 (out of 6) or 4 (out of 8)
   - RQ that rephrases the SoI: 0 marks
   - Essay with only one perspective: capped at 3–4 for Criterion D
   - If student uses actual historical figure in biography task (M23): capped at 3 for Criterion A

## Revision priorities

### Tier 1 — Highest frequency (every paper)

- **Criterion B skills**: Formulating research questions (clear + focused), justifying to a SoI, evaluating investigative processes (strengths / limitations / appraisal)
- **Source analysis**: State origin, purpose; explain value or limitation with reference to origin/purpose; compare/contrast two sources
- **Scientific/technical innovation**: Have 2–3 detailed case studies ready (e.g. steam engine, penicillin, washing machine, nuclear energy, wristwatch, printing press)
- **Essay structure**: Introduction + body + conclusion in separate paragraphs; formal language; effective transitions

### Tier 2 — Very frequent (10+ of 13 sessions)

- **Independence movements**: Know at least 2 movements in depth (e.g. Indian independence, American Revolution, Ghanaian independence) — causes, key events, outcome, national identity angle
- **Pioneers/innovators**: One individual ready with: their period context, their innovation, short and long-term impacts, how the period shaped them
- **Industrialization**: Causes (economic, technological, political, social) and impacts (child labour, urbanization, environmental) for at least one country (Britain is the model)

### Tier 3 — Frequent (5–9 sessions)

- **Social protest movements**: Causes, methods (violent vs. non-violent), outcomes, supporting groups
- **Empires and superpowers**: One empire + one superpower/supranational organisation; how they influenced people; peace and stability argument
- **Globalization**: Benefits and drawbacks; at least one historical trade example (Silk Road, Atlantic slave trade, British opium trade)

### Tier 4 — Occasional (2–4 sessions)

- **Ideological movements**: Fascism, Nazism, Communism, Maoism — key beliefs and impacts
- **Health and medicine** timeline: Black Death response (14th century) vs. germ theory/antibiotics (19th–20th century)
- **Significant individuals**: Mandela, Gandhi, Hitler, Stalin — contrasting leadership styles; sacrifice; legacy

## Key examiner language to know

These phrases appear in mark scheme band descriptors — understanding them signals what depth is needed:

- **"does not reach standard"** = 0 marks
- **"states"** = one sentence, no elaboration needed
- **"outlines"** = states + brief elaboration (2 marks)
- **"describes"** = detail without comprehensive reasoning (short paragraph)
- **"explains"** = detail + reasoning/cause-effect (paragraph with "because…" logic)
- **"thoroughly explains"** = multiple causal chains, accurate examples, appropriate terminology throughout
- **"satisfactory"** = adequate but not exemplary; minor gaps in depth or accuracy
- **"accurate and effective"** = specific, correct, well-deployed to support the argument
- **"appropriate relevant terminology"** = using the right historical vocabulary in the right context (not just listing terms)
- **"quality and depth of response is more important than length"** = don't pad; don't list 5 weak points
