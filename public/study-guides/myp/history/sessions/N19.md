Eventually, some of the policies that were imposed by the US in Latin American countries in the 1990s created instability and much discontent in those countries.* *Even though not all countries benefited in the same ways, the world as a whole became wealthier when dominated by a single power than by two opposing powers, as in the Cold War. And during this period the world was much more stable than it is today, as with only one power deciding the rules, there is less conflict around the world.* |

**Criterion D — Essay Writing Skills (up to 16 marks)** — assessed across three sub-criteria (same rubric used for Q6 Criterion C):

**D1 — Format (up to 2 marks)**:
- Note: Response must be in essay format — paragraph form required. A conclusion paragraph/statement should summarise/synthesise arguments made in the essay.
- 0: Does not reach standard
- 1: Content is structured in a way that is **occasionally appropriate** to the essay format — two of the following elements: introduction, a main body of argument, or a conclusion
- 2: Content is structured in a way that is **consistently appropriate** — all three elements present: introduction, main body of argument, and conclusion

**D2 — Communicating Information and Ideas (up to 6 marks)**:
- 0: Does not reach standard
- 1: Style and language **rarely appropriate** — writing is too informal and/or oversimplified or unclear for an essay
- 2: Style and language **occasionally appropriate** — not always consistent with what would be expected in an essay; may appear more informal; may use colloquialisms or first person
- 3: Style and language **consistently appropriate** — consistent with what would be expected in an essay; text has a formal tone throughout; language is formal and clear

**D3 — Organizational Structure (up to 4 marks)**:
- 0: Does not reach standard
- 1: Organization is **rarely effective** — ideas rarely distinct and linked in a logical manner; paragraphs sometimes build on each other
- 2: Organization is **occasionally effective** — ideas occasionally build on each other in a logical manner; sometimes uses effective transitions within sentences and between sentences and/or paragraphs; some relationships between ideas shown
- 3: Organization is **frequently effective** — ideas frequently build on each other in a logical and convincing manner; clear links between context and developments discussed; uses effective transitions

## Key Patterns

1. **Dual-school research scenario**: The paper is structured around a paired investigation between two schools on World Health Day — one investigating the *positive* impact of innovation (student's own school) and one investigating the *negative* impact (the partner school, focusing on Chernobyl). This scenario threads through Q3, Q4, and Q5.

2. **"Museum of the People" framework for Q6**: The Roaring Twenties room is shown as a model, then students must write a tour guide script for a *different* period they have studied. This is a creative output question requiring knowledge transfer, not just recall of the stimulus.

3. **Quality over quantity principle**: The mark scheme repeatedly states "The quality of the response is what is rewarded, not the number of [reasons/factors/strengths]" — particularly for Q4, Q8a, Q8b. Students are not rewarded for listing multiple weak points.

4. **Consistent markband vocabulary**: Descriptors escalate predictably — State → Outline → Describe → Explain → Justify (for lower-mark questions) and Limited → Adequate → Substantial → Detailed (for higher-mark questions). This language is standardised across the paper.

5. **Open-ended content tolerance**: The mark scheme explicitly accepts "any valid response" for Q1, Q5a, and Q5b — these are not restricted to particular historical events or periods.

6. **Research skills focus**: Five of the nine questions (Q3a, Q3b, Q4, Q5a–c) directly assess historical investigation methodology — formulating research questions, evaluating methods, identifying sources, and improving research plans. This reflects MYP Criterion B weighting.

7. **Source analysis pairing (Q8)**: Q8 uses a single text source (Colombo Telegraph / Palmerston) and splits into value (8a) and limitation (8b) sub-questions for 3 marks each — a common pattern for source evaluation in this paper.

8. **Essay breadth requirement (Q9)**: The essay requires consideration of *at least one* empire, superpower, or supranational institution/organization, but the mark scheme examples predominantly use the US as a superpower post-Cold War, suggesting this is a common student choice the examiners modelled responses around.

9. **Interactive/multimedia stimulus**: Q4's notebook is described as interactive in the original digital exam, showing a two-page notebook with clickable contents. Paper-09 and paper-10 show the notebook pages as static images.

10. **No stimulus for Q9**: The 24-mark essay is entirely knowledge-from-memory — no sources provided, testing pure historical argument construction with examples drawn from the student's own learning.
