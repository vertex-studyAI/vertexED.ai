### Q1 (2 marks) — Outline — Superpowers and Supra-national Institutions

**Question**: Outline **one** reason for the creation of **one** superpower **or** supra-national institution/organization in history.

**Stimulus**: None.

**Mark scheme**:
- **0 marks**: Does not reach any descriptor.
- **1 mark**: Student *states* one reason for the creation of one superpower/empire/supra-national institution/organization in history. Response is a single statement of their choice. Example: *The UN was set up after World War II.* / *The USSR became a superpower because of policies of Joseph Stalin.*
- **2 marks**: Student *outlines* one reason for the creation of one superpower/empire/supra-national institution/organization. Elaboration with some brief detail is required. Example: *The UN was set up in order to prevent future wars and conflicts to promote world peace. The USSR became a superpower because of expansionist policies of Joseph Stalin to spread communism.*

**Examiner notes**: Answer must name a specific superpower or institution and give a reason (not just name it). For 2 marks, student must elaborate on the reason with at least some brief detail.

---

### Q2 (4 marks) — Explain — Empires

**Question**: Explain how **one** empire influenced the people over which it ruled.

**Stimulus**: None.

**Mark scheme**:
- **0 marks**: Does not reach any descriptor.
- **1 mark**: Student *states* how one empire influenced the people it ruled over. Example: *The British Empire eliminated the people through military control.* (one sentence, hints at influence — positive or negative; no detail needed on the empire or people ruled).
- **2 marks**: Student *outlines* how one empire influenced the people it ruled over. Example: *The British Empire dominated the people of India through its military control as well as through its system of government.* (2-mark response is likely a single sentence with two clauses or two/three short sentences; brief detail about empire's influence on the people).
- **3 marks**: Student *describes* how one empire influenced the people it ruled over. Example: *The British Empire dominated the people of India with its military control, violently suppressing uprisings like the Amritsar Massacre. The system of government and the laws implemented also influenced the Indian population.* (Detail without comprehensive reasoning; an example may support the description but is not a formal requirement for 3 marks).
- **4 marks**: Student *explains* how one empire influenced the people it ruled over. The response should be detailed and provide reasoning about the empire's influence. The response might make a connection between cause and effect. Example: *The British Empire dominated the people of India with both its military control, violently suppressing uprisings like the Amritsar Massacre. The system of government and the laws implemented also influenced the Indian population. Eventually this dominance **influenced the growth of a nationalist movement that aimed to challenge Britain and be independent from British rule**.* An example may be used to support the explanation but is not a formal requirement to achieve 4 marks. Quality and depth matter more than length.

**Examiner notes**: Any empire is acceptable. The key at 4 marks is reasoning about the influence — cause-and-effect connections. Examples strengthen but are not mandatory.

### Q3 (12 marks total) — Research Methods: Global Interactions

**Context/Stimulus provided for Q3**: As part of MYP studies, the student has explored the global context of **identities and relationships**. The teacher has asked students to plan an investigation on global interactions in history. The results will be used to write an article for the school magazine.

**Statement of Inquiry (given)**: *"Global interactions cause significant impacts on the identities and relationships of the stakeholders involved."*

---

### Q3a (2 marks) — Formulate — Research Question

**Question**: Formulate a clear and focused research question to investigate the statement of inquiry.

**Mark scheme**:
- **0 marks**: Does not reach any descriptor.
- **1 mark**: Student formulates a research question that is **either** clear **or** focused and connected to the statement of inquiry. Example: *How did the outcome of WWII lead to the Cold War?* (The RQ is clear, easily understandable and connected to global interactions, but is not dealing with a specific relationship or interaction between stakeholders — it is focused on an outcome, not impact on identities/relationships.)
- **2 marks**: Student formulates a research question that is **both** clear **and** focused **and** connected to the statement of inquiry. Example: *How did tensions between the US and USSR following WWII lead to the Cold War?* (The RQ is focused on a specific relationship/interaction and clearly refers to identities and relationships.)

**Examiner notes**:
- Students do **not** have to mention both identities and relationships for the RQ to be linked to the SoI — "identities" or "relationships" may be stated explicitly.
- The research question must also be set in the context of global interactions.
- If the research question is a rephrasing of the SoI (e.g., *To what extent did global interactions impact the identities and relationships of stakeholders?*), award **0 marks**.
- An answer that is purely domestic (not involving global interactions) should be awarded fewer marks.

---

### Q3b (4 marks) — Justify — Relevance of Research Question

**Question**: Justify the relevance of your research question to the statement of inquiry:
*"Global interactions cause significant impacts on the identities and relationships of the stakeholders involved."*

**Mark scheme**:
- **0 marks**: Does not reach any descriptor.
- **1 mark**: Student *states* the relevance of the research question to the SoI. Example: *My research question deals with the relationship between two superpowers.* (Single sentence; no detail about the event required.)
- **2 marks**: Student *outlines* the relevance of the RQ to the SoI. Example: *My research question deals with the change in relationship between two superpowers as a result of their interactions after WWII.* (Single sentence with brief detail providing a single factor.)
- **3 marks**: Student *describes* the relevance of the RQ to the SoI. Example: *My research question deals with the change in relationship between two superpowers as a result of their interactions after WWII. By investigating the key events between these two countries and how they dealt with tension between them, we can see how they went from allies to enemies.* (Provides brief detail and some reasoning linking the RQ to the SoI.)
- **4 marks**: Student *justifies* the relevance of the RQ to the SoI. Example: *My research question deals with the change in relationship between two superpowers as a result of their interactions after WWII. By investigating the key events between these two countries and how they dealt with tension between them, we can see how they went from allies to enemies. **This will help me determine how significant their interactions were to the start of the Cold War and is a good example of how global interactions can impact relationships.*** (At least one argument/factor should be considered in detail, along with reasoning why the RQ is relevant to the SoI.)

**Examiner notes**:
- Students do **not** have to mention both identities and relationships.
- Students should **not** be penalized if their response to 3b does not match their stated RQ in 3a.
- Examiners should **not** re-read the Q3a response before reading Q3b.

### Q4 (8 marks) — Evaluate — Investigative Process

**Question**: As part of the class activity to plan an investigation on global interactions in history, your teacher gave one of your classmates the following statement of inquiry:

*"A country's identity can influence how it interacts with other countries."*

This student has already completed their investigation and finished writing their article for the school magazine. Your teacher has given you their investigative process and asked you to evaluate it. Look at the investigative process shown below and answer the question that follows.

**Evaluate** the investigative process carried out by your classmate.

**Stimulus — The classmate's investigative process** (shown as a timeline with four stages):

- **Day 1**: Came up with a research question: *What were the key reasons for the expansion of European empires in the nineteenth century?* Started to develop sub-questions.
- **By Week 2**: Found answers to the following sub-questions:
  - To what extent did nationalism contribute to the expansion of empires?
  - How did the empires come to an end?
  - What role did the empires play in the start of World War I?
- **By Week 3**: Conducted research using the following books and articles:
  - New World Encyclopedia, 2016. *"History of the British Empire"*. (https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/British_Empire)
  - Wesseling, H. L., 2004. *The European Colonial Empires 1815–1919*. London: Routledge.
  - Hyam R., 2010. *Understanding the British Empire*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
  - Reviewed findings and wrote first draft of the magazine article.
- **By Week 4**: Revised findings with additional research:
  - Darwin, J., 2009. *The Empire Project: The Rise and Fall of the British World System 1830–1970*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
  - Perras, A., 2006. *Carl Peters and German Imperialism 1856–1918: A Political Bibliography*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.
  - Evans, R. J. W., 2006. *Austria, Hungary, and the Habsburgs*. Oxford: Oxford University Press.
  - Updated the magazine article with references and bibliography.
  - Completed reflection of the investigation process.

**Mark scheme — Criterion B**:

**Strengths** section:

- **0 marks**: Does not reach any descriptor.
- **1 mark**: Student *states* the strength(s) of the investigative process. Example: *The research question is appropriate to the statement of inquiry.* (A (1 mark) response can be one sentence that refers to a single strength.)
- **2 marks**: Student *outlines* the strength(s) of the investigative process. Example: *The research question is appropriate to the statement of inquiry. It **focuses on European empires and by asking for the main reasons for their expansion**, it can show us how much their identity played a role in their actions toward expansion of European empires.* (Only one strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on to warrant 2 marks; if a student outlines three or more strengths, they can also be awarded 3 marks.)
- **3 marks**: Student *explains* the strength(s) of the investigative process. Example: *The research question is appropriate to the SoI. It focuses on European empires and asking for the main reasons for their expansion by investigating the key events from the nineteenth century. **This means the investigation should produce valid results that are supported by evidence based on several factors from a variety of European empires in the nineteenth century.*** (Only one strength is needed; if a student outlines three or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks.)

**Limitations** section:

- **1 mark**: Student *states* limitation(s) of the investigative process. Example: *The sub-questions do not help answer the research question.* (A single limitation; the second and third divert attention away from the focus on reasons for expansion of European empires.)
- **2 marks**: Student *outlines* the limitation(s). Example: *The second and third divert attention away from the focus on the reasons for expansion of European empires.* (If a student outlines three or more limitations, they can also be awarded 3 marks.)
- **3 marks**: Student *explains* the limitation(s). Example: *The sub-questions do not help answer the research question. The second and third divert attention away from the focus on the reasons for expansion by including evidence and details that are not relevant to the investigation and therefore produce less credible results.* (If a student outlines three or more limitations, they can be awarded 3 marks.)

**Appraisal**:

- **1 mark (appraisal stated)**: The investigation process is good but needs to focus more on the reasons for expansion. (A brief summary of strengths/limitations or a brief overall judgment on the investigative process. The appraisal can appear anywhere in the response; it does not have to be a concluding statement.)
- **2 marks (appraisal outlined)**: Overall, although the investigation process has a strong research question, it needs to be more focused on the reasons for expansion by **improving the sub-questions for the investigation to be more successful**. (The appraisal weighs up both positive and negative points; makes an overall judgment; can appear anywhere.)

**Examiner notes**: Students do not need to discuss both strengths and limitations of every part of the investigative process to access full marks. For best-fit marking, responses may be awarded the same mark for equivalent demonstrations at different levels.

However, source C gives a commentary along with images to provide more detail on the outcome of the WTO protests. Source D does not show the impacts of the protest. **The sources differ in terms of the methods of protesting they show: Source C shows more violence being used against police and businesses, while Source D suggests the civil rights march was more peaceful. This allows the student to understand that protests occur for many different reasons.*** (Specific details of the content of both sources are used to compare and contrast; references to usefulness in studying protest movements.)
- **6 marks**: Student *compares and contrasts* the usefulness of the sources **thoroughly**. Example adds: *Both sources are primary sources and provide a visual to give the student direct accounts of the events. However, source C gives a commentary along with images to provide more detail on the outcome of the WTO protests. Source D does not show the impacts of the protest. The sources differ in terms of the methods of protesting they show: Source C shows more violence being used against police and businesses, while Source D suggests the civil rights march was more peaceful. This allows the student to understand that protests occur for many different reasons. Additionally, Source C uses emotive language [...] **to make clear claims about their usefulness** to the student — both through similarities and differences, supported by specific details from both sources.* (There should be in-depth coverage of similarities and differences at this level, using specific details of the content of both sources to make clear claims about their usefulness. More than length, quality and depth provided matter.)

**Examiner notes**: At marks 2–3, the focus should be on comparing and contrasting the sources' features, not just stating what each source contains. At marks 4–5 and 6, students must use specific details from both sources and relate these clearly to usefulness for studying protest movements. A short paragraph is likely to be required to provide the required level of detail at 6 marks.

### Q8 (24 marks) — To What Extent — Social Protest Movements Essay

**Question**: **To what extent** do you agree that gaining support from different groups in society is the most important factor in making sure a social protest movement is successful?

In a well-structured essay, you should:
- refer to **at least one** social protest movement from your MYP history studies
- consider **at least two** other factors that could lead to a successful social protest movement, such as:
  - the methods used (violent *vs.* non-violent, for example)
  - the cause being protested about
  - the time period in which the protest takes place.

**Note**: you can refer to factors other than those given as examples above.

## Key Patterns

1. **Broad open-choice questions dominate Q1 and Q2**: Students choose their own superpower/institution (Q1) and any empire (Q2), giving maximum flexibility. No single period of history is prescribed for these.

2. **Research skills are heavily examined (Q3 + Q4 = 20 marks)**: Q3 walks through the full investigative cycle — formulating an RQ, justifying it to the SoI, selecting sources, and explaining usefulness. Q4 asks students to evaluate a *classmate's* process — a meta-cognitive task requiring identification of strengths, limitations, and an appraisal. The classmate's RQ ("What were the key reasons for the expansion of European empires in the nineteenth century?") has a clear weakness: the sub-questions (how empires ended, role in WWI) diverge from the stated focus on reasons for expansion.

3. **Q5 is the major creative writing task (18 marks)**: The political commentary format tests Criterion C explicitly. Students must write in first-person, from the perspective of a politician during industrialization, balancing positive and negative arguments. Source A (American factory conditions ~1900) and supplementary sources on Meiji Japan, British child labour (1836), and Robert Owen's reforms provide material. The sources span c.1836–1900 and Japan as well as Britain/America, giving students wide choice.

4. **Two sources for source analysis in Q6**: Source B is a 2019 retrospective news article (ABC News/Associated Press) about the 1999 Seattle WTO protests — a secondary source written 20 years after the event, making it particularly interesting for value/limitation discussion in Q6c. Its purpose (to inform about lasting impact) and origin (mainstream news, not a participant) are key to analysis.

5. **Q7 cross-source comparison (Source C vs Source D)**: Source C is an eyewitness TV broadcast transcript about the Seattle WTO protests (1999) — a primary source with dramatic, emotive language. Source D is a photograph from the 1963 civil rights march on Washington — a different era, different methods (more peaceful). The comparison invites discussion of violent vs. non-violent protest, different protest eras, and the nature of visual vs. audio-visual evidence.

6. **Q8 is the big essay (24 marks)**: "To what extent" question about social protest movements. The question gives students considerable freedom — they can discuss any protest movement from MYP studies. The scaffold explicitly mentions: methods (violent vs. non-violent), cause, and time period as possible "other factors." The mark scheme rewards synthesis (combining perspectives into a coherent argument) at the highest levels.

7. **Consistent markband logic across questions**: For questions using markbands (Q2, Q3b, Q3e, Q4, Q5, Q6c, Q7, Q8), the progression is: *state → outline → describe → explain*. The key differentiators are: depth of detail (outline = brief detail; describe = detail without reasoning; explain = detail + reasoning/cause-effect).

8. **Source B is a 2019 article about a 1999 event**: Examiners specifically note that students must go beyond generic purpose ("to inform an audience") to get the mark for Q6b — they must specify what the article informs about (the impacts/lasting legacy of the WTO protest movement).

9. **No stimulus/sources for Q1 and Q2**: These are pure knowledge recall questions testing Criterion A without any source scaffold.

10. **Digital exam format**: The paper uses interactive digital features (click to reveal timeline stages in Q4, scroll-to-continue in some source pages). This is a fully online MYP e-assessment, not a traditional paper exam.
