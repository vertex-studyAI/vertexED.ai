## Question 1 — Research project design (18 marks total)

**Context / Stimulus**: A teacher assigns a research project investigating how an ordinary individual of the student's choice responded to a significant event in history.

**Statement of inquiry (SOI)**: *"It is not only events that make the study of history significant, but how individuals respond to those events."*

**Teacher voice message (transcript)**: The individual chosen must be someone who has NOT made it into history books — not Winston Churchill, Edward Jenner, Martin Luther King etc. Could be a grandparent who served in a war, a local leader who helped their community during a crisis, or an imagined individual from a dramatic period.

**Messaging app clarification**:
- NOT ALLOWED: Queen Elizabeth I, Winston Churchill, Edward Jenner, Joan of Arc, Martin Luther King, Joseph Stalin, Mahatma Gandhi
- ALLOWED: a grandparent/relative, a local leader, a factory worker, a peasant, a soldier

## Question 4 (2 marks) — Identify — Italian Renaissance cultural change

**Context / Stimulus**: **Source A** is a video about the Italian Renaissance (transcript provided). Students watch a video and answer the question.

**Source A transcript**:
"During the Renaissance which began in the 14th century, Italy rediscovered the cultures of Ancient Rome and Greece. It inspired artists like Botticelli and Raphael to create extraordinary works of art that celebrated these cultures. This new style of art relied on new techniques that made it more natural and realistic; the aim was to capture the beauty of the world as it really was. Renaissance thinkers believed in human potential and had a more optimistic view of what life had to offer. However, in Renaissance Italy, most women from the upper classes had only two options in life: marriage or becoming a Christian nun, both of which required a dowry. Once married, an upper-class woman was expected to take care of the home and have children. She was also responsible for the education of her young children. These children were later sent to school, or in the case of young girls, sometimes boarded at convents. There, the nuns taught them sewing, reading and writing. Poorer women had a hard life, working in the fields or in cities as servants. Many turned to prostitution to earn money or joined convents to work as servants for the nuns."

**Glossary**: Nun: a member of a religious community of women; Dowry: an amount of property or money given to the church or a husband; Convents: a place where nuns lived together

**Question**: **Identify two** examples of cultural change in **Source A**.

**Mark scheme** (Criterion D):
- Accept any valid response (1 mark per example, up to a maximum of 2 marks)
- Responses may include:
  - People had a more optimistic view on life
  - New techniques in art
  - The rediscovered Ancient Greece and Rome
- **Note**: References to **women's roles** during the Renaissance (paragraphs 3–5) do not relate to cultural change and should **not** be awarded any marks

  - There is no requirement to include an example but the lack of one does not mean the student cannot be awarded (1 mark)
  - The response will show little or no evidence of examples being used to help inform the student's argument; the argument may not always be relevant or appropriate
- **3–4**: Student provides different perspectives in an analysis and summarizes information to make clear arguments in response to the question posed
  - Two perspectives (intellectual/ideological movements and conflicts) must be included with development of these to show an understanding of both and their implications; the two perspectives do not have to be equally considered/balanced
  - A conclusion should include an overall decision on whether movements or conflicts bring about more change (students may conclude that they are equally impactful), which is likely to be not fully formed at this level
  - The response should include evidence of examples being used to help inform the student's argument
- **5–6**: Student provides different perspectives in an analysis and synthesizes information to make clear arguments in response to the question posed
  - Two perspectives (intellectual/ideological movements and conflicts) must be included with substantial development of these to show a good understanding of both and their implications; the two perspectives do not have to be equally considered/balanced
  - Ideas on movements and conflicts are combined to form a clear, coherent and convincing conclusion debating whether which brings about the most significant change is required (students may conclude they are equally impactful)
  - The response should include evidence of examples being used to help inform the student's argument
- **7–8**: Student provides different perspectives in a detailed discussion and synthesizes information to make clear arguments in response to the question posed
  - Two perspectives (intellectual/ideological movements and conflicts) must be included with substantial development of these to show an excellent understanding of both and their implications
  - Ideas on movements and conflicts are combined to form a clear, coherent and convincing conclusion whether intellectual and ideological movements bring about the most significant change is required (students may conclude that they are equally impactful)
  - The response should include evidence of examples being used to help inform the student's argument

**Criterion C1 — Format** (for essay writing, marks 0–2):
- **0**: Does not reach a standard
- **1**: Two of the following elements are included: Introduction; Main body of text; Conclusion — the elements must appear as separate paragraphs
- **2**: Three of the following elements are included: Introduction; Main body of text; Conclusion — the elements must appear as separate paragraphs

**Criterion C2 — Communicating information and ideas** (marks 0–3):
- **0**: Does not reach a standard
- **1**: The style and language used are rarely appropriate to the audience and purpose of informing — the language is too informal AND/OR the language is oversimplified or unclear for an essay
- **2**: The style and language used are occasionally appropriate — style of writing is most consistent with what would be expected in an essay; it may appear to be more informal, for example using colloquialisms or first-person expression
- **3**: The style and language used are consistently appropriate — style of writing is completely consistent with what would be expected in an essay, for example using third-person expression; language is formal and clear throughout

**Criterion C3 — Organizational structure** (marks 0–3):
- **0**: Does not reach a standard
- **1**: The organization structure is rarely effective — ideas rarely build and linked in a logical manner; when appropriate, the student rarely uses effective transitions within sentences and/or between sentences/paragraphs to show relationships between ideas; ideas are rarely distinct and linked in a logical manner
- **2**: The organizational structure is occasionally effective — ideas occasionally build on each other in a logical manner, making use of paragraphs where appropriate; the student sometimes uses effective transitions within sentences and between sentences/paragraphs to show relationships between ideas; paragraphs/ideas build on each other in a mainly logical manner
- **3**: The organizational structure is consistently effective — paragraphs and ideas build on each other in a consistently clear, logical and convincing manner, making use of paragraphs when appropriate; uses effective transitions within sentences and between sentences/paragraphs to show relationships between ideas; ideas are distinct, and are linked in a consistently clear, logical and convincing manner

## Key patterns

1. **Open-ended investigation design (Q1)**: Q1 is entirely student-generated — students bring their own chosen individual and event, making it fully personalized. The mark scheme accommodates any ordinary individual (e.g., William O'Connor / Great Depression is used as the worked example throughout). This is unlike most source-based history exams; the entire first question tests the research process rather than a particular topic.

2. **"Ordinary individuals" constraint**: The paper explicitly directs students away from famous historical figures; this is a defining feature of MYP History's focus on personal investigation and the perspective of marginalized or everyday people.

3. **Interdisciplinary language skills (Q3, Q7)**: Two of the largest questions are extended writing tasks marked on format, communication, and organizational structure as well as historical knowledge (Criterion C sub-criteria). This is distinctive — format marks reward proper formal email conventions and essay structure separately from content.

4. **Multi-source single-topic cluster (Q4–Q7)**: Questions 4–7 all draw from the same source cluster (Sources A, B, C) on the Italian Renaissance and WWII women, linking across vastly different periods to explore the common theme: *intellectual/ideological movements vs conflicts as agents of change*.

5. **Lavinia Fontana perspective analysis (Q5)**: Students must view a statement from Source A specifically through the lens of a named historical individual from Source B — a dual-source comparative analysis framed as a perspective exercise. The mark scheme explicitly requires both agreement and disagreement perspectives at high mark levels.

6. **Source value/limitation framed for a specific audience (Q6)**: The question asks for value/limitation specifically to "an MYP Year 5 student studying the impact of World War II" — the mark scheme penalizes general statements about reliability and requires relevance to that specific audience and purpose.

7. **Extended essay has five assessment components (Q7)**: Criterion A, Criterion D, C1, C2, C3 are all separately marked, making Q7 the most heavily weighted question (24/80 marks = 30%) and the most multi-dimensional in terms of what is assessed.

8. **Action plan evaluation (Q2)**: Students assess a peer's real-sounding action plan about Pastor André Trocmé and the Holocaust — the mark scheme rewards analysis of both strengths and limitations with an overall appraisal weighing both sides. This mirrors the internal assessment (IA) evaluation skills.

9. **No named "correct" answers for Q1**: The mark scheme makes clear that "the individual/event does not have to be explicitly mentioned in the response" — students are not penalized for naming different individuals or events, as long as the response quality meets the descriptor.

10. **DRAFT watermark**: The mark scheme is labelled as DRAFT — indicating this is a pre-standardization version. Examiners would use this as a starting guide but refine it after seeing actual student responses at standardization meetings.
