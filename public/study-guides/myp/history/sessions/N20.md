## Question 1 (2 marks total) — Stimulus: TV documentary transcript

**Context/Stimulus**: Students are cast as researchers for a TV documentary series on the significance of innovation in history. The first episode covers the invention of the microscope. A transcript is provided from an unfinished episode:

> "It is unclear who should be given credit for inventing the microscope. And over time, it has provided us with improved knowledge so that we can better combat disease. Like many other innovations and developments in history, the microscope has made further discoveries possible and provided new ways of overcoming the challenges facing society."

## Key patterns

1. **Documentary framing device**: The entire paper is structured around a TV documentary about innovation, with Q1–Q4 all tied to this narrative frame. This is characteristic of MYP contextual learning — students are "researchers" producing a documentary, not just answering abstract history questions.

2. **Scale progression within questions**: Most multi-part questions (Q2, Q3, Q4b, Q4c, Q6c) follow the same four-level pattern of State → Outline → Describe → Explain, rewarding increasing analytical depth at each mark level.

3. **Two distinct topics in the same paper**: Questions 1–5 focus on **innovation/invention** (microscope, pioneers, globalization); Questions 6–8 shift to **industrialization** (child labour in Britain, Japan's industrialization, causes of industrialization globally). This reflects the MYP approach of assessing across different historical topics in a single sitting.

4. **Source A is a social media infographic** (a "Photogram" post by a history teacher): This is a deliberately modern/contemporary source format, testing students on OPVL for a non-traditional source type. The mark scheme notes that 'rosajune16' or 'Instagram' alone are not acceptable as origins — students must identify the creator's role/credentials.

5. **Source B uses a visual comparison (then/now images)**: The Japan Q7 uses two annotated illustrations rather than a text extract, requiring students to "read" a visual source and analyse change over time — a less common stimulus format.

6. **Q5 is the most complex question (18 marks)**: It is simultaneously a content task (Criterion A), a communication task (Criteria C2 and C3), and a cartographic/visual task (Criterion C1 — annotated world map). The map annotation is assessed as a separate format criterion. The model answer uses the **Atlantic Slave Trade** as the exemplar example.

7. **Q8 model answer** focuses on Britain and the **steam engine/technological factor** for the top band (7–8 marks), with emphasis on James Watt's improvements, the factory system, and Britain's emergence as "the workshop of the world."

8. **Q3 is a pure Criterion B question** (investigation evaluation): No content knowledge is tested — marking is entirely on the quality of evaluation of the research process (purpose, research question/sub-questions, sources). Students are explicitly told they do NOT need to address both strengths and limitations to access full marks.

9. **Q4a carries a hard zero condition**: If there is no link between the chosen innovator and the episode title ("Where you are matters as much as who you are"), the response receives 0 marks — an unusually strict condition that students must be aware of.

10. **Total mark breakdown by criterion**: A = ~32 marks (Q2, Q3 partial, Q4c, Q7, Q8); B = ~14 marks (Q3 full, Q4a, Q4b, Q4d); C = ~18 marks (Q5 communication+format, Q8 format+communication); D = ~6 marks (Q6c, Q7, Q8 critical thinking component). (Exact allocation varies as some questions are assessed on multiple criteria simultaneously.)
