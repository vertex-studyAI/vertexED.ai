# MYP History — November 2023 (N23)

**Session**: November 2023
**Paper code**: histmmoeengtz0xxm (on-screen examination)
**Total marks**: 70
**Format**: On-screen examination, 8 questions (one multi-part)

## Paper structure overview

The paper has two main thematic sections:

**Section 1 — Industrialization (Questions 1–5)**
Framed around an MYP student conference on the theme *"Innovations are most useful when they arrive at the right time, place and space."* Students evaluate a peer group's investigative process on the Industrial Revolution in 18th-century Britain, then carry out their own related research tasks and produce a creative journal response.

**Section 2 — Independence and National Identity (Questions 6–8)**
Focuses on personal and cultural expression of independence and national identity during the 20th century. Uses primary and secondary visual/textual sources about the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (India) and Ghanaian post-independence national identity. Culminates in a 24-mark essay on factors contributing to the success or failure of independence movements.

### Q2 (8 marks) — Evaluate — Historical Investigation Process

**Context / Stimulus**: A group of MYP students is investigating the Industrial Revolution in 18th-century Britain for a conference on the theme *"Innovations are most useful when they arrive at the right time, place and space."* Their investigative process is shown across 4 steps:

- **Step 1 — Research Question**: *"What were the key reasons for the Industrial Revolution starting in 18th century Great Britain?"*
- **Step 2 — Key sub-questions**: When did the Industrial Revolution begin? / What were the key characteristics of 18th-century Britain? / Who were the significant innovators in Britain during the Industrial Revolution?
- **Step 3 — Research Methods**: Mind map of possible reasons (Economic causes, Political causes, Technological causes, Social causes, Other causes), with six sources listed:
  - Perkin, H. J. (1969). *The Social Causes of the British Industrial Revolution.* Transactions of the Royal Historical Society, pp. 123–143. Cambridge University Press.
  - Taucar, C.E. (2014) *The British System of Government and its Historical Development.* McGill-Queen's University Press.
  - Kiger, Patrick J. *7 Negative Effects of the Industrial Revolution.* https://www.history.com/news/industrial-revolution-negative-effects
  - Stearns, Peter N. (2021). *The Industrial Revolution in World History.* New York: Routledge.
  - Outman, James L.; Outman, Elisabeth M. (2003). *Industrial Revolution: Primary Sources.* UXL.
  - Hughes, Tristan. *10 Key Inventions of the Industrial Revolution.* History Hit, published 17 Apr. 2020. www.historyhit.com/key-inventions-of-the-industrial-revolution/
- **Step 4**: Evaluate sources used
- **Step 5**: Present the key reasons for the Industrial Revolution in order of importance

**Question**: **Evaluate** the investigative process undertaken by the team.
In your answer, you may consider:
- the relevance of the research question
- the choice of sub-questions
- the research methodology
- the sources consulted.

**Mark scheme**: Marked in three components — **Strengths** (0–3), **Limitations** (0–3), **Appraisal** (0–2). Maximum = 8 marks.

Note: Students do not need to discuss both strengths and limitations of every part of the investigation to access full marks. Marks are awarded based on quality of the response, not number of strengths or limitations referred to.

**Strengths (0–3)**:
- **0**: Does not reach a standard described by any descriptor.
- **1**: The student *states* the strength(s) of the investigative process. Only one strength needs to be mentioned to warrant 1 mark. A 1-mark response can be one sentence referring to a single strength (e.g., *"The student identifies the different reasons."*).
- **2**: The student *outlines* the strength(s) of the investigative process. One strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on to warrant 2 marks. However if a student outlines 3 or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks. A 2-mark response is likely a single sentence with two clauses or two/three short sentences; detail will be brief. (e.g., *"The student identifies the different reasons by breaking them down through a mind map."*)
- **3**: The student *explains* the strength(s) of the investigative process. One strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on in additional detail with reasoning to warrant 3 marks. A 3-mark example: *"The student identifies the different reasons by breaking them down through a mind map. This is a good idea because by clarifying the different reasons it makes it easier to find the most important one."* If a student outlines 3 or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks.

**Limitations (0–3)** (Note: suggesting alternative methods can be interpreted as addressing limitations):
- **0**: Does not reach a standard.
- **1**: The student *states* the limitation(s). One limitation needs to be mentioned. A 1-mark response is one sentence that hints at a single challenge (e.g., *"Only including one source for most of the reasons is insufficient."*).
- **2**: The student *outlines* the limitation(s). One limitation needs to be mentioned and elaborated on. A 2-mark response is likely a single sentence with a single challenge that is elaborated on (e.g., *"Only including one source for most of the reasons is insufficient. A wider variety of perspectives would improve the investigation by strengthening the evidence which could lead to a more convincing conclusion."*).
- **3**: The student *explains* the limitation(s) with additional detail with reasoning to warrant 3 marks. (e.g., *"Only including one source for most of the reasons is insufficient. A wider variety of perspectives would improve the investigation by strengthening the evidence which could lead to a more convincing conclusion."* + additional reasoning). If a student outlines 3 or more limitations, they can be awarded 3 marks.

**Appraisal (0–2)**:
- **0**: Does not reach a standard.
- **1**: The student *voices* an appraisal of the investigative process. The appraisal is likely to be a brief judgement (e.g., *"The research statistical process is adequate."*). The appraisal can appear anywhere in the response; it does not have to be a concluding statement.
- **2**: The student *outlines* an appraisal of the investigative process. The appraisal statement will weigh up both positive and negative points (this may be done implicitly or explicitly) and make an overall judgement on the students' investigative process. (e.g., *"The research method process is adequate. It has a good structure but for the findings to be more credible, more sources are needed."*). The appraisal can appear anywhere.

### Q5 (18 marks) — Explain / Creative — Historical Journal Entries

**Context**: Historians can gain a lot of information by investigating primary sources, such as the personal journals of significant individuals, when investigating a historical event. Personal journals provide first-hand evidence of what a significant individual was thinking or how they felt during a turning point in history. They also provide a valuable perspective on events that help the historian gain a better understanding of why events occurred as they did.

**Question**: Imagine that you are a significant individual in history: someone who challenged a political, economic or social system. Write **three** separate journal entries to **explain** your thoughts and feelings on the system you were challenging.

Your journal entries must include:
- An accurate and detailed perspective of your chosen individual regarding a specific political, economic or social system
- References to the effect(s) the system had on your chosen individual and their society **and** how they wanted to change it
- References to how your chosen individual succeeded, or failed, in their efforts to challenge the system.

**Structure**: No one specific way is required. Students may choose:
- Option 1: Entry 1 — Before a key event; Entry 2 — During a key event; Entry 3 — After a key event
- Option 2: Entry 1 — During event A; Entry 2 — During event B; Entry 3 — During event C
- Option 3: Entry 1 — Initial perspective on the system; Entry 2 — Development of perspective on the system; Entry 3 — Further development of perspective on the system

Students provide: Name of chosen individual / Date of journal entry 1 / Journal entry 1 / Date of journal entry 2 / Journal entry 2 / Date of journal entry 3 / Journal entry 3.

**Mark scheme**: 18 marks total across three criteria.

**Criterion A — Knowledge and Understanding (0–8)**:

Note: Students should mark positively; terminology/dates should not be assessed for the format, style or organisation criteria. Note for Criterion A: students should mark all three journal entries only. Criterion A does not assess format, style, or organization.

- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The student demonstrates limited knowledge and conceptual understanding by providing limited examples and limited terminology. Responses are likely to be 'very brief', with little detail. There is a requirement to include an example but the lack of one does not mean the student cannot be awarded 1 mark.
- **2–3**: The student demonstrates some knowledge and understanding by describing a significant individual's perspective of a system, using basic examples and some terminology. Example of 2-mark response: *"I am here in Paris the night before the Peace Conference is about to begin and what a large job I have in front of me. - **4–5**: The student demonstrates adequate knowledge and understanding by describing a significant individual's perspective of a system, using satisfactory examples and appropriate terminology. Example of 4-mark response includes named individual (e.g., *"Name of Individual: Woodrow Wilson / Date: Jan 1st 1919"*) and detailed entry showing knowledge of the individual's perspective on the system by providing a detailed response. *"I am here in Paris the night before the Peace Conference is about to begin and what a large job I have in front of me. ### Q6 (6 marks total) — Source analysis — Jallianwala Bagh / Independence

**Context and Source A**: The Singh Twins are contemporary British artists behind several award-winning paintings. In April 2019 they unveiled their artwork *Jallianwala: Repression and Retribution* (Source A) in the Manchester Museum in the United Kingdom to mark the 100th anniversary of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919. It depicts the moment on 13th April 1919, when Reginald Dyer, a General of the British Raj, ordered his troops to open fire, without warning, on a peaceful demonstration of un-armed Indian people in the city of Amritsar, Punjab, in northern India. The protesters were Indian nationalists protesting against the British government policy that forced Indians to serve in the army, and also protesting against the heavy war tax imposed against the Indian people. Some historians consider the outrage that followed the massacre a significant step towards the end of British rule in India.

### Q7 (6 marks) — Analyse — Sources B and C — Ghana's National Identity

At this level, students *should provide detailed evidence from the sources* that Ghana's national identity can be expressed in different ways — e.g. political and artistic. (e.g., *"In Source B the author shows how Kwame Nkrumah has taken a political approach and used basic tenets of nationhood, inspired by his international experience, to express Ghanaian national identity such as through rich traditions, a national flag, currency and anthem. While in Source C the artist Wiz Kudowor uses traditional African symbolism to express Ghanaian national identity through art so that Ghanaians develop pride in their heritage when celebrating their independence from colonial rule. This demonstrates how symbolism is significant in expressing national identity, such as using ancient symbols derived from the rich cultural heritage and depicting the way of life and aspirations of Ghanaians in addition with the usual symbols of nationhood such as the flag and anthem. To reach this mark band, students should provide detailed evidence from the sources that Ghana's national identity can be expressed in different ways."*) The two sources do not need to be covered in equal depth to be awarded 6 marks. Implicit/explicit connection(s) between the sources may be made but are not essential to be awarded 6 marks.

### Q8 (24 marks) — Discuss — Essay — Independence Movements

**Context / Stimulus**: The success or failure of independence movements in history were dependent on certain factors. Some possible factors are provided in the following diagram: **Leadership**, **Protest**, **Culture**, **Religion**, **Ideology** (pie-chart style diagram labelled "Factors contributing to the success **or** failure of an independence movement").

**Question**: **Discuss** the **two** most important factors that have contributed to the outcome of **one or more** independence movements.

In a well-structured essay, you:
- can choose to refer to **one or more** independence movement(s)
- may use the factors shown in the diagram **or** other factors from your MYP History studies
- must provide explanations and examples to support your arguments.

**Mark scheme**: 24 marks total across four criteria. Note — if a student only discusses one factor, the maximum mark that can be awarded is 6 marks (in the knowledge criterion D).

**Criterion D — Knowledge and Understanding (0–8)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1–2**: The student demonstrates limited conceptual and contextual knowledge and understanding in an explanation, providing only general or vague points limited to the question asked. Responses are likely to be 'very brief' with little detail. There is a requirement to include an example but the lack of one does not mean the student cannot be awarded a mark in this band. Terminology may be limited.
- **3–4**: The student demonstrates adequate contextual and conceptual knowledge and understanding in an explanation, using satisfactory examples and appropriate terminology. Candidates to support their point(s) relating to their chosen independence movement(s) and factor(s) are accurate but might be lacking in detail or not fully developed. Terminology may be limited. Some reasons may be provided but not sufficient enough to earn a mark in the higher band. To be awarded (3 marks): a short paragraph is likely to be required. Terminology should be accurately used in the correct context.
- **5–6**: The student demonstrates substantial contextual and conceptual knowledge and understanding in an explanation, using effective examples and relevant terminology. At this level, students need to demonstrate a very good understanding of the independence movement(s) and factors they've chosen by providing a well-substantiated argument. The student should call on relevant examples that support their point(s). At this markband, students are likely to provide evidence of understanding of both of the factors and their influence on the outcome of the movement. Terminology could be accurately used in the correct context some of the time.
- **7–8**: The student demonstrates detailed contextual and conceptual knowledge and understanding in an explanation, using thorough and effective examples and appropriate terminology. The response should be very detailed and provide clear reasoning, making use of example(s). A (7–8 mark) response should reference at least one independence movement and clearly state two reasons that support the point(s) being made. A (7–8 mark) response should include at least one independence movement and two other factors that influenced the outcome of the independence movement. Terminology should be accurately and effectively used in the correct context all of the time. The student may have demonstrated an excellent understanding; however this does not mean that the response must be perfect or the best example of a response you have seen; there are multiple ways of demonstrating ability.

**Criterion D additional note**: If a student only discusses one factor, the maximum mark that can be awarded is 6 marks.

**Criterion C1 — Format (0–2)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard described.
- **1**: Two of the following elements are included: introduction, a main body of argument or a conclusion. Note: The elements must appear as separate paragraphs. If this is not the case, 0 marks should be awarded.
- **2**: Three of the following elements are included: introduction, a main body of argument and a conclusion. Note: The elements must appear as separate paragraphs.

**Criterion C2 — Communicating information and ideas (0–3)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The style and language used are *rarely* appropriate to the audience and purpose of informing. The style of writing is rarely consistent with what would be expected in an essay. Language may be mostly unclear. Text may be extremely short so that not enough evidence is available to award a higher level.
- **2**: The style and language used are *occasionally* appropriate to the audience and purpose of informing. The style of writing is not always consistent. It may appear to be more informal in style than expected in an essay. Language may be less clear in places.
- **3**: The style and language used are *consistently* appropriate to the audience and purpose of informing. The style of writing is consistent with what would be expected in an essay. Language is formal and clear.

**Criterion C3 — Organizational structure (0–4)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The organizational structure is rarely effective. Paragraphs/ideas sometimes build on each other in a logical manner. The student rarely uses effective transitions within sentences and/or between sentences and/or paragraphs to show relationships between ideas.
- **2**: The organizational structure is occasionally effective. Paragraphs/ideas sometimes build on each other. When appropriate, the student sometimes uses effective transitions within sentences and/or between sentences. AND/OR paragraphs to show relationships between ideas.
- **3**: The organizational structure is mainly effective. Paragraphs/ideas frequently build on each other in a logical manner. When appropriate, the student often uses effective transitions within sentences and/or between sentences and/or paragraphs to show relationships between ideas.
- **4**: The organizational structure is consistently effective. Ideas and paragraphs consistently build on each other in a clear and logical manner. When appropriate, the student always uses effective transitions within sentences and/or between sentences and/or paragraphs to show relationships between ideas.

**Criterion D additional guidance** (from mark scheme):

- At the 3–4 band: *"clear arguments in response to the question posed. A conclusion indicating why the two factors are the most important is expected (students may conclude that they are equally important). The response must include evidence of understanding of both factors. Factors and their influence on the outcome of the movement. Terminology may be limited in terms of accuracy and/or frequency."*

- At the 5–6 band: *"The student provides different perspectives in a detailed response and synthesises information to form a clear and cohesive argument [...] using accurate examples being used and appropriate relevant terminology. Reasons why these factors are the most important is required (students may conclude that they are equally important). The two factors must be covered in a fairly equal manner. Synthesis combining ideas could appear in the main body of the essay or in the conclusion. The response should include absence of examples being used effectively to inform and support a thorough description of the individual."*

- At the 7–8 band: *"The student provides different perspectives (thoroughness) in a detailed response and synthesises information to form a clear and cohesive argument [...] using accurate examples and relevant terminology. Reasons why these factors are the most important is required. The synthesis combining ideas could appear in the main body of the essay or in the conclusion."*

## Key patterns

1. **Two distinct thematic sections**: The paper cleanly divides into Industrialization (Qs 1–5) and Independence/National Identity (Qs 6–8), a pattern that requires students to demonstrate breadth across very different historical periods and topics.

2. **Heavy weighting on extended response**: Q5 (18 marks) and Q8 (24 marks) together account for 42 of 70 marks (60%). Both require sustained, well-structured writing. Students who perform poorly on either of these will struggle to achieve high overall marks.

3. **Q5 is a unique creative task**: The journal-entry format (three separate dated first-person entries) tests both historical knowledge and communication skills simultaneously. It is assessed on three criteria: Knowledge (8 marks), C1 Format (2 marks), C2 Communication (4 marks), C3 Organisation (4 marks). This is the only question in the paper assessed against communication criteria alongside knowledge.

4. **Q8 essay is assessed on four criteria**: Knowledge (D, 8 marks) + C1 Format (2) + C2 Communication (3) + C3 Organisation (4) = 17 raw marks scaled to 24. Students must write a formal essay structure (intro, body, conclusion in separate paragraphs) or the C1 format mark is 0.

5. **Q2 evaluative process task**: This is a distinctive MYP-specific question type that assesses student understanding of historical inquiry methodology — evaluating a peer group's investigative process rather than writing about content. It is subdivided into Strengths (3), Limitations (3), and Appraisal (2).

6. **Source work in Q6 and Q7 is tightly contextualised**: Source A (Jallianwala Bagh painting by the Singh Twins, 2019) is used for Q6a/b/c; Sources B (Nkrumah academic article) and C (CGTN news transcript on Wiz Kudowor) are used only for Q7. No cross-section comparison between Section 1 and Section 2 sources is required.

7. **"Best fit" marking model**: The mark scheme explicitly states that examiners apply a "best fit" approach — not every aspect of an achievement level must be satisfied for the mark to be awarded. This rewards students for overall quality rather than checklist compliance.

8. **Spelling errors explicitly not penalised**: The mark scheme notes that *"spelling errors should not impact the marks awarded in any part of the examination."*

9. **Open independence movement choice for Q8**: Students may refer to any independence movement(s) from their MYP History studies — the question does not prescribe a particular movement. This rewards students who have studied any of the standard movements (Indian independence, Ghanaian independence, American Revolution, etc.).

10. **Q4 is a scaffolded research design task**: The sub-questions (4a–4e) systematically walk students through formulating a research question, justifying it, identifying source types, and anticipating difficulties — mirroring the structure of a genuine historical inquiry. This reflects a core MYP Criterion B (Investigating) skill set.
