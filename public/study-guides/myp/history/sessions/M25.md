### Q6 (6 marks total) — Scientific & technical innovation / daily life

**Context**: "Aside from playing a key role in large-scale historical events, scientific and technical innovations have had a significant impact on the daily life of individuals. Respond to the questions below using information from **Source A** and **Source B**."

**Source A**: Excerpt from the History.com website, "The Washing Machine: Killing Off 'Wash Day'":
> "At the dawn of the 20th century, most women could expect to devote an entire day to washing (and drying) their families' clothes by hand, using big pots of boiling water and a scrub board. > Then came Frederick Maytag, a partner in a farm equipment business. In 1907, he was looking for new products to sell that would help the company ride out agriculture's [falling profits]. > "A frozen, ready-made dinner doesn't really feel like progress, compared with healthy home-cooked food. But a washing machine is neat and efficient and replaces work that was always drudgery. How could it not have been revolutionary?

When it took all day to wash and dry a few shirts, people would use replaceable collars and cuffs or dark outer layers to hide the grime on their clothes. But we cannot skip many meals in the way that we can skip the laundry. When it took two or three hours to prepare a meal, that was a job someone had to be willing to do. The washing machine didn't save much time, and the ready meal did, because we were willing to stink, but we weren't willing to starve."

## General examiner notes

- **Best fit model**: Examiners choose the achievement level that overall best matches the work. Not necessary for every detailed aspect to be satisfied. If a student's work spans multiple markbands, compensation depends on performance in higher-order skills.
- **Mark positively**: Reward what is correct; do not take marks away for incorrect material.
- **Spelling errors**: Should not impact marks awarded in any part of the examination.
- **Partial credit**: If a student outlines 3 or more strengths/limitations (Q3), they can be awarded the higher mark in that sub-section.

## Key patterns

1. **Consistent overarching theme**: The entire paper is unified under a single theme — the impact of scientific and technical innovations and discoveries on society (both positive and negative). Every question connects back to this theme, which is unusual and coherent.

2. **Two contrasting SoIs in parallel**: Q2 explores "positive" impact (SoI for one group) while Q3 explores "negative" impact (a second group). This creates a deliberate tension and requires students to think from both perspectives across the paper.

3. **Industrial Revolution as the dominant historical focus**: Questions 3, 4, and implicitly Q1 all use child labour and the Industrial Revolution as their contextual content. This suggests a school/teacher decision to study this particular topic during the year.

4. **Source-heavy questioning**: Q6 and Q7 both test source analysis skills in different ways — Q6 tests understanding and contrast of two written sources; Q7 tests value and limitation of a single source. Q4 asks students to select sources themselves and justify their usefulness.

5. **Question 5 is the creative task (18 marks)**: Unusually high marks (18) for a social media response format. It is assessed on three criteria — historical knowledge (Criterion A, up to 6), format (Criterion C1, up to 2), communication (Criterion C2, up to 6), and organisation (Criterion C3, up to 4).

6. **Question 8 is the full essay (24 marks)**: The essay requires engagement with both macro-level (major historical events) and micro-level (daily life) impacts of scientific/technical innovation. Students must include at least one example of each. Assessed on Criterion A (up to 8), C1 Format (up to 3), C2 Style (up to 3), C3 Organisation (up to 4), and Criterion D (up to 8 — though the MS shows the combined C total aligns).

7. **Dropdown source selection in Q4**: Students choose their sources from a dropdown menu (Source 1–6) in the on-screen exam format, making this a personalised response. The mark scheme covers all source options generically.

8. **Washing machine as case study**: The specific focus on Frederick Maytag and the washing machine (Q6) as the technology innovation for daily life is notable — it allows rich source-based contrast work.

9. **Wristwatch and WWI (Source C, Q7)**: The choice of the wristwatch as a war-related innovation affecting daily life is an engaging and specific case study; the source is a university lecture excerpt, allowing testing of source type and reliability.

10. **Research methodology skills explicitly tested**: Q2 (formulating a research question and justifying it) and Q3 (evaluating an investigation process with Strengths/Limitations/Appraisal) together constitute a substantial methodology section (14 marks) — a strong indicator that historical inquiry skills are central to the MYP History course assessment.
