## Context / Scenario

The paper is built around **two broad inquiry contexts**:

1. **Q1 (Criterion A) + Q2 (Criterion B)**: Social protest movements — the student outlines a movement they have studied (example used throughout: the women's liberation movement, 1960s–70s).
2. **Q3 (Criterion B)**: A historical investigation conducted by a French historian (Professor Sen) in 1968, involving a survey of Parisian public opinion on social protest movements.
3. **Q4 (Criterion A + C)**: A creative task — write an informative online article for the school website about one innovation from the timeline of history (10 000 BC–2005 AD), explaining short- and long-term human development significance. Audience: MYP Year 5 students.
4. **Q5 (Criterion D)**: A video — "Globalization For All" — analysed for usefulness to a MYP student studying globalization.
5. **Q6 (Criterion D)**: Analyse Ferguson's views on imperialism from the perspective of the NGO Globalization For All.
6. **Q7 (Criterion D)**: Compare and contrast Source C and Source D in terms of how they portray global interactions involving the US.
7. **Q8 (Criterion A + C + D)**: Extended essay — discuss the argument that increased interconnectedness in history (driven by reasons including wealth, power, curiosity, ideas, goods, technology) has been beneficial for peoples and societies.

### Q3 (12 marks) — Evaluate — Investigative Process (Criterion B)
**Question** (reconstructed from MS): Evaluate the process of the investigation undertaken by the French historian in 1968.

*In your response you should consider:*
- *its relevance to a historical study on social protest movements*
- *the lessons that could be learned about the investigative process by Professor Sen*

**Context**: Professor Sen surveyed Parisian public opinion; the survey included number of people surveyed, range of ages represented, close to even gender split, different occupational groups. Rating scale used. Questions focused on social protest movements. Flaw: survey did not allow people to explain their decisions.

**Mark scheme**:
| Marks | Descriptor | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 0 | Does not reach standard | |
| 1–3 | **Limited** evaluation by **outlining** the strengths **or** weaknesses of the investigation process | "The strengths of the investigative process that Professor Sen could use in her study include the number of people surveyed, the range of ages represented, a close to even split among gender and a number of different occupational groups. The questions are focused on the topic of social protest and are therefore relevant to a study on social protest movements. The rating scale is easy to understand." (3 marks) |
| 4–6 | **Limited** evaluation by **outlining** the strengths **and** weaknesses | Above strengths PLUS: "because it was only a survey the questions did not allow people to explain their decisions." (6 marks) |
| 7–9 | **Describes** the strengths and weaknesses, providing an **argument** | "The strengths of the investigative process … the Professor should avoid repeating some of these mistakes. For example, the questions did not allow the respondents to clarify." (7 marks). Conclusion: useful for Professor Sen to learn lessons about conducting surveys about historic events. |
| 10–12 | **Thorough** evaluation by **explaining** the strengths and weaknesses, providing a **detailed** argument | Full analysis of both strengths (demographics, scale, relevance) and weaknesses (no open-ended questions, only quantitative data, timing relative to events, not primary-source historical evidence). Conclusion synthesises both sides: "the study is limited in its relevance to a historical study as a standalone source but is valuable to Professor Sen because she could learn lessons from it about conducting surveys about historic events." |

### Q8 (30 marks) — Discuss — Interconnectedness Essay (Criterion A + C + D)

**Question** (reconstructed from MS): In a well-structured essay, discuss the argument that increased interconnectedness in history resulting from these reasons has been beneficial for peoples and societies.

*Possible reasons for increased interconnectedness given on the paper:*
- to increase wealth
- to conquer / exert individual or national power
- a curiosity for the world
- to share or spread ideas
- to exchange or obtain goods
- the development of suitable technologies

*In your essay you should:*
- tell the reader what you think are the **two most important reasons** for the world becoming more interconnected throughout history (from the list or from your own knowledge)
- provide examples and explanations from history to support your arguments
- write about how increased interconnectedness **impacted different individuals and societies** in history
- consider **different perspectives and counter-arguments** to the benefits of increased interconnectedness

**Criterion A — Knowing and understanding (strand ii)**:
| Marks | Descriptor | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 0 | Does not reach standard | |
| 1–2 | **Adequate** knowledge and understanding by **identifying** reason(s) for increased interconnectedness with **minimal** examples and **limited** relevant terminology | "One of the most important reasons for increased interconnectedness in history has been human beings' desire to increase wealth, like during Columbus' explorations." |
| 3–5 | **Adequate** knowledge and understanding by **describing** the reasons for increased interconnectedness, using **satisfactory** examples | "One of the most important reasons … has been human beings' desire to increase wealth, like during Columbus' time … Another important reason was the development of necessary technologies, such as the caravel and new navigational tools like the astrolabe." |
| 6–7 | **Substantial** knowledge and understanding by **explaining** the reasons for increased interconnectedness, with **accurate** and **effective** examples | Adds causal chain: "The desire to increase wealth and the role of technological advancements have often been used by the more dominant societies as a way of conquering weaker societies; and profiting from them. This led to the discovery of the Americas by Europeans …" |
| 8–10 | **Detailed** knowledge and understanding and **in-depth explanation** of both reasons, using **accurate** and **effective** examples | Full analysis: desire to increase wealth (European explorations of 15th–16th century, caravel development, astrolabe), weaker societies exploited, disproportionate benefit; most powerful nations used interconnectedness self-interestedly, yet overall progress for the world. |

**Criterion D — strands ii and iv**:
| Marks | Descriptor | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 0 | Does not reach standard | |
| 1–2 | **Summarises** information to a **limited** extent to make arguments; limited interpretation of differing perspectives | "The desire to increase one's wealth, and the role of technological advancements have helped ideas and innovation spread to all parts of the world, and therefore increased interconnectedness has been overall beneficial for individuals and societies." |
| 3–5 | **Analyses** issues/theories; **summarises** information regarding benefits; **interprets** different perspectives and their possible implications | "… the desire to increase both individual and national wealth, and the role of technological advancements from increased interconnectedness … But I without increased interconnectedness, the less developed peoples of the world would not have the opportunity to experience and share in ideas and innovations …" |

### Mark scheme insights
- The **Outline** command requires both identification **and** brief elaboration with context; a bare name = 1 mark only.
- **Evaluate** (Q3, 12 marks) is a multi-strand task: strengths only = capped at 3; strengths + weaknesses = up to 6; adding an argument/conclusion pushes into 7–9 and 10–12 bands.
- **Analyse perspective** (Q6) requires the student to adopt an NGO viewpoint explicitly — generic criticism of Ferguson without invoking the NGO's frame stays in bands 1–2.
- **Compare and contrast** (Q7): responses that give only similarities or only differences are capped at 4 marks.
- The extended essay (Q8) is marked on four separate criteria simultaneously: content knowledge (Crit A), analytical argument (Crit D), communication style (Crit C2), and organisational structure (Crit C3).
- Example innovation in Q4 mark scheme is the **radio** and its Nazi propaganda use — a useful model answer to study.
- The statement of inquiry underpinning Q2 is: *"Historically, social protest movements have succeeded in bringing about positive change to systems."*
