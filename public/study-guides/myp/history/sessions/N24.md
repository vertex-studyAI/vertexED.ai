- **Q1 stimulus**: Teacher presents the question "What motivates individuals to support ideological movements?" with a definition: "a political or social plan for how society should be organized, based on a specific set of beliefs, values, and/or ideas."
- **Q2 stimulus**: Teacher provides a list of factors that could influence support for an ideological movement under the MYP key concept of **time, place and space**: Economic conditions; Education; Family influence; Propaganda and news media; Any others.
- **Q3 stimulus**: Students' investigation summary. Research question: "Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?" Sub-questions: (1) What were the most violent ideologies in the past century? (2) How many of the most devastating wars were started by ideological movements? (3) How do ideological movements create divisions in society? Sources used across four weeks include: *Bloodlands: Europe Between Hitler and Stalin* (Timothy Snyder), *Victims of the Cultural Revolution* / *Cultural Revolution: Testimonies of China's Tragedy* (Wang Youqin), *Nationalism and War* (ed. John A. Hall and Sinisa Malesevic, Cambridge), *Survival in Auschwitz* (Primo Levi), *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (Edmund Burke). Reflection on research findings: "By investigating Nazism in Germany, Stalinism in the Soviet Union, the chaos from the French Revolution and Maoism in China, we have proved that ideological movements cause more problems than they solve."
- **Q4 stimulus**: Podcast transcript — *Middle School Extra* podcast, with a Year 5 student discussing global interactions and the role of supra-national organizations. The host's question: "Just how successful have supra-national organizations or institutions been in reducing conflict and promoting peace in history?"
- **Q5 / Q6 stimulus — Source A**: Interactive slideshow titled **"Nelson Mandela's Most Significant Achievements"** (6 slides): (1) Mandela campaigned for political and social change, stood up to oppressors; (2) Sent to prison in 1962 for 27 years for resisting South Africa's apartheid system; apartheid = separation of White and non-White people in all aspects of society; refused to give up on equality; (3) Released 11 February 1990; became first Black democratically elected president of South Africa in 1994; used words "we", "us", "our" in first presidential speech to unite divided nation; (4) Jointly awarded Nobel Peace Prize in 1993 with FW de Klerk for efforts to end apartheid; (5) Used rugby as symbol of South African unity in a divided nation to foster shared national pride; (6) Established the Nelson Mandela Children's Fund; donated a third of his presidential salary to the Fund.
- **Q6 stimulus — Source B**: Image of **Joseph Stalin** — "Leader of the Soviet Union from 1924 to 1953." Extended text describes Stalin's rule: ruled by terror; expanded secret police powers; encouraged citizens to spy; millions killed or sent to forced labour camps; totalitarian leadership through purges; cult of personality; cities renamed; history books rewritten; subject of flattering artwork, literature, music; name in national anthem; banned/edited photographs; removed former associates through purges; controlled Soviet media. Able to lead for nearly 30 years.

## Questions and mark schemes

### Q1 (3 marks) — Describe — Ideological movements

**Question**: With reference to your MYP History studies, choose **one** example of an ideological movement that fits the definition provided. Name your ideological movement. **Describe** one key idea or belief related to this ideological movement.

**Stimulus**: Teacher presents definition: an ideological movement is "a political or social plan for how society should be organized, based on a specific set of beliefs, values, and/or ideas."

**Mark scheme**:
- **0 marks**: Does not achieve a standard described below.
- **1 mark**: The student states the idea that the ideological movement believed in.
  - Example (1 mark): *Ideological movement: Nazism. They believed in the purity of the German race.* A 1-mark response can simply summarise the belief.
- **2 marks**: The student outlines the idea that the ideological movement believed in.
  - Example (2 marks): *Ideological movement: Nazism. They believed in the purity of the German race by removing non-Germans from the country.* A 2-mark response is likely a single sentence with brief detail on the belief.
- **3 marks**: The student describes the idea that the ideological movement believed in.
  - Example (3 marks): *Ideological movement: Nazism. They believed in the purity of the German race by removing non-Germans, such as Jews. Once Germany was made of only pure Germans, it would become a great empire again.* One belief should be considered in detail; comprehensive reasons not mandatory. An example may be used to support the description. 2–3 sentences likely required; quality and depth more important than length.

### Q3 (8 marks) — Evaluate — Evaluating a student investigation

**Question**: Some students in your class have argued that "most ideologies only create divisions in society, or worse still, revolution". These students carried out an investigation to prove this point. You will be asked to evaluate this investigation.

**Evaluate** the investigation carried out by your classmates.

**Stimulus**: The students' investigation (Research question: "Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?"; three sub-questions; sources across four weeks; final reflection concluding ideological movements cause more problems than they solve — based on Nazism, Stalinism, French Revolution, Maoism).

**Mark scheme** — marked in two sections: **Strengths** (0–3) and **Limitations** (0–3) plus **Appraisal** (0–2). Total = 8 marks.

**Strengths (0–3)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The student states the strength(s) of the investigation. Example: *The sources used all come from credible writers.* Only one strength needs to be mentioned; quality of response is what is rewarded, not number of strengths. A 1-mark response can be one sentence.
- **2**: The student outlines the strength(s). Example: *The sources used all come from credible writers and they are all relevant to the research question and to their specific sub-question.* Only one strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on. A 2-mark response is likely a single sentence with two clauses or two/three short sentences.
- **3**: The student explains the strength(s). Example: *The sources used all come from credible writers and they are all relevant to the research question and to their specific sub-question. The sources and sub-questions all appear to be linked to at least one side of the research question.* One strength in additional detail with reasoning. If a student outlines 3 or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks.

**Limitations (0–3)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The student states the limitation(s). Example: *The students don't consider different perspectives.* Only one limitation; quality rewarded, not number.
- **2**: The student outlines the limitation(s). Example: *They make the assumption that ideological movements always cause more problems than they solved. By wording the RQ as they did, they have not really looked for examples where ideological movements were successful.* Only one limitation mentioned and elaborated on. A 2-mark response is likely a single sentence with two clauses or two/three short sentences.
- **3**: The student explains the limitation(s). Example: *The students don't consider different perspectives. They make the assumption that ideological movements always cause more problems than they solved. By wording the RQ as it is, they have not really looked for examples where ideological movements were successful, so this would result in an investigation that was not objective.* One limitation in detail with reasoning. If a student outlines 3 or more limitations, they can be awarded 3 marks.

**Appraisal (0–2)**:
- **0**: Does not achieve a standard.
- **1**: The student states an appraisal of the investigation. Example: *Overall, the investigation is too biased.* The appraisal statement is likely to be a summary of the strengths and limitations or a brief judgment on the investigative process. Can appear anywhere in the response.
- **2**: The student outlines an appraisal. Example: *Overall, the sources used are credible but because of the way the RQ is phrased, the investigation is too biased.* The appraisal statement will weigh up both positive and negative points (may be done implicitly or explicitly) and make an overall judgment on the students' investigative process.

It should be evident that the student has knowledge of the impact of one factor on historical events; they should be able to discuss one perspective. The examples/terminology in the response may not be sufficient to warrant a higher band mark. Terminology should be accurately used in the correct context.
- **5–6**: The student demonstrates substantial contextual and conceptual understanding by outlining different perspectives in an analysis to make clear arguments in response to the question posed. At least two perspectives (e.g. the impact caused by a significant individual as well as one other factor) must be included with development of at least one, providing a response that is analytical and responds to the question posed. At this level, students need to demonstrate a good understanding of the impact of the significant individual on historical events by providing detailed development of these to show a good understanding and their implications. A conclusion is expected but is likely to not be fully developed at this level.
- **7–8**: The student provides different perspectives in an analysis and summaries information in a thorough discussion and to make clear arguments in response to the question posed. At least two perspectives (e.g. the impact caused by a significant individual as well as one other factor) must be included with thorough development of these to show an excellent understanding and their implications. Ideas on the impacts that an innovation has had on individuals' well-being are combined to form a clear, coherent and convincing conclusion to give a judgment on whether the impact is more positive or negative or equally balanced. The synthesis (combining ideas) could appear in the main body of the essay or in the conclusion. Although the student will have demonstrated critical thinking, this does not mean that the response must be perfect or the best example of a response you have seen; there are multiple ways of demonstrating analytical skills and quite different responses may be awarded the same mark.

## Key patterns

1. **Open-choice ideological movement**: Q1 is entirely open — students choose any ideological movement from their studies. This is unusual as it requires the student to bring their own content without any source prompts. The Nazism exemplar is used throughout the mark scheme as the default illustration.

2. **Staged research methodology section (Q2)**: Q2 walks students through every stage of a historical inquiry in five sub-parts (formulate, justify, describe event, list sources, explain usefulness) — totalling 15 marks. This is the largest block of research-skills questions seen across sessions.

3. **Student investigation as stimulus (Q3)**: The classmates' investigation has a deliberately biased research question ("Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?") with one-sided sources. The mark scheme explicitly identifies the biased RQ phrasing and failure to consider different perspectives as the central limitation to award.

4. **Podcast format for Q4**: Students must write in a school podcast script format, not an essay. The mark scheme penalises essay-like writing (capped at 3/4 for C2) and rewards rhetorical questions, analogies and concise sentences. The question requires the student to choose a supra-national organization themselves (e.g. League of Nations).

5. **Nelson Mandela as the significant individual**: Q5–Q6 all use Mandela as the case study. Source A is an interactive slideshow (visual/digital format) presented as six annotated photograph slides. This is an unusually positive and celebratory source format.

6. **Stalin as contrasting leader in Q6**: Source B on Stalin is used purely as a contrast to Mandela — students are asked to analyse the two leaders' different approaches. The mark scheme highlights the key distinction: Mandela focused on bettering all South Africans; Stalin focused on personal power. The model 4-mark response explicitly uses the bolded contrast phrases.

7. **Q7 essay — pre-2010 time period constraint**: The essay requires a time period prior to 2010, ensuring historical grounding. The question allows any significant individual and any other factor (political, economic, social, or event), giving broad choice.

8. **Mark scheme uses "best-fit" explicitly**: The general marking notes specify that the "best-fit" model applies throughout — examiners choose the achievement level that overall best matches the work; it is not necessary for every detail to be satisfied. This is explicitly noted for Q3 and Q4.

9. **Q3 evaluation structure is highly scaffolded**: The 8 marks for Q3 break down as Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2), with a clear single-point exemplar at each mark level. Notably, the limitation mark scheme cites only the biased RQ as the primary example — no other limitations are mentioned.

10. **Total marks distribution**: Short-answer/research skills (Q1–Q3): 18 marks; Extended/creative writing (Q4): 18 marks; Source analysis (Q5–Q6): 12 marks; Essay (Q7): 24 marks. This gives equal weight to creative/extended communication and analytical essay writing at 18 and 24 marks respectively.
