### Q1 (2 marks) — Outline — Industrialization / Innovation

**Question**: Outline **one** innovation that was developed during a period of industrialization.

**Mark scheme**:
- 0 marks: Does not reach standard of any descriptor.
- 1 mark: States one innovation developed during industrialization (e.g., "The steam engine was invented during the Industrial Revolution"). Name + brief identification required.
- 2 marks: Outlines one innovation — provides a brief account with some detail of what it was and/or its significance (e.g., "The steam engine was developed during the Industrial Revolution. It used steam power to drive machinery, which transformed manufacturing and transport."). A 2-mark response is a single sentence with a single impact briefly elaborated.

**Examiner note**: Students should outline the innovation itself, not just state its name. The context of industrialization must be implicit or explicit.

### Q2 (8 marks) — Evaluate — Investigative process / Industrial Revolution

**Stimulus**: An MYP student team has designed an investigation into the Industrial Revolution in 18th-century Great Britain. Their investigative process is shown across several interactive screens (paper pp. 2–6), including:

- **Step 1 — Research Question**: "What were the key reasons for the Industrial Revolution starting in 18th century Great Britain?"
- **Step 2 — Key sub-questions**: When did the Industrial Revolution begin? What were the key characteristics of 18th century Britain? Who were the significant innovators in Britain during the Industrial Revolution?
- **Step 3 — Research Methods**: Mind map of possible reasons (social causes, political causes, technological causes, other causes). Sources consulted include:
  - Perkin, H. J. (1988). *The Social Causes of the British Industrial Revolution*. Transactions of the Royal Historical Society, pp. 123–143. Cambridge University Press.
  - Taucar, C.E. (2014). *The British System of Government and Its Historical Development*. McGill-Queen's University Press.
  - Kiger, Patrick J. *7 Negative Effects of the Industrial Revolution*. www.history.com/news/industrial-revolution-negative-effects.
  - Hughes, Tristian. *10 Key Inventions of the Industrial Revolution*. History Hit, published 17 Apr. 2020. www.historyhit.com/key-inventions-of-the-industrial-revolution/
  - Stearns, Peter N. (2021). *The Industrial Revolution in World History*. New York: Routledge.
  - Oulman, James L.; Oulman, Elizabeth M. (2003). *Industrial Revolution: Primary Sources*. UXL.
- **Step 4 — Evaluate sources used**
- **Step 5 — Present the key reasons for the Industrial Revolution in order of importance**

**Question**: Evaluate the investigative process undertaken by the team.

In your answer, you may consider:
- the relevance of the research question
- the choice of sub-questions
- the research methodology
- the sources consulted

**Mark scheme** (8 marks — Criterion A: Knowing and understanding, applied to investigative process evaluation):

| Marks | Descriptor | Notes |
|-------|------------|-------|
| 0 | Does not reach standard | |
| 1–2 | States strengths of the research process | Quality is rewarded, not the number of strengths/limitations. Only one strength needs to be mentioned (1 mark). A 1–2 mark response is likely a single sentence that stays focused on the research process but lacks detail of the civil rights/social protest context (can be rewarded if addressing value of the question). |
| 3–4 | Outlines strengths of the research process | Student outlines strengths by selecting important events relevant to the research question. A 3–4 mark response is likely to be a single sentence with two clauses or short sentences providing brief detail on the strengths. |
| 5–6 | Student explains strengths of the research process | Student explains by selecting important events that started the civil rights movement. Explanations address both strengths and limitations. One limitation is mentioned and elaborated. Short-to-mid paragraph. |
| 7–8 | Student explains strengths and comprehensively explains at least 3 or more limitations | Student justifies their evaluation through detailed reasoning. More important than length is quality and depth. |

**Note**: Suggesting alternative methods can be interpreted as addressing limitations.

### Q5 (18 marks) — Explain — Journal entries / Historical individual and a system

**Context**: Historians can gain a lot of information by investigating primary sources, such as the personal journals of significant individuals. Personal journals provide first-hand evidence of what a significant individual was thinking or how they felt during a turning point in history. They also provide a valuable perspective on events that help the historian gain a better understanding of why events occurred as they did.

Imagine that you are a significant individual in history, someone who challenged a political, economic or social system.

**Question**: Write **three** separate journal entries to **explain** your thoughts and feelings on the system you were challenging.

Your journal entries must include:
- an accurate and detailed perspective of your chosen individual regarding a specific political, economic or social system
- references to the effect(s) the system had on your chosen individual and their society **and** how they wanted to change it
- references to how your chosen individual succeeded, or failed, in their efforts to challenge the system

There is no one specific way to structure journal entries. Options provided:

| Option 1 | Option 2 | Option 3 |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Entry 1 – Before a key event | Entry 1 – During event A | Entry 1 – Initial perspective on the system |
| Entry 2 – During a key event | Entry 2 – During event B | Entry 2 – Development of perspective on the system |
| Entry 3 – After a key event | Entry 3 – During event C | Entry 3 – Further development of perspective on the system |

**Response fields**: Name of chosen individual; Date of journal entry 1; Journal entry 1; Date of journal entry 2; Journal entry 2; Date of journal entry 3; Journal entry 3.

**Mark scheme** — assessed on Criterion A (Knowing and understanding) and Criterion C (Communicating):

*Criterion A: Knowing and understanding*

| Marks | Descriptor | Notes |
|-------|------------|-------|

| 1–3 | Demonstrates limited knowledge and understanding | Responses are likely to be brief with little detail — an outline. | 4–5 | Demonstrates some knowledge and understanding | At this level, students need to demonstrate some understanding of the relationship between the concept and the innovation by providing a limited response to the question. The visual aid/diagram/graph should be somewhat relevant and may include the information on the time period. Location (place) and context (space) should be included. Terminology should be accurately used in the correct context. |
| 6–8 | Demonstrates substantial knowledge and understanding | The student should call on adequate examples, which can include the information of the time period, location or the medical innovation itself. ### Q6 (6 marks) — Sources — Jallianwala Bagh / National identity

**Context**: The sources in this task explore personal and cultural expression of independence and national identity during the 20th century.

**Stimulus text** (paper p. 19–21): The Singh Twins are contemporary British artists behind several award-winning paintings. In April 2019 they unveiled their artwork *Jallianwala: Repression and Retribution* (Source A) in the Manchester Museum in the United Kingdom to mark the 100th anniversary of the Amritsar or Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919. It depicts the moment on 13th April, 1919, when Reginald Dyer, a General of the British Raj, ordered his troops to open fire, without warning, on a peaceful demonstration of un-armed Indian people in the city of Amritsar, Punjab, in northern India. The protesters were Indian nationalists protesting against the British government policy that forced Indians to serve in the army. They were also protesting against the heavy war tax imposed against the Indian people. Some historians consider the outrage that followed the massacre a significant step towards the end of British rule in India.

**Source A**: A cropped version of *Jallianwala: Repression and Retribution* by the Singh Twins — a large, densely populated painting depicting the massacre scene with people, soldiers, and symbolic imagery.

### Q7 (6 marks) — Analyse — Sources B and C / Ghana national identity

**Context**: Sources B and C relate to Ghana's expression of national identity since gaining independence in 1957.

The years that he spent living and traveling overseas, particularly in the United States of America and Britain, as well as his studies of modern nations and nationalism, gave him a detailed level of understanding about [...] nation building.*

*He found that there were certain basic tenets of nationhood that modern nation-states had in common [...]*

*Nkrumah felt that Ghana would have to emulate these traditions, albeit in an Africanized way. This included constructing a national narrative that centered on having a glorious historical past and rich traditions, as well as having founding fathers, national heroes, a currency, postage stamps, a flag, an anthem, a coat of arms, museums, and monuments.*

[Source B shows Ghana's coat of arms]

**Source C** (paper pp. 29–30): Transcript of a news interview (CGTN, Accra, Ghana) about Ghanaian artist Wiz Kudowor showcasing his work to celebrate 63 years of the country's independence. Kudowor's paintings are influenced by rich African symbolism. Key quotes:

- *"Wiz Kudowor hopes it will make Ghanaians feel proud about their heritage as they celebrate their independence from colonial rule." — Nabil Ahmed Rifai, CGTN, Accra, Ghana*

## General Examiner Notes (Both Variants)

1. **Best-fit model**: The approach used in assessment is a "best fit" model. The examiner must choose the achievement level that overall best matches the piece of work. It is not necessary for every detailed aspect of an achievement level to be satisfied. If student work spans multiple markbands, compensation depends on performance in higher order skills.

2. **Mark positively**: Examiners should reward what is correct and not take marks away for what is incorrect.

3. **Spelling errors**: Should not impact marks awarded in any part of the examination.

4. **Length vs quality**: More important than the length of the response is the quality and depth provided. Comprehensive responses are rewarded over long but vague ones.

5. **Command term definitions** (from MYP: From Principles into Practice):

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Analyse | Break down in order to bring out the essential elements or structure. To identify parts and relationships, and to interpret information to reach conclusions. |
| Describe | Give a detailed account or picture of a situation, event, pattern or process. |
| Discuss | Offer a considered and balanced review that includes a range of arguments, factors or hypotheses. Opinions or conclusions should be presented clearly and supported by appropriate evidence. |
| Evaluate | Make an appraisal by weighing up the strengths and limitations. |
| Explain | Give a detailed account including reasons or causes. |
| Formulate | Express precisely and systematically the relevant concept(s) or argument(s). |
| Identify | Provide an answer from a number of possibilities. Recognize and state briefly a distinguishing fact or feature. |
| Justify | Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion. |
| Outline | Give a brief account or summary. |
| Select | Choose from a list or group. |
| State | Give a specific name, value or other brief answer without explanation or calculation. |
| Summarize | Abstract a general theme or major point(s). |
| Synthesize | Combine different ideas in order to create new understanding. |
| To what extent | Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument or concept. Opinions and conclusions should be presented clearly and supported with appropriate evidence and sound argument. |

## Key Patterns — M24 Session

1. **Two-variant structure confirmed**: M24 used at least two distinct topic variants (Industrialization/Independence vs. Social Protest/Globalization) within the same exam structure and mark allocation. Students likely selected or were assigned a variant based on the topics they studied.

2. **Consistent 8-question structure across variants**: Both variants have the same skeleton: Q1 outline (2), Q2 evaluate process (8), Q3/Q4a formulate RQ (2), justify RQ (4), primary source (1), secondary source (1), difficulty/usefulness (4), extended task (18), short source analysis (2), medium source analysis (4–6), extended essay (24–29).

3. **On-screen format**: First confirmed on-screen delivery — students typed responses into text boxes, selected radio buttons for true/false and multiple choice (Q3 cause selection, Q4a concept selection, Q6a/6b). This is a significant shift from paper-based exams.

4. **Journal entry task (Variant A Q5)**: Unusual format — students write three dated journal entries from the perspective of a historical individual who challenged a system. Very open-ended with structured response fields. No specific historical figure required — any valid individual acceptable.

5. **Visual aid requirement (Variant B Q5)**: The 18-mark extended task explicitly requires a visual aid (diagram, graph, flow chart) showing the relationship between the key concept (time, place, space) and the chosen innovation in health and medicine. C1 Format criterion (2 marks) specifically assesses this.

6. **True/false questions appear (Variant B Q6a/6b)**: A rare question type in MYP History — straightforward 1-mark select true/false questions about source content. These test close reading skills.

7. **Mark scheme uses example-heavy descriptors**: Every markband includes a concrete example response with the example drawn from civil rights movement scenarios, even when the question topic differs. This signals that markers are trained on specific exemplars.

8. **"Best fit" emphasis**: Markscheme repeatedly stresses quality and depth over length, and "best fit" over checklist marking. Examiners are explicitly told not to penalise for incorrect content, spelling, or shorter responses if the quality is present.

9. **Criterion C assessed on extended tasks only**: C1, C2, C3 are applied to Q5 (18-mark task) and Q8/Q10 (essay tasks). Short-answer and source-analysis questions are assessed on Criterion A only.

10. **No requirement for explicit source citation in Q7**: For the 4-mark "justify using Source A and B" question, the markscheme notes that students don't have to explicitly reference the sources — using the ideas contained in them is sufficient.
