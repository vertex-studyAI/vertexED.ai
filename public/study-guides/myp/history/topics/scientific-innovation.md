# Scientific Innovation

Scientific and technical innovation appears across 6+ sessions — from the microscope and germ theory to the wristwatch, washing machine, and oil spills. Questions test knowledge of specific innovations, source analysis of their impacts, and essay evaluation of whether innovation has always been beneficial. The two key essays (M22 Q7, M25 Q8) require both positive and negative perspectives; M25 Q8 specifically demands one major-event innovation AND one daily-life innovation.

---

## Key concepts

- **Germ theory**: Pasteur's and Koch's 19th-century discovery that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases — overthrew the miasma theory (bad air causes disease) that had dominated medicine since ancient Rome and enabled modern medicine, vaccines, and antibiotics; the M22 Q6 question asks students to *explain* why 14th-century and 20th-century perspectives on disease differ, which requires explaining this development.
- **Black Death**: the bubonic plague that killed up to 50 million people in Europe between 1347 and 1352, reducing the population from ~80 million to ~30 million; 14th-century doctors believed it was God's punishment or caused by bad air — cities that implemented quarantine were more successful but this was rare and not scientifically understood (M22).
- **Wristwatch**: during WWI, synchronized timing was essential for creeping barrage artillery tactics on the Western Front; soldiers wearing wristwatches enabled coordinated infantry-artillery attacks; soldiers brought the habit home after the war and the wristwatch became universal male fashion (Source C, M25 — lecture by a university professor of fashion design to first-year students).
- **Washing machine**: Frederick Maytag's 1907 invention is examined through two contrasting sources in M25 — Source A (History.com) calls it revolutionary for saving time for farm wives; Source B (Tim Harford, *Fifty Things*) argues the real significance was hygiene because people rarely washed clothes before the machine existed, and other innovations like the frozen dinner saved more time.
- **Unintended consequence**: an outcome not planned by the innovator — Alfred Nobel created dynamite for mining and construction but his obituary called him "merchant of death"; chlorine was developed for water purification but was first used as a mass-casualty chemical weapon at Ypres in 1915 (N22 Q6 contrast question).
- **Exxon Valdez oil spill**: a 1989 tanker disaster in Alaska that became a symbol of industrial innovation's environmental costs; NOAA's infographic (N22 Source E) shows the *continued* impact — key limitation: NOAA both produced the source and led the investigation, creating potential institutional bias.
- **Major event vs. daily life innovation**: the M25 Q8 essay requires explicitly addressing BOTH — a major event where innovation was significant (e.g. wristwatch in WWI, penicillin in WWII) AND an innovation that changed daily life (e.g. washing machine, steam engine); missing either = question requirements not met.

## Case studies

### Black Death and germ theory (M22)

- Bubonic plague killed ~50 million in Europe 1347–1352; population fell from ~80 million to ~30 million
- 14th-century doctors: God's punishment or miasma (bad air); remedies included prayer, self-flagellation, burning herbs, bloodletting
- Cities that enforced quarantine were more successful but this was rare and not scientifically understood
- 1840s–1860s: Pasteur developed germ theory — specific microorganisms cause specific diseases, overturning miasma theory entirely
- Koch identified specific disease-causing bacteria; 1880s scientists isolated *Yersinia pestis* and discovered rats' role as carriers
- 1900–1904: San Francisco plague outbreak — 700,000 rats destroyed, ships disinfected using germ-theory methods
- 1940s: antibiotics developed; bubonic plague became treatable
- Source B (M22): unnamed doctor's advice from 1349, Islamic Spain — keep air pure, sleep facing north wind, read the Quran; valuable as a contemporary medical document showing 14th-century thinking from the inside; limited by being anonymous and from one small region

### Chlorine and dynamite — contrasting innovations (N22)

- Chlorine: developed for water purification and industrial use; now used in contact lenses, solar panels, computers, medicines — Source C shows wide-ranging benefits to billions
- Chlorine as weapon: first deployed as a mass-casualty chemical weapon at Ypres, April 1915 — same compound, catastrophic new use
- Alfred Nobel: created dynamite in 1867 for mining and construction, to replace unstable nitroglycerine
- His premature obituary called him "merchant of death" for creating more efficient killing — Nobel established his Peace Prize in response
- Source D (N22): retrospective account of Nobel reading his own obituary — shows the gap between inventor's intention and real-world impact

### Exxon Valdez oil spill (N22)

- 1989 tanker disaster in Prince William Sound, Alaska — one of the worst environmental disasters in US history
- Source E: NOAA infographic showing the *continued* impact on Alaskan wildlife and ecosystem
- Purpose (precise wording required): "to show the **continued** impact of the **Exxon Valdez** oil spill" — "effects of the oil spill" is rejected as too vague
- Key limitation: NOAA both produced the infographic AND led the investigation into the spill — institutional bias; they may minimise their own investigation's failings

### Washing machine (M25)

- Frederick Maytag created the first hand-cranked washing machine in 1907 (electric/gasoline version 1911) during an agricultural downturn, seeking new products
- Source A (History.com): revolutionary time-saver — farm wives previously spent entire days washing by hand; the machine liberated them
- Source B (Tim Harford, *Fifty Things*): people rarely washed clothes before the machine existed — real revolution was hygiene, not time; ready meals and frozen dinners saved more time than washing machines
- Key contrast: Source A = time-saving revolution; Source B = hygiene revolution that also challenges the time-saving claim

### Wristwatch and WWI (M25)

- Creeping barrage: artillery fired a moving wall of shells advancing ahead of infantry; required infantry and artillery to coordinate timing to the minute — wristwatches made this practical
- Before wristwatches, soldiers used pocket watches — impractical in a trench
- After the war, returning soldiers brought the habit home; wristwatches became standard male fashion by the 1920s
- Source C (M25): lecture by a university professor of fashion design to first-year students — specialist perspective on fashion but not a military historian; limited compared to a dedicated military history source

### Scientific innovations — major event vs. daily life

| Innovation | Major event significance | Daily life significance |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Wristwatch | WWI — synchronized creeping barrage tactics | Universal male fashion; changed time-keeping habits |
| Penicillin | WWII — saved millions of wounded soldiers | Transformed disease treatment; raised life expectancy |
| Radar | WWII — aircraft detection; Battle of Britain | Primarily military; civilian air travel later |
| Atomic bomb | WWII — ended Pacific war | Cold War nuclear deterrence; shaped daily lives through fear |
| Microscope | Germ theory → modern medicine | Enabled diagnosis of infectious disease |
| Chlorine | WWI — mass-casualty chemical weapon | Water purification; manufacture of plastics, medicines |
| Washing machine | N/A | Changed hygiene standards and domestic labour for billions |

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- Explaining **WHY** perspectives on disease changed (germ theory, Pasteur/Koch) — not just stating that they changed
- Naming the specific innovation AND the specific context ("chlorine at Ypres 1915" not just "chemical weapons")
- For purpose questions: both the precise event name (Exxon Valdez) AND the ongoing/continued nature of the impact
- In M25 Q8: explicitly addressing both a major-event innovation AND a daily-life innovation
- For contrast questions: genuine differences between sources — what one shows that the other does not

### What examiners penalise

- "To show the effects of the oil spill" — rejected; must name the Exxon Valdez and specify "continued"
- Writing similarities in the N22 contrast question — similarities score zero
- Describing the Black Death without explaining why societies failed to respond (no germ theory, religious framework)
- Not referencing origin/purpose in 3+ mark OPVL answers — the jump from 2 to 3 marks requires it
- Missing either a major-event or daily-life innovation in the M25 Q8 essay

## Common question types

1. **Outline/Explain why societies failed to deal with disease (2–4 marks)** — explain the absence of germ theory and presence of religious explanations; not just a description of the plague itself
2. **OPVL on a single source (3–4 marks)** — must reference origin and/or purpose at 3+ marks; one well-explained value beats a list of weak points
3. **Contrast two sources on innovation impact (3 marks)** — differences only; must address both sources; no marks for similarities
4. **State origin / Outline purpose (1–2 marks)** — origin needs creator + role/credentials; purpose needs specific event + specific aim
5. **24-mark "to what extent" essay (M22 Q7 / M25 Q8)** — two perspectives fully developed + synthesis conclusion; M25 Q8 must explicitly include both major-event and daily-life innovations
6. **18-mark extended writing + visual aid (M24B Q5)** — visual aid must be created AND explicitly referenced in the written response

## Key arguments

### M22 Q7 — Has scientific innovation always brought positive change to health and wellbeing?

**YES — innovation has transformed health:**
- Germ theory (Pasteur/Koch) overturned miasma theory and enabled vaccines, antibiotics, and modern medicine
- Penicillin (1940s) saved millions of lives in WWII; bubonic plague that killed 50 million in the 14th century became treatable
- Microscope enabled identification of disease-causing organisms and transformed understanding of epidemics

**NO — innovation has not always brought positive change:**
- Chlorine, developed for water purification, became the first mass-casualty chemical weapon at Ypres, 1915
- Dynamite, created for mining safety, made warfare more destructive — Nobel was labelled "merchant of death"
- Industrial technology caused the Exxon Valdez disaster — catastrophic, long-lasting environmental harm

**Model conclusion:** Scientific innovation has been the single greatest force for improving human health — germ theory alone transformed life expectancy more than any other development in history. However, the word "always" cannot be sustained. The same chemistry that purified water was weaponised at Ypres; the same energy technology that powered hospitals caused environmental catastrophes. The honest claim is that innovation has *predominantly* brought positive change, but its benefits are inseparable from the contexts of application — and those contexts have frequently been destructive.

---

### M25 Q8 — Do historians pay too much attention to scientific innovations in major events?

**YES — daily-life innovations deserve more attention:**
- The washing machine changed hygiene standards for billions over generations — a more consistent, widespread impact than any battlefield technology
- The wristwatch's enduring legacy is a fashion and timekeeping revolution; the military use was temporary
- Most people experience history through daily life, not through the major events that fill textbooks

**NO — major-event innovations are historically decisive:**
- The wristwatch enabled synchronized artillery tactics in WWI that directly changed military outcomes
- Penicillin in WWII changed survival rates for wounded soldiers and affected campaign outcomes at scale
- The atomic bomb ended WWII in the Pacific and reshaped global politics for 50 years; its daily-life consequences (Cold War, nuclear deterrence) were enormous

**Model conclusion:** The question creates a false choice: the most powerful innovations shaped both major events and daily life simultaneously. The wristwatch was a battlefield necessity and a postwar fashion icon. Penicillin saved soldiers and transformed civilian medicine. However, academic history has traditionally privileged the dramatic turning points of war over the quieter, cumulative transformations of domestic life. The washing machine affected more people for longer than any single weapon. A more complete history would give equal space to both — but on current evidence, the accusation that historians overweight major events has genuine force.
