# Ideological Movements

Ideological and intellectual movements appear primarily in N21, M22, and N24, with partial appearances in M23 and across research methods sections. Questions cover: describing key beliefs of an ideology, writing persuasive speeches for or against a movement, evaluating biased investigations, and the core essay debate about whether intellectual/ideological movements bring more significant change than conflicts. Nazism and the Enlightenment are the mark scheme's preferred model examples throughout.

---

## Key concepts

- **Ideology**: a political or social plan for how society should be organized, based on a specific set of beliefs, values, and ideas — the N24 stimulus defines it this way; Nazism, Communism, and Pan-Africanism are the three most examined ideologies in MYP History.
- **Nazism**: the ideology of Hitler's Germany (1933–1945), built on racial purity of the "Aryan" race and the removal or extermination of "inferior" races, primarily Jews; mass unemployment during the Great Depression 1929 created the conditions for extreme nationalism to gain support — resulted in the Holocaust (6 million Jews murdered) and WWII.
- **Stalinism**: the totalitarian communist regime of Joseph Stalin (1924–1953); industrialized the USSR rapidly but killed an estimated 6–20 million people through purges, gulags, forced collectivization, and engineered famine; cities were renamed (Stalingrad), photographs were altered to erase rivals, and history books were rewritten.
- **Propaganda**: biased information designed to shape public opinion — Goebbels ran Nazi Germany's propaganda ministry, controlling films, radio, and mass rallies; the N24 Q3 mark scheme identifies a biased research question ("Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?") as itself a form of propagandistic thinking.
- **Cult of personality**: when a leader uses mass media, censorship, and public spectacle to project an image of near-divine authority — Stalin had cities renamed after him, commissioned flattering artwork, and revised school textbooks; the N24 mark scheme contrasts this explicitly with Mandela's inclusive leadership style.
- **Enlightenment**: the 17th–18th century intellectual movement centred on reason, individual rights, and separation of church and state; directly inspired the American Revolution (1776) and the French Revolution (1789), producing the first modern democratic constitutions — the N21 Q7 essay model example of an intellectual movement bringing significant change.
- **Pan-Africanism**: the 20th-century ideology of African unity and anti-colonialism, championed by Ghana's Kwame Nkrumah; drove Ghanaian independence in 1957 and influenced the broader decolonization of sub-Saharan Africa through the 1960s.

## Case studies

### Nazism — model example for N24

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Core belief | Racial purity of the German ("Aryan") race; removal or extermination of "inferior" races, primarily Jews |
| Rise to power | Great Depression 1929 → mass unemployment → appeal of extreme nationalism; 1932 elections; Hitler appointed Chancellor January 1933 |
| Methods | Propaganda (Goebbels), Nuremberg racial laws (1935), Kristallnacht (1938), secret police (Gestapo), cult of the Führer |
| Outcome | Holocaust (6 million Jews murdered); WWII; Germany defeated and occupied 1945 |
| Why people supported | Economic desperation; nationalist resentment of Treaty of Versailles; fear; propaganda; desire for strong leadership |

**Describing Nazism — mark scale (N24 Q1):**
- 1 mark: "Ideological movement: Nazism. They believed in the purity of the German race."
- 2 marks: "They believed in the purity of the German race by removing non-Germans from the country."
- 3 marks: "They believed in the purity of the German race by removing non-Germans, such as Jews. Once Germany was made of only pure Germans, it would become a great empire again."

### Stalinism — for N24 Q3 evaluation

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Core belief | Communist state; Stalin as absolute authority; socialism in one country |
| Methods | Secret police (NKVD); gulags; purges of Party rivals; collectivization; manufactured famines |
| Cult of personality | Cities renamed (Stalingrad); history books rewritten; photographs altered; flattering artwork and music commissioned |
| Death toll | Estimated 6–20 million through purges, gulags, forced famine |
| Contrast with Mandela | Ruled by terror for personal power; Mandela focused on bettering all South Africans |

### The Enlightenment — model example for N21 Q7 essay

- 17th–18th century intellectual movement; core ideas: reason, individual rights, separation of church and state
- Inspired the American Revolution (1776) → Declaration of Independence → first modern democratic constitution
- Inspired the French Revolution (1789) → overthrow of monarchy → Declaration of Rights of Man
- Shows ideological/intellectual movement bringing significant change WITHOUT necessarily being associated with conflict

### N24 Q3 — the biased investigation

- Research question: "Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?"
- Sources used: *Bloodlands* (Snyder), *Survival in Auschwitz* (Levi), *Nationalism and War*, Cultural Revolution testimony, Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France*
- **Key strength**: sources are credible and relevant to the research question
- **Key limitation (most important)**: the RQ presupposes its conclusion — it only looks for negative examples (Nazism, Stalinism, Maoism); no successful movements like the Enlightenment or Pan-Africanism are considered; the investigation is not objective
- **Appraisal formula**: "Overall, the sources used are credible but because of the way the RQ is phrased, the investigation is too biased to produce objective findings."

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- In the N24 Q1 description: naming the belief + explaining what it means in practice + showing how it connects to the ideology's broader goals (3 marks requires all three)
- In the N21 Q7 essay: both an intellectual/ideological movement AND a conflict covered with substantial development
- In M22 Q3 speech: direct address ("Dear German workers!"), emotive language, rhetorical questions, specific historical details (dates, names, events), clear call to action
- In N24 Q3 evaluation: identifying the biased RQ phrasing as the central limitation, not just noting the sources are narrow
- Research questions with specific time period AND specific location (2 marks) rather than general questions (1 mark)

### What examiners penalise

- N21 Q7 essay covering only one type (only a movement OR only a conflict) — caps Criterion D
- M22 Q3 speech written like an essay — caps C2 at 3/6
- M23 Q4a research question that directly restates the SoI — explicitly 0 marks
- N24 Q3 evaluation that only notes "limited sources" without identifying the biased RQ as the core problem
- N24 Q1 description that names the belief without explaining what it means in practice

## Common question types

1. **Describe a key belief of one ideological movement (3 marks)** — name the movement, name the belief, explain what it means in practice, show how it fits the broader ideology; Nazism is the safest example
2. **Formulate a research question about ideological movements (2 marks)** — 1 mark = clear OR focused; 2 marks = clear AND focused (must include specific time period); do NOT rephrase the SoI
3. **Evaluate a biased investigation (8 marks)** — strengths (3) + limitations (3) + appraisal (2); the most important limitation to identify is always the biased phrasing of the RQ
4. **Persuasive speech on an ideological movement (18 marks, M22 Q3)** — must name movement, date, location, audience; use speech techniques throughout; essay prose = C2 capped at 3
5. **24-mark essay: movements vs. conflicts (N21 Q7)** — must cover both types with substantial development; synthesis conclusion weighing which brings more significant change

## Key arguments

### N21 Q7 — Do intellectual/ideological movements bring more significant change than conflicts?

**YES — movements bring more significant change:**
- The Enlightenment produced democratic revolutions (American 1776, French 1789) that permanently transformed political systems across the Western world — more lasting than any single conflict
- Pan-Africanism drove Ghanaian independence (1957) and the decolonization of an entire continent — a profound structural change without a single major military conflict
- Ideological movements create the intellectual framework that shapes how people live long after conflicts end

**NO — conflicts bring more significant change:**
- WWI ended four empires (Ottoman, Habsburg, Romanov, German) and redrew the map of Europe in four years — no ideological movement changed borders so dramatically so quickly
- WWII produced the United Nations, the end of European colonial empires, and the Cold War — structural changes on a global scale that no purely intellectual movement achieved
- Conflicts force rapid, irreversible change; ideological movements often operate slowly over generations

**Model conclusion:** The false dichotomy in this question is that movements and conflicts are typically intertwined — Nazism was an ideological movement that produced the most destructive conflict in human history; the Enlightenment was an intellectual movement that triggered the French Revolution's violent upheaval. However, the intellectual and ideological movements that proved most transformative — the Enlightenment, Pan-Africanism — achieved changes that endured long after any specific conflict had ended. The depth and permanence of change tend to favour movements; the speed and scale tend to favour conflicts. On balance, the long-run reshaping of societies is more often traced to ideas than to battles.

## Revision priorities

1. **N21 Q7 hard requirement** — must cover BOTH a movement AND a conflict with substantial development; missing one type caps Criterion D
2. **N24 Q3 biased RQ** — the central limitation is always the phrasing of the research question itself, not just the range of sources
3. **M22 Q3 speech register** — direct address, emotive language, rhetorical questions throughout; essay prose = C2 cap at 3
4. **Nazism facts** — the mark scheme's model example for almost every ideological question; Great Depression → rise of Nazism → Holocaust → WWII chain
5. **N24 Q1 depth** — at 3 marks: name the belief + what it means in practice + how it connects to the ideology's broader purpose; single sentence = 1 mark only
6. **Research question specificity** — adding a time period ("of the 1930s") turns a 1-mark RQ into a 2-mark RQ every time
7. **M23 Q4a zero-mark trap** — directly rephrasing the SoI as the research question earns 0 marks
