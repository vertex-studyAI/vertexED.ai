# Significant Individuals

Significant individuals appear across most MYP History sessions either as the subject of questions, as sources for analysis, or as the focus of extended writing. Key individuals examined include Thomas Edison, Henry Ford, Nelson Mandela, Joseph Stalin, Christopher Columbus, Frederick Maytag, and Kwame Nkrumah. Essays require balanced treatment — positive AND negative impacts — and at least one named individual. The M23 biography task requires a fictional unknown individual; using a real historical figure caps marks.

---

## Key concepts

- **Pioneer**: a person who opens new territory — in ideas, technology, or social change — for others to follow; Thomas Edison (1847–1931) held over 1,000 patents and created the first industrial research laboratory at Menlo Park, making systematic innovation itself into a process.
- **Henry Ford**: industrialist who introduced the moving assembly line in 1913, cutting Model T assembly time from 12 hours to 93 minutes and making cars affordable for ordinary Americans — but forced workers into monotonous repetitive tasks and aggressively suppressed unions (M17 context).
- **Nelson Mandela**: South African anti-apartheid leader who served 27 years in prison (1964–1990), shared the Nobel Peace Prize with F.W. de Klerk in 1993, and became South Africa's first Black president in 1994; used inclusive language ("we", "us", "our") and the 1995 Rugby World Cup as symbols of national reconciliation — Source A in N24.
- **Joseph Stalin**: Soviet leader 1924–1953, who industrialized the USSR through Five-Year Plans but killed an estimated 6–20 million people through purges, gulags, and forced collectivization; rewrote history books and edited photographs to erase rivals; cities were renamed after him — Source B in N24, used to contrast with Mandela.
- **Christopher Columbus**: Italian-born Spanish explorer whose 1492 voyages opened permanent contact between Europe and the Americas; celebrated in traditional accounts as a discoverer but responsible for enslaving and killing hundreds of thousands of Indigenous people and triggering the Columbian Exchange diseases that decimated populations (N22, M25).
- **Frederick Maytag**: American entrepreneur who created the first hand-cranked washing machine in 1907 (electric/gasoline version 1911), motivated by a need for new products during an agricultural downturn — Source A (M25) calls it a revolutionary time-saver; Source B (Tim Harford) argues the real significance was hygiene improvement because people rarely washed clothes before it existed.
- **Legacy**: the long-term impact of an individual on society — what survives them and continues to shape the world; at 7–8 marks in N22 Q8 and N24 Q7, the essay must address at least one individual's legacy with both positive and negative dimensions.

## Case studies

### Mandela vs. Stalin — N24 Q6 contrasts

| Mandela (Source A) | Stalin (Source B) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| Led through peaceful means; 27 years in prison rather than violent revolt | Violent dictatorship; secret police (NKVD); purges of rivals |
| Focused on bettering the lives of all South Africans | Focused on consolidating personal power |
| Used inclusive language ("we", "us", "our") in his presidential speech | Rewrote history books; edited photographs to erase opponents |
| Used rugby World Cup 1995 as symbol of cross-racial national unity | Cities renamed after him (Stalingrad); cult of personality |
| Shared Nobel Peace Prize with F.W. de Klerk, 1993 | Estimated 6–20 million killed through purges, gulags, engineered famine |
| First Black president of South Africa, 1994 | Ruled by terror for nearly 30 years (1924–1953) |

**At 4 marks**: Must interpret leadership styles, not just list facts — "Mandela was focused on making the lives of all South Africans better; Stalin's totalitarian regime was clearly more focused on his own power."

### Washing machine contrast (M25 Q6b)

- Source A (History.com): washing machine was revolutionary because it saved hours of intensive labour for farm wives who previously spent entire days washing by hand
- Source B (Tim Harford, *Fifty Things*): the time-saving claim is wrong because people didn't wash clothes often before the machine; the real revolution was hygiene ("lemon-fresh we all started to smell"); frozen dinners saved more time
- **Key contrast**: time-saving revolution (A) vs. hygiene revolution that also challenges the time-saving narrative (B)
- Full 4-mark answer: cites both sources with specific detail AND identifies the specific point of disagreement

### Positive vs. negative impact — key individuals

| Individual | Positive impacts | Negative impacts |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Columbus | Opened exchange of goods, crops, animals between Americas and Europe | Enslaved and killed hundreds of thousands of Native peoples; introduced devastating diseases |
| Nobel | Invented dynamite for mining safety; established Nobel Peace Prize | Enabled more efficient warfare; called "merchant of death" |
| Maytag | Lightened domestic labour; improved hygiene standards | Debatable time-saving; agricultural market exploitation as motivation |
| Ford | Mass production made cars affordable; created jobs; raised wages | Monotonous assembly-line work; aggressive union suppression |
| Stalin | Industrialized USSR rapidly; played key role in defeating Nazi Germany | Killed millions through purges, gulags, collectivization |

### Research methods for pioneer investigation (N20 Q4c)

| Method | Best for | Limitation |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Archival research | Historical documents, government records, evidence about context | May lack personal/subjective views of the individual |
| Personal interviews | First-hand accounts from people who knew the period | Difficult/impossible for historical figures; memory unreliable |
| Surveys | Broad data on opinions | Difficult historically; not useful for individual pioneers |
| Biographical accounts | Understanding the individual's life in historical context; "time, place and space" | Secondary sources; may be biased by author's perspective |

**Mark scheme consistently recommends** biographical accounts as the most appropriate method for a pioneer/time-place-space investigation.

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- For N22 Q8 / N24 Q7 essays: naming a specific pioneer/individual with both positive AND negative impacts developed with evidence
- For N24 Q6: explicit interpretation comparing leadership approach (people's benefit vs. personal power) — not just listing facts from each source
- For N20 Q4a: research question that explicitly links the pioneer to time, place, or space — the episode title is about how context shapes individuals
- For N24 Q7: at least one factor beyond the individual (political, economic, social) developed with evidence

### What examiners penalise

- N22 Q8 and N24 Q7 without a named individual — maximum marks severely limited
- One-sided treatment in N22 Q8 — must include both positive AND negative impacts; one-sided = capped at Criterion D
- No "other factor" in N24 Q7 — must include at least one factor beyond the significant individual
- N20 Q4a: RQ with no link to "where you are matters as much as who you are" — explicitly 0 marks
- Using a real historical figure in M23 Q5 biography — Criterion A capped at 3/6
- Describing source content rather than interpreting leadership styles in N24 Q6

## Key arguments

### N22 Q8 — Have pioneers/innovators/developers brought about change for the good of society?

**YES — pioneers have brought positive change:**
- Maytag's washing machine transformed hygiene and domestic labour for millions of women over generations
- Mandela's leadership brought an end to apartheid and established a democratic, multi-racial South Africa without the civil war many feared
- Edison's systematic innovation model created the framework for modern research and development — the process of invention itself changed

**NO — pioneers have not always brought positive change:**
- Columbus's voyages opened the Columbian Exchange which decimated Indigenous populations through disease, slavery, and violence
- Nobel's dynamite made warfare more efficient and lethal — the "merchant of death" legacy persisted despite his Peace Prize intentions
- Ford's assembly line created jobs but simultaneously degraded the nature of work and enabled his virulent anti-union campaigns

**Model conclusion:** Pioneers and innovators have, on balance, generated enormous positive change — the scale of medical, technological, and social advancement driven by individual innovators is indisputable. However, "for the good of society" is inseparable from the question of which society: Columbus was a pioneer for European expansion but a catastrophe for the peoples he encountered. The honest assessment is that pioneers have consistently changed society, often dramatically — but whether that change is "good" depends entirely on who you ask.

---

### N24 Q7 — Have significant individuals been the main factor in influencing historical events?

**YES — individuals are the main factor:**
- Without Gandhi's strategic philosophy of satyagraha, the Indian independence movement had no coherent non-violent method — the conditions existed before 1919, but Gandhi turned them into a strategy
- Mandela's willingness to negotiate rather than demand revenge transformed South Africa's transition; another leader might have produced a civil war
- Individual vision shapes how structural conditions are used

**NO — structural factors are more important:**
- The Great Depression (1929) created the economic conditions for Nazism; Hitler exploited them but did not create them — another demagogue might have emerged in his place
- Post-WWII financial collapse of the British Empire made decolonization inevitable regardless of who led specific movements
- Technology, ideology, and economic forces create the context within which individuals act

**Model conclusion:** Significant individuals matter most as the mechanism through which structural conditions produce specific outcomes. The economic collapse of the Great Depression would have generated extreme politics regardless of Hitler; but the specific outcome — systematic genocide — required Hitler's individual ideology and decisions. Gandhi did not create Indian nationalism; he gave it a method and a moral language that made British repression globally untenable. The individual is not the main factor in producing historical change, but is often the decisive factor in determining what form that change takes.
