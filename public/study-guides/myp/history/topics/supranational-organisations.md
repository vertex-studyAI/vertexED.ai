# Supranational Organisations

Supranational organisations appear across multiple MYP History sessions as both a topic for knowledge questions and as the focus of the extended podcast writing task in N24. Key organisations include the League of Nations, the United Nations, the WTO, and the EU. Questions cover: reasons for creation, successes and failures in reducing conflict and promoting peace, the WTO Seattle protests (1999), and the cartoon message about lack of UN unity (M23). The League of Nations/Manchuria crisis is the mark scheme's primary model example.

---

## Key concepts

- **League of Nations**: created in 1919 after WWI to prevent future wars through collective security; fatally weakened from the start because the US Senate refused to join; failed to stop Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931 (Japan left the League rather than comply), Italy's invasion of Abyssinia in 1935, or Germany's remilitarization of the Rhineland in 1936 — dissolved in 1946 and replaced by the UN.
- **United Nations**: founded in 1945 with 51 member states to maintain peace and promote human rights; its Security Council gives veto power to five permanent members (USA, UK, France, Russia, China), meaning any one of them can block collective action — the source of the "playing different games" problem in the M23 cartoon (Q7).
- **WTO (World Trade Organization)**: established in 1995 to regulate international trade with binding rules; the 1999 Seattle ministerial conference was disrupted by tens of thousands of protesters from labour, environmental, and human rights groups — the talks collapsed after four days (M21 Source B, a 2019 ABC News retrospective).
- **Collective security**: the principle that an attack on one member is treated as an attack on all — the theory behind both the League of Nations and NATO; it only works when all great powers agree to enforce it, which they rarely did during the Cold War.
- **Veto power**: the right of the five permanent UN Security Council members (USA, UK, France, Russia, China) to unilaterally block resolutions — during the Cold War, the US and USSR regularly vetoed each other's proposals, paralysing the UN on major conflicts.
- **Manchuria crisis (1931)**: Japan invaded the Chinese province of Manchuria; the League condemned Japan but could not enforce withdrawal — Japan announced in 1933 it would invade more of China and simply left the League, exposing the organisation's total lack of enforcement power (N24 Q4 mark scheme model).

## Case studies

### League of Nations — failures (N24 Q4 model)

| Event | Year | Outcome | Significance |
|-------|------|---------|-------------|
| Japan invades Manchuria | 1931 | League condemned Japan; Japan refused to withdraw; announced further China invasion 1933; left the League | Showed League could not enforce decisions on great powers |
| Italy invades Abyssinia | 1935–36 | Partial sanctions imposed but oil excluded; Italy left League | Economic sanctions proved ineffective |
| Germany remilitarizes Rhineland | 1936 | No action taken | Emboldened Hitler; League shown to be powerless |
| League dissolved | 1946 | Replaced by UN | Acknowledged collective failure |

**N24 Q4 Criterion A scale:**
- 1–2 marks: "When Japan invaded Manchuria, the League failed to take serious action."
- 3–4 marks: Adds: "There was a long debate before the League decided Japan had to abandon Manchuria; Japan refused and in 1933 announced it would invade more of China. The League could not agree on sanctions to impose."
- 5–6 marks: Adds: "The League of Nations, set up in 1919, aimed to discourage aggression from any nation, but its success was limited... Japan eventually abandoned the League, and this damaged its reputation as a peace-keeping organization."

### UN cartoon message — M23 Q7

- Source D shows UN member nations "playing different games with different rules" — visual metaphor for lack of cooperation
- The message: even though UN leaders should be working together, competing national interests prevent effective collective action
- **Critical rule**: "A description of the cartoon is not required and should not be the basis for awarding marks." — must explain the MESSAGE, not describe what the image shows
- Mark scale: 1 mark = "no unity among countries" → 4 marks = "even though leaders should cooperate, playing different games with different rules makes it difficult, undermining the UN's ability to maintain peace"

### WTO Seattle 1999 — M21

- Source B: ABC News article by Gene Johnson (Associated Press), written 29 November 2019 — 20 years after the event
- Origin: ABC News / Gene Johnson / Associated Press
- Purpose: "to inform about the lasting legacy/impacts of the 1999 WTO protests" — must specify LASTING and WTO
- Value: long-term retrospective perspective; 20 years of hindsight about impact
- Limitation: journalist was not a participant; secondary source written 20 years later; cannot provide first-hand account of the event

### Key supranational organisations — overview

| Organisation | Founded | Purpose | Key success | Key failure |
|-------------|---------|---------|------------|------------|
| League of Nations | 1919 | Prevent war, collective security | Settled minor disputes in 1920s | Failed on Japan/Italy/Germany; US never joined |
| United Nations | 1945 | Peace, human rights, international cooperation | Korean War intervention (1950) | Cold War paralysis; Rwanda genocide; veto system blocks action |
| WTO | 1995 | Regulate international trade | Binding trade rules | Seattle 1999 protests; accused of favouring rich nations |
| European Union | 1993 | Economic/political integration, prevent European war | No major war between members | Brexit; democratic deficit criticisms |

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- For N24 Q4 podcast: rhetorical questions, analogies, concise sentences — features of spoken/podcast style throughout, not essay prose
- For M21 Q1: naming a specific institution AND giving a reason with brief elaboration (both required for 2 marks)
- For M21 Q6b purpose: "lasting legacy/impacts of the WTO protests" — both the specific subject and the "lasting" nature required
- For M23 Q7: explaining what the UN cartoon MEANS about international cooperation — not describing what each figure in the cartoon is doing
- League of Nations Manchuria crisis with specific dates, specific sequence, and consequences = top-band Criterion A for N24 Q4

### What examiners penalise

- N24 Q4 podcast script written like an essay — caps C2 at 3/4; must use rhetorical questions, analogies, conversational engagement
- No title and no introduction/ending in N24 Q4 — C1 requires two of: title, introduction, ending; missing both = 0 on C1
- M21 Q1: naming the UN without explaining why it was created — 1 mark only; must give a reason AND elaborate
- M23 Q7: describing the cartoon instead of explaining its message — descriptions are not the basis for marks
- M21 Q6b too vague — "to inform an audience" always rejected

## Common question types

1. **Outline one reason for creating a supranational organisation (2 marks, M21 Q1)** — name the organisation + reason + brief elaboration; both are required for 2 marks
2. **State origin / Outline purpose of a source (1+1 marks, M21 Q6a/6b)** — "ABC News / Gene Johnson"; "to inform about the lasting legacy of the 1999 WTO protests"
3. **Explain the message of a cartoon about international organisations (4 marks, M23 Q7)** — explain meaning/message; do not describe what is depicted
4. **Podcast script on a supranational institution (18 marks, N24 Q4)** — podcast register throughout (rhetorical questions, analogies); title + introduction OR ending for C1

## Revision priorities

1. **League of Nations failure sequence** — Japan/Manchuria 1931 → Italy/Abyssinia 1935 → Germany/Rhineland 1936 → dissolved 1946; this is the N24 Q4 mark scheme model
2. **Podcast register (N24 Q4)** — rhetorical questions, analogies, concise conversational sentences; essay prose = C2 3/4
3. **C1 for podcast** — title + introduction OR ending; both elements can earn 2 marks; missing both = 0
4. **M23 Q7 message vs. description** — explain what the cartoon means about international cooperation; description of what figures are doing earns nothing
5. **M21 Q6b purpose precision** — "lasting legacy" + "WTO protests" both required; "to inform an audience" always rejected
6. **M21 Q1 elaboration** — naming the organisation alone earns 1 mark; adding a specific reason why it was created earns 2 marks
