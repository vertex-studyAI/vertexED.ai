# Research Methods (Criterion B)

Research methods is the single most consistently examined topic across all MYP History sessions — every paper tests Criterion B. Questions cover: formulating a research question, justifying its relevance to a statement of inquiry, identifying primary and secondary sources, selecting research methods, explaining source usefulness, and evaluating a complete investigative process. The 8-mark evaluate question has a fixed structure: Strengths (0–3) + Limitations (0–3) + Appraisal (0–2).

---

## Key concepts

- **Research question (RQ)**: earns **1 mark** if clear OR focused; earns **2 marks** if BOTH clear AND focused; earns **0 marks** if it rephrases the Statement of Inquiry — "How did the economic conditions of the 1930s create support for Nazism?" scores 2 marks (specific time period added); "How did economic conditions create support for Nazism?" scores 1 mark only.
- **Statement of Inquiry (SoI)**: the broad thematic framing given in the exam; the RQ must investigate a *specific dimension* of the SoI, never restate it — directly rephrasing the SoI is explicitly awarded 0 marks across M21, M23, and N24 mark schemes.
- **Focused research question**: specifies a location, time period, individual, or specific aspect — "How has Syrian refugee migration to Germany changed its population structure?" scores 2 marks; "How does migration change population structure?" scores 1 mark.
- **Justification (4 marks)**: describing the link between RQ and SoI earns 3 marks; the 4th mark requires a "because..." or "this means that..." construction that explains *why* the connection matters — reasoning, not just description.
- **Biased RQ**: a research question that assumes its conclusion — "Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?" (N24 Q3) is the central limitation of that investigation; it presupposes the answer and will only find supporting evidence, making the investigation not objective.
- **Evaluating an investigation (8 marks)**: always Strengths (0–3) + Limitations (0–3) + Appraisal (0–2); a response covering only strengths is capped at 3 marks; appraisal formula: "Overall, [positive aspect] but [negative aspect], which means [consequence for the investigation]."
- **Primary source specificity**: "newspapers" or "books" alone scores 0 marks on Criterion B questions — must specify the type and its relevance to the specific investigation; "a contemporary newspaper article from 1930s Germany reporting on unemployment" = acceptable.
- **Sub-question drift**: a key limitation to identify in evaluation questions — when sub-questions stop serving the main RQ; in M21 Q4, sub-questions about how empires ended and their role in WWI drifted from the stated focus on *reasons for expansion*.

## Case studies

### The research question mark scale

| Marks | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 0 | Directly rephrases the SoI word-for-word |
| 1 | Clear OR focused — has a specific topic but no time/place, OR has time/place but is vague |
| 2 | Clear AND focused — specific topic + specific time period or location |

**Transformation examples:**
| 1-mark RQ | 2-mark RQ |
|-----------|-----------|
| "How did economic conditions create support for Nazism?" | "How did the economic conditions of the 1930s create support for Nazism?" |
| "How did the Industrial Revolution change lives?" | "How did the Industrial Revolution change family life in the UK between 1760 and 1850?" |
| "To what extent has nuclear energy impacted health?" | "To what extent has nuclear energy improved the health of people in Japan since 1945?" |

### The justification mark scale (4 marks)

| Marks | What is required |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | One simple sentence connecting RQ to SoI |
| 2 | Brief detail on one factor of connection |
| 3 | Detail on one factor WITHOUT comprehensive reasoning |
| 4 | Detail on one factor WITH reasoning — "because..." or "this means that..." construction |

**Model justification (4 marks, M23):** "It focuses on one leader who has used national identity and the significance it had in helping him obtain power. Authoritarian leaders often use a variety of tools to achieve power. This investigation asks the role that national identity played compared to other tactics. By investigating Hitler's use of nationalism in his rise to power **we can find out** the uses to which national identity can be put if taken to their extreme." — the bolded phrase provides the "because this matters" reasoning.

### The 8-mark evaluate structure

| Component | 1 mark | 2 marks | 3 marks |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------|
| Strengths | States one strength | Outlines with brief detail | Explains with reasoning OR outlines 3+ strengths |
| Limitations | States one limitation | Outlines with brief detail | Explains with reasoning OR outlines 3+ limitations |
| Appraisal | Brief overall judgment | Weighs positives AND negatives in one statement with consequence |

**Appraisal formula:** "Overall, [positive aspect] but [negative aspect], which means [consequence for the investigation]."

**Critical rule:** A response covering only strengths is capped at 3/8 marks. Covering only strengths AND limitations (no appraisal) is capped at 6/8 marks.

### Common strengths and limitations to identify

**Strengths:**
- Research question is clear, focused, and relevant to the SoI
- Uses both primary AND secondary sources
- Uses a variety of source types (interviews, photographs, documents, academic sources)
- Sub-questions are well-designed to guide the investigation

**Limitations:**
- Sub-questions drift from the RQ focus (sub-question drift — M21 Q4 model example)
- RQ is biased — presupposes its conclusion (N24 Q3: "Why do ideological movements cause more problems than they solve?")
- Only secondary sources used; no primary sources
- Only one type of source; insufficient variety
- Sources too general for the specific topic (M22: music history book too broad for hip-hop)
- Interview subjects not broad enough (N21: no interview with Trocmé himself; N19: only one scientist interviewed)

### Primary vs. secondary sources

**Primary** (created at the time of the event):
- Diaries, letters, memoirs, personal accounts
- Government documents and archives from the period
- Newspaper articles from the time of the event
- Photographs, propaganda posters, speeches, recordings from the time
- Eyewitness interviews

**Secondary** (created after the event):
- History textbooks and academic books
- Documentaries (usually)
- Encyclopaedias (Britannica, etc.)
- Websites and articles by historians
- Biographies written after the person's life
- The M21 ABC News article (2019, about 1999 WTO protests) = secondary source despite being journalism

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- Research questions with a specific time period AND location — this is what transforms 1 mark into 2 marks every time
- Justifications with "because..." or "this means that..." reasoning — not just describing the connection
- Evaluate responses that identify a specific limitation (biased RQ, sub-question drift, overly broad source) — not vague "limited sources"
- Specific source citations in investigation planning — "a contemporary newspaper from 1930s Germany" not just "newspapers"
- Appraisal that explicitly weighs both strengths and limitations with a judgment about the overall investigation quality

### What examiners penalise

- Rephrasing the SoI as the RQ — explicitly 0 marks in M21, M23, N24 mark schemes
- Vague sources: "Newspapers," "Books," "the internet" — most sessions explicitly award 0 marks for this
- Evaluate responses covering only strengths — capped at 3/8
- Missing the appraisal component entirely — costs 2 marks
- Identifying "limited sources" as the limitation without specifying what is missing or why it matters
- Sub-question drift not identified (M21 Q4) — the key limitation is specific; general criticism of the investigation misses the mark

## Common question types

1. **Formulate a research question (2 marks)** — clear AND focused; specific country/time/person; rephrasing SoI = 0; general question = 1
2. **Justify RQ relevance (4 marks)** — describe the connection (3 marks) + explain WHY it matters with "because..." (4 marks)
3. **Identify primary/secondary sources (1 mark each)** — must be specific to the investigation; "newspapers" alone = 0
4. **Explain source usefulness (3–4 marks)** — what information it provides + why that information is useful for this specific investigation
5. **Explain one research difficulty (4 marks, N23 Q4e)** — name the difficulty + explain how it would specifically prevent answering the RQ
6. **Evaluate an investigative process (8 marks)** — strengths + limitations + appraisal; must include all three; cover strengths only = capped at 3

---

## Revision priorities

1. **The 2-mark RQ formula** — clear AND focused; adding a specific time period ("of the 1930s") is the most reliable way to move from 1 mark to 2 marks
2. **Appraisal formula** — "Overall, [positive] but [negative], which means [consequence]"; this 2-mark component is the most commonly lost in evaluate questions
3. **Sub-question drift** — the most frequently targeted limitation in evaluate questions (M21 Q4); always check whether sub-questions actually serve the main RQ
4. **Biased RQ as a limitation (N24 Q3)** — the most important limitation of the N24 classmates' investigation; not just "limited sources" but the RQ itself presupposes its conclusion
5. **Justification requires reasoning** — describing the link = 3 marks; explaining WHY the link matters with "because..." = 4 marks
6. **Vague sources = 0 marks** — always specify type + relevance; "a newspaper article from 1930s Germany about unemployment" not "a newspaper"
