# Industrialization

Industrialization is one of the most heavily examined topics across MYP History sessions, appearing as the primary theme in N20, M21, N23, and M24A. Questions cover: the four causes of the Industrial Revolution (technological, economic, political, social), factory conditions, child labour, the Meiji era in Japan, and political responses to industrialization. The mark scheme consistently uses Britain and the steam engine as the model examples for top-band essay answers; essays must cover at least two of the four accepted causes.

---

## Key concepts

- **Industrial Revolution**: the transition from agrarian, craft-based economies to factory and machine-based production, beginning in Britain ~1760 and spreading globally through the 19th century; by 1851, Britain produced one-third of the world's manufactured goods and was called "the workshop of the world."
- **Steam engine**: improved by James Watt in 1769, it powered textile mills and iron foundries and, by 1790, enabled railways and canals that turned local markets into national ones — the N20/N23/M24A mark scheme consistently cites this as the top-band technological cause of the Industrial Revolution.
- **Factory system**: production concentrated in large buildings where workers performed the same task 10+ hours per day (M21 Source A), replacing the cottage industries where families produced goods at home.
- **Child labour**: in 1842, one quarter of coal mine workers in Britain were children; boys worked from Friday 6am to Saturday 4pm continuously without rest — from the 1836 Factory Inspectors Report (M21 stimulus); girls were used more than boys in textile factories (N20 Source A bar chart).
- **Urbanization**: the mass movement of workers from rural areas to industrial cities, driven by the enclosure acts that removed peasants from common land; brought severe overcrowding, cholera outbreaks, and open sewers — cities like Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to over 300,000 by 1850.
- **Meiji era**: Japan's state-directed industrialization, 1868–1912, under the slogan "Fukoku Kyohei" (Rich Country, Strong Army); the Japanese government — not private entrepreneurs — drove the process, building railways, steam ships, and a modern military; within one generation Japan defeated Russia in the 1905 war.
- **Technological cause**: the four accepted causes for the Industrial Revolution's start in Britain are technological, economic, political, and social (N20 Q8, N23 Q3, M24A Q3); at least two must be covered in essays — a one-cause response cannot reach the top marks.

## Case studies

### The four causes of the Industrial Revolution

| Cause | Mark scheme model content |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Technological** | Watt's steam engine (1769) → powered mills and foundries → railways/canals by 1790 → local markets became national → more investment → "workshop of the world" |
| **Economic** | Rising demand for goods + availability of capital + profitable overseas trade + merchant class growth → entrepreneurs invested → factories profitable → expansion |
| **Political** | Stable government + protection of private property + laws protecting investors' profits → investors felt safe → capital flowed into factories |
| **Social** | Population growth → more workers available + more consumers → factories needed to meet demand; enclosure acts pushed rural workers into cities |

**Mark scheme model (N20/N23 7-mark band):** "James Watt improved the first steam engine so it could be used to drive machinery in textile mills and iron foundries. By 1790 it became much easier to transport goods and natural resources farther and faster by railroad or the new canal system. Factory manufactured goods could now travel to nearly all parts of Britain so the market became national rather than local, which encouraged more investment and provided a boost for the British economy that would later become 'the workshop of the world.'"

### Japan vs. Britain industrialization

| Feature | Britain (18th–19th century) | Japan / Meiji (1868–1912) |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Driving force | Economic demand, private entrepreneurs | Government policy ("Fukoku Kyohei" — Rich Country, Strong Army) |
| Role of state | Limited government intervention | State-directed industrialization |
| Key symbol | Steam engine, factory system | Railways, steam ships, modern military |
| Social costs | Child labour, poor factory conditions, urbanization | Environmental damage, lifestyle disruption |
| Outcome | "Workshop of the world"; first industrial nation | Defeated Russia 1905; became a modern great power |

### Child labour facts (M21/N20/M25)

- 1842: one quarter of coal mine workers in Britain were children
- Boys worked from Friday 6am to Saturday 4pm continuously — 1836 Factory Inspectors Report
- Girls used more in textile factories than boys (N20 Source A bar chart)
- Common jobs: chimney sweep, coal miner, factory worker, pottery maker, mill worker
- Robert Owen stopped child labour at his New Lanark mills and provided education — model alternative

### N23/M24A Q2 — evaluating the Industrial Revolution investigation

- RQ: "What were the key reasons for the Industrial Revolution starting in 18th century Great Britain?"
- **Key strength**: mind map identifies multiple cause categories (social, political, technological, economic) — systematic approach
- **Key limitation**: most categories have only one source; the History.com article (Kiger, "7 Negative Effects") addresses *negatives*, not *causes* — mismatch with the RQ's focus on reasons; also insufficient source variety for credible conclusions

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- For essay questions: specific named example + causal chain showing HOW the cause led to industrialization (not just stating it did)
- Steam engine / James Watt named with specific detail (date, impact on transport, national market) earns the top band
- Both advantages AND disadvantages of Japanese industrialization in N20 Q7 — one-sided = capped
- Political commentary: emotive language, hyperbole, rhetorical questions throughout (not just essay prose)
- Investigation evaluation: identifying the sub-question drift (History.com article about negatives ≠ causes) as the specific limitation

### What examiners penalise

- Only one cause in N20/N23/M24A essay — explicitly caps marks; must cover at least two factors
- Advantages only or disadvantages only in Japan analysis (N20 Q7) — must address both
- Political commentary written like an essay — C2 capped at 3/6; must use emotive language and rhetorical questions
- Journal entries too formal (N23/M24A Q5) — too formal = C2 1–2 marks; must read like a private diary/reflection
- Naming an invention without explaining its significance (N23 Q1) — 1 mark only; needs brief detail about impact

## Common question types

1. **Explain one cause of the Industrial Revolution (4 marks)** — name cause + specific example + how it contributed + causal chain; steam engine/Watt is the safest example
2. **24-mark discuss/essay on causes (N20 Q8)** — at least two factors with equal depth; synthesis conclusion; Britain is the expected location
3. **Analyse Japanese industrialization from two images (6 marks, N20 Q7)** — must address both advantages and disadvantages; use specific visual details from Source B
4. **Political commentary on industrialization (18 marks, M21 Q5)** — emotive language, rhetorical questions, first person; must cover both positive AND negative aspects
5. **Three journal entries as a historical individual (18 marks, N23/M24A Q5)** — first person, reflective, colloquial; three entries with three dates; must reference effects on society
6. **Evaluate investigative process (8 marks, N23/M24A Q2)** — strengths (3) + limitations (3) + appraisal (2); identify the sub-question drift as the core limitation

## Revision priorities

1. **Steam engine causal chain** — Watt (1769) → powered mills → railways/canals (1790) → national market → more investment → "workshop of the world"; this is the mark scheme's own model answer
2. **Two causes minimum in essays** — one-cause responses are explicitly capped; always prepare a second cause with its own example
3. **Japan: both advantages and disadvantages** — the N20 Q7 mark scheme requires this; environmental costs and lifestyle disruption alongside trains and ships
4. **Political commentary register** — emotive language, first person, rhetorical questions throughout; essay prose = C2 3/6
5. **Journal entry register** — colloquial, reflective, first person throughout; too formal = C2 1–2/8
6. **Investigation evaluation** — always identify sub-question drift as the primary limitation; the appraisal weighs both sides with an overall judgment
