# Source Analysis (Criterion D)

Source analysis appears in every single session across all 13 papers. Criterion D (Thinking Critically) covers OPVL work (Origin, Purpose, Value, Limitation) on individual sources, comparative analysis of two sources, and perspective-based analysis. The core skill is specificity: vague origin statements, vague purpose statements, and analysis that ignores who made a source or why it was made are the most consistent routes to losing marks.

---

## Key concepts

- **Origin**: who created a source, when, in what context — specificity is always required; "a news website" is rejected; "ABC News / Gene Johnson / Associated Press, 29 November 2019" is accepted; "The Singh Twins" is accepted because it names the specific creators.
- **Purpose**: what the source was created *to do* — "to inform an audience" is always rejected; "to inform readers about the lasting legacy of the 1999 WTO Seattle protests" is accepted because it names the specific subject and the specific aim; "to show the effects of the oil spill" is rejected — must say "to show the *continued* impact of the *Exxon Valdez* oil spill."
- **Value**: what useful information the source provides and why it's relevant — a doctor's 1349 advice note (M22 Source B) is valuable because it gives direct access to what a contemporary doctor actually believed, showing 14th-century medical thinking from the inside.
- **Limitation**: what the source cannot tell us or why it may be unreliable — the Singh Twins' 2019 painting of the 1919 Amritsar massacre is limited because the artists did not witness it; NOAA's Exxon Valdez infographic is limited because NOAA led the investigation it is reporting on, creating potential institutional bias.
- **OPVL**: the analytical framework — Origin, Purpose, Value, Limitation; at 3+ marks, origin and/or purpose must be explicitly referenced; a 4-mark quality explanation that never mentions who made the source or why = capped at 3 marks.
- **Primary source**: created at or near the time of the event — diaries, speeches, contemporary photographs, government documents from the period; the Singh Twins 2019 painting is NOT a primary source of the 1919 massacre even though it depicts it.
- **Secondary source**: created after the event — textbooks, academic articles, biographies, documentaries; the M21 ABC News article (published 2019, about the 1999 WTO protests) is a secondary source despite being journalism.
- **Perspective analysis**: when asked to analyse from a specified viewpoint (N16 Q6: "from the perspective of Globalization For All"; N21 Q5: "from Lavinia Fontana's perspective"), must argue from *within* that perspective — both agreement AND disagreement — not describe it from the outside.

### Analytical depth scale

| Level | What is required | Typical marks |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| State | One sentence; a fact or brief statement | 1 |
| Outline | Brief detail — two clauses or 2–3 short sentences | 2 |
| Describe | Short paragraph with detail but limited full reasoning | 3 |
| Explain | Detail + reasoning ("because / therefore / as a result") | 4 |

### What "specific" means for origin and purpose

**Origin — rejected vs accepted:**
- ✗ "Instagram" — platform only, not origin
- ✗ "rosajune16" — username only; must add "an MYP history teacher"
- ✗ "the ABC" alone — must say "ABC News / Gene Johnson / Associated Press"
- ✓ "The Singh Twins" — artist names are sufficient
- ✓ "NOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration)"

**Purpose — rejected vs accepted:**
- ✗ "to inform an audience" — always too vague
- ✗ "to show the effects of the oil spill" — must name Exxon Valdez AND say continued
- ✗ "to inform about child labour" — must specify "during the Industrial Revolution"
- ✓ "to show the continued impact of the Exxon Valdez oil spill"
- ✓ "to inform about the lasting legacy of the 1999 WTO Seattle protests"

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- Specific origin: creator's name + role/credentials + date/publication
- Specific purpose: what the source aims to do + for whom + about what specific event/subject
- At 3+ marks: explicitly connecting value or limitation back to who made the source or why it was made
- For comparative questions: addressing both sources; genuine contrast (what one shows that the other does not)
- For perspective analysis: arguing from inside the perspective — both agreement and disagreement from that viewpoint

### What examiners penalise

- Vague origin ("a news website," "Instagram," "the ABC")
- Vague purpose ("to inform an audience" — always rejected)
- A 4-mark-quality explanation that never mentions origin or purpose — capped at 3 marks
- Only similarities in a "Compare and contrast" question (caps at 4/6) or only differences
- Referencing only one source in a two-source analysis question (caps at half marks)
- Describing a cartoon or artwork instead of explaining its message — "description of the cartoon is not required" per the mark scheme
- Not adopting the specified perspective in perspective analysis questions

---

## Common question types

1. **State origin (1 mark)** — name of creator + role/credentials; artist name alone acceptable; platform name alone is not
2. **Outline purpose (1–2 marks)** — specific event + specific aim; "to inform" alone = 0 marks
3. **Describe/Explain value or limitation (3–4 marks)** — at 3+ marks always reference origin and/or purpose; one well-explained point beats a list
4. **Compare and contrast two sources (6–8 marks)** — must include both similarities AND differences unless command term is "contrast"; both sources must be used
5. **Contrast two sources (3 marks)** — differences only; similarities score zero
6. **Justify using sources (4 marks)** — must use both named sources; referencing only one = max 2 marks
7. **Explain source message/cartoon message (4 marks)** — analyse the meaning, not what the image depicts
8. **Analyse from a perspective (6–8 marks)** — must argue from inside that perspective; both agreement and disagreement required at top marks

---

## Revision priorities

1. **OPVL formula at 3+ marks** — always tie value or limitation back to origin and/or purpose; this is the single most commonly lost mark in source questions
2. **Purpose specificity** — "to inform an audience" is always rejected; memorise: specific event + specific aim
3. **Origin specificity** — creator name + role; not platform, not URL
4. **Both sources in comparative questions** — one source only = capped; confirm both are addressed before finishing
5. **Contrast = differences only** — when command term is "contrast," similarities score zero (N22 Q6, M25 Q6b)
6. **Cartoon/artwork = message analysis** — do not describe what is shown; explain what it means
7. **Perspective analysis = argue from inside** — not "the NGO would disagree because..." but "as Globalization For All, we would argue..."
