# Globalization

Globalization is one of the most consistently examined topics in MYP History, appearing as the context for essays, source analysis, research planning, and extended writing across nearly every session. Questions cover: positive and negative impacts of trade and cultural exchange, the Silk Road, the Atlantic Slave Trade, the opium trade, and the debate about whether globalization benefits or harms societies. The topic also underpins M25's social media response task and N22's research planning on cultural sharing.

---

## Key concepts

- **Silk Road**: the ancient overland and maritime trade network connecting China to the Mediterranean, active from ~200 BC to the 15th century, carrying silk, spices, Buddhism, and Islam westward and gold and glass eastward — the M25 mark scheme model answer uses this as the primary example of global interactions and cultural diffusion.
- **Atlantic slave trade**: the forced transportation of approximately 12 million enslaved Africans to the Americas between the 16th and 19th centuries; generated enormous wealth for British and Spanish empires while causing catastrophic human suffering — the N20 mark scheme model answer uses this as the exemplar for global trade and exchange.
- **Columbian Exchange**: the transfer of crops (potatoes, tomatoes, maize), animals, and diseases between the Americas and Europe/Africa following Columbus's 1492 voyages; smallpox alone decimated Indigenous American populations, reducing some communities by up to 90%.
- **Opium trade**: Britain forced China to accept opium exports from India in exchange for tea in the 19th century — the N22 mark scheme uses this as the model answer for "one negative impact of globalization," where benefits were "unfairly distributed" as countries became more integrated.
- **Cultural imperialism**: the dominance of one culture over others through economic or political power — British colonial rule suppressed Indigenous languages, religions, and governance systems across India, Africa, and the Caribbean.
- **Interdependence**: the state where countries mutually rely on each other for goods, resources, or security — a consequence of globalization where economic disruption in one nation cascades globally.
- **Cultural exchange**: the voluntary or forced spread of ideas, food, art, religion, and technology between societies — the Silk Road carried Buddhism into China and Islam into Southeast Asia; European colonization spread Christianity and Western education into Asia, Africa, and the Americas.

## Case studies

### Silk Road (M25 model answer)

- Active from ~200 BC to the 15th century; connected China to the Mediterranean via overland and maritime routes
- Eastward: gold, glass, wool from Rome; Buddhism, Hinduism from India into China and Southeast Asia
- Westward: silk, porcelain, tea, spices from China; paper-making, gunpowder technology spread to Europe
- Cultural diffusion: Buddhism established in China; Islam spread into Central Asia and Southeast Asia
- M25 Criterion A scale: 1 mark = "Silk Roads were a vast trade network"; 6 marks = specific dates, multiple goods, cultural and dietary impacts with causal reasoning

### Atlantic slave trade (N20 model answer)

- Triangle of trade: guns/iron/cloth from Britain → enslaved people from Africa → raw materials (cotton, sugar, tobacco) from colonies back to Britain
- ~12 million enslaved Africans transported across the Middle Passage, 16th–19th century; horrific conditions (branding, chains, disease)
- Generated enormous wealth for British and Spanish empires; funded the Industrial Revolution
- N20 6-mark answer requires: both positive (economic growth for European powers) AND negative (human catastrophe, exploitation) impacts — one-sided = cannot reach 6 marks

### Opium trade with China (N22 Q1b model answer)

- Britain forced China to import opium from India in exchange for tea; when China resisted → Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60)
- British policy helped traders find new markets after opium was banned in Europe
- China suffered addiction crisis; unequal treaties signed; Hong Kong ceded to Britain
- Mark scheme scale: 1 mark = exploitation statement; 2 marks = adds opium/tea exchange; 3 marks = adds British motivation; 4 marks = adds causation: "as countries became more integrated through globalization, benefits were unfairly distributed"

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- Covering **both** positive economic impacts AND negative human costs in the N20 Atlantic slave trade presentation (one-sided = cannot reach 6 marks)
- For the N22 Q1b negative impact answer: the causation statement — "benefits were unfairly distributed as countries became more integrated" — is what earns the 4th mark
- For M21 Q3a research question: both clear AND focused (specific relationship between identified stakeholders) — rephrasing the SoI = 0 marks
- For M25 Q5 social media: professional/official tone throughout (not personal/casual); original hashtag that connects to the specific historical example used
- For N22 Q3 research question: specifying a place AND a factor earns 2 marks; missing the specific location = 1 mark only

### What examiners penalise

- One-sided Atlantic slave trade presentation — all negatives or all positives cannot reach full marks
- Annotated map not referred to in script (N20 Q5) — must create the map AND mention it explicitly in the written script
- Rephrasing the SoI as the research question — explicitly 0 marks across M21, N22, M23
- Social media response too informal — this is an OFFICIAL school social media page, not a personal post; too casual = C2 1–2 marks
- Vague purpose: hashtag must be specific and connected to the historical example, not generic like "#globalinteractions"

## Common question types

1. **Negative impact of globalization — explain (4 marks)** — state exploitation + name specific case (opium trade/China) + context + causation; the 4th mark always requires a causal reasoning statement
2. **Research question for a globalization SoI (2 marks)** — clear AND focused (specific country, time period, factor); rephrasing SoI = 0; no specifics = 1 mark
3. **Presentation/extended writing on global trade (18 marks, N20 Q5)** — must annotate a world map AND refer to it in the script; both positive and negative impacts required
4. **Social media response (18 marks, M25 Q5)** — official/professional tone throughout; formal greeting + original hashtag for C1 marks
5. **Essay: has globalization been beneficial? (N16 Q8 / M24B Q10)** — requires both beneficial and harmful arguments with specific historical examples; synthesis conclusion with a clear position

## Key arguments

### N16 Q8 / M24B Q10 — To what extent has globalization been beneficial for societies?

**YES — globalization has been beneficial:**
- Silk Road spread Buddhism, Islam, technology, spices — enriched societies intellectually and materially over centuries
- Global trade created economic growth; raised living standards in trading nations
- International institutions (UN, WTO) attempted to regulate global interactions and promote fairer trade
- Cultural exchange through food, music, art, fashion has enriched societies that engaged with it voluntarily

**NO — globalization has not always been beneficial:**
- Atlantic slave trade: economic benefit for Europe built entirely on catastrophic human cost for Africa and the enslaved
- Opium trade: British imperialism exploited China; addiction, wars, unequal treaties followed
- Cultural imperialism: dominant cultures overwrote local traditions; languages, religions, governance systems suppressed
- WTO accused of favouring rich nations; sweatshop labour; environmental degradation tied to global supply chains

**Model conclusion:** Globalization has been undeniably beneficial for some societies at some times — the Silk Road's cultural diffusion and the Industrial Revolution's trade-driven growth are genuine examples of material improvement. But the benefit has been deeply uneven: the same processes that enriched European empires systematically impoverished and violated the societies they exploited. The honest assessment is that globalization has been enormously beneficial for those with power to shape its terms, and harmful or indifferent to those without that power. "Beneficial" cannot be applied to globalization as a whole without specifying — beneficial for whom?
