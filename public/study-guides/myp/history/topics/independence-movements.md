# Independence Movements

Independence movements appear in nearly every MYP History session — as essay topics, source analysis contexts, extended writing tasks, and research planning subjects. Key movements studied include Indian independence (Jallianwala Bagh, Gandhi), Ghanaian independence (Nkrumah, national identity-building), Latin American independence (Napoleon's invasion of Spain triggering colonial collapse), and the American Revolution. Essays require two distinct factors covered with roughly equal depth.

---

## Key concepts

- **Jallianwala Bagh massacre**: on 13 April 1919, General Reginald Dyer ordered British troops to fire without warning on unarmed Indian civilians in a walled garden in Amritsar; historians regard it as a turning point that accelerated Indian independence — the Singh Twins' 2019 painting marking the 100th anniversary is Source A in N23/M24A.
- **Ghanaian independence**: led by Kwame Nkrumah and the Convention People's Party (CPP), Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African colony to gain independence, in 1957; Nkrumah built national identity through new symbols — a national flag, currency, anthem, coat of arms, museums, and monuments — drawing on African rather than Western traditions (Source B, N23/M24A).
- **Latin American independence**: triggered when Napoleon invaded Spain in 1808 and broke the colonies' political link to the Crown; independence declarations across most of Spanish-speaking Latin America followed between 1810 and 1825, led by figures like Bolívar and San Martín.
- **American Revolution**: North American colonists protested British taxation without parliamentary representation; after the Crown refused to submit, they declared independence in 1776 and won it formally with the Treaty of Paris in 1783 — the mark scheme model for M23 Q3.
- **Satyagraha**: Gandhi's philosophy of non-violent resistance ("truth-force"), applied in the Salt March of 1930 — Gandhi led a 240-mile march to the sea to protest the British salt tax, drawing global media attention; it influenced Martin Luther King Jr.'s civil rights strategy.
- **National identity**: the shared symbols, history, and culture that unite a post-independence nation — Nkrumah built Ghana's through state institutions AND cultural art; Wiz Kudowor's paintings used ancient African symbolism to celebrate independence (Source C, N23/M24A).
- **Decolonization**: the accelerating post-WWII process by which colonies gained independence — Britain was weakened, the UN promoted self-determination, and Cold War pressure on Western democracies created a political window for independence movements.
- **Self-determination**: the right of a people to determine their own political status; championed by US President Woodrow Wilson in his 14 Points after WWI, making it a principle of international relations.

## Case studies

### Jallianwala Bagh massacre (N23/M24A)

- 13 April 1919; Jallianwala Bagh (walled garden), Amritsar, Punjab, India
- General Reginald Dyer ordered British troops to fire on peaceful unarmed demonstrators without warning
- Protesters opposing the Rowlatt Act (forced military service) and heavy war tax
- Hundreds killed (disputed); thousands wounded; protestors had no exit — walled garden trapped them
- Historians regard the massacre as a significant step toward the end of British rule in India
- Singh Twins' painting (2019): created for the 100th anniversary; exhibited in Manchester Museum; value = shows colonial violence that fuelled Indian nationalism; limitation = artists did not witness the event, created 100 years later

### Ghanaian national identity (N23/M24A Q7)

| Feature | Source B (Nkrumah — academic article) | Source C (Wiz Kudowor — CGTN transcript) |
|---------|---------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Focus | Political and institutional expressions of identity | Cultural and artistic expressions of identity |
| Content | Flag, currency, anthem, coat of arms, founding fathers, museums, monuments | Paintings using ancient African symbolism to celebrate independence |
| Method | Government-led state-building | Individual artist using traditional cultural symbols |
| Source type | Secondary academic article | Contemporary news broadcast (primary) |

- Both sources must be used for 4–6 marks; only one source = max 3 marks
- At 6 marks: must show that national identity can be expressed in different ways — political/institutional AND cultural/artistic

### American Revolution (M23 Q3 model example)

- 1774: British imposed Intolerable Acts → colonial resistance grew
- 1775: Battles of Lexington and Concord → armed conflict began
- 1776: Declaration of Independence (crown refused to submit to colonial demands)
- 1783: Treaty of Paris → formal British recognition of US independence
- Mark scheme scale: state protest (1 mark) → add demands (2 marks) → add Crown's refusal (3 marks) → add consequence/outcome (4 marks)

### Latin American independence (M23)

- Napoleon's 1808 invasion of Spain removed the Spanish King → power vacuum → colonies lost link to Crown
- Viceroyalty system had created resentment: peninsulares (Spanish-born) dominated over criollos (American-born)
- Bolívar, San Martín, and others led independence declarations across Latin America 1810–1825
- Argentina, Venezuela, Mexico, Bolivia all gained independence during this wave
- Concept links: Governance (Spanish king's authority), Causality (Napoleon triggered it), Conflict (wars of independence)

### M23 Q5 biography — Luigi model example

- Must use a fictional unknown individual, NOT a real historical figure
- Using Gandhi, Mandela, Churchill etc. = Criterion A capped at 3/6 marks
- Mark scheme model: "Luigi, an Italian soldier after WWI" who joins Mussolini's Fascist party — fictional person, real historical events
- C1 requirements: title, unknown fictional individual, third-person voice

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- For N23/M24A Q8: exactly two factors with roughly equal development; synthesis conclusion stating which is most important (or why they're equally important)
- For M23 Q3: full causal chain — group/cause → specific demands → escalation → outcome; all four stages for 4 marks
- For N23 Q7: evidence from BOTH sources; showing that national identity takes different forms (political and cultural)
- For M23 Q5: fictional unknown individual living through real historical events; third person throughout

### What examiners penalise

- Only one factor in N23/M24A Q8 — explicitly capped at maximum 6 marks (Criterion D)
- Using a real historical figure in M23 Q5 biography — Criterion A capped at 3/6
- Rephrasing the SoI as the research question (M23 Q4a) — explicitly 0 marks
- Only analysing one source in N23 Q7 — maximum 3 marks
- Verbatim quotes from von Bülow or other sources counting as detail — must paraphrase and elaborate

1. **Explain how one group caused their country's independence (4 marks, M23 Q3)** — state group + demands + government's refusal/response + outcome; American Revolution is the safest example

6. **Biography of fictional unknown individual (18 marks, M23 Q5)** — invent the person; third person; title + historical accuracy throughout

## Key arguments

### N23/M24A Q8 — What are the two most important factors in the outcome of independence movements?

**Leadership as a factor:**
- Gandhi's satyagraha (non-violent resistance) turned local grievances into a global moral argument against British rule
- Nkrumah's organisational ability through the CPP translated Pan-Africanist ideology into Ghana's actual independence in 1957
- Leaders gave movements strategic direction, international credibility, and a unifying symbol

**External/international pressure as a factor:**
- Post-WWII: Britain's financial exhaustion weakened its ability to sustain empire
- Cold War: USA and USSR both pressured Western powers to decolonise to win non-aligned nations
- UN Charter (1945) embedded self-determination as a global principle, giving independence movements legitimate international backing

**Culture and national identity as a factor:**
- Nkrumah built Ghanaian identity through symbols that pre-dated colonialism — demonstrating the nation existed before the empire did
- Cultural identity created the "we" that protest alone could not

**Model conclusion:** No single factor determines the outcome of independence movements — the most successful movements combined strong leadership (Gandhi, Nkrumah) with external pressures that made continued colonial rule politically untenable (post-WWII weakness, Cold War, UN self-determination). Of these, leadership tends to be the most decisive factor, because it is what converts favourable conditions into actual independence. The Jallianwala Bagh massacre created conditions for Indian nationalism; Gandhi's strategy determined what that nationalism achieved. Without the leader, the conditions wait indefinitely.
