# Empires and Colonialism

Empires and colonialism appear across many MYP History sessions as contexts for knowledge questions, source analysis, and essays. The British Empire is the most frequently examined — particularly its rule over India (Jallianwala Bagh 1919), the opium trade with China, and its role in global trade (Atlantic slave trade). German imperialism and WWI causation appears in M23. Questions require students to explain how empires influenced the people they ruled, to analyse sources about imperial relationships, and to assess consequences of colonial power.

---

## Key concepts

- **British Empire**: at its peak in 1922, the largest empire in history, covering ~25% of the world's land surface and governing ~450 million people — examined through three main case studies: the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (India 1919), the opium trade with China (19th century), and the Atlantic slave trade.
- **Jallianwala Bagh massacre**: on 13 April 1919, General Reginald Dyer ordered British troops to fire without warning on unarmed Indian civilians protesting the Rowlatt Act in a walled garden in Amritsar; the event became a symbol of colonial brutality and catalyst for Indian nationalism (N23, M24A Source A).
- **Opium trade**: Britain forced China to accept opium exports from India in exchange for tea, provoking the Opium Wars (1839–42 and 1856–60); benefits flowed to Britain while China suffered addiction crises — the N22 model answer for a negative impact of globalization.
- **Spanish colonialism**: the viceroyalty system in Latin America created a racial hierarchy with peninsulares (Spanish-born) above criollos (American-born), generating resentment that drove independence movements when Napoleon's 1808 invasion of Spain broke the political link between colonies and Crown (M23 Source A).
- **Von Bülow's "place in the sun"**: German Chancellor von Bülow argued Germany deserved global influence because other powers had it — his speech (Source B, M23) exemplifies the imperial entitlement mindset and is used to justify Germany's responsibility for WWI through expansionism.
- **Cultural destruction**: colonial rule suppressed Indigenous languages, religions, and governance systems across Asia, Africa, and the Americas — used as evidence in globalization essays (N22) and empire benefit/harm questions (N19).
- **Imperialism**: extending power through colonization, military force, or economic pressure — Ferguson's pro-empire argument (N16 Q6) is analysed from the perspective of the NGO Globalization For All, which challenges the claim that empires were beneficial.

## Case studies

### British Empire in India — M21 Q2 model answer

| Level | Example |
|-------|---------|
| 1 mark | "The British Empire eliminated the people through military control." |
| 2 marks | "The British Empire dominated India through military control and its system of government." |
| 3 marks | "The British Empire dominated India with military control, violently suppressing uprisings like the Amritsar massacre. The laws implemented also influenced the Indian population." |
| 4 marks | Same as above, plus: "Eventually this dominance influenced the growth of a nationalist movement that aimed to challenge Britain and be independent from British rule." |

**Key at 4 marks**: cause-and-effect reasoning — show how the empire's influence led to a consequence (nationalism, independence movement). Description alone = 2–3 marks.

### Opium trade with China — N22 Q1b model answer

| Level | Example |
|-------|---------|
| 1 mark | "The British Empire was able to exploit societies that were less powerful." |
| 2 marks | Adds: "such as when they forced China to exchange opium for tea." |
| 3 marks | Adds context: "The British policy was to help traders find new markets when opium was banned in Europe." |
| 4 marks | Adds causation: "So as countries became more integrated through globalization, the benefits were unfairly distributed." |

### German imperialism and WWI — M23 Q6

- Von Bülow's argument: Germany is powerful and should do what other powers are doing ("place in the sun")
- Source B is a **primary source** (contemporary government speech); Source C is used alongside it
- Germany's expansionism was perceived as a threat → other powers formed counter-alliances → chain of alliance obligations → WWI

**M23 Q6c mark scale (justify Germany's responsibility):**
- 1 mark: implicit reference to one source — "Germany had an expansionist policy"
- 2 marks: one source explicitly referenced — "Source B shows the German government was calling for expansionism, which could have been seen as a threat"
- 3 marks: both sources referenced — adds "Source C shows that when countries feel threatened, they seek out alliances"
- 4 marks: both sources + detailed causal chain — names alliances; shows expansion → threat → alliances → war

**Cap rule**: Referencing only one source = maximum 2 marks. Both sources required for 3–4 marks.

### Columbus and Indigenous peoples — N22 Q5

- Source B: newsletter from a Penn Law student who is a member of the Schaghticoke Tribal Nation in Connecticut
- Perspective: negative view of Columbus as a "discoverer" — challenges the term because of the harm his voyages caused to Native peoples
- **0 marks**: stating general facts about what happened to Native peoples — must identify the PERSPECTIVE on Columbus as a "discoverer"
- **1 mark**: "The student takes a negative perspective toward Columbus."
- **2 marks**: "The student takes a negative perspective toward Columbus by pointing out the harmful impacts his discovery caused."

### M21 Q4 — European empires investigation evaluation

- RQ: "What were the key reasons for the expansion of European empires in the 19th century?"
- **Key strength**: research question is appropriate to the SoI about identity influencing how nations interact with others
- **Key limitation**: sub-questions 2 and 3 ("How did empires come to an end?" and "What role did empires play in WWI?") address *outcomes*, not *reasons for expansion* — they drift from the stated focus of the RQ, producing less credible results

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

- For M21 Q2: cause-and-effect reasoning at 4 marks — how the empire's influence provoked a nationalist response; description alone is 2–3 marks
- For N22 Q1b: the causation statement ("as countries became more integrated through globalization, benefits were unfairly distributed") earns the 4th mark
- For M23 Q6c: both Sources B and C referenced for 3–4 marks; explicit causal chain from expansion to alliances to war
- For N22 Q5: identifying the specific perspective on Columbus as a "discoverer" — not describing what happened to Native peoples
- For M21 Q4: identifying sub-question drift as the specific limitation

### What examiners penalise

- M21 Q2 at 4 marks: describing what the empire did without showing its consequence (provoked nationalism, independence movement)
- M23 Q6b: verbatim quotes from von Bülow — explicitly do not count as additional detail; must paraphrase
- M23 Q6c: referencing only one source — maximum 2 marks; both required for full marks
- N22 Q5: describing the Columbus source or recounting what happened to Native peoples without identifying the student's specific perspective — 0 marks
- M21 Q4: identifying "limited sources" generally without pinpointing the sub-question drift as the core limitation

## Common question types

1. **Explain how one empire influenced the people it ruled (4 marks, M21 Q2)** — describe influence + show how it led to a consequence (nationalism, independence); British Empire in India is the model example
2. **Explain one negative impact of globalization (4 marks, N22 Q1b)** — state exploitation + name specific case (opium trade) + context + causation
3. **Outline a perspective on Columbus (2 marks, N22 Q5)** — identify the perspective (negative/critical), not the facts about Native peoples
4. **Outline von Bülow's justification (2 marks, M23 Q6b)** — paraphrase the "other countries are doing it" argument; no verbatim quotes
5. **Justify Germany's WWI responsibility using sources (4 marks, M23 Q6c)** — both sources referenced; causal chain from expansion to war
6. **Evaluate an investigation into empire expansion (8 marks, M21 Q4)** — identify sub-question drift as the key limitation; strengths + limitations + appraisal
