# English LAL — Command Terms

## Quick Reference

| Command term | Criterion | Frequency | What it means | What the mark scheme rewards |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Analyse / Analyze | A | 12 sessions | Break down a technique or text feature and explain its effect on the audience | Technique named + embedded quote/reference + effect on reader/viewer explained |
| Comment | A | 11 sessions | Make observations about a text feature, developing them with examples | A stated observation with at least one example or explanation; identifies + develops |
| Compare and contrast | A + B | 14 sessions (Q1e every session) | Identify similarities AND differences between two texts in a single integrated response | Both texts covered; explicit comparison language; analysis of technique + effect in both |
| Create | C + D (Q2) / B + C + D (Q3) | 14 sessions | Produce an original text (literary or non-literary) responding to an image or prompt | Insight, imagination, stylistic choices; appropriate register; accurate grammar |
| Describe | A | 6 sessions | Give a detailed account of a text feature, with supporting evidence | More than identification — a developed account with reference to the text |
| Explain | A | 11 sessions | Give reasons for a text feature, purpose, or effect — go beyond identification | Purpose/effect stated + reasons given + supporting evidence |
| Evaluate | A | 1 session (M22 Q1b) | Weigh up the effect of a text feature and reach a considered judgement | Effect stated, developed with examples AND explanation; conclusion or judgement implied |
| Examine | A | 1 session (N21 Q1d) | Look closely at a text feature and consider how and why it works | Close reading of specific techniques/choices + analysis of audience impact |
| Identify | A | 1 session (N16 Q1a) | Name or state a text feature, character aspect, or example | Correct, relevant point stated; no development required for full marks |
| Interpret | A | 1 session (M25 Q1c) | Use knowledge and understanding to draw meaning from a text feature, title, or phrase | A stated meaning developed with explanation drawn from the text OR from knowledge/understanding; valid even without direct textual reference |
| Justify | A | 2 sessions (M19 Q1d, M23 Q1d) | Give reasons why something is significant, relevant, or important | Significance stated + reasons supported by evidence from the text |
| List | A | 1 session (N16 Q1e) | Name a series of relevant points or examples | Correct, relevant points; no development required |
| Outline | A | 2 sessions (N16 Q1b, N20 Q1c) | Give a brief, clear account of the main features | Key points identified; minimal development expected |
| Present | B + C + D | 1 session (N16 Q3) | Deliver ideas in a structured, purposeful way for a given audience | Structured form; persuasive/rhetorical language; appropriate register |
| Select and examine | A | 1 session (M18 Q1d) | Choose a specific moment or example and analyse it in detail | Specific selection + close analysis of content, technique, and effect |

## Detailed Breakdown

### Analyse / Analyze

- **Used in:** M19 Q1b, M21 Q1b, M22 Q1d, M25 Q1b, N20 Q1b, N20 Q1d, N21 Q1b, N22 Q1b, N22 Q1d, N23 Q1a, N23 Q1d, N24 Q1c
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 2–4 marks per question
- **What it means:** Break a text feature down into its components — name the technique, show where it appears, and explain its effect on the reader or viewer.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1 (limited): identifies a technique or effect but does not link them. Example: "The poem uses imagery to show uncertainty."
  - Level 2 (adequate): identifies technique + provides example OR brief explanation. Example: "The poem uses imagery such as 'balloon like Uncle Harry' to show the speaker's anxiety."
  - Level 3/top band (effective): technique + embedded quote + effect + explanation, sustained. Example (M25 MS): "The poem uses alliteration in 'scary speculation' to emphasise how stressed out the speaker is … the repetition of 's' calls attention to her fear when she thinks about her future."
  - For 4-mark questions: two points fully developed, OR one point with sustained analysis.
- **What NOT to do:** Identify the technique without explaining its effect — that caps at level 1. Do not simply paraphrase what the text says.
- **Model approach:** State technique → embed short quote → explain effect on reader/audience. "The writer uses [technique], as seen in '[quote]', which [effect on reader]."

### Comment

- **Used in:** M18 Q1b, M18 Q1c, M19 Q1c (implied), M21 Q1d, M22 Q1a, M24 Q1c, M24 Q1d, M25 Q1a, N19 Q1a, N19 Q1c, N21 Q1c, N22 Q1a, N22 Q1c, N23 Q1c, N24 Q1a, N24 Q1d
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 2 marks per question
- **What it means:** Make an observation about a text feature and develop it with at least one example or explanation. Sits between "identify" and "analyse" in demand.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1: identifies a feature or effect without development. Example (N22 Q1a MS): "The reference to ornaments is a metaphor." / "It shows the beauty of the forest."
  - Level 2: comments on the feature with an example OR explanation. Example (N22 Q1a MS): "Comparing the trees to ornaments, which are decorations to make things beautiful, is a way to express how Astroft values the trees for their beauty and what they add to life."
  - For film questions (N21 Q1c): names a technique AND explains its effect on the viewer. Example: "Title card / text overlay introduces the central concept/thesis of the film; gives the viewer a framework for what follows."
- **What NOT to do:** Simply name the technique or repeat what the text says — that scores only level 1.
- **Model approach:** State what you notice → give the example → say what it does to the audience. Keep responses focused — typically one well-developed point is sufficient for 2 marks.

### Compare and Contrast

- **Used in:** Q1e in every session (M18, M19, M21, M22, M23, M24, M25, N16 Q1f, N19, N20, N21, N22, N23, N24) — 14 sessions
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing) 10 marks + B (Organizing) 10 marks = 20 marks total (35 marks in N16 with Criterion D included)
- **Typical word count:** approximately 300–350 words
- **What it means:** Identify and discuss both similarities AND differences in how two texts use techniques to achieve their effects. Must cover both texts.
- **What the mark scheme rewards — Criterion A (10 marks):**
  - 1–2: superficial ideas about one or both texts; little or no comparison
  - 3–5: obvious connections described; may summarise more than analyse
  - 6–8: clear comparison and contrast; analysis of techniques in both texts; implicit connections explored
  - 9–10: perceptive analysis; subtle connections; conclusions drawn; sophisticated use of terminology
  - Key strand: "Candidates' ability to compare and contrast the two texts (strand iv.) should be the deciding factor when placing students in a band." (Appears verbatim in almost every session's MS)
- **What the mark scheme rewards — Criterion B (10 marks):**
  - 1–2: writes about each text separately; minimal coherence
  - 3–5: attempts to compare and contrast; begins to make connections
  - 6–8: effective structure; compares and contrasts across paragraphs; ideas build on each other
  - 9–10: sophisticated structure; sustained, coherent, logical; structural devices used deliberately
- **Hard rules from mark schemes (consistent across all sessions):**
  - Only one text covered: cannot exceed **2 marks** for Criterion A AND cannot exceed **2 marks** for Criterion B
  - Compares but does not contrast (or vice versa): maximum **5 marks** for Criterion A
  - Bullet points: maximum **2 marks** for Criterion B (does not affect Criterion A)
  - Terminology can be implied — it does not need to be explicitly named to count
- **What NOT to do:** Write about Text 1 fully, then write about Text 2 fully as a separate essay — that is the structural failure that scores 1–2 for Criterion B. Do not only compare without contrasting.
- **Model approach:** Write in thematic or technique-based paragraphs that weave both texts together. Each paragraph should name a technique in Text 1, show where it appears, explain its effect, then immediately compare/contrast with the equivalent technique or its absence in Text 2. Open with a brief framing sentence about what both texts share, then move to differences. Close with a concluding observation.

### Create

- **Used in:** Q2 in all 14 sessions; Q3 in all 14 sessions; also Q2 N16
- **Criterion:** Q2: Criterion C (Producing text, 10 marks) + Criterion D (Using language, 10 marks). Q3: Criterion B (Organizing, 10 marks) + Criterion C (10 marks) + Criterion D (10 marks)
- **What it means:** Produce an original piece of writing — either a literary text (Q2: story, monologue, screenplay, narrative) or a non-literary text (Q3: proposal, letter, speech, script, blog post, interview, podcast script) — responding to a visual or written prompt.
- **What the mark scheme rewards — Criterion C (10 marks, consistent across sessions):**
  - 1–2: limited insight or imagination; minimal stylistic choices; few relevant details
  - 3–5: some insight; some stylistic/literary device choices; some relevant development
  - 6–8: considerable insight; thoughtful stylistic choices; sufficient relevant detail; engaging
  - 9–10: high degree of insight, imagination or sensitivity; perceptive stylistic choices; extensive detail developed with precision; highly engaging
  - Key phrase across Q2 sessions: "Reward all valid responses." There is no single correct answer — the marker rewards quality of craft.
  - Zero mark rule (Q2): responses that use neither the images nor the theme of the prompt score 0 for Criterion C.
- **What the mark scheme rewards — Criterion D (10 marks, identical wording across all sessions):**
  - 1–2: limited vocabulary; inappropriate register; minimal accuracy; errors often hinder communication
  - 3–5: adequate vocabulary; sometimes appropriate register; errors sometimes hinder communication
  - 6–8: varied vocabulary and sentence structures; appropriate register; errors do not hinder communication
  - 9–10: effective vocabulary and sentence structures; consistently purposeful register; high accuracy; minor errors; communication is effective
  - Key instruction in every session: "Examiners are reminded to consider best fit/balance in rewarding marks for all strands in Criterion D."
- **What the mark scheme rewards — Criterion B (Q3, 10 marks):**
  - 1–2: minimal organisational structures; basic chronological structure; elements may be absent; bullet points cap at 2 marks
  - 3–5: adequate structure; adheres to the given form
  - 6–8: effective structure; structural devices begin to add to overall effect; ideas build logically
  - 9–10: sophisticated structure; devices such as sentence/paragraph length crafted for effect; sustained, coherent, logical throughout
- **What NOT to do (Q2):** Ignore the image entirely — this risks a 0. Write a generic story that could have been written without the prompt.
- **What NOT to do (Q3):** Use bullet points — this caps Criterion B at 2/10. Write in an inappropriate register for the form (e.g., an informal letter to a chief architect).
- **Model approach (Q2):** Use the image as a specific anchor — reference details visible in or implied by it. Employ at least two or three deliberate literary devices (imagery, characterisation, point of view, mood, setting). Make language choices purposeful: vary sentence length, choose specific rather than general vocabulary.
- **Model approach (Q3):** Identify the required form (letter, speech, proposal, script, blog post) and commit to its conventions (salutation, sign-off, rhetorical address, etc.). Open with a clear purpose; develop with evidence, examples, or argument; close with impact. Match register to audience.

### Describe

- **Used in:** M21 Q1c, M22 Q1c, M24 Q1a, N16 Q1d, N23 Q1b, N24 Q1c (note: N24 Q1c is Analyse — see Analyse entry)
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 2 marks per question
- **What it means:** Give a detailed account of a text feature, how something is presented, or what something does — with supporting detail from the text.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1: identifies a feature or gives a basic example without development. Example (M21 Q1c MS): "It shows how Greta's individual actions have spread to others." / "The film shows the crowds/protestors increasing."
  - Level 2: describes with reference to the text, including an example and at least a brief explanation. Example (M21 Q1c MS): "It shows how Greta's individual actions have spread to others through images of other young people participating in (global) climate strikes."
  - Distinction from "comment": describe asks for a more factual, content-based account; comment asks for interpretive observation.
- **What NOT to do:** Simply name what is shown without explaining how or what it conveys.
- **Model approach:** Identify what is being described → give a specific example from the text → briefly explain what it shows or contributes. One developed point is typically sufficient for 2 marks.

### Explain

- **Used in:** M18 Q1a, M19 Q1a, M21 Q1a, M23 Q1b, M24 Q1b, M25 Q1d, N19 Q1d, N20 Q1a, N21 Q1a, N22 Q1d, N24 Q1b
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 2–3 marks per question
- **What it means:** Give reasons for a text feature, purpose, or effect. Goes further than "identify" or "describe" — you must state *why* as well as *what*.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1 / 1 mark: identifies a purpose or effect without explanation. Example (M18 Q1a MS): "The purpose of the questions is to introduce the text." / "The questions engage the reader." — these score 1.
  - Level 2 / 2 marks: explains the purpose AND its effect. Example (M18 Q1a MS): "The questions at the beginning of the text act as an introduction for the text. By addressing the reader directly, the author engages the interest of the reader."
  - Level 3 / 3 marks (for 3-mark questions): develops explanation with examples AND explanations. Example (M24 Q1b MS): "The line adds suspense to the story. It says he 'approaches' his depth, which means it is uncertain if he'll make it or not at this point." / "[The repetition of alarms] adds tension to the text because each alert signals that he is deeper and things are becoming more dangerous."
- **What NOT to do:** Simply state what the text says (paraphrase) without explaining the writer's purpose or effect on the audience — this scores only level 1.
- **Model approach:** State the purpose/effect → explain why the writer made that choice → show the effect on the reader, with a reference or quote. For 3-mark questions, develop at least two linked points or one point with full technique + quote + effect.

### Evaluate

- **Used in:** M22 Q1b only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 3
- **What it means:** Weigh up the effect of a specific line or feature, reaching a considered judgement about its significance.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1: identifies an effect but does not develop — "The line shows Margaret is positive."
  - Level 2: describes the effect with examples or explanations — "The effect is to show Margaret is resilient. Although the farm may go bankrupt, she is still able to find positive things."
  - Level 3: evaluates fully with examples AND explanation — demonstrates understanding of the character, the significance of the specific wording, and the effect on the audience. Example (M22 MS): "The effect of the line is to show Margaret as resilient in the face of adversity … she frames it as something 'not everyone has seen', as if watching the locusts form an army is an extraordinary privilege. This positions her as someone who…"
- **What NOT to do:** Give a general comment about the character without engaging with the specific language of the line.
- **Model approach:** Address the specific words in the quoted line → explain what the writer achieves → comment on how this affects the reader's perception of the character or situation.

### Examine

- **Used in:** N21 Q1d only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 3
- **What it means:** Look closely and carefully at a text feature, considering how and why it works on the audience.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - References to the closing of the film (Jason Silva's *Seeking the Aesthetic Experience*): accumulated imagery, direct address, rhetorical questions, repeated motif, call to action.
  - Top-band responses go beyond simple identification: they analyse how specific choices (slow motion, accumulation of images, the glowing orb motif, rhetorical questions) encourage the viewer and explain the mechanism of engagement.
  - Example (N21 MS): "The repeated motif of the glowing orb held by Silva suggests the aesthetic experience is something tangible, within reach, that the viewer can access." / "The use of slow motion / time-lapse in the closing shots encourages contemplation and slowing down, mirroring the act of appreciating beauty."
- **What NOT to do:** List techniques without explaining how they work on the viewer.
- **Model approach:** Identify a specific moment or choice → analyse why the filmmaker made it → explain the intended effect on the audience.

### Identify

- **Used in:** N16 Q1a only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 2 (1 mark per valid point)
- **What it means:** Name or state a text feature, example, or aspect. The lowest-demand command term — no explanation required.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:** Any two valid, relevant examples from the text. Example (N16 MS): personification, simile, metaphor — specific examples from the rain passage. Each valid point scores 1 mark independently.
- **What NOT to do:** Over-explain — marks are awarded per correct identification.
- **Model approach:** Name the feature and quote or paraphrase the example. No analysis needed.

### Interpret

- **Used in:** M25 Q1c only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 2
- **What it means:** Use knowledge and understanding to draw a meaning from a text feature, title, or phrase. Notably, the M25 mark scheme explicitly states: "it is valid for candidates to interpret the phrase 'Finding Myself' without referencing the text" — interpretations grounded in personal knowledge or experience of the phrase are accepted at level 2.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Level 1: states a meaning without development — "It is a metaphor for self-discovery."
  - Level 2: interprets the meaning with development drawn from the film OR from knowledge/understanding outside the text. Example (M25 MS): "The title reveals the theme of the video. The speaker describes her family, then explains that she still has time to find out more about herself." / "'Finding yourself' is a metaphor for self-discovery. When we are growing up, we try a lot of different activities to figure out what we like and 'find' who we are."
- **What NOT to do:** Simply repeat the title or give a one-word synonym without developing the interpretation.
- **Model approach:** State what you think the title/phrase means → develop with reference to the text or to broader meaning → explain how this connects to the text's central ideas.

---

### Justify

- **Used in:** M19 Q1d, M23 Q1d
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 1–3 marks
- **What it means:** Give reasons why something is significant, choosing evidence and explaining why it supports the claim.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - M19 Q1d (1 mark): any valid justification that links the scene to a turning point in Andy's conflict, with a reason.
  - M23 Q1d (2–3 marks): significance of a ballet clip stated + reasons supported by specific examples from the film. Example (M23 MS): "This clip shows how ballet combines all forms together to tell a story … it shows 'The Sleeping Beauty' to illustrate the combination of choreography, music, and story." / "This clip is significant because it is the moment Aurora is cursed. It is significant because it is the climax of the ballet and is an important turning point for the narrative."
- **What NOT to do:** Simply assert that something is significant without giving reasons.
- **Model approach:** State the significance → give the reason → support with an example from the text. The word "because" is your structural anchor.

### List

- **Used in:** N16 Q1e only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 3 (1 mark per valid point)
- **What it means:** Name several relevant points, examples, or details from the text. No development required.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:** Three correct, distinct points from the text. Example (N16 MS, predicting effects of climate change): "more rain at sea" / "less rain on land" / "hotter and/or drier monsoon seasons" / "migration/places become ghost towns" / "crops don't grow."
- **Hard rule:** "Do not award marks for points not mentioned in the text." (N16 MS) — invented or general knowledge points score 0.
- **Model approach:** Write three clear, separate points. Quote or paraphrase directly from the text.

---

### Outline

- **Used in:** N16 Q1b, N20 Q1c
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Typical mark range:** 2–4 marks
- **What it means:** Give a brief, clear account of the key features — more than identification but less analytical depth than "analyse."
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - N16 Q1b (4 marks, "Outline two aspects of the father's character and select two examples"): 2 marks for each character aspect linked to a relevant example. Aspect + example = 2 marks; aspect alone or example alone = 1 mark each.
  - N20 Q1c (2 marks, "Outline two features"): 1 mark per feature outlined, including how or why it makes the companions appealing. Example (N20 MS): "The 'strange beasts' are interactive, which makes them fun to play with." / "They act like pets … since they don't die there is less emotional risk for the human."
- **What NOT to do:** Give features without any indication of their effect or relevance.
- **Model approach:** Name the feature + give the example + add a brief explanatory link. Keep it concise.

---

### Present

- **Used in:** N16 Q3 only
- **Criterion:** B (Organizing) + C (Producing text) + D (Using language)
- **Marks:** 35 (N16 had an older, higher-mark structure)
- **What it means:** Deliver ideas to an audience in a structured, purposeful way — specifically for a persuasive speech in N16.
- **What the mark scheme rewards (N16):** Persuasive language (pathos, logos, ethos); rhetorical devices; structured delivery; appropriate register for a school assembly speech; engagement with the global context.
- **Model approach:** Same as "Create" for non-literary text — commit to the form (speech), use rhetorical address ("Ladies and gentlemen…"), build a structured argument, end with a call to action.

---

### Select and Examine

- **Used in:** M18 Q1d only
- **Criterion:** A (Analysing)
- **Marks:** 8
- **What it means:** Two-stage command. First, choose a specific moment from a visual text. Second, examine closely how that moment conveys the given idea.
- **What the mark scheme rewards:**
  - Lower bands: generic identification of a moment; vague link to the given phrase.
  - Mid bands: describes the moment with some detail; makes a connection to the phrase without consistent analysis.
  - Upper bands: specific selection + perceptive analysis of content, language, structure, technique, and/or style + awareness of cultural or social meaning + sustained commentary. (M18 MS)
- **What NOT to do:** Select a moment without examining it, or examine a technique without connecting it to the specific phrase or concept in the question.
- **Model approach:** Name the specific moment you are selecting and say why you chose it → close-read the visual details → link those details to the concept in the question → comment on the effect on the audience.

## Notes on Command Term Patterns

**The hierarchy in practice (for Criterion A short questions):**

Identify → List → Outline → Describe → Comment → Justify / Explain → Analyse / Evaluate / Examine

Each step up requires more development: identification of a feature, then a reason or effect, then a developed explanation with evidence, then full technique + quote + effect + evaluation.

**"Comment" vs "Analyse" distinction:**
Mark schemes consistently show that "comment" is satisfied by one developed observation with an example. "Analyse" at 3 marks requires technique + quote + effect + explanation in full, and at 4 marks typically requires two fully developed analytical points.

**"How" as a command term (M23 Q1a):**
The question "How is Ximena characterized in the opening?" uses "how" rather than a formal command term. The mark scheme treats it like "analyse characterisation" — technique or aspect identified, with examples and explanations at the top band.

**Q1e "Compare and contrast" — the non-negotiable rules (appear verbatim across 12+ sessions):**
1. Must write about both texts — one text only = max 2 marks on both A and B
2. Must compare AND contrast — missing either = max 5 marks on Criterion A
3. Bullet points = max 2 marks on Criterion B
4. Terminology can be implied — does not need to be explicitly named
