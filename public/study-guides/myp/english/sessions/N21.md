## Text 1

**Read the excerpt from the novel *Perfume* by Patrick Süskind about a scent that intrigues and perplexes the experienced perfumer Giuseppe Baldini in eighteenth-century France. The scent was crafted by his competitor, Pélissier.**

Before him stood the flacon with Pélissier's perfume. Glistening golden brown in the sunlight, the liquid was clear, not clouded in the least. It looked totally innocent, like a light tea—and yet contained, in addition to four-fifths alcohol, one-fifth of a mysterious mixture that could count an entire city among its admirers. Before him about the flacon with Pélissier's perfume. Glistening golden brown in the sunlight, the liquid was clear, not clouded in the least. It looked totally innocent, like a light tea—and yet contained, in addition to four-fifths alcohol, one-fifth of a mysterious mixture that could count an entire city among its admirers.

Baldini tore his nose carefully. [...] He pulled a fresh white lace handkerchief out of a desk drawer and unfolded it. Then, holding his head far back and pinching his nostrils together, he opened the flacon with a gentle turn of the stopper. He did not want to smell it. He did not want to smell it at all. He just wanted, for God's sake, to get a premature sense of what his nose was in for. Perfumes must be smelled in the differenced, generous state, never as a premature abstraction the drops shaped in a closed bottle. He approached the glass stopper cautiously to his nose, crushed up the scent as if it were a powder, immediately blew it out again, turned the stopper, and finally—in one tiny deep breath—breathed in the scent again, and finally drew one tiny deep breath, which he then exhaled slowly with several pauses, as if letting it slide down a long, gently sloping staircase. He tossed the handkerchief onto his desk and fell back into his armchair.

The perfume was disgustingly good. That miserable Pélissier was unfortunately a virtuoso. A master. [...] Baldini wished he had created it himself, this Amor and Psyche. There was nothing common about it. An absolute classic—full and harmonious. And for all that, fascinatingly new. It was fresh, but not frenetic. It was floral, without being unctuous. It possessed depth, a splendid, abiding, voluptuous, rich brown depth—and yet was not in the least excessive or bombastic.

Baldini stood up almost in reverence and held the handkerchief under his nose once again. "Wonderful, wonderful..." he murmured, sniffing greedily. "It has a cheerful character, it's charming, it's like a melody, puts you in a good mood at once... What nonsense, a good mood!" And he flung the handkerchief back onto his desk in anger, turned away, and walked to the farthest corner of the room, as if ashamed of his enthusiasm.

### Task 1 — Analysing (30 marks, Criteria A and B)

**Timing recommendation: 35 minutes**
- Questions 1a to 1e are assessed according to criterion A (Analysing).

#### Q1a
- Criterion: A
- Marks: 2
- Command term: Explain
- Question text: **Explain** the significance of the phrase "beau of the perfume" (paragraph 1).

**Mark Scheme:**

Award 1 mark for each relevant point up to a maximum of 2 marks.

Indicative content:
- The phrase suggests that Pélissier's perfume is the finest/most beautiful perfume / best example of perfume-making
- It implies rivalry — Baldini recognises his competitor has created something exceptional
- The word "beau" (French for beautiful/handsome) signals the perfume has an almost human-like appeal or attractiveness
- It signals Baldini's reluctant admiration despite his envy
- Accept any other valid interpretation supported by the text

#### Q1b
- Criterion: A
- Marks: 2
- Command term: Analyse
- Question text: **Analyse** how the writer creates a sense of Baldini's conflict as he smells the perfume.

**Mark Scheme:**

Award 1 mark for each relevant point up to a maximum of 2 marks.

Indicative content:
- References to Baldini's physical actions (pinching nostrils, holding head back) suggest his resistance / reluctance to be affected by the perfume
- His internal acknowledgement that the perfume is "disgustingly good" shows the conflict between his professional jealousy and his expert appreciation
- The contrast between his outward behaviour (flinging the handkerchief, walking away "as if ashamed") and his internal reaction ("wonderful, wonderful") illustrates his inner conflict
- His use of self-deprecating language ("What nonsense, a good mood!") shows him fighting against his own positive emotional response
- The deliberate, controlled way he smells the perfume (cautiously, in tiny breaths) contrasts with his eventual involuntary reaction of standing "almost in reverence"
- Accept any other valid interpretation supported by the text

#### Q1e
- Criterion: A and B
- Marks: 20 (A — 10 marks, B — 10 marks)
- Command term: Compare and contrast
- Question text (Texts 1 and 2): **Compare and contrast** how the creators portray the impact of sensory experiences.

(Approximately 350 words)

**Timing recommendation: 35 minutes**
- This question part is assessed according to criteria A (Analysing) and B (Organizing).
- This question part is worth 20 marks (A — 10 marks and B — 10 marks).

**Mark Scheme:**

**Criterion A — Analysing (10 marks)**

Band descriptors (apply holistically):

- **9–10**: Insightful, convincing analysis of both texts. The response identifies and analyses a range of language and stylistic features in both texts with convincing, well-developed commentary. Clear understanding of the effect of choices on the audience/reader. Sustained, detailed comparison and contrast throughout.

- **7–8**: Detailed analysis of both texts. The response identifies and analyses language and stylistic features in both texts with developed commentary. Good understanding of the effect of choices on the audience/reader. Comparison and contrast is maintained throughout.

- **5–6**: Some analysis of both texts. The response identifies some language and stylistic features in both texts. Some commentary on effect. Comparison and contrast is attempted.

- **3–4**: Limited analysis. The response identifies some features but with limited commentary. Understanding of effect is superficial. The comparison/contrast may be uneven (stronger on one text than the other).

- **1–2**: Minimal analysis. Features are listed or paraphrased rather than analysed. Little or no understanding of effect. The comparison/contrast is mostly absent.

- **0**: Nothing worthy of credit.

Indicative content for comparison and contrast:

*Similarities:*
- Both texts explore the powerful, almost overwhelming impact that sensory experiences (smell / visual and auditory beauty) can have on individuals
- Both texts suggest that sensory experience produces an involuntary emotional/physical response — Baldini is overcome despite his resistance; Silva describes being "held in aesthetic arrest"
- Both texts use vivid, elevated language to convey the intensity of sensory experience
- Both texts suggest that sensory beauty has the power to transform one's mood or state of consciousness

*Differences:*
- Text 1 is a literary prose excerpt; Text 2 is a multimodal short film — the medium differs fundamentally, with Text 2 able to combine image, sound, and speech simultaneously
- In Text 1, the sensory experience (smell) is presented as an internal, private, even reluctant experience — Baldini fights against his response; in Text 2, Silva actively celebrates and seeks out aesthetic experience
- Text 1 focuses on a single, specific sensory experience (one perfume); Text 2 ranges across multiple senses and many contexts
- Text 1 presents sensory impact through narrative and characterisation; Text 2 uses direct address and rhetorical questions to involve the viewer personally
- Baldini's experience is marked by conflict and shame; Silva's is marked by enthusiasm and advocacy
