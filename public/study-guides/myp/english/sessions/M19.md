## Questions

### Q1a
- **Criterion:** Language analysis (Criterion A)
- **Marks:** [not clearly stated — implied short answer]
- **Command term:** Explain
- **Question text:** Explain one technique the writer uses for effect in the following line:

  > "The swallows perform their complicated aerial gyrations with a grace that's alien to our clumsy human world, and without crashing into each other, yet falling to the ground like Icarus, or me."

**Mark Scheme / Indicative Content:**

The writer uses the line to contrast the swallows' freedom and natural grace with the narrator's own entrapment and clumsiness. Acceptable techniques and effects include:

- **Allusion / Classical reference** — "like Icarus, or me": Icarus fell because he overreached (flew too high, too close to the sun). By placing "or me" alongside Icarus, the writer equates the narrator's own downfall with the myth — suggesting pride, punishment, or fatal error. This gives the narrator a tragic, self-aware quality.
- **Contrast / Juxtaposition** — "alien to our clumsy human world" vs. the swallows' graceful gyrations. The birds embody effortless freedom while the humans (prisoners) are literally grounded. The contrast underscores the narrator's loss of freedom.
- **Listing / Tricolon** — "complicated aerial gyrations… without crashing… yet falling": the build-up emphasises the swallows' skill before the final ironic fall, which implicates the narrator.
- **Irony** — The swallows "fall to the ground" but this is natural, controlled, elegant; the narrator's fall is metaphorical and permanent.
- Students should identify technique + quote + effect on reader.

### Q2 — Comparative Question (Texts 1 and 2)
- **Criterion:** Language analysis / comparative writing (Criterion A)
- **Marks:** [not stated — extended response]
- **Command term:** Compare and contrast
- **Question text:**

  **Texts 1 and 2**

  Compare and contrast how the writer and filmmaker portray prisoners.

  *(Approximately 350 words)*

**Mark Scheme / Indicative Content:**

From the sample student answer and examiner commentary visible in the document:

- Both texts present prisoners as more than their crimes — both the non-fiction writer (Harris Wood) and the filmmaker humanise their subjects.
- **Text 1** uses first-person narration, which gives direct access to the prisoner's thoughts and feelings, creating intimacy and empathy. The mention of birds/swallows and nature connects the prisoner narrator to beauty and the outside world.
- **Text 2** uses visual imagery and a voice-over to achieve a similar effect — the audience sees prisoners as individuals with inner lives (via Andy's story and the music).
- **Similarity:** Both portray prisoners as feeling trapped and longing for freedom; both use natural imagery or sensory experience (swallows / music) to express this longing.
- **Difference:** Text 1 is personal and introspective — the narrator speaks for himself. Text 2 is more observational — the filmmaker presents prisoners from a slight distance, mediating through camera and voice-over.
- **Difference:** Text 1's narrator accepts his fate with irony ("or me"); Text 2's Andy is more actively in conflict — he resists and asserts himself.
- High-band responses should provide specific textual evidence from both texts, use comparison connectives, and discuss the effect of the portrayal on the audience/reader.
