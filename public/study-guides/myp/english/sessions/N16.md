### Task 1 — Analysing (Texts 1 and 2)

---

### Q1a
- **Criterion:** A
- **Marks:** 2
- **Command term:** Identify
- **Question text (Reconstructed from MS):** Identify **two** ways in which the author brings the rain to life in paragraph 1.
- **Sub-parts:** None

**Mark Scheme:**

Award **(1 mark)** for any combination of relevant examples or literary features up to a maximum of **(2 marks)**. Reward all valid points.

The author brings the rain to life by:
- using personification/simile/metaphor ('merciless', 'plays tricks', is so 'corrosive' he can 'eat through the zinc roof')
- describing it as demanding (needs 'sacrifices' of the pots and pans)
- describing/comparing it as powerful (water 'inexhaustible', 'rivals the seas')

### Q1c
- **Criterion:** A
- **Marks:** 8
- **Command term:** Analyse (inferred — "analyse the effect")
- **Question text (Reconstructed from MS):** The author repeats the motif of rain for effect throughout the extract. Based on **two** questions from the writer's perspective, analyse its effect on the reader. *(Reconstruction uncertain — exact wording of Q1c question not fully legible due to watermark)*
- **Sub-parts:** None

**Mark Scheme:**

Award **(2 marks)** for each valid effect on reader (up to maximum of **(2 marks)** per effect, up to total of **(8 marks)**). Reward all valid points. Responses should show understanding of the writer's use of the rain motif AND its effect on the reader.

Indicative content — effect on reader:
- rain conveys a sense of menace/threat/danger/overwhelming power
- rain has become to have become to have a deadening/numbing effect on narrator
- rain is shown as ambivalent — simultaneously threatening and life-giving
- highlights India's poverty and desperation
- conveys a suffocating feeling of confinement/entrapment within the family home
- "The trick the rain girl was playing on me" — rain is anthropomorphised/personified; can use this for irony, a hint of failure
- 'slugs, worms, millipedes, thistles' (Paragraph 1) — rain creates/enables life but the list shows the narrator is overwhelmed
- can also showcase the narrator's own changing perspective on the rain
- the community is shown as defined by the rain — it structures time, life, economics
- concluding statement highlights power of rain: (depending on use of symbols of the rain linking it to life within the community and the text beyond)

### Q1d
- **Criterion:** A
- **Marks:** 2
- **Command term:** Describe
- **Question text (Reconstructed from MS):** Describe **two** contrasting images from the start of the film, 00:00 to 00:28, that convey the **mixed emotions** the people of India have about the monsoon.
- **Sub-parts:** None

**Mark Scheme:**

Award **(2 marks)** for candidates who contrast any two images from 00:00–00:28. No marks are awarded if the candidate provides only one image.

Responses **may** contrast the following images:

**Image 1 vs. Image 2**
- An **image** of people dancing in the water **versus an image** of people working in the water
- An **image** of a child looking out of a window, staring nervously at the rain **versus an image** of boys dancing in/flipping into the water
- An **image** of furled brow/worried of the girl (while narrator says, "They can't wait for it to end.") **versus an image** of boys dancing in/flipping into the water (while the narrator says, "They celebrate its arrival!")
- An **image** of skies with imminent rain and threatening storms **versus an image** of boys dancing in/flipping into the water

- **Marks:** 3
- **Command term:** List

- **Sub-parts:** None

- **Marks:** 35

- **Sub-parts:** None — single extended response assessed on Criteria A, B, and D

**Mark Scheme Notes:**

- Examiners need to award **a mark for each of Criterion A, B and D**.

- In descriptor strand i and ii, not all elements need to be addressed in the piece for the mark to be awarded. In descriptor strand iii, terminology can be implied.

## General Marking Guidelines (from mark scheme front matter)

- Recommendations for awarding word counts and timings are advisory only. Candidates should not be penalised if they exceed or fall short of advisory word counts, unless the quality of the response is affected.
- If a candidate uses only one question to answer a multi-part question, apply the full rubric.
- Where example responses in the mark scheme illustrate the top end of each mark band, these examples given are not exhaustive.
- Where example responses include citations and quotations, these are from the text and are there to give context to the type of response expected.
- Use of + / – indicates alternatives acceptable to the expected response.
- For the compare and contrast question, candidates are required to write about both texts. If students only write about one text, they cannot go beyond 3 marks for Criterion A.
