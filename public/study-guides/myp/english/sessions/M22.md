### Text 1: Extract from *"A Wild Attack of Locusts"* by Doris Lessing

This excerpt describes the morning after a devastating locust attack on a South African farm. Margaret, a fourth-generation farmer's wife, watches the recovery with her father-in-law, Stephen, and her husband, Richard, as they survey the broad-damaged landscape and watch the locusts depart.

*[Opening of excerpt — in the morning, the sky is described with colours including "red-tinged gold light"; Margaret and the men survey the aftermath of the locust attack on the farm.]*

In the morning, [Margaret] woke to yellow sunshine lying across the bed [...] She went to the window. Old Stephen was ahead of her. There he stood, outside, gazing down the valley. And she gazed, astounded — and entranced, much against her will. For it looked as if the world was covered with a large shining cloth that glittered and moved. The locusts were fanning their wings to free them of the night dews. The sky was a shimmer of red-tinged gold light, and the country was golden.

She went out to her, the men, stepping carefully among the insects. The two dogs were yapping at the end of the verandah, restrained by her husband from chasing the locusts.

"Well," thought Margaret, we may be bankrupt, but not everyone has seen a locust army forming their wings to fly.

After that, little by little, another thought was on the way, though it would be painful. Without a locust, there are no snows showed in the sky. It thickened and spread. "The time to fly has come," she was trying to tell herself. She tried to think that she was lucky.

Like that Friday morning, she went into the fields. The air was heavy and still. Everything was to be replaced. With a bit of luck, another season would not come travelling down just now for the family. Happened that there was not one bit of grass showed in the sky. It thickened and spread: there was not a blade of grass left from their struggle of three or four years to bring the farm up. As for Margaret, she was trying to get used to the idea of three or four years on locusts. Locusts were going to be the norm for a while. She said to herself, she was lucky to be alive with some ambition overtures. She felt like a survivor after a war: if this devastated and mangled countryside was all that remained to him / her.

"But the men all sat with good appetites."

"It could have been worse. It could be much worse."
