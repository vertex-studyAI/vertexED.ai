## Text 1

**Read the excerpt from the play *Uncle Vanya* by Anton Chekhov, which is set in nineteenth-century Russia. In this scene Helena, a woman from the city, and Sonia, a woman who lives in the country, discuss the care of forests with a doctor named Astrov and his companion Totski.**

HELENA: You are still young, not over thirty-six or seven, I should say, and I suspect that the woods do not interest you as much as you say they do. I should think you would find them monotonous.

SONIA: No, the work is thrilling. Dr. Astroft watches over the old woods and sets out new plantations every year. He has already been presented with a diploma and a bronze medal. If you will listen to what he has to say, you will agree with him entirely. He says that forests are the ornaments of the earth, that they teach mankind to understand beauty and attune his mind to lofty sentiments. Forests temper the severity of climate. In countries where the climate is milder, less strength is wasted in the battle with nature, and the people are kind and gentle. In such countries men are handsome, tractable, sensitive, graceful in speech and gesture. Their arts and sciences blossom, their philosophy is not gloomy, and their treatment of women is full of refined courtesy—

HELENA: [laughing] Bravo! Bravo! All that is very pretty, but it is also unconvincing. So [to Astroft] I hope you will allow me to go on burning logs in my stoves and building my barns of wood.

ASTROV: You can burn peat in your stoves and build your sheds of stone. Oh, I don't object, of course, to cutting wood from necessity, but why destroy the forests? The woods of Russia are trembling under the blows of the axe. Millions of trees have perished. The homes of the wild animals and birds have been desolated; the rivers are shrunken; and many beautiful landscapes are gone forever, never to return. [To HELENA] Am I not right? Why should any sane man destroy what he cannot create? God has given us the earth, with its forests and fields and skies; we are the guests here, not the masters; and our duty is to protect it, to improve it, to embellish it with beauty. I trust the future to those who plant and tend the forests, who improve the soil, and who protect the animals. No, I do not want to cut down forests; on the contrary, I want to plant new ones. I have passionate forests that I have preserved from the axe, or hear the rustling of the young plantations set out with my own hands, when I realise that the climate is in my power, when I feel that if in a thousand years man is to be happy, I too shall have had some small share in improving the climate, and that if mankind is happy a thousand years from now, I shall have played some small part in bringing it about. When I plant a young birch tree and then see it budding into leaf and swaying in the wind, my heart swells with pride and I— [seeing the labourer who has brought a glass of vodka on a tray] however— [drinks] I must be off. Probably it is all idiocy, anyway. I bid you good day!

## Text 2

**"Rookie Reporter: Yindali Explains Why Forests Are Important"**

This rookie report is from Yindali, a 10-year-old who lives on the edge of the Daintree Forest in Australia, the oldest rainforest in the world and home to the native Kuku Yalanji people.

[Video content: Yindali speaks to camera in the forest setting. She introduces herself in the Guugu Yalandji language, mentions the Kuku Yalanji people, shows forest landmarks including waterfalls, rivers, and native wildlife (Forest Kingfisher, cassowary, green tree frog, Ulysses butterfly — 60%, 131 types of reptiles, crocodile, masked owl). The video shows aerial footage of the Daintree, maps of Queensland. Captions include statistics: "180 million years" old, "60 seconds = 36 football fields" of deforestation. The video calls for: Sustainable food, Use less paper, Reduce carbon footprint, Respect the land.]
