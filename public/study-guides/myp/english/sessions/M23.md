### Text 1: Extract from *Woven in Moonlight* by Isabel Ibañez

Read the excerpt from the novel *Woven in Moonlight* by Isabel Ibañez about an Illustrian living in the fictional nation of Inkasisa. Ximena, the narrator, has been captured by a rival tribe, the Llacsans. Ximena has the power to weave, and she is also weaving, adding the colourful thread of the Llacsans to the silver thread given to her by Luna, their moon.

> "I like to weave."
>
> Ximena Triana. "That's not an Illustrian hobby."
>
> I shrug. *So I shouldn't like to weave because of who I am? That's ridiculous. I like creating with my hands. There's something rewarding about making art out of nothing. The looking and unlooking, the folding over and over. Repeating until a bright new thing winks to life. I don't care if it's an Illustrian or not.*
>
> I shake my head. "No, I really love to weave."
>
> "You really like to weave?"
>
> Starting at the middle, I begin with a simple diamond pattern, weaving a red strand from below. I know countless techniques by heart, but this one is my favorite. It's the first one I learned.
>
> "Under one, over one, under one, over two, under one, over two, and repeat," I whisper to my fingers. The looking and unlooking, the folding over and over, and repeat," I mutter under my breath. Then I start over from the beginning.
>
> As I work, the moonlight grows around me, growing brighter. My fingers blur as I count: over, under, over, and back again. I can't tell if the darkness or the light is winning.
>
> My breath catches in the back of my throat. I realize I have not moved. I have not looked up from my work in some time. A tapestry of magic circled around me, and it surprises me. I can feel my wounds, and internal, circling and healing. I feel more alive.
>
> With the moon thread wrapped around my index finger, I wind the strand around and around, forming a glimmering silver thread. It lights up the whole room, and everything it touches shimmers and shines, awash in Luna's glow.
>
> The tapestry winks silver in the moonlight as I draw it closer. A slight movement makes me slow. I lean forward, and the loom folds into the darkness.
>
> Nothing like this has ever happened. I have not been wearing stars and constellations of my life. And now they glimmer and shine like they belong in the night sky. But I know they're mine... mine...
>
> My knees quake. *What if this is part of my Illustrian magic? Part of Luna's gift? My moon thread breathes life into my creations. The fabric folds the color of moonlight.*
>
> *Beautiful. Vibrant. Messy. Forbidden color.*

### Text 2: Short Film — *The Sleeping Beauty* (ballet documentary film)

In this short film, *What is the point of ballet?*, the filmmaker Ted Ed explains how ballet works and where it comes from *The Sleeping Beauty*, a ballet in which a princess is cursed to sleep for a hundred years and then wakes.

[The film contains animated sequences showing ballet terminology and techniques including: Échappé (sequence of positions a–e), Pas de Bourrée (with change of feet, sequence of positions a–e), and Pas de Chat (sequence of positions a–c).
