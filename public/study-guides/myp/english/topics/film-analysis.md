# Film & Visual Text Analysis

## Key concepts

Film analysis differs from literary analysis in one fundamental way: you are analysing **visual and auditory choices** made by a director, not words chosen by a writer. Every exam question about a film rewards the same structure — **technique → effect → purpose** — but the range of available techniques is wider.

### Core film techniques

**Mise-en-scène** (everything placed within the frame)
- Lighting: bright/high-key lighting creates warmth or clarity; dim/low-key lighting creates tension, mystery, or vulnerability. e.g. the elderly woman in *Dreamcatcher* (N24) seated in a "dimly lit room" signals solitude and reflection.
- Colour grading: saturated, vivid colours heighten beauty or emotional intensity (Jason Silva's *Seeking the Aesthetic Experience*, N21); muted tones suggest sadness or realism.
- Setting and props: objects placed deliberately carry symbolic weight. The roly-poly toys in *A Lesson on Resilience* (M22) physically embody the concept of resilience — they bounce back when knocked over.
- Costume and appearance: how a character is dressed signals social role, era, or identity.

**Cinematography** (how the camera records the frame)
- Shot types: extreme close-up (face, object — emphasises emotion or detail); close-up (head and shoulders — establishes character mood); medium shot (character in context); wide/establishing shot (setting, scale, isolation).
- Camera angles: high angle (character appears small, vulnerable, powerless); low angle (character appears imposing, powerful); eye level (neutral, equal, familiar).
- Camera movement: tracking/panning (follows action, creates energy); handheld/shaky cam (rawness, documentary realism); static shot (stability, formality).
- Point-of-view shot: places viewer directly in a character's perspective, creating identification or unease.

**Editing** (how shots are joined)
- Cut: the basic transition between shots.
- Pace / rhythm: rapid cutting creates urgency and excitement; slow cutting creates contemplation and unease.
- Flashback: shows past events, used to characterise or explain the present. In *Dreamcatcher* (N24), flashbacks characterise the elderly woman by showing her as "an energetic, confident child."
- Montage: a sequence of images edited together to build meaning cumulatively. In *Seeking the Aesthetic Experience* (N21), landscapes, butterflies, and slow motion accumulate to "overwhelm the viewer with beauty."

**Sound**
- Diegetic sound: sound that exists within the world of the film (a character speaking, footsteps, ambient noise).
- Non-diegetic sound: sound added in post-production, not present in the scene (score, soundtrack, voiceover narration).
- Score / background music: upbeat music creates positivity and energy (cherry-picking film in *Remember Childhood?*, N23); tense music builds suspense; ambient music signals wonder or transcendence (*Seeking the Aesthetic Experience*, N21). When the roly-poly toy bounces back in *A Lesson on Resilience* (M22), "the music becomes cheerful, reinforcing the positive tone."
- Silence: the absence of sound creates unease, emphasis, or a moment of stillness.
- Voiceover: narration heard over images. In *Dreamcatcher* (N24), the voiceover "makes viewers appreciate the woman's wisdom via her reflection, connecting past to present."
- Direct address to camera: when a speaker looks directly at the lens, creating intimacy and personal connection. Jason Silva's direct address in N21 "positions the viewer as a direct participant."

**Other visual and structural techniques**
- Subtitle / caption: text overlaid on the image. In *Dreamcatcher* (N24), the subtitle "What do I have to say?" appears over the still of the woman and signals introspection.
- Title card: a text screen inserted between or before footage. *Seeking the Aesthetic Experience* (N21) opens with the card: "The aesthetic experience is a simple beholding of the object, you experience radiance. You are held in aesthetic arrest" — this introduces the film's central thesis immediately.
- Symbol: a visual object that carries meaning beyond its literal presence. The fireball above the woman's head in *Dreamcatcher* (N24) "represents the collective knowledge and wisdom she and her people have accumulated."
- Aerial / drone footage: signals scale, freedom, or the smallness of individuals within a landscape.
- Slow motion / time-lapse: slow motion encourages contemplation and extends emotionally charged moments; time-lapse compresses time to suggest change or passing.
- Lens flare / over-exposure: suggests brilliance, illumination, or the overwhelming nature of beauty.

### How film analysis differs from literary analysis

| Literary analysis | Film analysis |
|-------------------|--------------|
| Technique drawn from language (imagery, metaphor, syntax, tone) | Technique drawn from visuals and sound (cinematography, editing, score, lighting) |
| "The writer uses the metaphor of..." | "The director uses a close-up to..." |
| Effect is on the reader | Effect is on the viewer |
| Quote the text directly | Reference the technique and describe what you see/hear |

In both cases, the structure is identical: **name the technique → quote or describe the example → explain the effect on the audience → link to the creator's purpose**.

## Films used in past papers

### M21 — *Greta Thunberg* (Time magazine short documentary)
A documentary short produced by *Time* magazine about their 2019 Person of the Year. Shows footage of Greta's school strikes, global climate marches, meetings with politicians, and her Atlantic crossing. Q1c asked about a clip (1:35–2:05) showing how Greta's impact spread to other young people. The MS expected students to notice: montage sequences of increasing crowds at global protests, and voiceovers showing the spread of the movement.

### M22 — *A Lesson on Resilience* by The Learning Lab
A short educational film in which a teacher uses roly-poly weighted toys (which bounce back when knocked over) to teach young children about resilience. Ends with the caption: "Let's teach our children to get up more times than they fall." Q1c asked about the purpose of the toys (visual metaphor for resilience — they literally demonstrate bouncing back). Q1d asked about a technique in a clip. The MS expected: close-ups of children's faces to show their reactions; music changing to cheerful when the toy bounces back.

### N21 — *Seeking the Aesthetic Experience* by Jason Silva (YouTube short film)
Jason Silva presents directly to camera against natural backdrops, holding a glowing orb. Combines aerial footage, slow motion, extreme close-ups of natural forms, lens flare, saturated colour grading, and rhetorical questions. Explores the idea that beauty can alter consciousness. Q1c asked about one film technique in the opening — the MS named: title card, aerial shots, lens flare, uplifting music, direct address, saturated colour grading. Q1d asked how viewers are encouraged to appreciate aesthetic experience in the closing — the MS expected: accumulation of natural images, direct address, rhetorical questions, slow motion.

### N22 — *Yindali Explains Why Forests Are Important* (rookie news video)
A 10-year-old named Yindali speaks to camera from the edge of the Daintree Forest in Australia, home to the Kuku Yalanji people. Introduces herself in the Guugu Yalandji language. Uses aerial footage, statistics captions (e.g. "60 seconds = 36 football fields" of deforestation), and images of wildlife. Q1c asked about Yindali's opening remarks — the MS expected: direct address to camera, use of native language, naming the Kuku Yalanji people to establish credibility and connection. Q1d asked about a clip (2:08–2:42) — the MS expected: combination of images of animals and destruction to build urgency; dramatic footage creates a contrast between beauty and threat.

### N23 — *Remember Childhood?* (short film — "Me and Margo")
A home-video style film following a family trip to pick cherries. Uses upbeat music, flashback title cards, close-ups of the child's face, and low-angle shots showing the child's point of view among the cherry trees. Q1c asked how tone is established at the beginning — the MS expected: upbeat music and/or cheerful voiceover establish a happy, friendly tone. Q1d asked how the child's perspective is communicated — the MS expected: extreme close-up shots capturing joy and wonder; low-angle shots placing the camera at the child's eye level.

### M24 — *The Walk* (biographical drama; directed by Robert Zemeckis)
Presented as a series of film stills with subtitled quotes. Philippe Petit walks on a wire between the Twin Towers in New York City. Stills show extreme heights, close-ups of feet on the wire, and the wire stretching "to infinity." Q1c asked about characterisation of the wire walker — the MS expected: focused, fearless, single-minded, risk-taking (identify + develop with evidence). Q1d asked about the effect of a technique — the MS expected: camera angles showing the city far below to create fear; clouds clearing as he steps on the wire to symbolise clarity of mind; music shifts from tense to joyful; close-ups of feet emphasise difficulty.

### N24 — *Dreamcatcher* by Matty Brown (short film)
An elderly Native American woman looks back on her life as the line between fantasy and reality blurs. Uses flashbacks to her childhood (running through fields, climbing trees), voiceover narration including "And I feel that girl still pulsing through my veins like butterflies fluttering by," and ends with a figure in traditional dress standing in a dark forest as a fireball appears above their head. Q1c asked about a technique used to characterise the elderly woman — the MS expected: voiceover (reflective nature, rhetorical questions, wisdom); flashbacks (showing her as energetic and confident as a child). Q1d asked about the fireball — the MS expected: "timeless wisdom and power" or "collective knowledge and wisdom she and her people have accumulated."

### M25 — *Finding Myself* by Bonnie Belcher (digital story / video)
A young woman named Bonnie describes her family members and their accomplishments, then reflects on not yet knowing what her own "calling" is. Her family includes a creative, thoughtful sister who bakes; a mother who teaches; and a "legendary" great-grandmother. The clip at 1:00–1:34 shows her describing these family members using positive adjectives ("creative," "thoughtful," "legendary"). Q1c asked about the meaning of the title — the MS accepted interpretations based on the film OR on general life experience. Q1d asked about the purpose of the 1:00–1:34 clip — the MS expected: shows how much she admires her family; contrasts their clear callings with her own uncertainty; motivates the viewer to find their own purpose.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

**1. Named technique + specific example + developed effect.** This is the top-band formula across all sessions. A response that only names a technique ("the director uses close-ups") cannot go above band 1. A response that names, quotes or describes, AND explains the effect reaches band 2–3.

Exact MS language from sessions:
- "The voiceover makes viewers appreciate the woman's wisdom via her reflection, connecting past to present using simile for effect: 'And I feel that girl still pulsing through my veins like butterflies fluttering by.'" (N24 Q1c — 3 marks)
- "The camera angles make the viewer afraid because they show the entire city below him, making the viewer fully aware of how far he can fall and how dangerous it is." (M24 Q1d — 3 marks)
- "When the toy falls on the ground and bounces back up, the music becomes cheerful, reinforcing the positive tone of the lesson on resilience." (M22 Q1d — 3 marks)
- "The filmmaker uses extreme close-up shots of the child's face, capturing joy and wonder. As she plays, the camera gets low to show the child's point of view." (N23 Q1d — 3 marks)
- "The creator establishes a happy tone by using upbeat music in the opening." (N23 Q1c — 2 marks)
- "The director uses a dramatic symbol of a fireball, placing it above the woman's head to illustrate her timeless wisdom and power." (N24 Q1d — 2 marks)

**2. Linking technique to audience impact.** The mark scheme consistently uses phrases like "makes the viewer feel," "encourages the audience to," "positions the viewer as." Top-band responses explain why the technique works on an audience, not just what it does.

**3. For "comment" questions (2 marks): one technique + one example OR explanation earns full marks.** You do not need both an example and an explanation for a 2-mark question — one well-developed point is sufficient.

**4. For "analyse/explain" questions (3 marks): technique + example + explanation of effect all present.** The 3-mark questions require the full chain. The N24 Q1c mark scheme explicitly distinguishes: level 1 = identify technique only; level 2 = name technique + example or explanation; level 3 = technique + example AND explanation.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Identifying a technique without any effect:** "The director uses close-ups" scores 1 at most. This is explicitly the level 1 descriptor in every session: "identifies a technique… does not develop ideas with examples or explanations."
- **Plot summary:** Describing what happens in the clip without linking it to technique or effect. e.g. "The clip shows her talking about her family" = level 1. "The clip shows her family's accomplishments to contrast with her own uncertainty" = level 2.
- **Vague effects:** "It makes the film interesting" or "it helps the viewer understand" are rejected at full-mark level because they do not specify what the viewer understands or why the technique creates that effect.
- **Misidentifying the text:** Applying a literary technique to a film (e.g. analysing "word choice" in a voiceover when the question is about a visual technique) may still be rewarded if the analysis is valid — but film technique vocabulary (camera angle, close-up, music, etc.) is more precise and reliable.

## Model answer formula

**Technique → Effect → Purpose**

Structure: *[Name technique] + [describe/quote specific example] + [effect on the viewer] + [why the creator made this choice / what it achieves]*

**Real example from M24 Q1d (3-mark "Analyse" question):**

> "The camera angles make the viewer afraid because they show the entire city below him, making the viewer fully aware of how far he can fall and how dangerous it is."

Broken down:
- Technique: camera angles (looking down from height)
- Example: they "show the entire city below him"
- Effect on viewer: makes the viewer afraid
- Purpose: makes the viewer "fully aware of how far he can fall" — reinforces the theme of risk

**Real example from N24 Q1c (3-mark "Analyse" question — top-band MS response):**

> "The voiceover makes viewers appreciate the woman's wisdom via her reflection, connecting past to present using simile for effect: 'And I feel that girl still pulsing through my veins like butterflies fluttering by.'"

Broken down:
- Technique: voiceover
- Example: the specific simile quoted
- Effect: viewers appreciate her wisdom
- Purpose: connects past to present, characterises her as reflective and wise

**Real example from N23 Q1c (2-mark "Comment" question):**

> "The creator establishes a happy tone by using upbeat music in the opening."

Broken down:
- Technique: upbeat music (non-diegetic sound)
- Effect / link: establishes a happy tone
- That is all that is needed for 2 marks — one technique + one developed effect

## Revision priorities

1. **Memorise the technique vocabulary.** The exam always rewards precision. "Camera angle" is better than "the way the camera is positioned." "Non-diegetic score" is better than "background music." Learn the six categories: mise-en-scène, cinematography, editing, sound, text/subtitle, and symbol.

2. **Always name the technique first, then describe/quote, then explain the effect.** Every single top-band MS response follows this order. Never start with "I think the purpose is..." without first naming what the director actually did.

3. **Know the difference between purpose and effect.** Effect = what the viewer feels or understands. Purpose = why the creator wanted that effect — linked to theme, characterisation, or meaning. For 3-mark questions, you need both. For 2-mark questions, one well-developed point is enough.

4. **Practise writing about specific clips.** Several questions name a timestamp (1:35–2:05 in M21, 2:08–2:42 in N22, 1:00–1:34 in M25). The exam platform shows you the clip. Your job is to identify what is happening technically in that clip, not just what is happening narratively.

5. **For "comment on significance of a visual symbol" questions (N24 Q1d pattern), link to theme or character.** The fireball MS response says it represents "timeless wisdom and power" — it is not enough to say "the fireball is dramatic." Connect the symbol to what the film is about.

6. **Do not confuse the film questions with literary questions.** Q1a–1b are about the written text; Q1c–1d are always about the film. Students who discuss the wrong text lose marks. Check which text the question specifies.

7. **Films appear in every session.** The format has been consistent across all 8 sessions in this dataset (M21–M25, N21–N24). Film analysis is not optional revision — it is guaranteed to appear.
