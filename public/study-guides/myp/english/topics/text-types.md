# Text Types

## Key concepts

- **Literary text**: A text created primarily for aesthetic, imaginative, or expressive purposes. Includes poems, prose fiction (novels, short stories), plays, and narrative non-fiction written with strong stylistic intent.
- **Non-literary text**: A text created primarily for functional, informational, or persuasive purposes. Includes newspaper articles, speeches, letters, proposals, blog posts, advertisements, podcast scripts, product reviews, opinion pieces, and interviews.
- **Text type**: The broad category a text belongs to (poem, prose fiction, etc.).
- **Form**: The specific shape or subgenre within a type (e.g. a speech is a form of non-literary persuasive writing; a sonnet is a form of poetry).
- **Convention**: The expected structural or stylistic features that signal a text belongs to a particular form (e.g. a formal letter opens with a salutation, uses paragraphs, closes with a sign-off).
- **Register**: The level of formality and the specific vocabulary choices appropriate to a given audience and purpose.
- **Purpose**: What the text is designed to do — entertain, inform, persuade, inspire, instruct, describe.
- **Audience**: Who the text is addressed to — general public, students, decision-makers, peers, etc.

## Literary vs Non-literary: the core distinction

| Feature | Literary | Non-literary |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| Primary purpose | Aesthetic / expressive / imaginative | Functional / informational / persuasive |
| Language style | Heightened, figurative, crafted for effect | Clear, purposeful, audience-directed |
| Structure | Determined by artistic choice | Determined by form conventions |
| Examples in past papers | Poem, prose fiction (novel excerpt), play excerpt, literary non-fiction | Newspaper article, speech, proposal, letter, blog post, advertisement script, podcast script, product review, opinion piece, interview |
| Where they appear | Task 1 Text 1 (written); Task 2 (created by student) | Task 1 Text 1 or 2 (occasionally); Task 3 (created by student) |

## Text types in past papers (case-study style)

### Poem (Text 1)
**Sessions:** M25, M21
- **M25** — *Blueprints?* by Sara Holbrook: A performance poem using rhetorical questions, alliteration, imagery, and first-person voice to explore inherited identity. MS expected: technique named + quotation + effect on reader. Rewarded: recognising rhetorical questions ("Who will I become?") as a structural/thematic device, not just labelling them. Q1a asked for comment on a single line; Q1b asked for analysis of one technique — the technique-quote-effect formula was essential for full marks.
- **M21** — *Peaceful Transition* by Tony Hoagland: Used to explore the theme of climate change significance. Same analytical expectations apply.

**Key conventions to activate:** Free verse or regular stanza form; speaker vs poet distinction; figurative language (imagery, metaphor, personification); sound devices (alliteration, rhyme, rhythm); enjambment and line breaks as structural choices; rhetorical questions for thematic emphasis.

### Literary prose fiction (Text 1)
**Sessions:** M23, M22, N21, N23, N20 (partly)
- **M23** — *Woven in Moonlight* (Ibañez): Fantasy/magical realism. MS rewarded: characterisation analysis (technique + quotation + how it builds character), significance of a specific passage (technique + effect). Q1a asked "how is Ximena characterised?" — basic identification capped at 1–2 marks; full marks required technique + quote + explanation of effect.
- **M22** — *A Wild Attack of Locusts* (Lessing): Realist fiction. MS rewarded: comment on colour and mood, evaluation of a single line with full causal analysis. Q1b used the highest command term "evaluate" — required cause-effect-assessment chain.
- **N21** — *Perfume* (Süskind): Third-person close narrative. MS rewarded: analysis of conflict and internal state through physical action and contradiction ("disgustingly good"; flinging the handkerchief vs reverence).

**Key conventions to activate:** Narrator and point of view (first- vs third-person); characterisation through action, dialogue, internal thought; figurative language (imagery, metaphor, simile, personification); mood and atmosphere; setting as symbol; sentence length for pacing.

### Newspaper article (Text 1)
**Sessions:** N24
- **N24** — *How the elderly can help the young* (Tapper, *The Guardian*): Journalistic non-fiction. MS rewarded: identifying the purpose of the article's photograph (must link photograph to article's argument, not just describe it); explaining how the article informs readers using specific techniques (expert quotations, examples from multiple countries, balance of evidence).

**Key conventions to activate:** Headline, subheading, byline (though not always visible in exam extracts); opening paragraph (inverted pyramid — most important information first); expert quotation to build credibility; objective tone modulated with persuasive intent; specific statistics and examples; balance of perspectives.

### Play excerpt (Text 1)
**Sessions:** N22
- **N22** — *Uncle Vanya* (Chekhov): Nineteenth-century drama. ### Non-fiction / biographical article (Text 1)
**Sessions:** M24, M19, N19
- Feature article / long-form journalism. Combines factual reporting with narrative technique (characterisation of real individuals, scene-setting, chronological storytelling). Register is often slightly elevated compared to hard news.

**Key conventions to activate:** Narrative structure applied to real events; first-person or close-third focalization on a real person; descriptive and figurative language used to engage; statistics and quotations as evidence; headline and pull-quotes where present.

## Task 3 forms: conventions to know

### Speech (school assembly / public awareness)
**Sessions:** N16, N21, N22 — 3 of 14 sessions
**Conventions:** Direct address ("Good morning, everyone" / "Fellow students"); rhetorical questions; rule of three; anaphora; inclusive language ("we", "our", "together"); strong opening hook; call to action at close; personal anecdote or vivid example; formal but accessible register.
**MS rewards:** Structural devices (deliberate paragraph length, repeated motifs), coherent argument build, register appropriate to a live audience, a clear and actionable conclusion.

### Proposal (conference / event)
**Sessions:** M18, M19, M21 — 3 of 14 sessions
**Conventions:** Clear statement of purpose in opening; justification of the proposal's merit; specific details (topic, speaker, format, audience); formal register; logical structure (problem → solution or context → proposal → benefit); sign-off.

### Letter (formal / semi-formal)
**Sessions:** M22, N23
**Conventions:** Heading with date and recipient; salutation ("Dear [name/title]"); purpose stated in opening paragraph; body paragraphs with supporting reasons or evidence; closing paragraph; formal sign-off ("Yours sincerely" / "Yours faithfully"); appropriate formal register throughout.

### Blog post
**Sessions:** N24
**Conventions:** Informal-to-semi-formal register; engaging title or headline; direct address to readers; subheadings optional; personal voice with opinions; paragraphs typically shorter than formal writing; call to action or question to reader at close.

### Video advertisement script
**Sessions:** M23
**Conventions:** Script format indicators (action directions, speaker labels or implied); persuasive and engaging language; short punchy sentences and slogans; audience-directed appeal; clear product or cause; sense of visual/audio elements described or implied; call to action.

### Interview (Q&A format)
**Sessions:** M24
**Conventions:** Question and answer format with clear labelling (Q: / A: or named speakers); questions that build on previous answers; conversational register; interviewer's voice distinct from interviewee's; narrative arc across the exchange.

### Opinion piece / newspaper article (student-produced)
**Sessions:** M25
**Conventions:** Engaging headline; clear thesis stated early; argument developed through paragraphs with evidence and examples; counterargument acknowledged and refuted; persuasive language and rhetorical devices; objective journalistic register but with clear authorial perspective; byline optional.

### Podcast script
**Sessions:** N20
**Conventions:** Informal register; direct address to listeners ("Welcome back" / "Today we're going to talk about"); conversational sentence structure; audio-specific language (no visual descriptions); sound cues or music notes optional; clear topic introduction and sign-off.

### Product review
**Sessions:** N19
**Conventions:** Description of the product; evaluative language (positive and negative); star rating or score optional; personal experience as evidence; clear recommendation; semi-formal register; audience of potential buyers or users.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the MS rewards in text-type recognition (Task 1)

- **Technique + quotation + effect** is the universal formula for Q1a–1d. Naming a technique alone scores 1 mark. Adding a relevant quotation or example earns the second mark. Adding an explanation of why that technique has that specific effect on the audience earns the highest mark at any given band.
- **Form-specific analysis**: For a newspaper article (N24), the MS rewards identifying how journalistic conventions (expert quotation, multiple examples, balanced evidence) serve the article's purpose to inform and persuade. Generic observations ("the article is well written") score nothing.
- **Film techniques must be named accurately**: "a camera angle" is weaker than "a close-up shot of the children's faces." The more precisely the technique is named, the more easily effect can be explained.
- **Register awareness in Task 3**: The MS consistently rewards register "appropriate to the form and context." A blog post written in the style of an academic essay scores lower on Criterion D. A speech written without direct address or rhetorical devices scores lower on Criterion B.
- **Convention activation**: For Task 3, the MS at 6–8 band describes structure as "secure and structural devices begin to add to the overall effect." Top band requires structural devices used "coherently and cohesively throughout" — meaning conventions are sustained, not just present at the start.

### What the MS penalises

- **Bullet points in any extended writing**: Criterion B is automatically capped at 2 marks (out of 10) if bullets are used. This applies to Q1e, Task 2, and Task 3.
- **Writing about only one text in Q1e**: Maximum 2 marks for Criterion A AND maximum 2 marks for Criterion B. This is an absolute cap regardless of quality.
- **Comparing without contrasting in Q1e**: Maximum 5 marks for Criterion A. Both similarities AND differences must be addressed.
- **Misidentifying the register**: If a Task 3 response is in the wrong register for the form (e.g. too formal for a blog post, too informal for a formal letter), Criterion D is penalised on the register strand.
- **Ignoring the image/prompt in Task 2**: 0 marks for Criterion C if neither the image nor the theme of the prompt is used.
- **Ignoring the global context in Task 3**: 0 marks for Criterion C if the response is neither linked to the global context nor the theme of the prompt.

## Common question types involving text types

### Q1a–1b (Text 1, written text)
Questions consistently ask about technique and effect in the written text. The command term varies (Comment, Explain, Analyse, Evaluate, Describe, Justify, How) but the underlying demand is the same: identify a feature of the text → explain its effect on the reader. In every session, the written Text 1 is a specific type — knowing its conventions helps you generate relevant technique vocabulary quickly.

**Pattern by text type:**
- Poem: questions often focus on a specific line or device (Q1a M25: "Comment on the significance of the line 'Who will I become?'"); full marks require linking the device to the poem's overall theme.
- Prose fiction: questions focus on characterisation, mood, or a significant passage ("How is Ximena characterized?" M23; "Analyse how the writer creates a sense of Baldini's conflict" N21).
- Play: questions often focus on dialogue structure and its dramatic function ("Analyse the dialogue between Astroft and Sonia" N22).
- Newspaper article: questions focus on journalistic techniques and purpose ("Explain how the article informs readers about intergenerational care" N24).

### Q1c–1d (Text 2, film/visual text)
Every session has a film as Text 2. Q1c–1d always test film analysis. Questions typically ask for one technique and its effect, or the purpose of a specific clip or element. See `film-analysis.md` for full film technique vocabulary. The text type here is fixed (short film / documentary) — what varies is the film's genre and subject.

### Q1e (Compare and contrast essay)
The comparison always involves two different text types: a written text (Text 1) and a film (Text 2). The text-type contrast is itself a valid analytical point: "While Süskind uses literary prose with a close-third narrator to create interiority, Silva employs direct address to camera and multimodal layering to achieve the same effect of immersing the audience in sensory experience." Noting the difference in medium is expected at the 1–2 band; exploiting it analytically is what distinguishes 6–8 and 9–10.

### Task 3 (produce a non-literary text)
The form is specified each session. The student must apply the correct conventions of that form. The table in the "Task 3 forms" section above shows which forms have appeared — speeches and proposals together account for 6 of 14 sessions. Blog post and letter have each appeared twice. Any of the 10 documented forms could appear.

## Model approach: identifying a text type quickly

**Step 1 — Scan for layout clues** (30 seconds)
- Does the text have stanzas and line breaks? → Poem
- Does the text have stage directions in brackets and character names before speech? → Play excerpt
- Does the text have a headline, byline, and inverted-pyramid opening? → Newspaper article
- Does the text have a greeting/salutation and sign-off? → Letter
- Is it a continuous prose narrative with a named fictional character? → Prose fiction
- Is it a film? → Identify the film subtype: documentary, short film, dramatic excerpt, digital story

**Step 2 — Activate the purpose/audience/register trio**
Once you have identified the type, immediately ask:
- **Purpose**: What is this text designed to do? (entertain / inform / persuade / describe / inspire)
- **Audience**: Who is it for? (general public / students / policy makers / peers / children)
- **Register**: How formal is it? How does it address the reader/viewer?

**Step 3 — List 3–4 conventions to look for**
Before reading closely, set up a mental checklist. For a poem: line breaks, stanza structure, imagery, sound devices, speaker. For a newspaper article: headline, expert quotation, statistics, balance of evidence. This makes technique-spotting systematic rather than random.

**Step 4 — Link technique → effect → purpose**
Every analytical comment must complete the chain: "The writer uses [technique] — for example [quotation] — which creates [effect] on the reader/viewer because [reason]. This connects to the text's purpose of [purpose]." Not every step needs to be written out for short-answer questions, but the reasoning must be present.

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the conventions of the 10 Task 3 forms** — speech, proposal, letter, blog post, advertisement script, interview, opinion piece, podcast script, product review, and formal letter. Speeches and proposals are most likely (6 of 14 sessions). For each form, memorise: how it opens, how it addresses its audience, its register, and how it closes.

2. **Apply technique-quote-effect to every Q1a–1d answer** — this is the formula that separates 1-mark from 2-mark and 2-mark from 3-mark responses in every session and every text type. Practice it until it is automatic.

3. **Film is always Text 2 — treat it as its own text type** — see `film-analysis.md` for the full vocabulary. Every session tests Q1c and Q1d on a film. Knowing camera, sound, editing, and narrative techniques for film saves time and marks.

4. **For Q1e, exploit the literary vs non-literary contrast** — the two texts are always of different types (written + film). Identifying similarities in purpose but differences in medium and technique is a reliable path to the 6–8 band. Going further to analyse how the medium shapes meaning is the 9–10 move.

5. **Bullet points cost you Criterion B** — never use bullet points in Q1e, Task 2, or Task 3. Maximum 2 marks on Criterion B is a loss of up to 8 marks (out of 10). This is the single most avoidable error on the paper.

6. **Register is marked on Criterion D in Tasks 2 and 3** — writing a speech in the wrong register or a blog post that sounds like a formal essay will cost marks on both D and B. Match your register to the form's conventions from the first sentence.

7. **Prose fiction is Text 1 most often** (M22, M23, N21, N23, N20 — at least 5 of 14 sessions). Know how to analyse: characterisation through action/thought/dialogue, mood and atmosphere through imagery and setting, narrative point of view and its effect on how information is revealed, and significant passages with figurative language.
