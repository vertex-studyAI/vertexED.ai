# Literary Writing (Task 2)

## Key concepts

- **Criterion C — Producing text**: Tests insight, imagination, and sensitivity; stylistic and literary choices; development of ideas and perspectives. This is where the writing itself is judged — does it show real craft and creative thinking, not just competent execution?
- **Criterion D — Using language**: Tests range of vocabulary and sentence structures; register and style appropriate to the chosen form; accuracy of grammar, syntax, and punctuation. Both criteria are marked independently using a best-fit approach.
- **The image-anchoring rule**: Task 2 always provides exactly two photographs. The question instructs candidates to choose **one** image and write from it. Responses that use neither image nor the theme of the prompt score **0 marks for Criterion C**. The image must be used as inspiration — not merely described.
- **Expected length**: Approximately 350 words in most sessions; M24 specifies approximately 200 words and M22/M23/N23/N24/M25 specify approximately 350 words. Do not be penalised for exceeding or falling short, unless quality is affected.
- **Writing types seen**: Scene in a story (most common), narrative, monologue, creative description of an event, original piece expressing strong emotion. The exact form is always specified in the question; candidates do not choose the form — only the image.
- **Craft elements offered in the prompt**: Every Task 2 question includes an advisory list: "You may consider using any of the following: purpose, characterization, setting, mood, and/or point of view." These are not required but signal what the mark scheme looks for at higher bands.

## Task types from past papers

### M21 — Scene with doubt (Identities and relationships)
- **Images**: group of students socialising (Image 1) / lone young man sitting outdoors (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: A scene in which a character is internally or externally experiencing doubt. Doubt could arise from belonging (fitting in vs. standing apart), a decision, or a relationship moment. Image 2 (the lone figure) naturally invites isolation and internal conflict; Image 1 invites social doubt and belonging anxiety. The MS awards upper bands for: purpose, characterisation, setting, mood, and/or point of view deployed with sophistication; compelling and convincing narrative; strong sense of audience.

### N21 — Narrative focusing on a moment of victory (~350 words)
- **Images**: architectural staircase viewed from below (Image 1) / group outdoor celebratory sporting scene (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: The moment of victory must be evoked — not just described as "they won" but made vivid through craft. Image 1 (staircase) invites metaphorical treatment of victory as a climb; Image 2 invites physical, communal triumph. Top band: narrative is convincing and compelling; image is used effectively as inspiration; purpose, characterisation, setting, mood, and/or point of view used with sophistication; strong sense of audience.

### M22 — Scene in a science fiction story (~350 words)
- **Images**: busy robotics/science workshop scene with children and equipment (Image 1) / colourful theatrical or sci-fi stage/set scene (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: The science fiction genre must be present — a character and an object's power are the two required story elements. Top band: high degree of insight, imagination, or sensitivity; perceptive exploration of perspectives; highly effective stylistic choices; extensive relevant detail to develop ideas with precision.

### N23 — Original piece expressing strong emotion (~350 words)
- **Images**: young woman in a field with golden light (Image 1) / dramatic blue sky with stars/cosmos (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: The emotion must be communicated, not merely named. This is the most open-ended Task 2 prompt — any form of literary writing is valid (narrative, poem, dramatic monologue, lyric prose). At top band: perceptive insight, imagination or sensitivity; perceptive and effective stylistic choices; precise use of relevant detail; highly engaging response.

### M24 — Monologue reflecting on an important decision (~200 words)
- **Images**: person with colourful sticky notes (Image 1) / person in mountainous landscape (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: The form is specified as monologue — first-person voice, internal or spoken, directed at an audience or at the self. The decision and its weight must be felt. This session's shorter word count (200 vs 350) puts premium on economy and precision. Top band requires high degree of craft and accuracy.

### N24 — Scene about connecting with one's surroundings (~350 words)
- **Images**: person silhouetted in front of a large aquarium (Image 1) / bees clustered on a branch (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: "Connecting with one's surroundings" — could be physical, emotional, or spiritual. Image 1 (aquarium) invites a human connecting with an underwater world; Image 2 (bees) invites a nature/ecology perspective or a moment of stillness. The MS states: reward all valid responses. Upper bands require the response to be "inspired by the prompt and image" — not merely "addressing" it.

### M25 — Creative description of the event that led to the image (~350 words)
- **Images**: giraffe standing inside a domestic living room (Image 1) / person beside a large pink balloon-animal dog sculpture in a park (Image 2)
- **What MS expected**: This prompt is notably different — the task is to describe the *event that led to* the image, not to write a story set inside it. This demands backstory, cause-and-effect thinking, and imaginative construction. The giraffe image invites absurdist or comic scenarios; the balloon-dog image invites something about art, installation, or community event. Both reward: characterisation, setting, mood, and point of view.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the mark scheme rewards (Criterion C)

The four band levels in Criterion C map to these MS phrases across all sessions:

| Band | MS language | Marking note |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 1–2 | "limited degree of insight, imagination or sensitivity"; "minimal stylistic choices"; "few relevant details" | "attempts a basic link to the image or prompt" |
| 3–5 | "some insight, imagination or sensitivity"; "some stylistic choices"; "some relevant details" | "addresses the prompt and image" |
| 6–8 | "considerable insight, imagination or sensitivity"; "thoughtful stylistic choices"; "good awareness of impact on audience" | "produces an engaging response that elaborates on the prompt and image" |
| 9–10 | "high degree of insight, imagination or sensitivity"; "perceptive stylistic choices"; "sophisticated awareness of impact"; "extensive relevant details with precision" | "produces a highly engaging response that is inspired by the prompt and image" |

The key progression is:
- **Basic → addressing**: Something happens that links to the image, but it feels generic.
- **Addressing → engaging**: The image genuinely shapes the writing; there are deliberate craft choices.
- **Engaging → inspired**: The writing feels as if the image prompted a unique imaginative response; details are precise and purposeful; the effect on the reader is controlled.

**Specific techniques the MS consistently values**: characterisation (giving the character a distinct voice or psychology), setting (using sensory detail to place the reader), mood (creating atmosphere that shifts or sustains across the piece), point of view (choosing first, second, or limited third with intention), imagery, and structural choices (how the piece opens and closes).

### What the mark scheme rewards (Criterion D)

The same four-band structure applies to language:

| Band | Vocabulary | Register/style | Accuracy |
|------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| 1–2 | Limited | Inappropriate, does not serve context | Minimal — errors often hinder communication |
| 3–5 | Adequate range of vocab and sentence structures | Sometimes serves context | Degree of accuracy — errors sometimes hinder |
| 6–8 | Varied range of vocab and sentence structures | Serves context and intention | Considerable accuracy — errors do not hinder |
| 9–10 | Effective range of vocab and sentence structures | Consistently purposeful | High degree of accuracy — errors are minor |

Key strand: **register and style must serve the context and intention**. A narrative should read like a narrative, not a factual account; a monologue must have the cadences of a speaking voice. The MS explicitly notes examiners should consider best fit/balance — a response with strong register but some grammar errors can still score high.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Using neither the image nor the theme**: 0 marks for Criterion C. This is the only hard penalty. The MS is otherwise generous — "reward all valid responses."
- **Writing a plot summary instead of a scene**: This caps Criterion C at the lower bands — it shows no insight or stylistic choice, just narration.
- **Generic, unanchored writing**: A piece that could be about any scene anywhere, with no specific sensory detail or characterisation, sits in the 3–5 band regardless of language quality.
- **Language errors that hinder communication**: These prevent progression above the 3–5 band for Criterion D even if register is appropriate.
- **Addressing the prompt superficially**: The MS repeatedly distinguishes "addressing" (3–5) from "elaborating" (6–8) from being "inspired by" (9–10). A response that merely acknowledges the image and writes something loosely related cannot reach 9–10.

## Common question types

The formula across all 7 sessions is consistent: **"Using one of the images, [create / write] a [specified text type] [specifying the required focus or theme]."**

Image pairs are always two contrasting photographs. Patterns in image choices:
- **Human alone vs. human in group**: N21 (stairs vs. crowd), M21 (lone figure vs. students)
- **Indoor artificial vs. outdoor natural**: M25 (giraffe in living room vs. balloon-dog in park), N24 (aquarium vs. bees)
- **Nature-oriented**: N23 (golden field vs. cosmos), N24 (aquarium vs. bees), N21 (stairs vs. group)

The two images typically invite different emotional registers — one tends to invite interiority, solitude, or reflection; the other tends to invite action, community, or scale. Choosing well matters: pick the image where you have more specific detail and a clearer character or perspective in mind.

**What the images invite (from the MS)**:
- An isolated figure image → internal conflict, doubt, reflection, longing
- A group or celebration image → shared moment, belonging, victory, communal energy
- A surreal or striking image (giraffe, balloon-dog) → absurdist or comic backstory, creative explanation
- A nature detail image (bees, cosmos) → sensory immersion, emotional connection to the non-human

## Model approach

Task 2 has a recommended timing of **35 minutes** (or 25–35 minutes depending on session). With approximately 350 words to write, this breaks down:

**Step 1 — Choose the image (1–2 minutes)**
Read both image descriptions carefully. Ask: which one gives me a specific character, a clear emotional moment, and at least two or three sensory details I can use? Do not choose based on which scene seems easier — choose based on which one you can make vivid and specific.

**Step 2 — Plan before writing (3–5 minutes)**
Decide:
- Point of view (first person is most common; third limited also works)
- The character's internal state and one external detail that reveals it
- How the piece opens (in medias res or with an establishing image)
- What the pivot or development is (something shifts — a decision, a realisation, an action)
- How it closes (does not need to resolve, but must feel deliberate — the MS rewards structural awareness)

**Step 3 — Draft with technique (25 minutes)**
Write with these targets:
- Open with a sentence that establishes mood through a concrete image, not a summary ("The giraffe watched her from above the mantelpiece" not "This is the story of how a giraffe ended up in our house.")
- Use characterisation: give the character a specific gesture, thought, or line of dialogue that makes them real
- Vary sentence structures deliberately: mix short declarative sentences for impact with longer sentences for atmosphere
- Use at least one piece of specific sensory detail (sight, sound, texture, smell) drawn directly from or plausibly suggested by the image
- Make the image feel integral, not decorative

**Step 4 — Check language (2–3 minutes)**
Read through for: a) errors that would hinder communication (tense shifts, sentence fragments without intent, unclear referents); b) any paragraph that is pure plot summary with no craft — add a detail or vary the syntax.

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the 0-mark rule.** Using neither the image nor its theme scores 0 for Criterion C. Always connect the image to the writing explicitly — at least one detail from the image must appear.

2. **Distinguish "addressing" from "elaborating" from "inspired by."** These are the three-level distinction in the MS. Practice with past images: after writing, ask honestly — does this merely address the prompt, or is it genuinely shaped by this specific image? Elaborating means the image affects the setting, character, and mood; inspired means the image unlocked an angle you wouldn't have taken without it.

3. **Practice point of view as a craft choice.** The MS rewards point of view as a stylistic choice at 6–8 and above. Know the difference between: first-person (intimacy, unreliability), second-person (unusual, high impact), close third (access to interiority without "I"). Try writing the same image prompt in first and third to feel the difference.

4. **Build a bank of opening techniques.** The opening is where Criterion C is often decided. Strong openings: in medias res (starting in the middle of action), a sensory image that establishes mood immediately, a line of dialogue that reveals character. Weak openings: "In this story…", "One day…", "This is about a girl who…"

5. **Sentence variety for Criterion D.** The D band at 6–8 requires a "varied range" of sentence structures. At 9–10 the register must be "consistently purposeful." Practice: after drafting, identify any run of three consecutive sentences with the same structure and rewrite at least one of them.

6. **Understand what each writing type requires.** The form is specified in the task, not chosen freely:
   - **Scene**: requires setting, character, and action — something happens
   - **Narrative**: sequence of events with emotional trajectory
   - **Monologue**: single voice, present-tense or reflective, spoken or internal — must feel spoken
   - **Creative description**: sensory and evocative — does not need plot, but needs cumulative effect
   - **Original piece**: most open — match the form to the emotion or idea the image suggests

7. **Use the advisory craft elements in the task.** "You may consider using any of the following: purpose, characterization, setting, mood, and/or point of view" is a direct signal of what the MS is looking for at higher bands. A response that uses none of these consciously will not reach 9–10 for Criterion C.
