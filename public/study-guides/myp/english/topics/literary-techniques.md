# Literary Techniques

## Key concepts

### Figurative language

- **Metaphor** — a direct comparison that states one thing *is* another, without "like" or "as". Creates a vivid, immediate image and transfers the qualities of the compared thing to the subject. Effect: makes abstract ideas concrete; adds emotional intensity. Example: "locust army" (M22) — calling the insects an army transfers connotations of organised, unstoppable destruction.
- **Simile** — a comparison using "like" or "as". Effect: helps the reader visualise something unfamiliar by linking it to something familiar. Example: the perfume description "like a light tea" (N21) — deceptively simple appearance masking complexity.
- **Personification** — attributing human qualities to non-human things. Effect: creates emotional connection; makes the non-human feel alive or threatening. Example: "The tapestry winks silver in the moonlight" (M23) — gives the tapestry agency and magical life.
- **Symbolism** — an object, image, or event that stands for something beyond its literal meaning. Effect: layers meaning; allows ideas to be communicated without direct statement. Example: the fireball in *Dreamcatcher* (N24) — symbolises the woman's wisdom, power, and cultural heritage.
- **Imagery** — language that appeals to the senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell). Effect: immerses the reader in the scene; creates mood; makes abstract emotions tangible. Includes visual imagery ("red-tinged gold light", M22), auditory imagery ("alarms", M24), and olfactory imagery (the perfume passage, N21).

### Sound devices

- **Alliteration** — repetition of the same initial consonant sound in nearby words. Effect: draws attention to those words; creates rhythm; can reinforce mood (harsh consonants = tension; soft sounds = calm). Example: "scary speculation" (M25) — the repeated "s" emphasises the speaker's anxious, obsessive thinking.
- **Assonance** — repetition of vowel sounds within nearby words. Effect: creates internal rhyme and musicality; slows or accelerates pace depending on the vowel used.
- **Onomatopoeia** — words whose sound imitates the thing they describe (e.g. "crash", "hiss", "murmur"). Effect: makes description immediate and sensory; increases realism.
- **Rhyme** — repetition of similar end-sounds in lines of a poem. Effect: creates musicality and rhythm; can make a poem feel playful, memorable, or ironic; can signal a conclusion. Example: *Blueprints?* (M25) uses end rhyme ("kin/chin", "talk/walk") to give the poem an accessible, sing-song quality, which contrasts with the serious underlying anxiety.

### Structural techniques

- **Enjambment** — a line of poetry that runs on to the next without a pause or punctuation at the line end. Effect: creates momentum; mirrors the speaker's rushing thoughts; can emphasise a word placed at the start of the following line.
- **Caesura** — a strong pause within a line of poetry, often marked by punctuation. Effect: slows the reader down; creates emphasis; can signal a shift in thought.
- **Stanza form** — the grouping of lines into stanzas (verses). Effect: controls pacing and emphasis; a short stanza or isolated line draws the eye and suggests importance.
- **Repetition** — deliberate reuse of a word, phrase, or structural element. Effect: emphasises an idea; can show obsession, fear, or insistence. Example: the repeated questions in *Blueprints?* ("Am I going to lose my teeth… / My hair? / My mind?", M25) accumulate to show the speaker's mounting anxiety.
- **Contrast** — placing opposite or different ideas, images, or characters side by side to highlight difference. Effect: makes each side more vivid; creates tension or irony. Example: M23 — "silver moonlight" (beauty, magic) vs. "folds into the darkness" (uncertainty, threat).
- **Juxtaposition** — the placement of two contrasting elements close together. Closely related to contrast but specifically refers to the structural act of placing them adjacent. Effect: forces the reader to compare and notice the difference. Example: N21 — Baldini's outward resistance ("flung the handkerchief") vs. his inner admiration ("Wonderful, wonderful").
- **Irony** — a gap between what is said and what is meant, or between expectation and reality. Effect: creates complexity; invites the reader to read beneath the surface. Example: M22 — Margaret says she is "lucky" to see the locusts even as the farm faces bankruptcy.

### Tonal techniques

- **Tone** — the attitude of the writer or speaker towards the subject or audience (e.g. celebratory, anxious, elegiac, ironic). Effect: shapes how the reader feels about the content.
- **Mood** — the emotional atmosphere created in the text for the reader or viewer. Often created by setting, imagery, and word choice.
- **Register** — the level of formality and the vocabulary choices appropriate to a particular context, audience, and purpose (e.g. formal, colloquial, academic, conversational).
- **Voice** — the distinctive personality and perspective of the narrator or speaker, created by diction, syntax, and tone.
- **Diction** — the specific word choices a writer makes. Effect: individual words carry connotations (positive/negative associations) that colour meaning. Example: M22 MS explicitly rewards noticing that "red-tinged gold light" uses colour to hint at the conflict to come.

### Narrative techniques

- **Characterisation** — the methods by which a writer constructs a character: through dialogue, actions, descriptions, and the reactions of others. For film: also through camera angles, voiceover, and casting. Example: M24 Q1c rewards specific comments about how the wire walker's characterisation (fearless, focused, single-minded) is built.
- **Setting** — the time, place, and context in which a narrative takes place. Effect: creates mood; can symbolise the character's emotional state; can establish conflict. Example: *Woven in Moonlight* (M23) — the moonlight setting reinforces the magic of Luna's gift.
- **Foreshadowing** — hints at events that will occur later in the narrative. Effect: builds suspense; creates inevitability. Example: M25 Q1c MS identifies "foreshadowing" as a valid technique for interpreting the title *Finding Myself*.
- **Motif** — a recurring image, symbol, or idea that develops thematic meaning throughout a text. Example: N24 — the fireball in *Dreamcatcher* is both a climactic visual and a motif representing the woman's accumulated cultural knowledge.
- **Rhetorical questions** — questions not intended to be answered, but to make the reader reflect. Effect: engages the audience; implies that the answer is obvious; creates a conversational or urgent tone. Example: M25 *Blueprints?* — "Am I naturally this crazy? / Is it something in my genes?" draws the reader into the speaker's self-questioning.

## Techniques in past papers — real examples

### Metaphor

**N22 Q1a — "forests are the ornaments of the earth"** (*Uncle Vanya*)
- Question: Comment on the significance of this quotation.
- MS expected: Level 1 just identifies it as a metaphor. Level 2 comments on what the comparison achieves: "Comparing the trees to ornaments, which are decorations to make things beautiful, is a way to express how Astroft values the trees for their beauty and what they add to life."
- Key lesson: Naming the technique alone scores 1. You must explain what the metaphor *does* — what qualities it transfers and what that reveals about character or theme.

**M22 Q1b — "locust army"** (*A Wild Attack of Locusts*)
- Question: Evaluate the effect of the line about possible bankruptcy.
- MS expected: The metaphor of "army" shows the locusts as organised, dangerous, unstoppable — but Margaret's resilience means she finds this impressive rather than just terrifying.
- Key lesson: Technique identification + connotation + effect on characterisation = top band.

### Colour and imagery

**M22 Q1a — "red-tinged gold light"** (*A Wild Attack of Locusts*)
- Question: Comment on the use of colour in the opening.
- MS expected: Level 1 identifies the colour reference. Level 2 explains that "red" hints at the conflict and trouble ahead, while "gold" creates an initially positive mood — the contrast of the two colours foreshadows the ambivalent tone of the whole passage.
- Key lesson: Single colour names are not enough — comment on connotation and what dual colour palettes imply about mood and foreshadowing.

**M23 Q1b — "The tapestry winks silver in the moonlight … folds into the darkness"** (*Woven in Moonlight*)
- Question: Explain the significance of the line.
- MS expected: Personification ("winks") gives the tapestry agency and magical life, reinforced by the colour "silver" evoking Luna's magic. The contrasting darkness creates suspense. The full significance = personification + colour symbolism + contrast (light/dark) = a climactic, magical, ambiguous ending to the scene.
- Key lesson: When a line contains multiple techniques, the mark scheme rewards noticing more than one and showing how they interact.

### Alliteration and sound

**M25 Q1b — "scary speculation"** (*Blueprints?*)
- Question: Analyse the effect of one technique used in the poem.
- MS top-band example: "The poem uses alliteration in 'scary speculation' to emphasise how stressed out the speaker is about how she'll turn out. The repetition of 's' here calls attention to her fear when she thinks about her future."
- Key lesson: Name the device + name the specific letters + explain the emotional effect the sound pattern creates.

### Rhetorical questions / repetition

**M25 Q1b and Q1a — questions throughout *Blueprints?***
- MS examples for Q1b: "The speaker uses questions over and over in the poem, like 'Am I naturally this crazy?' and 'Am I going to lose my teeth someday?' Every question brings up something she is worried about, helping the reader understand her fears and anxieties."
- MS examples for Q1a: Ending the poem on "Who will I become?" creates tension because we don't know how the speaker will turn out; it summarises all the speaker's questions about herself — this is a conclusion, not just a question.
- Key lesson: Comment on what accumulated repetition achieves (intensification, obsession, anxiety), not just that repetition exists.

### Symbolism and visual motif

**N24 Q1d — the fireball in *Dreamcatcher***
- Question: Comment on the significance of the fireball.
- MS top-band examples: "The director uses a dramatic symbol of a fireball, placing it above the woman's head to illustrate her timeless wisdom and power." / "The appearance of the fireball above her head represents the collective knowledge and wisdom she and her people have accumulated."
- Key lesson: Symbolism questions require: what the symbol literally is + what it stands for + why that matters in context. Saying "the fireball is a symbol" alone scores 0.

**N24 Q1c — voiceover simile in *Dreamcatcher***
- Question: Analyse the effect of one technique used to characterise the elderly woman.
- MS top-band example: "The voiceover makes viewers appreciate the woman's wisdom via her reflection, connecting past to present using simile for effect: 'And I feel that girl still pulsing through my veins like butterflies fluttering by.'"
- Key lesson: For film analysis, the technique (voiceover), the device within the technique (simile), the exact quote, and the effect on the viewer are all needed for the top band.

### Tone, voice, and characterisation

**M24 Q1d — techniques in *The Walk***
- Question: Analyse the effect of a technique in this film excerpt.
- MS top-band examples: "The camera angles make the viewer afraid because they show the entire city below him … [showing] how far he can fall." / "The moment that the wire walker steps onto the wire … the filmmaker shows the clouds clearing … this change from a cloudy sky to a clear sky symbolizes his state of mind as calm, focused and clear."
- Key lesson: Technical film language (camera angle, mise-en-scène) counts as technique. Always connect visual choice to character's emotional state and audience impact.

**M22 Q1b — tone in Margaret's line** (*A Wild Attack of Locusts*)
- Question: Evaluate the effect of the line.
- MS top-band example: "She frames it as something 'not everyone has seen', as if watching the locusts form an army is an extraordinary privilege. This positions her as someone who…"
- Key lesson: Evaluating a character line requires connecting diction to character traits — here, the framing language reveals resilience.

### Contrast and juxtaposition

**N21 Q1b — Baldini's inner conflict** (*Perfume*)
- Question: Analyse how the writer creates a sense of Baldini's conflict.
- MS expected: The contrast between his outward behaviour (flinging the handkerchief, walking away "as if ashamed") and his internal reaction ("Wonderful, wonderful") illustrates his inner conflict. His physical, controlled approach to smelling contrasts with his involuntary reverence.
- Key lesson: Juxtaposition between action and reaction, or external vs. internal, is a powerful analytical angle for characterisation questions.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

**The full formula at top band** (consistent across all sessions and all command terms for Q1a–1d):
1. **Technique named** — state specifically what technique is being used (alliteration, not just "word choice"; metaphor, not just "language"; close-up shot, not just "camera").
2. **Embedded quote or specific reference** — show exactly where in the text the technique appears. For film, describe the specific visual, audio, or structural choice.
3. **Effect on audience or reader** — explain *how* the reader or viewer responds emotionally or cognitively. Use language of effect: "creates", "makes the reader feel", "suggests", "implies", "emphasises".
4. **Explanation of why** — the extra layer that separates 2-mark from 3-mark answers: not just "this creates tension" but *why* this particular choice creates tension at this particular moment.

The mark scheme phrases this consistently across every session as:
- Level 1 (limited): "identifies … but does not develop ideas with examples or explanations"
- Level 2 (adequate): "justifies opinions and ideas with some examples or explanations"
- Level 3 (effective): "consistently justifies opinions and ideas with examples and explanations"

### What the mark scheme does not accept

- **Technique named without effect** — "The poem uses alliteration" scores level 1 only. The technique must be linked to its effect.
- **Paraphrase instead of analysis** — retelling what happens in the text ("she says she loves weaving") without commenting on technique earns level 1 at best.
- **Wrong technique identification** — if you label something incorrectly (e.g. calling a simile a metaphor), the mark scheme may still award marks if the effect analysis is valid, but it is preferable to be accurate.
- **Vague effect language** — "it makes the poem more interesting" or "it engages the reader" without saying *how* or *why* scores level 1.
- **General claims about theme without technique** — saying "the text is about identity" without linking it to a specific technique or language choice does not earn Criterion A marks.

## Common question types

### Q1a — 2 marks, Text 1 (written text)
- Command terms used: Comment (M25, M22, N22, N24), Describe (M24, M23 partial), Explain (N21), How (M23)
- What it tests: A single, focused observation about one technique or effect in the written text. Typically a short passage, line, or quotation is specified.
- What earns 2 marks: One technique or observation identified + developed with an example or brief explanation. One well-focused point is enough.
- What earns 1 mark: Identification only, with no development.

### Q1b — 3 marks, Text 1 (written text)
- Command terms used: Analyse (M25, N21, N22), Explain (M24, M23, N24), Evaluate (M22)
- What it tests: A more developed analytical response. The 3-mark structure requires technique + quote + effect + explanation. For Analyse and Evaluate, judgement is expected; for Explain, cause-and-effect reasoning is expected.
- What earns 3 marks: Technique or observation + embedded quote + effect + explanation, all consistently connected.
- What earns 2 marks: Technique + quote OR technique + effect, but not both aspects fully developed.
- What earns 1 mark: Identification of effect without example or explanation.

### Q1c — 2–3 marks, Text 2 (film/visual text)
- Command terms used: Analyse (N24), Comment (M25, M24, N21, N22), Describe (M22, M23)
- What it tests: Analysis of a film or visual technique in Text 2. Questions frequently specify a clip, still, or scene. Film techniques include: camera angle, close-up, slow motion, voiceover, music, lighting, colour, subtitles, direct address, montage.
- What earns full marks: Film technique named specifically + reference to where it appears + effect on viewer explained.
- What earns 1 mark: Technique named or effect named, but not linked to each other.

### Q1d — 2–3 marks, Text 2 (film/visual text)
- Command terms used: Explain (M25, N22), Analyse (M24, M22), Comment (N24), Examine (N21), Justify (M23)
- What it tests: A slightly deeper question about Text 2 — often asking about a specific clip, a symbolic visual element, or the overall effect of a technique.
- What earns full marks: For 3-mark questions (Analyse/Explain): technique + reference + effect + explanation. For Examine and Justify: goes further — connecting the technique to the text's broader purpose or the audience's experience.

### The Analyse/Comment/Explain distinction in practice

| Command term | Minimum for full marks | Example of top-band answer |
|---|---|---|
| Comment (2 marks) | One observation with one example OR one explanation | "The fireball symbol places power above the woman's head, illustrating her accumulated wisdom" — observation + what it conveys |
| Explain (2–3 marks) | Effect + reason; for 3 marks, technique + example + causal chain | "The repeated alarms add tension because each one signals deeper danger — the reader wonders whether he will make it back" |
| Analyse (3 marks) | Technique named + quote + effect + explanation consistently | "Alliteration in 'scary speculation' emphasises the speaker's stress — the 's' sound calls attention to the fear underneath her humour" |

## Model answer formula

The formula that consistently reaches the top band across all Q1a–1d questions:

**TEE+ (Technique — Evidence — Effect — Elaboration)**

1. **Technique**: Name the specific literary or film technique precisely.
2. **Evidence**: Embed a short, exact quote (for written texts) or describe the specific visual/audio moment (for film). Do not quote large blocks — one phrase or image is enough.
3. **Effect**: State the effect on the reader or viewer, using effect language: "creates", "suggests", "makes the reader/viewer feel", "implies", "emphasises".
4. **Elaboration**: Add one more sentence explaining *why* this effect matters here — what it reveals about character, theme, or the creator's purpose.

**Worked example from M25 Q1b (Analyse, 3 marks):**

> "The poet uses alliteration in 'scary speculation' [Technique + Evidence] to emphasise how stressed the speaker is about how she'll turn out. The repetition of the 's' sound calls attention to her anxiety [Effect]. This phrase comes just before the poem's final question, which means the alliteration prepares the reader to feel the weight of that last line — her fear is not casual, but consuming [Elaboration]."

**Worked example from N24 Q1d (Comment, 2 marks):**

> "The director uses the fireball as a dramatic symbol [Technique], placing it directly above the woman's head [Evidence]. This illustrates her timeless wisdom and power — the fire rising above her suggests her knowledge is both forceful and elevated [Effect]."

Note: For 2-mark Comment questions, you do not need the full Elaboration step. Technique + Evidence + Effect is sufficient for 2/2.

**Worked example from N22 Q1a (Comment, 2 marks):**

> "The phrase uses a metaphor, comparing forests to 'ornaments' [Technique + Evidence]. Ornaments are decorations that make something beautiful, so Astroft is saying the forests add beauty and value to the earth — they are not just resources but something worth preserving for their own sake [Effect]."

## Revision priorities

1. **Master the TEE+ formula for Analyse questions (Q1b, Q1d)** — these are the most frequent 3-mark questions. Practice applying it to any technique in any text: always name the technique precisely, embed a short quote, and explain the effect in one specific sentence.

2. **Know ten film techniques by name and effect** — close-up, camera angle, voiceover, music/soundtrack, slow motion, montage, direct address, lighting, colour, subtitle/title card. Every session has a film text. Being precise about technique separates level 1 from level 3.

3. **Understand the Comment/Explain/Analyse hierarchy** — Comment (2 marks) requires one developed point; Explain (3 marks) requires a cause-effect chain; Analyse (3 marks) requires full technique + quote + effect. Know the difference so you calibrate the length and depth of your response.

4. **Colour and imagery questions recur** — M22 (colour in opening), M23 (light and dark imagery), N21 (visual imagery in film), N24 (visual symbol). Practice commenting on colour connotation: what associations does the colour carry? What mood does it create? Does it foreshadow or contrast?

5. **Characterisation questions recur** — M24 Q1c, M23 Q1a, N24 Q1c, N21 Q1b all focus on how a character is constructed. Practice distinguishing between what a character does (action), what a character says (diction/dialogue), and how the text frames them (narrator or filmmaker's choices). Commenting on all three levels earns more marks.

6. **Symbolism and motif** — N24 Q1d (fireball), M22 Q1b (army metaphor), M23 Q1b (tapestry) all ask about symbolic significance. The formula: what the symbol literally is → what it stands for → why that meaning matters in context. "It is a symbol" alone scores 0.

7. **Rhetorical questions and repetition** — these appear across multiple sessions (M25 *Blueprints?*, N21 *Perfume* internal questions, N21 film). Comment on their cumulative or structural effect, not just their presence.
