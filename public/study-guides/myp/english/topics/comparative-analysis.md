# Comparative Analysis (Q1e)

## Key concepts

Compare and contrast is the only question on the paper that tests **both** Criterion A (Analysing) and Criterion B (Organising) simultaneously, making it the single highest-stakes question at 20 marks. It requires the student to:

- Identify **similarities** AND **differences** between two texts simultaneously — not one then the other
- Analyse the **techniques** each creator uses to present a shared theme, not just summarise what happens
- Move **across** both texts throughout the response — the "woven" approach, where each paragraph refers to both texts, rather than a text-by-text block structure
- Justify opinions with **specific examples and explanations** (quote + technique + effect)

The two texts are always a written text (prose, poem, article, or play excerpt) paired with a visual/film text (short film, documentary clip, biographical film excerpt, or digital story). This means students must use literary terminology for Text 1 and film/visual terminology for Text 2.

**HARD RULES from mark schemes — these appear in every session:**

- **One text only** → capped at **2 marks** for Criterion A and **2 marks** for Criterion B
- **No contrast** (compare only, no differences) → capped at **5 marks** for Criterion A
- **Bullet points** → capped at **2 marks** for Criterion B
- **Text-by-text structure** (all of Text 1 then all of Text 2) → described by the MS as "minimal use of organisational structures" — places student in the 1–2 band for Criterion B
- **Identification without analysis** (naming a technique but not explaining its effect) → capped at the 3–5 band for Criterion A

## Text pairs — real examples from past papers

### M25: Identity and family inheritance
- **Text 1:** *Blueprints?* — poem by Sara Holbrook. Speaker uses rhetorical questions ("Who will I become?", "Am I naturally this crazy?"), imagery ("balloon like Uncle Harry"), and alliteration ("scary speculation", "dizzy daydreams") to convey anxiety about inherited traits.
- **Text 2:** *Finding Myself* — digital story/video by Bonnie Belcher. Young woman describes family members (sister as "creative" and "thoughtful", great-grandmother as "legendary") through voiceover, positive adjectives, and personal anecdote. Ends with uncertainty but hope: she "still has time to find her place."
- **MS comparison focus:** Both texts explore a young person's uncertainty about identity. The poem focuses on inherited physical and personality traits through anxious questioning; the video focuses on role models within the family through admiring narration. Both end in open-ended uncertainty — the poem with a question, the video with the phrase "doesn't know yet."

### N24: Intergenerational relationships
- **Text 1:** *How the elderly can help the young — and help themselves* — Guardian article by James Tapper. Uses expert quotations ("the outcomes for the children were very positive"), specific examples from multiple countries (UK and US), balanced evidence of benefits to both generations, and an embedded photograph of a girl and elderly woman at a tea party.
- **Text 2:** *Dreamcatcher* — short film by Matty Brown. Features an elderly Native American woman using voiceover, flashbacks to childhood (running through fields, climbing trees), simile ("I feel that girl still pulsing through my veins like butterflies fluttering by"), and a dramatic fireball symbol in the final scene.
- **MS comparison focus:** Both texts present the elderly as possessing wisdom and the young as benefiting from connection with older generations, but through contrasting modes — the article uses factual, journalistic evidence; the film uses sensory, poetic imagery and symbol.

### M24: Risk-taking
- **Text 1:** *The Secrets of the World's Greatest Freediver* (article by Daniel Riley). Builds tension through repeated alarm imagery, precise technical language ("plate at the bottom of the dive line"), and the phrase "There but not there" to capture altered mental states during risk.
- **Text 2:** *The Walk* (biographical film, Philippe Petit). Uses close-up camera angles on feet and the drop below, music shifting from tense to joyful, and Petit's voiceover: "All I could see was the wire floating out in a straight line to infinity" — conveying total mental focus.
- **MS comparison focus:** Both texts show characters who enter an altered, hyper-focused mental state during risk. The article conveys this through physiological description; the film through camera angle, music, and first-person voiceover.

### N23: Childhood
- **Text 1:** *The Conservationist* (Nadine Gordimer prose). Presents childhood as a world of private pride and ritual through imagery ("eggs arranged like marbles"), social hierarchy among children ("only the most pigeon-toed one is allowed to touch"), and the adult outsider's perspective that cannot penetrate the children's language.
- **Text 2:** *Remember Childhood?* (short film). Uses home-video style footage, upbeat music, flashback title cards, and a child's-eye camera angle to present cherry-picking as magical and joyful. Ends with the caption "just for a little."
- **MS comparison focus:** Both texts show childhood as a world with its own rules and magic. The prose presents it from an adult observer's outside perspective; the film presents it from within the child's experience. Both suggest childhood is fleeting and precious.

### N22: Preservation
- **Text 1:** *Uncle Vanya* (Chekhov play excerpt). Astrov uses language of destruction ("trembling under the blows of the axe", "Millions of trees have perished", "never to return"), emotional appeals ("Why should any sane man destroy what he cannot create?"), and personal passion ("my heart swells with pride") to argue for forest preservation. Sonia acts as admiring amplifier.
- **Text 2:** *Yindali Explains Why Forests Are Important* (rookie news video). 10-year-old Yindali uses her native Kuku Yalanji language, statistics ("60 seconds = 36 football fields" of deforestation, "180 million years" old), aerial footage, animal close-ups, and direct address to camera to make the same case for the Daintree.
- **MS comparison focus:** Both creators use emotional and factual appeals to argue for preservation, but the play uses dramatic, passionate rhetoric from an adult male authority figure while the video uses a child's authentic personal connection and data-driven urgency.

### M23: Creative expression
- **Text 1:** *Woven in Moonlight* (Isabel Ibañez novel excerpt). First-person narration captures the meditative trance of weaving through sensory detail ("over, under, over"), personification of the tapestry ("winks silver"), and the moment where magic and craft merge ("What if this is part of my Illustrian magic?").
- **Text 2:** *The Sleeping Beauty* (TED-Ed ballet film). Uses animation to break down ballet techniques (échappé, pas de bourrée), contrasts technical instruction with emotional performance, and shows how choreographer Petipa strings gestures "like musical notes to form pieces of music."
- **MS comparison focus:** Both texts show creative expression as both technical and transcendent. The novel emphasises the spiritual/magical dimension; the film emphasises the technical construction that enables performance.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **"Perceptively compares and contrasts, making extensive connections in features across and within genres and texts"** — this is the 9–10 band descriptor for Criterion A (exact wording from M25, N24, N23, M23 MS)
- **Discussing "subtle connections"** beyond the obvious — the 9–10 band in Criterion A states: "The candidate discusses subtle connections and may draw conclusions within and beyond the two texts"
- **"Sophisticated use of organisational structures"** — the 9–10 band for Criterion B requires ideas that "build on each other in a sophisticated way," not just a list of comparisons
- **Accurate terminology** — named in strand iii at all bands from 6 upward; "terminology can be implied" at lower bands (meaning you can describe a metaphor without naming it, but naming it earns more credit)
- **Both analysis AND example AND explanation** — the 9–10 band for Criterion A requires all three consistently: "gives detailed justification of opinions and ideas with a range of examples, and thorough explanations"
- **The comparison strand (iv) is the deciding factor** — every MS from M23 onward states explicitly: "Candidates' ability to compare and contrast the two texts (strand iv.) should be the deciding factor when placing students in a band for Criterion A"
- **Cross-genre connections** — noting that a film uses voiceover where a poem uses direct address, or that both use first-person to create intimacy, scores higher than noting that both texts are "about the same theme"

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **One text only:** Cannot exceed **2 marks for Criterion A** and **2 marks for Criterion B** — absolute cap, regardless of quality of analysis (confirmed in every session from N22 onward, and stated in the General Marking Guidelines of all sessions)
- **Compare only, no contrast (or contrast only, no comparison):** Cannot exceed **5 marks for Criterion A** — stated explicitly in every session's MS
- **Bullet points:** Cannot exceed **2 marks for Criterion B** — does not affect Criterion A, but is noted in the General Marking Guidelines as applying to all extended writing (Q1e and Q3)
- **Text-by-text structure:** Placed in the 1–2 band for Criterion B — the MS example for the lowest band explicitly says "eg, writing about each text separately" as the failure mode
- **Summarising without analysing:** The 3–5 band for Criterion A states "candidates will tend to summarize with some exploration of features" — summary is the marker of a mid-band response, not a high-band one
- **Obvious comparisons only:** "Both texts show similar themes" or "One is a film and the other is written text" — these are the exact MS examples of 1–2 band responses
- **Identifying technique without explaining effect:** Not consistently justifying with "examples AND explanations" caps at the 6–8 band; the 9–10 band requires both

## Common question types

Q1e always follows the same formula across all 14 sessions:

**"Compare and contrast how [both creators / the writer and filmmaker] [present / explore / portray / develop] [a theme] in both texts."**

The theme varies — identity, risk-taking, preservation, childhood, creative expression — but the structure is identical: 20 marks, Criterion A (10) + Criterion B (10), approximately 300–350 words, 35 minutes recommended.

**Two structural approaches — and why one fails:**

1. **Text-by-text (block) structure:** Write everything about Text 1, then everything about Text 2, with a connecting sentence at the end. This is the approach the MS places in the 1–2 band for Criterion B. It demonstrates minimal organisation because the comparison only happens at a surface level.

2. **Woven (thematic) structure:** Each paragraph takes a comparison point (a technique, a theme, an effect on the audience) and discusses how *both* texts handle it within the same paragraph. This is what the 6–10 band for Criterion B describes as "compares and contrasts the two texts" with "ideas building on each other." The MS indicative content for the top band of Criterion A (9–10) always features a student who moves fluidly between both texts: "both texts use visual detail... both creators show..."

**Signalling language that earns Criterion B marks:**
- Similarity: "Similarly,..." / "Both creators use..." / "In the same way,..."
- Difference: "However,..." / "In contrast,..." / "While [Text 1] uses X to..., [Text 2] instead..."
- Development: "This is further developed in..." / "This connection is made explicit when..."

## Model approach

A reliable step-by-step formula for Q1e in 35 minutes:

**Step 1 — Thesis sentence (2–3 lines)**
State the comparison directly: name both texts, name the theme, and identify one key similarity and one key difference in technique or approach.
> Example: "Both *Blueprints?* and *Finding Myself* explore a young person's uncertainty about identity — the poem through anxious rhetorical questioning directed inward, the video through admiring outward narration of family role models — yet both ultimately leave the speaker's identity unresolved."

**Step 2 — Comparison paragraph 1: a similarity in theme or approach**
- Topic sentence stating the comparison point
- Quotation/example from Text 1 + technique label + effect on reader
- Linking connective ("Similarly," / "In the same way,")
- Quotation/example from Text 2 + technique label + effect on audience
- Mini-conclusion connecting both

**Step 3 — Comparison paragraph 2: a difference in technique or perspective**
- Topic sentence stating the contrast point
- Quotation/example from Text 1 + technique label + effect
- Linking connective ("However," / "In contrast,")
- Quotation/example from Text 2 + technique label + effect
- Mini-conclusion connecting both

**Step 4 — Comparison paragraph 3 (if time): a deeper / implicit connection**
This is what separates the 6–8 band from 9–10. Find a subtle link — an irony, a structural echo, or a way the same effect is achieved by opposite means. Example: "Both texts end in open-ended uncertainty — the poem with the question 'Who will I become?' and the video with the narrator saying she 'doesn't know yet' — but the tones are different: the poem is anxious, the video is hopeful."

**Step 5 — Brief conclusion (2–3 lines)**
Do not introduce new material. Synthesise: restate the key similarity and key difference in one sentence, and comment on what this reveals about how different text types approach the same theme.

**What to avoid:**
- Introducing one text, finishing all analysis of it, then starting the other (text-by-text)
- Writing "Text 1 uses imagery and Text 2 also uses imagery" without explaining the different effects
- Using bullet points at any point
- Forgetting to include both a similarity and a difference

## Revision priorities

1. **Drill the hard rules until they are automatic.** One text = 2 marks max. No contrast = 5 marks max. Bullet points = 2 marks max for Criterion B. These caps cannot be overcome by good analysis. Check before submitting.

2. **Practise woven paragraph structure.** Write one paragraph comparing and contrasting *Blueprints?* and *Finding Myself* on the theme of family. Force yourself to move between both texts within the same paragraph rather than finishing one before starting the other.

3. **Build a bank of film analysis vocabulary.** Every session has a film as Text 2. Know: voiceover, close-up, extreme close-up, camera angle, flashback, soundtrack/score, direct address, slow motion, editing pace, lighting, symbolism in visual form. Practice explaining the *effect* of each on the viewer, not just naming it.

4. **Know the comparison signal words.** Similarly, in the same way, likewise, both creators — for similarity. However, in contrast, while, whereas, unlike — for difference. "This is further reinforced in" and "this technique functions differently in" for development. The mark scheme rewards coherent organisation; these words are the evidence of it.

5. **Use the theme word from the question in every paragraph.** If the question asks about "identity," use the word "identity" (or a close synonym) in every paragraph. This keeps the response focused on comparison of theme rather than sliding into summary of what happens.

6. **Allocate time strictly.** Q1e is 35 minutes for 20 marks. Do not let Q1a–1d overrun. The 20 marks of Q1e must not be shortchanged: a strong Q1e (18/20) followed by adequate Q1a–1d (7/10) beats the reverse.

7. **Read both texts simultaneously during the 15-minute Q1a–1d period.** The texts are on screen for the whole exam. When answering Q1a–1d, already be noting: What technique does Text 1 use? What technique does Text 2 use? Where do they converge? Where do they diverge? This builds the comparison before the clock starts on Q1e.
