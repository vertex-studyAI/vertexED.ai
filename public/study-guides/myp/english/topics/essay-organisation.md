# Essay Organisation (Criterion B)

## Key concepts

**Criterion B — Organising** rewards the structural and logical quality of extended writing. It appears in two places on the paper:

- **Q1e (compare-and-contrast essay):** 10 marks for Criterion B, alongside 10 marks for Criterion A. Focus is on how coherently and comparatively the essay is structured — ideas must build on each other across both texts, not simply describe each text in turn.
- **Task 3 (non-literary writing):** 10 marks for Criterion B. Focus is on whether the response adheres to the required form (e.g. speech, letter, blog post, proposal), uses structural devices purposefully, and organises ideas with sustained coherence.

**What Criterion B does NOT assess:**
- Language accuracy (Criterion D)
- Quality of ideas or textual insight (Criterion A for Q1e; Criterion C for Task 3)

Criterion B is purely about **how well the writing is put together structurally**, not what it says.

---

### Q1e organisation: the comparative essay

The central demand of Q1e is integration. The examiner is not looking for a first-half-about-Text-1, second-half-about-Text-2 structure. That pattern is explicitly named in the 1–2 band descriptor as the defining failure ("eg, writing about each text separately"). Top-band responses weave both texts together in every paragraph around a shared theme, technique, or angle.

The structural progression the band descriptors describe:

| Band | What the structure does |
|------|------------------------|
| 1–2 | Writes about each text separately; little or no connection between them |
| 3–5 | Attempts to compare and contrast; begins to make connections |
| 6–8 | Compares and contrasts across paragraphs; ideas build on each other |
| 9–10 | Sophisticated structure; sustained, coherent, logical; structural devices used deliberately |

### Task 3 organisation: text-type specific

Task 3 requires adherence to a named form. The 3–5 band descriptor confirms that "the structure is straightforward and adheres to the given form" is what earns the middle band. The 6–8 band adds structural devices that "begin to add to the overall effect" (e.g. referring back to an opening detail at the end, or varying paragraph/sentence length for impact). The 9–10 band describes structural devices "utilised throughout" — sustained, not occasional.

Each Task 3 form has its own structural conventions:
- **Speech:** address (eg "Good morning"), call to action at close, logical argument sequence
- **Letter:** salutation, sign-off, paragraphs building a single request/recommendation
- **Blog post:** informal heading/opening, engaging hook, developed argument, reflective close
- **Proposal:** stated purpose, justified reasons, structured recommendations
- **Interview:** alternating Q&A format, introductory context, thematic progression through questions

## Organisation patterns from past papers

### The text-by-text trap (1–2 band — consistent across all sessions)

The single most consistent example in the 1–2 band descriptor for Q1e Criterion B is "writing about each text separately." This phrasing appears verbatim across M21, N22, M23, N23, M24, N24, and M25. A response that writes fully about Text 1 in the first half and then Text 2 in the second half — even if it analyses well — cannot exceed 2/10 for Criterion B. This is a structural failure that no amount of analytical quality can compensate for.

### Attempted connection (3–5 band)

The 3–5 descriptor says the structure "serves the context and intention" and there is "an attempt to compare and contrast the texts." The key word is *attempt*: the student is trying to weave texts together but not consistently doing so. Connections are made, but ideas do not build logically from one to the next.

### Ideas building on each other (6–8 band)

The 6–8 descriptor requires that "ideas build on each other" — not just that both texts are mentioned in each paragraph, but that the essay progresses. Each paragraph should advance the argument rather than simply add another similarity or difference. Structural devices begin to appear (eg referencing the introduction at the close, organising paragraphs by theme rather than by technique alone).

### Sophisticated structure throughout (9–10 band)

The 9–10 descriptor requires "sophisticated use of organisational structures" with ideas building "in a sophisticated way." In Task 3, the M25 and N24 mark schemes for 9–10 explicitly cite "crafting the length of sentences and/or paragraphs" as an example of sophisticated structural device. In Q1e, the top band requires that the comparative structure is "sustained" throughout — every paragraph maintains the integration, not just some of them.

## Mark scheme insights

### What earns top Criterion B (Q1e)

- **Thematic / technique-based paragraphs:** Each paragraph addresses a shared theme or technique and weaves both texts around it. The paragraph begins with a claim, develops it with reference to Text 1, then immediately pivots to Text 2 to compare or contrast.
- **Connective language:** Explicit comparison language ("Similarly, the filmmaker uses...", "In contrast, the poem...", "Both creators...", "While the article presents X, the film subverts this by...") signals active comparison rather than parallel description.
- **Ideas that build:** The essay does not simply list comparisons. Each paragraph moves the argument forward — from surface similarity to deeper insight, or from technique to effect to implication.
- **Introduction and conclusion:** A brief framing sentence about both texts opens the essay; a concluding observation (not just a summary) closes it. The mark schemes describe top-band structure as "sustained, coherent and logical" — an introduction and conclusion are part of that.

### What earns top Criterion B (Task 3)

- **Correct form conventions:** The required form must be identifiable from the structure alone — a speech has direct address and a call to action, a letter has salutation and sign-off, a blog post has an informal engaging opening.
- **Structural devices add to effect:** At 6–8, structural devices "begin to add to the overall effect." Examples from mark schemes: referring back to an early detail at the end (N23, N24); varying sentence/paragraph length for effect (M25, M24). At 9–10, these devices are used "throughout" — they are consistent, not incidental.
- **Logical sequence with building ideas:** Ideas do not just follow each other chronologically. Each paragraph or section develops from the previous one, advancing the argument or case being made.

### What is penalised

- **Bullet points:** Cap of **2/10 for Criterion B** in both Q1e and Task 3. This is stated in every session's mark scheme without exception. Bullet points signal that the writing is a list, not an essay or a structured text. The cap applies regardless of how perceptive the content is.
- **Text-by-text structure (Q1e only):** Cap of **2/10 for Criterion B** (and simultaneously **2/10 for Criterion A**). Named in the 1–2 band descriptor in every session.
- **Missing form elements (Task 3):** A speech without direct address, or a letter without a sign-off, scores in the 1–2 or 3–5 band for Criterion B because the form "may be absent" or only "adheres to the given form" at a basic level.
- **Points that are unconnected:** The 1–2 band descriptor for Task 3 specifically notes "points may be unconnected." Paragraphs that simply list separate ideas without transitions or logical sequencing cannot exceed the lower bands.
- **No conclusion or no introduction (Q1e):** Not explicitly named as a penalty, but implied by the requirement for "sustained, coherent and logical" structure at 9–10. A response without an opening framing sentence or a closing judgement is unlikely to reach the top band.

## Model essay structures

### Q1e — Comparative essay (thematic structure)

Target length: approximately 350 words. Target: 6–10 marks for Criterion B.

```
INTRODUCTION (2–3 sentences)
  → Frame the comparison: name both texts, identify what they share, 
    state the angle of the comparison.
  → "Both [Text 1] and [Text 2] explore [theme]. While [Text 1] 
    approaches this through [technique/form], [Text 2] uses 
    [technique/form] to [different/similar effect]."

BODY PARAGRAPH 1 — Similarity (4–6 sentences)
  → Topic sentence: name a shared approach/technique.
  → Develop Text 1: technique + quote/reference + effect on audience.
  → Pivot to Text 2: "Similarly, [Text 2] uses [technique] in [example] 
    to [effect]." OR "In the same way, ..."
  → Brief analytical link: why both creators use this approach.

BODY PARAGRAPH 2 — Contrast (4–6 sentences)
  → Topic sentence: name a key difference.
  → Develop Text 1: how it handles the theme/technique.
  → Pivot to Text 2: "In contrast, [Text 2]..." or "However, 
    [Text 2] subverts this by..."
  → Explain the significance of the contrast.

BODY PARAGRAPH 3 — Deeper connection (optional, 4–5 sentences)
  → Explore a more subtle similarity or contrast.
  → This is the paragraph that pushes into the 9–10 band for 
    Criterion A; the structure of comparing both texts still 
    supports 9–10 for Criterion B.

CONCLUSION (2–3 sentences)
  → Do not simply summarise. Offer a final judgement or insight.
  → "Overall, both texts use [technique/approach] to [effect], 
    though [Text 1] achieves [X] while [Text 2] emphasises [Y]."
  → Optional: a broader implication or observation about how 
    the two texts together illuminate the theme.
```

**Structure rules:**
- Never begin a new paragraph by writing "In Text 1..." followed by a separate "In Text 2..." paragraph. Always weave.
- Use explicit comparison connectives in every body paragraph: "Similarly", "In contrast", "Both", "While", "However", "Unlike".
- Both texts must appear in every body paragraph (or at minimum be clearly connected to the previous paragraph's point about the other text).

### Task 3 — Non-literary writing (form-specific structure)

Target length: approximately 350 words. Target: 6–10 marks for Criterion B.

**Universal structure principles for all Task 3 forms:**

```
OPENING — establish form and engage audience
  → Use the form's required opening convention immediately.
  → Speech: direct address ("Good morning, everyone...")
  → Letter: salutation ("Dear [Name/Title],")
  → Blog post: engaging hook or informal title/headline
  → Proposal: clear statement of purpose ("This proposal outlines...")
  → Interview: brief introduction identifying interviewee and context

BODY — develop ideas logically (2–4 paragraphs)
  → Each paragraph builds on the previous one — do not list 
    parallel points; sequence them so they escalate or deepen.
  → Use topic sentences that signal the development:
    "First...", "Beyond this...", "Perhaps most importantly..."
  → Include evidence, examples, or developed reasons — 
    not assertions alone.
  → Structural device for 6–8 band: refer back to an image or 
    idea from the opening at the end ("As I noted at the start...")
  → Structural device for 9–10 band: vary sentence and paragraph 
    length deliberately; use a short paragraph for impact, 
    a longer one for developed argument.

CLOSE — purposeful ending that matches form
  → Speech: call to action or resonant final line
  → Letter: formal sign-off ("Yours sincerely," / "Best regards,")
  → Blog post: reflective closing statement or invitation to respond
  → Proposal: restatement of the core recommendation
  → Interview: closing question or interviewer's sign-off
```

**Form-specific reminders:**
- **Speech:** Write as spoken language — rhetorical questions, direct address, tricolon. But paragraphs are still paragraphs; bullets are not sentences.
- **Letter:** The salutation and sign-off are non-negotiable for Criterion B. Without them, the form is absent.
- **Blog post:** A casual tone is expected, but organisation must still be logical. Headings or a hook-opening help signal the form.
- **Interview:** Questions and answers must alternate. The questions must relate thematically and progress logically — not repeat the same angle.
- **Opinion piece / newspaper article:** Begin with a stance (not neutrally). Develop with evidence. End with a call to action or reinforcement of the stance. A byline or headline can signal the form.

## Revision priorities

1. **Never write text-by-text in Q1e.** This is the single most penalised structural failure and is explicitly named in the 1–2 band descriptor of every session. Practice writing one body paragraph that mentions both texts before moving to the next point.

2. **Never use bullet points in any extended writing.** The 2/10 cap applies to Q1e and Task 3 without exception. Even one bullet point in an otherwise strong response triggers the cap.

3. **Learn the form conventions for Task 3 before the exam.** The 3–5 band for Task 3 requires nothing more than "adheres to the given form." The 6–8 band adds structural devices. You cannot score above 5 if the form is wrong or missing. Know: speech (direct address + call to action), letter (salutation + sign-off), blog post (hook + informal register), proposal (stated purpose + recommendations), interview (Q&A with context intro).

4. **Use explicit comparison connectives in Q1e.** "Similarly", "In contrast", "However", "While", "Both", "Unlike" signal to the examiner that you are actively comparing, not describing in parallel. The 6–8 band descriptor says the structure "compares and contrasts the two texts" — this must be visible in the language, not just implied.

5. **Aim for ideas that build, not ideas that accumulate.** The 6–8 and 9–10 bands both require "ideas build on each other." In Q1e, move from surface similarity to deeper contrast to broader implication. In Task 3, move from context to argument to evidence to conclusion — each paragraph advances, not just adds.

6. **Include a brief conclusion in Q1e.** The top band requires "sustained, coherent and logical" structure. A response that ends mid-analysis without a closing sentence is unlikely to sustain 9–10. Two sentences offering a final judgement about how both texts treat the theme is sufficient.

7. **Use structural devices for Task 3 (target 6–10).** The mark schemes give two examples: (a) referring back to an early detail at the end, and (b) crafting sentence/paragraph length for effect. Technique (a) is the simpler: open with a specific detail, image, or anecdote, then echo it in the final paragraph. This alone can push a Task 3 response from band 3–5 to band 6–8.

8. **Criterion B is worth 20 marks total.** Q1e (10 marks) + Task 3 (10 marks). A student who scores 3/10 on both because of structural failures loses 14 marks compared to a student scoring 8/10 on both. This is among the highest-yield improvements available on the paper.
