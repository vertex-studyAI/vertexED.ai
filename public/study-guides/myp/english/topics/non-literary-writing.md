# Non-Literary Writing (Task 3)

## Key concepts

### What Task 3 tests
Task 3 is the highest-mark task on the paper — 30 marks assessed across three criteria simultaneously:

- **Criterion B (Organising) — 10 marks:** Structure of the text; use of form conventions; logical sequencing of ideas; cohesive devices. Structural sophistication (varying sentence/paragraph length for effect, looping back to an opening idea at the end) earns the top band. Bullet points cap the mark at 2.
- **Criterion C (Producing text) — 10 marks:** Quality of ideas; insight, imagination, and sensitivity; stylistic choices appropriate to a non-literary form; engagement with the global context. Responses that score 0 are those with no link to the global context and no connection to the task prompt.
- **Criterion D (Using language) — 10 marks:** Vocabulary range and precision; register and style consistently matched to the text type and audience; grammar, syntax, and punctuation accuracy. Register is the deciding factor at the middle bands — a speech that sounds like an essay, or a blog post written in formal academic English, is penalised here.

### Global context — what it is and how to use it
Every Task 3 prompt carries a named global context. The mark scheme for Criterion C specifically tracks whether the response demonstrates "understanding" (middle band) or "excellent understanding" (top band) of it. The global context is not just a theme label — it shapes the angle of the content.

Six global contexts appear in the MYP framework:
- **Identities and relationships** — M21, N23
- **Orientation in space and time** — M24, N21
- **Scientific and technological innovation** — M25, N19, N22
- **Globalization and sustainability** — M23, N16
- **Fairness and development** — N20 (implied), N24
- **Personal and cultural expression** — M22

Practical rule: Embed the global context in the argument itself, not just in passing. A speech about disconnecting from technology (N22, "Scientific and technical innovation") should argue *that disconnecting is itself a form of technological thinking* — not just mention technology as a side note.

## Text type conventions — real examples

### Speech (N21, N22)
**N21 task:** "As part of a public awareness campaign, create a speech about how the representation of gender has evolved over time. End your speech with a call for action." (Global context: Orientation in space and time)

**N22 task:** "Create a speech for a school assembly reflecting on when and why disconnecting from technology can be its most innovative use." (Global context: Scientific and technical innovation)

What the mark scheme rewards in speeches:
- **Opening that hooks the audience** — direct address ("Good morning, everyone"), a rhetorical question, or a striking statement. The N22 mark scheme notes "ordering the points in a logical way, or using rhetorical devices" as examples of effective structural use (Criterion B, 6–8 band).
- **Logical development with building paragraphs** — ideas must build on each other, not sit in isolation. The N21 mark scheme describes the top Criterion B band as: "The call for action is powerful and well-targeted" and ideas are "logically developed and sequenced."
- **Register: spoken, not academic** — contractions, second-person address, rhetorical questions, tricolon, rule of three. These signal that the writer understands who they are writing for.
- **A genuine call for action** — for N21 this was a specific mark scheme requirement. A weak or absent call for action caps the Criterion C score.
- **Integration of global context** — N21 requires the speech to address "how representation has evolved *over time*" — the temporal dimension is the global context embedded in content, not appended as a sentence.

### Proposal (M21)
**M21 task:** "You are applying to present a talk about digital communication tools at a youth conference. Create a proposal of the ideas you wish to present under the title, 'Using the power of social media to express identity and build relationships.'" (Global context: Identities and relationships)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Formal proposal structure** — introduction explaining what the proposal is for, sections covering the main ideas, a conclusion or summary of what the audience will gain.
- **Persuasive purpose** — the proposal is selling the idea of the talk. It must explain *why* the topic matters (the global context) and *what* the audience will get from attending.
- **Specificity of content** — vague proposals score lower. The mark scheme notes that top-band responses produce content "with precision."
- **Appropriate register** — semi-formal; professional but not impenetrable. Written to a conference organiser, not to a friend or to an academic journal.

### Letter (N23, M22)
**N23 task:** "Your school has decided to offer a Language and Literature course built entirely of student-recommended texts. Create a letter to your Language and Literature teacher recommending a text and explaining why you feel it should be included in the course." (Global context: Identities and relationships)

**M22 task:** Create a formal letter nominating a guest speaker for a multi-generational event. (Global context: Personal and cultural expression)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Letter conventions** — address, date, salutation (Dear [Name]), sign-off (Yours sincerely / Kind regards). Absence of these is a structural failure (Criterion B).
- **Formal but personal register** — the letter to a teacher (N23) requires respect and appropriate formality but also a personal voice and genuine argument.
- **Structured argument** — for N23: introduce the text, explain its themes, connect to the global context (identities and relationships), argue why it belongs in the course. The mark scheme rewards responses that "specifically address the prompt within the global context" for Criterion C.
- **Persuasive not informative** — the letter is making a case, not just describing. Top-band Criterion C: "perceptive… sophisticated understanding of impact on an audience."

### Blog post (N24)
**N24 task:** "Create a blog post in which you propose ways your school can become a fairer and more equitable place for all." (Global context: Fairness and development)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Blog conventions** — a title, informal/conversational register, direct address to readers, paragraphs that flow naturally without heavy subheadings (though one heading can work). The N24 mark scheme notes "structural devices (such as crafting the length of sentences and/or paragraphs)" as what the top Criterion B band achieves.
- **Engaging opening** — blogs start with a hook, not a formal introduction. An anecdote, a provocative question, or a surprising statistic.
- **Concrete proposals** — the task says "propose ways." Vague gestures toward fairness score lower. Specific, credible, school-level suggestions (e.g. accessibility of spaces, representation in the curriculum, fair allocation of resources) allow for deeper engagement with the global context.
- **Voice** — blog posts have a distinctive authorial voice. This is rewarded under Criterion D's "consistently purposeful register and style."

### Opinion piece / newspaper article (M25)
**M25 task:** "Create an opinion piece for your local newspaper in which you propose an innovation that will improve the quality of life in your community." (Global context: Scientific and technological innovation)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Opinion piece conventions** — a headline (strongly implied by the form), clear argumentative stance from the first paragraph, evidence and examples to support the claim, acknowledgement of counterarguments at the top band, a conclusion that restates or intensifies the position.
- **Specific innovation** — the top Criterion C band ("highly engaging response that reflects on the prompt, demonstrating excellent understanding of the global context") implies the innovation must be genuinely engaging and concrete, not generic.
- **Newspaper register** — accessible but not colloquial; authoritative but not academic. Aimed at a general community readership.

### Interview (M24)
**M24 task:** "As a writer for your school newspaper, create an interview with a newcomer to your school in which you explore their experiences adapting to a new place. Be sure to include both questions and answers in your response." (Global context: Orientation in space and time)

What the mark scheme rewards (Criterion B specifically):
- **Q&A format** — alternating questions (from the interviewer) and answers (from the interviewee). The M24 mark scheme uses "crafting the length of questions and answers" as a specific example of what structural sophistication looks like at the top bands.
- **The interviewer's questions matter as much as the answers** — well-crafted questions that build on previous answers, move from general to specific, and allow the global context (adapting to a new place, sense of time and space) to emerge naturally.
- **Register** — the interview voice is conversational but purposeful. The interviewer's language and the newcomer's language can be distinct.

### Video advertisement script (M23)
**M23 task:** "You have started a new student organisation that will build intercultural connections with young people in other parts of the world. Create the script for a video advertisement promoting your organisation." (Global context: Globalization and sustainability)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Script format** — stage directions, speaker labels, a distinction between spoken words and visual instructions (e.g. "CUT TO:", "[Voiceover:]", "[On screen:]"). These are structural elements that earn Criterion B marks.
- **Promotional purpose** — the script must sell the organisation. Enthusiasm, appeal to shared values, a call to action (join us / find out more).
- **Intercultural angle** — the global context (globalisation and sustainability) must appear in content, not just be referenced. Examples of cross-cultural connection or sustainability-linked intercultural work raise the Criterion C score.

### Podcast script (N20)
**N20 task:** Create the script for an episode of a teen podcast called "Plastic Planet" on the topic of plastic pollution. (Global context: Fairness and development implied)

What the mark scheme rewards:
- **Podcast conventions** — an opening jingle reference or intro, host address to listeners ("Hey everyone, welcome back to..."), a topic introduction, segments or interview extracts, a sign-off. Scripted but conversational.
- **Teen audience register** — accessible, energetic, sometimes humorous, but substantive.

## Mark scheme insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

**Criterion B — Organising**
- The top band (9–10) is reached when "structural devices enhance coherence and cohesion" — this means not just having the right sections but using structure purposefully. In a speech: varying sentence length to build pace. In a blog: an opening that echoes in the conclusion. In a proposal: sections that logically build the case.
- "Ideas building on each other" is the key phrase in every session's Criterion B descriptor. Each paragraph or section must connect to the next, not stand alone.
- Bullet points in *any* extended writing task cap Criterion B at 2 marks. This applies across all sessions and all text types.
- For the interview (M24), the mark scheme describes top-band Criterion B as "crafting the length of questions and answers" — shorter sharp questions leading into longer reflective answers is itself a structural device.

**Criterion C — Producing text**
- A response that makes "no link to the global context" scores 0 for Criterion C. This is the single most dangerous way to lose marks.
- "Demonstrating understanding of the global context" (middle bands, 6–8) means the global context is present in the content and argument. "Excellent understanding" (top band, 9–10) means the global context shapes the perspective and argument throughout.
- Stylistic choices matter: rhetorical questions, anaphora, vivid examples, concrete scenarios. These are listed in the mark scheme as part of what "linguistic and literary devices" means for non-literary writing.
- "Perceptive exploration of perspectives and ideas" (top band) means recognising complexity — for example, a speech about fairness that acknowledges competing definitions of fairness before arguing for one.

**Criterion D — Using language**
- Register is the primary discriminator between middle and upper bands. A speech that reads as an essay, or a blog that reads as a formal report, is mismatched in register and will not reach the top band regardless of how accurate the grammar is.
- The top band (9–10) requires: "consistently purposeful register and style" AND "high degree of accuracy." Both are needed.
- Vocabulary range is also tested. Repetitive or imprecise vocabulary caps at the 3–5 band.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Bullet points** in any response to Task 3: maximum 2 marks for Criterion B regardless of how good the content is. This is stated explicitly in every session's mark scheme.
- **No connection to the global context**: 0 marks for Criterion C. The global context is named in every Task 3 prompt and must appear in the response.
- **Register mismatch**: Writing a school assembly speech that uses academic language, or a letter that uses slang, scores 3–5 at best for Criterion D.
- **Missing form conventions**: A letter without a salutation, a speech without direct address, an interview without questions — these are structural failures penalised under Criterion B.
- **Vague or generic content**: "We should all work together to make the world a better place" without specific ideas or examples does not demonstrate insight (Criterion C).

## Common question types

**The invariant pattern:** Every Task 3 question names a text type, a specific audience and purpose, an approximate word count (~300–350 words), and a global context. The command term is always "Create."

**How the global context appears:**
- Sometimes embedded in the topic directly (N21: speech about how gender representation has evolved *over time* — global context is Orientation in space and time)
- Sometimes stated as a label on the paper alongside the prompt (M25: global context "Scientific and technological innovation"; task is to write an opinion piece proposing an innovation)
- Always assessed via Criterion C — the mark scheme checks explicitly whether the response demonstrates understanding of it

**Word count guidance:**
- Advisory word counts range from 300 to 350 words across sessions. The mark scheme states candidates should not be penalised for exceeding or falling short "unless the quality of the response is affected" — meaning too short that ideas are underdeveloped, or so long that the piece loses focus.
- 350 words is the most common advisory target (M21, N21, N22, N23, N24, M25). 300 words appears in M23 and M24.

**Audience specificity:**
- The prompt always specifies a real audience: "your local newspaper," "a school assembly," "your Language and Literature teacher," "a youth conference." Writing for a generic or imaginary audience, instead of the one named, is a register and purpose failure.

## Model approach

**Time allocation:** Task 3 carries 30 marks and has a recommended 35 minutes. Spend the time.

### Step-by-step formula

**1. Read the prompt (1 minute)**
Identify: (a) text type, (b) audience, (c) purpose, (d) global context. Write these four things down mentally before writing a single word.

**2. Plan the structure (3–4 minutes)**
Sketch 4–6 sections appropriate to the form. For a speech: hook → context → argument 1 → argument 2 → counterargument/complication → call to action. For a blog: hook anecdote → problem statement → 2–3 concrete proposals → closing appeal to reader. For a letter: salutation → purpose of writing → main argument (2–3 paragraphs, each advancing the case) → conclusion → sign-off. For an interview: introductory framing sentence (contextualise the interviewee) → 5–6 Q&A exchanges that build toward the global context theme → closing remark.

**3. Embed the global context in paragraph 1 or 2 (not just at the end)**
If the global context is Fairness and development, the argument must be shaped by a concept of fairness from the beginning. Bolting it on at the end does not count as "demonstrating understanding."

**4. Use at least 2–3 form-specific devices**
For speeches: direct address ("you," "we"), rhetorical question, tricolon, call to action. For letters: formal salutation, stated purpose in paragraph 1, formal sign-off. For blog posts: informal conversational voice, short punchy sentences alongside longer explanatory ones, reader engagement ("think about it," "sound familiar?"). For interviews: clear Q: and A: labels, varied question lengths.

**5. Vary sentence structure deliberately**
Short sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences to develop complexity. Vary this across the piece. The top Criterion B band specifically names "crafting the length of sentences and/or paragraphs" as evidence of sophistication.

**6. Write ~300–350 words and stop**
Overlong responses lose focus and risk losing register consistency. Underlong responses fail to develop ideas adequately.

### Showing register awareness
Register is not the same as formality. It is matching the *style* to the *purpose and audience*.
- A speech to a school assembly can be warm and personal while still being organised and purposeful.
- A newspaper opinion piece can use vivid language and first person ("I believe...") while still making a coherent argument.
- A proposal to a conference committee should be professional and specific, but not dry.

Register markers to use deliberately:
- Speech: second person ("you," "we"), rhetorical question, direct appeal ("I urge you today..."), call to action as a final sentence.
- Blog post: first person, conversational tone, short punchy opener, reader-directed questions.
- Letter: formal salutation, stated purpose, paragraphs with clear topic sentences, formal sign-off.
- Opinion piece: clear stated position in paragraph 1, "I argue / I believe / In my view," evidence or examples, acknowledgement of the other side before rebutting it.

### Embedding the global context naturally
The global context is not a label to paste in. It is the lens through which the whole piece is written.

**Example — M25 (Scientific and technological innovation, opinion piece about a community innovation):**
Weak: "This innovation uses technology to improve life. Scientific and technological innovation is important."
Strong: The entire piece is structured around what makes an innovation genuinely beneficial — it engages with what "innovation" means (not just new, but purposefully designed), which is the Criterion C equivalent of demonstrating understanding of the global context.

**Example — N24 (Fairness and development, blog post about school equity):**
Weak: "Fairness is important at school."
Strong: The proposals are grounded in a specific understanding of what fairness means — not just equal treatment but equitable outcomes — which is the substance of the Fairness and development global context.

## Revision priorities

1. **Learn the conventions of speech, letter, and proposal cold.** These three forms account for 6 of 14 past sessions. If you know how to open, develop, and close all three in approximately 350 words, you are prepared for the most likely scenarios.

2. **Know all six global contexts and what each one implies for content.** The Criterion C mark scheme checks for understanding of the global context at every band above 2 marks. A response that does not engage with it scores 1–2 out of 10 for Criterion C.

3. **Never use bullet points in Task 3.** They cap Criterion B at 2/10. This is a free 8 marks to protect.

4. **Practice register matching.** Read examples of real speeches, real newspaper opinion pieces, and real formal letters. The distinction between what each sounds like is the core of Criterion D discrimination between bands 6–8 and 9–10.

5. **Treat structure as argument.** The top Criterion B band rewards structure that "enhances coherence and cohesion" — meaning the shape of the piece reinforces the message. A speech that builds to a strong call to action, a letter that withholds its recommendation until a key moment, a blog post whose conclusion echoes its opening anecdote — these are structural choices that score marks.

6. **Session-specific text types to practise:** blog post (N24), interview (M24), video advertisement script (M23), podcast script (N20), product review (N19). These are less intuitive than speeches and letters but have appeared in recent sessions.

7. **Time yourself.** 35 minutes for 350 words in an unfamiliar form under exam conditions is a skill that requires practice. Students who run out of time often leave Criterion B evidence (closing sections, sign-offs, calls to action) unwritten.
