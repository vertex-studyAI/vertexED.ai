# Acids, Bases and Salts

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Acids and Bases — Arrhenius Definitions

An **acid** is a substance that releases H⁺ ions (or H₃O⁺ ions) when dissolved in water. An acid donates a proton to water.

A **base** is a substance that releases OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution, or accepts a proton. An **alkali** is a soluble base — so all alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

| Property | Acid | Base / Alkali |
|----------|------|---------------|
| Ions in solution | H⁺ (or H₃O⁺) | OH⁻ |
| pH | Below 7 | Above 7 |
| Examples | HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CH₃COOH | NaOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₃ |
| Reaction with metals | Acid + metal → salt + H₂ | — |
| Reaction with carbonates | Acid + carbonate → salt + H₂O + CO₂ | — |

**Strong vs weak acids** — the key distinction is the degree of dissociation in water:

| Type | Dissociation | Arrow | Examples |
|------|-------------|-------|---------|
| Strong acid | Fully dissociates | → | HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃ |
| Weak acid | Partially dissociates (equilibrium) | ⇌ | CH₃COOH, HCOOH, H₂CO₃ |

This means a weak acid dissociation equation **must** use ⇌, not →. For example:

> HCOOH(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + HCOO⁻(aq)
> CH₃COOH(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq)

**M18 Q1d (4 marks):** Write a balanced equation for HCOOH dissociating in water including state symbols. Mark scheme awards: reactants correct [1], products correct [1], all state symbols correct [1], equilibrium arrow ⇌ correct [1]. Writing → instead of ⇌ loses the fourth mark.

**M25 Q6a (1 mark):** Students chose ethanoic acid (vinegar) over hydrochloric acid to remove limescale. Mark scheme: ethanoic acid is a weak acid [1] — it is safer and easier to dispose of. Accept "HCl is a strong acid" (ORA).

> **Do NOT** confuse **strong** (degree of dissociation) with **concentrated** (amount per volume). A concentrated weak acid is still weak. This distinction is tested directly in mark schemes.

### pH Scale

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14 and measures H⁺ ion concentration. **Lower pH = more acidic; higher pH = more alkaline.**

| pH range | Classification | Common examples |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| 0–3 | Strongly acidic | Battery acid (pH 0), stomach acid (pH 1–2), lemon juice (pH 2–3) |
| 4–6 | Weakly acidic | Vinegar (pH ~3), apple cider vinegar (pH ~5), acid rain (pH < 5.6) |
| 7 | Neutral | Pure water |
| 8–10 | Weakly alkaline | Baking soda solution, seawater |
| 11–14 | Strongly alkaline | NaOH solution, bleach |

Each whole-number decrease in pH represents a tenfold increase in H⁺ concentration. pH 2 is 10× more acidic than pH 3, and 100× more acidic than pH 4.

**M24 Q2a (1 mark):** Select the pH of pure water when universal indicator is added. Answer: pH 7. Simple recall — do not say "pH 6" due to dissolved CO₂; the question specifies pure water.

**M24 Q2f (1 mark):** State the pH of a solution of CaSO₄. Answer: pH 7 — it is a neutral salt formed from a strong acid (H₂SO₄) and a base.

**M22 Q2e (1 mark):** Select the most acidic chemical from a list of HF solutions at pH 3.5, 3.2, 4.8 and H₂O at pH 7. Answer: HF at pH 3.2 — the lowest pH = most acidic. Do not confuse "best results at pH 3.5" with "most acidic."

> **Do NOT** say that hard water is acidic. Hard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions and has a pH **above 7** — it is alkaline. M25 Q4f directly tests this: "Hard water is NOT acidic; pH is above 7."

### Neutralisation Reactions

Neutralisation is the reaction between an acid and a base to form a salt and water:

> **acid + base → salt + water**
> **acid + metal carbonate → salt + water + CO₂**
> **acid + metal → salt + H₂ (not neutralisation — a displacement reaction)**

In ionic terms: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O

**Salt naming rule:** The first word comes from the cation (metal or ammonium, from the base). The second word comes from the acid anion:

| Acid | Anion name | Example salt |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Hydrochloric acid (HCl) | chloride | NaCl (sodium chloride) |
| Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) | sulfate | CaSO₄ (calcium sulfate) |
| Nitric acid (HNO₃) | nitrate | Sr(NO₃)₂ (strontium nitrate) |
| Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) | ethanoate | CH₃COONa (sodium ethanoate) |
| Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) | carbonate | CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate) |

**Worked example — M22 Q2h (4 marks):** Balance HF + Ca(OH)₂ → CaF₂ + H₂O.
Balanced: **2HF + Ca(OH)₂ → CaF₂ + 2H₂O**. Award: 1 mark per correct coefficient. Penalty: changing formulae (e.g. writing CaF instead of CaF₂) — coefficients only can change.

**N19 Q3b (3 marks):** Write balanced equation with state symbols for SrCO₃ + HNO₃:
> SrCO₃(s) + 2HNO₃(aq) → Sr(NO₃)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
Mark scheme: balanced [1], correct products [1], state symbols [1]. Accept = instead of →. Ignore one subscript error.

**M16 Q2d (4 marks):** Neutralisation of CH₃COOH with NaOH:
> CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
Salt = sodium ethanoate [2]; base = NaOH [1]; base removes H⁺ / donates OH⁻ [1].

**M24 Q2b (2 marks):** Outline how to grow blue hydrangeas in soil with pH = 6. Answer: decrease the pH [1]; by adding sulfuric acid / any acid [1]. Second mark only awarded if first is awarded.

> **Do NOT** forget CO₂ when acid reacts with a carbonate. "Acid + carbonate → salt + water" is incomplete — all three products (salt, water, CO₂) are required. Stating only the salt loses marks.

### Indicators

Indicators are substances that change colour at different pH values. They are used to identify whether a solution is acidic, neutral, or alkaline, or to find the endpoint of a neutralisation.

| Indicator | Acidic colour | Neutral colour | Alkaline colour | Use |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|-----|
| Litmus | Red | Purple | Blue | Rough acid/base test |
| Universal indicator | Red/orange/yellow | Green | Blue/purple | Approximate pH (full range) |
| Phenolphthalein | Colourless | Colourless | Pink/magenta | Titration endpoint |
| Cherry juice (natural) | Red | Purple | Green | Range only — not exact |

**Key distinction:** Universal indicator gives an approximate pH across the full range (0–14). A single indicator (litmus, phenolphthalein) only shows the endpoint of a reaction — it does not give a pH number. Natural indicators (cherry juice) give a colour range — they cannot identify an exact pH value.

**M21 Q2d (2 marks):** Two indicators are shown on a chart. Indicator A: Red at pH 0–3, Yellow at pH 3–14. Indicator B: Yellow at pH 0–8, Blue at pH 8–14. A CO₂ solution turns Indicator A yellow and Indicator B yellow. Determine the pH range: pH 3 to 8 (both yellow = overlap of the two ranges). Mark scheme: top of range = 7 [1]; lower bound = 3 to 4.5 range [1]. Two marks for correct interval.

**N18 Q3g (4 marks):** Use cherry juice pH chart to assign colours:
- pH 3.2 → Red [1]; pH 9.5 → Green [1]; pH 7.0 → Purple [1]; pH 3.6 → Red [1]
Must match the chart provided in the question — read it carefully.

**N18 Q3h (1 mark):** State limitation of using cherry juice as indicator.
Mark scheme: "Gives a range of pH, not exact values." Accept: "cannot identify exact pH."

**M19 Q2c (1 mark):** Universal indicator turned red — what does this indicate?
Answer: solution is acidic / has a low pH. Do not say "it is neutral" because red is at the low end of the scale.

> **Do NOT** say a natural indicator gives an exact pH — it only gives a range. This is the standard limitation answer for cherry juice (N18 Q3h) and similar questions.

### Titration

Titration is a technique used to determine the exact volume of one solution (usually in a burette) needed to completely neutralise a known volume of another solution. The endpoint is found using an indicator or pH probe.

**What the question tests:**
1. Reading the endpoint volume from a pH–volume graph (S-curve) — identify the inflection point (steepest part of the curve)
2. Calculating amount of substance from titration data using n = c × V
3. Evaluating the precision of a titration (pH probe vs indicator)

**M17 Q5a (2 marks):** Determine volume of NaOH at endpoint from a pH/volume graph.
Read the inflection point: 25.00 cm³. Mark scheme: read two values either side of inflection and average [2].

**N22 Q3b (3 marks):** Evaluate whether pH probe or universal indicator paper gives more valid data.
- pH probe gives numerical/quantitative data [1]
- Indicator paper gives a colour range / qualitative data [1]
- Therefore pH probe gives more valid data [1] — third mark only awarded if first two are awarded.

> **Do NOT** award the conclusion mark alone. Evaluations comparing methods must state both sides before the conclusion. This is a common dependency condition in mark schemes.

### Amphoteric Substances

An **amphoteric** substance can act as either an acid or a base depending on what it reacts with. It can both donate and accept H⁺ ions.

Key amphoteric substances:
- **NaHCO₃** (sodium hydrogen carbonate / bicarbonate of soda) — reacts with both acids and bases
- **Water (H₂O)** — can act as both H⁺ donor and H⁺ acceptor
- **Al₂O₃** (aluminium oxide) — reacts with both acids and alkalis
- **HCO₃⁻** ion — can gain or lose a proton

**Why NaHCO₃ is useful in cooking and medicine:**
- With sour milk (lactic acid): NaHCO₃ acts as a **base** — it accepts H⁺ from the acid, neutralising the sour taste and releasing CO₂ (which makes bread rise)
- With a strong base: NaHCO₃ acts as a **weak acid** — donates H⁺

**M16 Q10b (1 mark):** Suggest why NaHCO₃ helps with sour milk (lactic acid context).
Answer: NaHCO₃ behaves as a base; removes H⁺ ions / neutralises the acid [1].

**M16 Q10c (4 marks):** Effect of adding acid on the equilibrium HCO₃⁻ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ + OH⁻:
- Adding acid increases H⁺ [1]
- H⁺ reacts with OH⁻ [1]
- Equilibrium shifts right (Le Chatelier's principle) [1]
- H₂CO₃ increases / OH⁻ decreases [1]

> **Do NOT** say amphoteric means "neutral." It means the substance can react as either an acid or a base — its actual role depends on the reaction partner.

### Acid Rain

Acid rain is precipitation with a pH below 5.6 caused by dissolved acidic gases released by human activity (burning fossil fuels, vehicle emissions, industrial processes).

**Sources of acidic gases:**

| Gas | Source | Acid formed in atmosphere |
|-----|--------|--------------------------|
| SO₂ (sulfur dioxide) | Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil) | H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) |
| NOₓ (nitrogen oxides) | Vehicle engines, high-temperature combustion | HNO₃ (nitric acid) |
| HCl (hydrogen chloride) | Industrial processes, volcanic activity | HCl dissolves directly |
| H₂S (hydrogen sulfide) | Volcanic gases | H₂SO₄ (after oxidation) |

**Effects of acid rain:**

| Effect | Chemical explanation |
|--------|---------------------|
| Damages limestone buildings and marble statues | CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂ |
| Acidifies lakes and rivers, kills aquatic life | H⁺ concentration rises; pH drops below survival range for fish |
| Damages forests | H⁺ ions leach minerals (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) from soil; nutrient deficiency in trees |
| Corrodes metals | Acid reacts with metal structures, bridges, pipes |

**Neutralisation of acid rain:** Basic substances (metal oxides, carbonates, hydroxides) neutralise acidic lakes and soils. For example, spreading CaCO₃ (limestone) on acidic lakes raises pH.

**N16 Q10b (3 marks):** How does iron oxide decrease acidity in lakes?
- Iron oxide is basic [1]
- Reacts with H⁺ ions [1]
- Neutralisation occurs / OH⁻ / water formed [1]

**M16 Q3a (8 marks, Criterion B):** Explain problems associated with acid rain.
Higher markband answers require: **multiple named effects** (aquatic life, buildings/limestone, forests) **with chemical explanation** for each. A vague "it harms the environment" scores only band 1.

**N20 Q2a (2 marks):** Suggest why volcanic lakes have pH ≈ 0.1.
- Volcanic gases (HCl, SO₂, H₂S) dissolve in lake water [1]
- Form acids / release H⁺ ions [1]

**N24 Q4 (Criterion B):** Students model acid rain using 1 mol dm⁻³ HCl on roofing materials (asphalt, clay, rubber, steel, slate). The IV is the type of roofing material; the DV is the rate of reaction with acid / mass change / pH change. Mark scheme requires a safety note explicitly linked to the acidity of the HCl.

> **Do NOT** say only "it is pollution" for acid rain questions. Mark schemes require you to name the gas (SO₂, NOₓ), the acid it forms, and the specific chemical or ecological effect. All three are required for the higher markbands.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Weak acid equations:** MUST use ⇌ not →. Using → for weak acid dissociation loses 1 mark (tested in M18 Q1d and multiple sessions).
- **State symbols:** Required when the question explicitly asks for them. HCOOH(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + HCOO⁻(aq) — all four symbols needed.
- **Carbonate reactions:** Must state all three products: salt + water + CO₂. Omitting any one loses marks.
- **Balancing equations:** Only coefficients may change. Changing a formula (e.g. CaF → CaF₂) is penalised.
- **Hard water is alkaline:** pH > 7. Stating hard water is acidic is a direct mark-losing error (M25 Q4f).
- **Natural indicators:** The standard limitation answer is "gives a range of pH, not exact values." Any phrasing to this effect is accepted; "it is not accurate" alone is not.
- **Acid rain answers:** Must name the specific gas (SO₂ or NOₓ), the acid formed, and the specific effect (aquatic life, buildings, forests). "It harms the environment" scores only band 1.
- **CON rule:** A correct answer followed by a contradictory statement scores 0. Example: "CaSO₄ is insoluble so it stays in solution" = 0 marks.
- **Solubility table:** When explaining selective precipitation (hard water), must reference the solubility table provided — do not just state "insoluble" without citing the data.
- **Strong vs concentrated:** Mark schemes penalise using these interchangeably. Strong = fully dissociates. Concentrated = high moles per litre.
- **Two-mark evaluation questions:** In comparisons (e.g. pH probe vs indicator), each side must be stated before the conclusion. The conclusion mark is conditional on both prior marks (N22 Q3b).
- **Indicator questions using a chart:** Always read the chart given in the question. Do not apply memorised colour associations if the question provides its own colour key (M21 Q2d, N18 Q3g).

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing → for weak acid dissociation | Weak acids use ⇌ — they are in equilibrium, not fully dissociated |
| Forgetting CO₂ in acid + carbonate reactions | Products = salt + water + CO₂ — all three required |
| Saying hard water is acidic | Hard water contains Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ ions; pH > 7; it is alkaline |
| "Indicator gives exact pH" for cherry juice | Cherry juice (natural indicator) gives a range, not an exact pH |
| Using → for all equations when state symbols needed | Check whether the acid is weak (⇌) or strong (→) and include all state symbols |
| Saying "strong" when meaning "concentrated" | Strong = fully dissociates; concentrated = high molarity. A concentrated weak acid is still weak |
| Acid rain answer: "it harms animals" | Must name gas (SO₂/NOₓ), acid formed (H₂SO₄/HNO₃), and specific effect (acidifies lakes → fish die) |
| Stating only the salt when balancing neutralisation with carbonate | Must include water AND CO₂ as products |
| Changing chemical formulae when balancing | Only coefficients change — formulae are fixed |
| Contradicting a correct answer (CON rule) | Stop after giving the correct answer; do not add a statement that cancels it |
| Saying amphoteric means neutral | Amphoteric means it can act as acid OR base — its pH depends on the reaction context |
| Concluding "pH probe is better" without comparing both methods | State quantitative vs qualitative for both, then draw the conclusion — otherwise the conclusion mark is not awarded |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise all four neutralisation product patterns (acid + base; acid + carbonate; acid + metal; acid + metal oxide) — these appear in almost every session.
2. Write weak acid dissociation equations from memory with ⇌ and all four state symbols — this is a reliable 4-mark question (M18 Q1d is the model).
3. Know the pH scale cold: what each range means, common substances at each level, and that pure water = pH 7.
4. Learn all four indicators (litmus, universal, phenolphthalein, cherry juice) and their colours — at least one indicator question appears per session. For chart-based questions, always use the chart given.
5. Practise salt naming: given acid + base, determine the salt name and formula before reading the answer choices.
6. For acid rain (Criterion B/D): always structure the answer as — gas source → acid formed → specific environmental effect. Aquatic life, limestone buildings, and forests are the three core effects.
7. Know that hard water is alkaline (pH > 7), not acidic — this is directly tested (M25 Q4f) and is a common misconception.
8. For titration graph questions: locate the inflection point (steepest part of the S-curve); do not read from the top or bottom of the curve.
9. Distinguish strong/weak from concentrated/dilute — these are tested separately and the mark scheme will not accept them interchangeably (M25 Q6a).
10. For two-method evaluation questions: always state one feature of each method, then the conclusion. The conclusion mark is conditional.
