# Experimental Design (Criterion B)

> **MOST IMPORTANT TOPIC.** Criterion B appears in every single session (M16–N24) and carries 11–19 marks on the major design question alone, plus a further 3–8 marks from standalone variables, hypothesis, and research question questions. This page covers everything needed to score the top markband on every strand.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

Criterion B assesses whether you can design a valid scientific investigation. Every Criterion B question gives you a chemistry context (a substance, a reaction, a consumer product) and asks you to plan an experiment. The examiner is NOT checking your content knowledge — they are checking whether your experimental design is scientifically valid.

There are always five independent strands marked:
1. **Variables** — IV, DV, and at least 2 CVs each with HOW they are controlled
2. **Equipment** — specific equipment named with size/precision
3. **Method** — numbered steps, fully replicable
4. **Sufficient data** — 5 IV levels × 3 repeats → calculate mean
5. **Safety** — specific hazard + specific precaution

---

### Research Question

A research question must be a single, testable, focused question that names both the independent variable (IV) and the dependent variable (DV) explicitly. The examiner checks three things: (1) is the IV named specifically? (2) is the DV named specifically and measurably? (3) does the question imply a causal relationship that could be tested?

**Accepted format:** "How does [specific IV] affect [measurable DV with unit]?"

| Acceptable | Not acceptable |
|-----------|----------------|
| How does the type of de-icing salt (NaCl, CaCl₂, KCl, MgCl₂, SrCl₂) affect the mass of ice melted (g) in 5 minutes? | Does salt melt ice? |
| How does the concentration of hydrochloric acid (mol dm⁻³) affect the volume of CO₂ produced (cm³) per minute? | How does acid concentration affect rate? |
| How does the type of catalyst affect the volume of O₂ produced (cm³) from H₂O₂ decomposition in 5 minutes? | What happens when you add a catalyst? |
| How does the pH of HCl solution affect the force (N) required to break a MY-P sample? | Why does pH cook MY-P? |

> Do NOT write "Why does..." — this is not a testable research question. Do NOT write "Does acid affect marble?" — "affect" must link to a specific, measurable DV. Do NOT leave "rate" unspecified — write "rate of CO₂ production (cm³/min)".

**N23 Q6e (1 mark):** "Suggest a research question for the student's investigation into what pH cooks MY-P." → *How does the pH of the HCl solution affect the force required to break a MY-P sample?* — Both IV (pH) and DV (force/N) named, causal link implied. Award 1 mark.

### Hypothesis

The hypothesis must follow the **If / Then / Because** structure. Every component is required:
- **If** [the IV changes in a specific direction or a specific value is used]
- **Then** [the DV will change in a specific direction]
- **Because** [a named chemical mechanism explains why]

The "Because" clause must contain real chemistry — collision theory, reactivity series, colligative properties, solubility rules, enzyme denaturation, etc. A vague "because it is more reactive" scores 0. From M17 onwards, **mark 1 (If/Then) is conditional on mark 2 (Because)** — no Because means no marks at all. The direction of change must be stated explicitly ("increases", "decreases" — not "will be affected").

| Acceptable | Not acceptable |
|-----------|----------------|
| If the concentration of HCl increases, then the volume of CO₂ produced per minute will increase, because a higher concentration means more H⁺ ions per unit volume, increasing the frequency of successful collisions between H⁺ and CaCO₃ particles. | If I add more acid, the reaction will be faster. (no "because") |
| If calcium chloride is used instead of sodium chloride, then the mass of ice melted will be greater, because CaCl₂ dissociates into three ions (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻) per formula unit, producing a greater depression of freezing point (colligative property). | Because it is a better de-icer. (no chemistry) |
| If temperature increases, then more solute will dissolve, because increased kinetic energy of solvent molecules allows them to overcome the intermolecular forces holding the solute lattice together more effectively. | The boiling point will rise. (no "if", no "because") |

> Do NOT write a prediction without a "Because" clause — the entire hypothesis scores 0. Do NOT say "because it is more reactive" — name the mechanism: ionisation energy, electron transfer, collision frequency, etc. "Lowers activation energy" alone is insufficient — say how that changes the proportion of particles with sufficient energy.

**M22 Q4b (3 marks):** "Formulate a hypothesis for the investigation of H₂O₂ decomposition with different catalysts." → Mark scheme:
1. If [named catalyst] is used, then the volume of O₂ produced per minute will be greatest, [1]
2. because [named catalyst] lowers the activation energy of the decomposition reaction more effectively, [1]
3. increasing the frequency of successful collisions between H₂O₂ molecules and the catalyst surface. [1]

### Independent Variable

The IV is what you deliberately change. It must be named specifically, stated with a range of at least **5 distinct values or 5 distinct levels**, and described with the method used to set each value.

| Acceptable | Not acceptable |
|-----------|----------------|
| Type of salt: NaCl, KCl, CaCl₂, MgCl₂, SrCl₂ | Type of salt (no values listed) |
| Concentration of HCl: 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5 mol dm⁻³ | Different concentrations of HCl |
| pH of HCl solution: pH 2.8, 3.0, 3.2, 3.4, 3.6 | Various pH values |
| Type of catalyst: MnO₂, Fe, Cu, Pt, no catalyst | Catalyst type |

> Do NOT say "a range of concentrations" — list the 5 specific values. Do NOT say "different salts" — list all five by name. A minimum of 5 IV levels is required for maximum marks on the Sufficient Data strand. If the paper already provides exactly 5 options (5 salts, 5 metals), list all five — they count as your 5 IV levels.

**M23 Q5b / Q6b (de-icing investigation):** IV = type of salt. Full mark answer lists all five by chemical formula: NaCl, KCl, CaCl₂, MgCl₂, SrCl₂. Simply writing "five different salts" scores 0 on the IV strand.

### Dependent Variable

The DV is what you measure as a result of changing the IV. It must be measurable (specific quantity), include the unit, and name the instrument used to measure it.

| Acceptable | Not acceptable |
|-----------|----------------|
| Volume of CO₂ produced (cm³), measured with a gas syringe | Amount of gas |
| Mass of ice melted (g), measured with an electronic balance (±0.01 g) | How much ice melts |
| Time for complete dissolution of CaCO₃ (s), measured with a stopwatch | Speed of reaction |
| Force required to break MY-P sample (N), measured with a force meter | How cooked the sample is |
| Temperature change (°C), measured with a thermometer (±0.1 °C) | Rate of reaction |
| Electrical conductivity (µS/cm), measured with a conductivity meter | How pure the water is |

> Do NOT write "rate" alone as the DV — this is rejected in every session. Do NOT write "amount" — specify volume (cm³), mass (g), or time (s). Do NOT write "colour change" alone — say "measured using a colorimeter (absorbance units)" or "compared to a colour chart."

**Specific DV phrasings accepted by mark schemes:**

| Investigation type | Accepted DV phrasing |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Gas production | Volume of gas (cm³) per unit time, collected in gas syringe |
| Mass loss (acid + solid) | Mass change (g) per unit time, measured on electronic balance |
| Temperature change | Temperature change (°C), measured with thermometer or probe |
| Melting / dissolution time | Time for complete dissolution/melting (s), measured with stopwatch |
| pH change | pH measured with pH meter or universal indicator compared to chart |
| Force / texture | Force required to break sample (N), measured with force meter |

**N18 mark scheme** explicitly rejects "speed" as DV — must say "time (s)" or "volume/time." **M22 mark scheme** rejects "number of bubbles" for O₂ volume — must specify cm³ collected in gas syringe.

### Controlled Variables

Control variables are factors that could affect the DV if they changed, so they must be kept constant. A complete CV answer requires: (1) the variable named specifically, (2) the method to keep it constant, (3) ideally the specific value it is kept at. Minimum **2 CVs** must be stated with HOW they are controlled.

| CV | Method to control (acceptable) | Weak version (not awarded) |
|----|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Volume of HCl solution | Use a measuring cylinder to add exactly 25 cm³ each time | "Same volume of acid" |
| Concentration of HCl | Use the same stock solution (1.0 mol dm⁻³) for all trials; verify with pH meter | "Keep acid concentration constant" |
| Mass of solid reactant | Weigh each sample on an electronic balance to 5.0 g (±0.01 g) before each trial | "Same amount of marble" |
| Temperature of solution | Place all beakers in a water bath set to 25 °C throughout | "Keep temperature the same" |
| Surface area of solid | Use uniform chip sizes, selected by passing through a sieve with 2 mm mesh | "Same chip size" |
| Mass of salt / de-icer | Weigh 5.0 g of each salt on electronic balance before adding | "Same amount of salt" |
| Exposure time | Time each trial for exactly 5 minutes using a stopwatch | "Same time" |

> Do NOT write "keep equipment the same" — this is not a variable. Do NOT write "same conditions" — name the specific variable. A CV without a HOW scores 0 in most sessions. Do NOT list the IV as a CV — if marble chip size is the IV, do not also write "chip size" as a CV (N22 mark scheme).

**N22 Q4a (3 marks) — marble chip size investigation:** CVs awarded marks: volume and concentration of acid (both must be stated with specific values and HOW); temperature (must say "water bath at 25 °C"); total mass of marble. Chip size = the IV; listing it as a CV scores 0.

### Method

A full-mark method must allow complete replication by a student who has never seen the experiment. Include all of the following:

1. **Equipment list** — with sizes and concentrations (e.g., "250 cm³ beaker", "1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl", "gas syringe (100 cm³)", "electronic balance ±0.01 g")
2. **Step-by-step procedure** — numbered steps, not bullet points
3. **How the IV is set** — e.g., "prepare solutions at 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5 mol dm⁻³ using the provided HCl solutions"
4. **How and when the DV is measured** — e.g., "record the mass of the beaker every 30 seconds for 5 minutes", "start the stopwatch when the acid is added"
5. **How CVs are controlled within the steps** — integrated into the procedure, not listed separately
6. **Evidence of sufficient data** — 5 IV levels × 3 repeats → calculate mean

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No method, or completely irrelevant |
| 1 | Steps present but key details missing; would not produce valid data |
| 2 | Partial method; unclear when/how DV is measured; would produce some data |
| 3 | Workable method; could be followed; will generate relevant data; DV measurement stated |
| 4 | Complete, fully replicable method; when/how DV is measured explicitly stated; CVs controlled within steps |

**Key phrases that appear in Band 3–4 mark scheme answers:**
- "Add X cm³ of Y mol dm⁻³ HCl to a conical flask"
- "Start the stopwatch when the first bubble appears / when the reactant is added"
- "Record the mass every 30 seconds for 5 minutes"
- "Rinse all glassware between trials with distilled water"
- "Allow the apparatus to return to room temperature between trials"
- "Repeat steps [n]–[n] with the next salt/concentration"

**M23 Q6b (16 marks) — Band 4 method for de-icing investigation:**
> 1. Measure 50 g of crushed ice using an electronic balance and place in a 250 cm³ beaker.
> 2. Measure 5.0 g of NaCl on the balance and distribute evenly over the ice surface.
> 3. Start the stopwatch immediately.
> 4. After exactly 5 minutes, pour the liquid through filter paper into a measuring cylinder. Record the volume (cm³) of melt water.
> 5. Repeat steps 1–4 two more times using fresh 50 g ice samples (3 trials total for NaCl).
> 6. Repeat the entire procedure for KCl, CaCl₂, MgCl₂, and SrCl₂.
> 7. Keep room temperature at 20 °C throughout (record and note any variation).
> 8. Calculate the mean volume of melt water for each salt.

### Safety

Safety marks require a specific hazard linked to this investigation and a specific precaution linked to that hazard. Generic statements are rejected.

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No safety mentioned, or completely generic ("be careful", "wear goggles always") |
| 1 | A specific safety hazard relevant to this experiment is named |
| 2 | Hazard named AND specific precaution linked to that hazard stated |
| 3 | (Where 3 marks available) Second distinct hazard + precaution, or an environmental consideration added |

| Hazard | Specific precaution (acceptable) | Generic version (not awarded) |
|--------|----------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Hydrochloric acid is corrosive | Wear nitrile gloves and safety goggles; work near running water for emergency rinsing; neutralise spills with NaHCO₃ | "Wear goggles" |
| Strontium chloride is toxic (GHS skull symbol) | Avoid ingestion; wear gloves; do not handle near food; wash hands after use | "Be careful with chemicals" |
| Hot water bath / boiling liquids | Wear insulated gloves when handling containers from water bath above 60 °C; risk of scalding | "Be careful with hot things" |
| Hydrogen peroxide is an oxidiser | Wear goggles and gloves; work in a fume hood as O₂ released may accelerate combustion of nearby materials | "Handle with care" |
| Broken glassware | Dispose of broken glass in a dedicated sharps bin, not general waste; wear gloves when handling fragments | "Be careful with glass" |
| Liquid nitrogen (−196 °C) | Use cryogenic gloves and face shield; never seal in a closed container as pressure build-up causes explosion risk | "It is very cold" |

> Do NOT write "wear goggles" without linking it to the specific hazard in this experiment. Do NOT write "be careful" — this scores 0. Always say WHAT the hazard is AND WHAT you will do to prevent harm.

**M25 mark scheme:** Environmental consideration (safe disposal of ethanoic acid — neutralise before disposal) awarded mark 3 on the safety strand. When a 3-mark safety strand appears, always look for an environmental or disposal consideration.

### Markband Rubric Table — All 5 Strands

The major design question is marked across 5 independent strands. Total marks per session vary (15–19) but the descriptors are consistent across all sessions.

**Strand 1: Variables (max 4 marks)**

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No variables identified, or completely irrelevant |
| 1 | IV or DV identified (not both); or both identified but one is incorrect |
| 2 | IV and DV both correctly identified |
| 3 | IV + DV + at least one CV (CV must state HOW it is controlled) |
| 4 | IV + DV + at least two CVs (both with HOW controlled, ideally with specific value) |

**Strand 2: Equipment (max 4 marks, some sessions max 2)**

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No equipment listed, or completely irrelevant |
| 1 | Some relevant equipment (generic or partial) |
| 2 | Equipment to measure DV OR control a CV (not both) |
| 3 | Equipment to measure DV AND control at least one CV |
| 4 | Specific, appropriate equipment (with size/precision) to measure DV and control CVs |

**Strand 3: Method (max 4 marks)**

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No method, or completely irrelevant |
| 1 | Steps present but key details missing; would not produce valid data |
| 2 | Partial method; unclear when/how DV is measured; would produce some data |
| 3 | Workable method; could be followed; will produce relevant data; DV measurement stated |
| 4 | Complete, fully replicable method; when/how DV is measured explicitly stated; CVs controlled within steps |

**Strand 4: Sufficient Data (max 4 marks)**

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No reference to data collection scope |
| 1 | Reference to different IV values OR one repeat trial mentioned |
| 2 | All IV values tested OR three repeat trials (not both) |
| 3 | All IV values tested AND three repeat trials |
| 4 | All IV values tested AND three repeat trials AND plan to calculate mean stated |

**Strand 5: Safety / Ethical / Environmental (max 2–3 marks)**

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No safety, or completely generic |
| 1 | A specific safety hazard relevant to this experiment named |
| 2 | Hazard named AND specific precaution linked to it |
| 3 | (Where 3 marks available) Second distinct hazard + precaution, or an environmental/disposal consideration added |

> Calculating mean is the single most commonly dropped mark. Almost every student reaches Band 3 (all IVs × 3 repeats) but forgets to write "calculate the mean." Add this phrase to every design answer.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **"Amount" as DV is rejected in every session without exception.** Always specify volume (cm³), mass (g), moles, or time (s).
- **"Rate" as DV is rejected without qualification.** Must say "rate of CO₂ production (cm³/min)" or "rate of mass loss (g/min)".
- **CVs without HOW are often awarded 0.** "Temperature kept constant" scores partial or 0 in many sessions — must say "at 25 °C using a water bath".
- **The mean is the single most commonly missed mark in Sufficient Data.** Students consistently stop at "5 IV values × 3 repeats" without writing "calculate the mean."
- **Hypothesis mark 1 is conditional on mark 2 in many sessions from M17 onwards.** A prediction without a chemical reason scores 0 for both marks.
- **Safety must be experiment-specific.** N23 and N24 mark schemes explicitly note that "wear goggles" without linking to the hazard is not awarded mark 2.
- **N18:** Mark scheme explicitly rejects "speed" as DV — must say "time (s)" or "volume/time".
- **M22:** "Number of bubbles" rejected for O₂ volume — must specify cm³ collected in gas syringe or measuring cylinder.
- **N22:** "Chip size" as a CV only gets credit when chip size is NOT the IV — do not list the IV as a CV.
- **M25:** Environmental consideration (safe disposal of ethanoic acid — neutralise before disposal) awarded mark 3 on the safety strand.
- **Equipment specificity matters:** "Gas syringe" beats "container to collect gas"; "balance accurate to ±0.01 g" beats "a balance"; "thermometer (±0.1 °C)" beats "thermometer".

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| DV stated as "amount" | Always specify unit: volume (cm³), mass (g), time (s), temperature (°C), pH, force (N) |
| DV stated as "rate" alone | Write what is measured to determine rate: "rate of CO₂ production (cm³/min)" |
| "Why does..." research question | Use "How does [IV] affect [DV with unit]?" — "why" questions are not testable |
| Research question with no measurable DV | "Does acid affect marble?" → "How does the concentration of HCl (mol dm⁻³) affect the mass change of marble (g/min)?" |
| Hypothesis without "Because" | In most sessions from M17, no Because = 0 marks for the entire hypothesis |
| "Because it is more reactive" in hypothesis | Name the mechanism: "because [element] has a lower ionisation energy, it loses electrons more readily, increasing the rate of electron transfer" |
| Only 3 or 4 IV levels listed | Always state at least 5 specific values, not just "a range" |
| CV stated without HOW | "CV: temperature" → "Temperature of the solution kept constant at 25 °C using a water bath" |
| "Keep equipment the same" as CV | Not a variable — name an actual variable: temperature, volume, concentration, surface area |
| Method steps without measurement timing | State WHEN and HOW OFTEN the DV is recorded: "record mass every 30 s for 5 minutes" |
| "3 repeats" in Sufficient Data without mean | Add "and calculate the mean of the three trials" — this is the Band 4 trigger phrase |
| Safety: "wear goggles" only | Link to specific hazard: "HCl is corrosive — wear goggles and nitrile gloves to prevent eye/skin burns" |
| Listing the IV as a CV | If marble chip size is the IV, do not also write "chip size" as a CV |
| Not listing 5 IV levels when only a few are given | If the paper provides exactly 5 options (5 salts, 5 metals), list all five — they count as your 5 IV levels |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Memorise the five-strand structure** — Variables / Equipment / Method / Sufficient Data / Safety. Write these five headings before you begin every design question. A student who scores 3/4 on all five strands = 15/20, which is a strong result.

2. **Drill the DV phrasing** — the most marks are lost here. Practise writing the DV for every common context: mass change (g/min), volume of gas (cm³), time for dissolution (s), temperature change (°C), force to break sample (N), electrical conductivity (µS/cm).

3. **Drill the CV+HOW formula** — "[Variable] kept constant at [value] by [method]." For every investigation type, be ready to name 3 relevant CVs with this full formula.

4. **If/Then/Because — the chemistry must be specific.** For each common context, practise the Because clause: acids + metals (collision theory, particle frequency), de-icers (colligative properties, ion count), catalysts (activation energy, collision frequency), combustion (enthalpy of combustion, bond energies).

5. **Always write "calculate the mean"** — this single phrase is the difference between Band 3 and Band 4 on Sufficient Data. It is worth 1 mark and takes 4 words.

6. **Safety: always link hazard to precaution.** Template: "[Chemical/apparatus] is [corrosive/toxic/flammable/hot] — [specific PPE or procedure] to prevent [specific harm]."

7. **Practise the most common investigation contexts** — based on 19 sessions, the highest-frequency contexts are: acids + metals/marble (N16, M18, N22, N24, M25); catalysts and rates (M22, N20); de-icing / salt chemistry (M23); consumer product testing (N18 heartburn remedies, N19 nappies, M24 cans); electrochemistry and reactivity (M16, N16, N17).

8. **Do the M23 de-icing question and the N23 MY-P question under timed conditions** — these are the two most detailed recent design questions and cover the full 17-mark rubric perfectly.
