# Periodicity

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Periodic Table Layout

The modern periodic table arranges all 118 known elements in order of increasing atomic number into rows and columns that reveal patterns in their properties.

**Periods** are the horizontal rows. The period number tells you exactly how many electron shells an atom of that element has. Period 1 has 1 shell (H, He only). Period 2 has 2 shells (Li through Ne). Period 3 has 3 shells (Na through Ar). This is one of the most direct and testable links between position and structure.

**Groups** are the vertical columns. Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outermost shell and therefore show closely similar chemical behaviour. Group 1 elements all have 1 outer electron; Group 7 elements all have 7; Group 18 elements all have full outer shells.

| Region | Groups | Block | Key characteristics |
|--------|--------|-------|---------------------|
| Alkali metals | 1 | s | Soft, very reactive metals; 1 outer electron; form 1+ ions |
| Alkaline earth metals | 2 | s | Reactive metals; 2 outer electrons; form 2+ ions |
| Transition metals | 3–12 | d | Variable oxidation states; coloured compounds; catalytic activity |
| Post-transition metals | 13–15 (left side) | p | e.g. Al, Pb, Sn — metallic but less typical (lower mp, softer) |
| Metalloids | Along the staircase | p | Si, Ge, As — semiconductor properties; on the metal/non-metal boundary |
| Non-metals | 13–17 (right side) | p | Poor conductors; low melting points; often gases or liquids at room temperature |
| Halogens | 17 | p | Reactive non-metals; 7 outer electrons; form −1 ions |
| Noble gases | 18 | p | Full outer shells; chemically inert; monatomic |

**Metals** occupy the left and centre of the table. **Non-metals** occupy the top-right corner. The diagonal **staircase line** running roughly from boron (B) to astatine (At) marks the boundary, with metalloids sitting on or near it.

**Mark scheme key phrase:** When asked whether an element is a metal or non-metal, you must cite specific physical properties — electrical conductivity, melting point, malleability, lustre — or position in the table. Do NOT simply write "it is a metal" with no supporting reasoning.

### Group 1 — Alkali Metals

Group 1 elements (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs) each have **one electron in their outermost shell**. In reactions they lose this electron to form 1+ ions. The defining trend is that **reactivity increases going down the group**.

The explanation follows three linked steps that the mark scheme requires you to state explicitly:

1. Each successive element has **more electron shells** than the one above it.
2. The single outer electron is therefore **further from the nucleus**.
3. The attractive force between the nucleus and that outer electron is **weaker** — it is both more shielded and physically more distant — so the electron is **lost more easily** and the element reacts more vigorously.

| Element | Outer electron shell | Relative reactivity |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Lithium (Li) | Shell 2 | Least reactive in Group 1 |
| Sodium (Na) | Shell 3 | More reactive than Li |
| Potassium (K) | Shell 4 | More reactive than Na |
| Rubidium (Rb) | Shell 5 | Very reactive |
| Caesium (Cs) | Shell 6 | Most reactive in Group 1 |

**N21 Q1d (3 marks):** *Using the Bohr model, explain why sodium is more reactive than lithium.* (Diagram shows Na with electron configuration 2, 8, 1 and Li with 2, 1.)

Mark scheme — all three points needed for full marks:
1. Less energy is needed to remove the electron / the electron is easier to remove (from sodium).
2. Because the outermost electron is further away from the nucleus in sodium.
3. Because there is less attraction from the nucleus / sodium has more electron shells (so greater shielding).

> Do NOT say "sodium has more electrons" — this earns zero marks. You must follow the chain: more shells → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction → lost more easily. All three steps are required for 3 marks.

The same logic applies in reverse (ORA): "lithium is less reactive than sodium because its outer electron is in shell 2 (closer to the nucleus), so the nuclear attraction is stronger, and more energy is required to remove it."

### Group 7 — Halogens

Group 7 elements (F, Cl, Br, I) each have **seven electrons in their outer shell**. In reactions they gain one electron to complete their outer shell and form −1 ions. The defining trend is that **reactivity decreases going down the group** — the exact opposite direction to Group 1.

The mechanism mirrors the Group 1 explanation but in reverse:

1. Each successive element has **more electron shells** and therefore a **larger atomic radius**.
2. The incoming electron must be added to a shell that is **further from the nucleus**.
3. The attraction between the nucleus and the incoming electron is **weaker** (more shielding, greater distance).
4. The extra electron is **gained less easily** — the element is less reactive.

| Element | Outer electron shell | Relative reactivity |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Fluorine (F) | Shell 2 | Most reactive halogen |
| Chlorine (Cl) | Shell 3 | Less reactive than F |
| Bromine (Br) | Shell 4 | Less reactive than Cl |
| Iodine (I) | Shell 5 | Least reactive common halogen |

Note the contrast with Group 1: both trends are driven by the same underlying principle (outer electrons become more distant from the nucleus going down a group), but reactivity moves in **opposite directions** because alkali metals *lose* electrons while halogens *gain* them.

> Do NOT say "it gets harder to attract an electron because iodine has more electrons." You must state that the atomic radius increases → the shell receiving the electron is further from the nucleus → the nuclear attraction is weaker → the electron is gained less easily.

### Group 18 — Noble Gases

Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe) are the rightmost group. Their defining characteristic is a **full outer electron shell**:

- Helium: 2 electrons (full for shell 1 — the duplet rule)
- Neon, Argon, Krypton, Xenon: 8 electrons in their outermost shell (complete octet)

Because no electrons need to be gained, lost, or shared, noble gases:
- Do **not** form chemical bonds under ordinary conditions.
- Exist as **monatomic** gases (single atoms, never molecules like N₂ or O₂).
- Are chemically **inert** — they do not react.

This explains a historical fact that frequently appears as context in exam papers: Mendeleev's 1869 table had no Group 18 because noble gases form no compounds, so they were never detected through chemical reactions. They were first isolated in 1894–1898 using physical methods.

**N24 Q3c (3 marks):** *Use the electronic configuration of argon to explain why it does not form compounds.*

Mark scheme — three separate marking points:
1. Electronic configuration: 2, 8, 8 (or 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶). *(Writing the configuration implies the first mark point.)*
2. Complete outer shell / full octet.
3. (Therefore) no need to share or donate electrons to form bonds.

> Do NOT stop at "argon has a full outer shell." Stating the configuration and the full shell earns only 2 marks. You must add the consequence: "therefore it has no need to share or donate electrons, so it does not form compounds." Three distinct points = 3 marks.

**N21 Q3c (1 mark):** *State a reason why noble gases are found naturally as monatomic gases.* — Any one of: "(Noble gases are) unreactive / inert / have full outer shells of electrons / don't share electrons with other atoms."

**M25 Q3e (1 mark):** *Suggest, with reference to their electronic structure, why passing electricity through noble gases does not cause fires or explosions.* — Mark scheme: "Have full / complete outer shells (so do not react)." Accept reference to 2 electrons for He; accept full octet.

### Transition Metals

Transition metals are the **d-block** elements occupying Groups 3–12, beginning from Period 4. Common examples tested in past papers: Fe (iron), Cu (copper), Co (cobalt), Ni (nickel), Cr (chromium), Mn (manganese), Au (gold), Ag (silver), Zn (zinc).

They are defined by three special properties, and mark schemes for 2-mark questions expect any two of them:

| Property | Explanation | Exam example |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Variable oxidation states | d-block electrons can be partially removed, giving different ionic charges for the same element | Fe²⁺ (iron II) and Fe³⁺ (iron III); Co²⁺ and Co³⁺; Cu⁺ and Cu²⁺ |
| Coloured compounds | Partially filled d-orbitals absorb visible light and transmit the complementary colour | CoCl₂ = blue/pink; Cu²⁺(aq) = blue; Cr³⁺ = green; MnO₄⁻ = purple |
| Catalytic properties | Transition metals and their compounds act as catalysts, speeding up reactions without being consumed | Fe in the Haber process (N₂ + H₂ → NH₃); MnO₂ in the decomposition of H₂O₂ |

They also share all typical metallic properties: high melting points, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, ductile, lustrous. These count as valid answers when asked for metallic properties but are not the defining d-block properties.

**N18 Q1b–c (3 marks):** *Select the block where cobalt appears; identify two properties cobalt shares with other elements in the same block.*
— Block answer: Transition metals / d-block.
— Two properties: any two from variable oxidation state, form coloured compounds, plus any named typical metallic property (conducts electricity, malleable, ductile, high mp).

> Do NOT accept "hard" alone as a transition metal property — the N18 mark scheme explicitly excludes it.
> Do NOT confuse p-block metals (Al, Pb, Sn) with d-block transition metals. Aluminium is a p-block metal in Group 13, not a transition metal.

**M17 Q1b (3 marks):** *Use the periodic table to identify another element that could have the same properties as iron or manganese. Justify your answer.* — Any named transition metal other than Fe or Mn, plus any two of: d-block element; variable oxidation states; forms coloured compounds; named metallic property.

### Using the Periodic Table: Group and Period

This is **the single most frequently tested periodicity skill** — it appears in almost every session, typically worth 1–2 marks, always Criterion A. The question gives you an element name or symbol and asks you to state or identify the group and period.

**Rule:** The **period** = the row number (count down from the top). The **group** = the column number (count across from the left). For d-block elements, count all 18 columns using IUPAC numbering.

**Critical mark scheme warning (multiple sessions):** If you write the group number where the period should be, or vice versa, you **lose ALL marks** — not just one. Always label your answers clearly: "Group ___, Period ___."

**Group numbering note:** Old notation uses Groups 1–8 (Roman numerals: IA, IIA, IVA, etc.); modern IUPAC numbering is 1–18. Both are accepted in mark schemes. Group 4 (old) = Group 14 (IUPAC) — these refer to the same column containing C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb. Do not mix systems in a single answer.

### Period Trends Across a Period

Going **left to right across a period**, the number of electron shells stays constant but the number of protons (nuclear charge) increases by one each step. This drives several measurable trends:

| Property | Trend across a period (left → right) | Reason |
|----------|--------------------------------------|--------|
| Metallic character | Decreases (metals → metalloids → non-metals) | Increasing nuclear charge makes it progressively harder to lose outer electrons |
| Non-metallic character | Increases | Higher nuclear charge makes it easier to attract and gain electrons |
| Number of outer electrons | Increases by 1 each step | One proton and one outer electron added at each element |
| Type of bonding | Metallic → giant covalent/ionic → simple molecular covalent | Reflects changing electron structure and bonding tendency |
| Reactivity | High at left (metals) and high at right (non-metals); lowest near middle | Group 1 = most reactive metal; Group 17 = most reactive non-metal in each period |

**Period 3 in full (Na to Ar) — the most tested individual period:**

| Element | Na | Mg | Al | Si | P | S | Cl | Ar |
|---------|----|----|----|----|----|----|----|-----|
| Group | 1 | 2 | 13 | 14 | 15 | 16 | 17 | 18 |
| Outer electrons | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 | 8 |
| Classification | Metal | Metal | Metal | Metalloid | Non-metal | Non-metal | Non-metal | Noble gas |
| Ion formed | Na⁺ | Mg²⁺ | Al³⁺ | — | P³⁻ | S²⁻ | Cl⁻ | — |

> Do NOT confuse period trends with group trends. Period trends run horizontally (left to right); group trends run vertically (top to bottom). Exam questions almost always specify which direction you are moving — read carefully before answering.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Swapping group and period = zero marks.** Explicitly stated in multiple sessions (M18, M19). Always label which is which.
- **Group 1 reactivity needs three steps:** (1) less energy needed / easier to remove the outer electron; (2) outer electron is further from the nucleus; (3) more shells / less nuclear attraction / greater shielding. Two steps for 2 marks; all three for 3 marks. "More electrons" alone — **NOT accepted.**
- **Noble gas explanation must go beyond "full outer shell."** For 3-mark questions (N24 Q3c), the third mark requires "no need to share or donate electrons." Writing the configuration + full shell = only 2 marks.
- **Transition metals — name two.** Variable oxidation states AND coloured compounds are the two expected answers. "Hard" — **NOT accepted** (N18). Catalytic activity is a valid third option.
- **Block identification:** correct terms are "d-block" or "transition metals." Do NOT write "p-block" or "s-block" for transition metals.
- **Old vs. new group numbering:** Group 4 (old) = Group 14 (IUPAC). Both accepted. Do not mix systems in one answer.
- **Mendeleev missing group:** answer is "noble gases / Group 0 / Group 18." Reason: "they had not yet been discovered / unreactive so not found in compounds / technology not available to isolate them." The reason must be correctly paired to the group named — marks are awarded together.
- **Group 2 flame colours (N16 Q3a):** must name all three colours — orange-red (Ca), bright red (Sr), green (Ba) — to earn the 1 mark. Partial answer = 0.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Swapping group and period numbers | Group = vertical column; Period = horizontal row. Label them explicitly every time. |
| "Sodium is more reactive because it has more electrons" | Must say: more shells → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction → lost more easily |
| "Argon has a full outer shell" (stopping there on a 3-mark question) | Add the consequence: "therefore it has no need to share or donate electrons, so it does not form compounds" |
| "Cobalt is hard" as a transition metal property | Say: variable oxidation state (e.g. Co²⁺ and Co³⁺) AND forms coloured compounds |
| Calling aluminium a transition metal | Aluminium is a p-block metal (Group 13), NOT a d-block transition metal |
| "Group 7 elements are less reactive down the group because they have more electrons" | Say: atomic radius increases → incoming electron added to a shell further from nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction → electron gained less easily |
| Giving only one transition metal property when 2 marks available | Give variable oxidation state AND coloured compounds for any 2-mark transition metals question |
| Mixing old and new IUPAC group numbers in one answer | Pick one system (Group 4 or Group 14) and use it consistently throughout your answer |
| Saying noble gases were missing from Mendeleev's table "because they are gases" | Correct reason: they are unreactive and do not form compounds, so they could not be detected chemically |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Drill the 20 most-tested elements table until you can state group and period for every element without looking at the periodic table. Group/period questions appear in almost every session.
2. Write out the three-step Group 1 reactivity explanation (more shells → further from nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction → lost more easily) until it is automatic. This is the most common 3-mark periodicity question.
3. Learn the three-step noble gas explanation in full: (1) write the electron configuration, (2) state full outer shell, (3) state no need to share or donate electrons. Argon and neon are tested most often.
4. Memorise the two defining transition metal properties by name: **variable oxidation states** and **coloured compounds**. Know at least one example of each and at least two named catalysts (Fe in Haber process; MnO₂ with H₂O₂).
5. Know the Period 3 row by heart: Na (Gp 1), Mg (Gp 2), Al (Gp 13), Si (Gp 14), P (Gp 15), S (Gp 16), Cl (Gp 17), Ar (Gp 18). It is the most tested individual period.
6. For Mendeleev questions: name "noble gases / Group 18" as the missing group; give the reason "they are unreactive / not found in compounds / could not be isolated with technology available at the time."
7. For Group 7 trends, always frame the answer in terms of gaining electrons — the mechanism is the mirror image of Group 1 (same principle, opposite direction of reactivity).
