# Rates of Reaction

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Definition of Rate of Reaction

The rate of reaction is the **change in amount or concentration of a reactant or product per unit time**. It measures how quickly reactants are consumed or products are formed — not just how "fast" a reaction seems.

The units depend on what is being measured:
- Volume of gas collected over time → cm³ s⁻¹
- Concentration change over time → mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
- Mass change over time → g min⁻¹
- Molecules consumed over time → molecules min⁻¹

**N20 Q4g (4 marks, Criteria C/D):** A glow stick contains 6.58 × 10⁵ molecules of phenyl oxalate. The reaction stops at 20°C in 260 minutes. Calculate the rate of consumption.
> Rate = 6.58 × 10⁵ ÷ 260 = **2530 molecules min⁻¹** (or 42.2 molecules s⁻¹). The unit mark (molecules min⁻¹) is awarded separately as a Criterion D mark.

> Do NOT say "the speed of the reaction" — say "change in concentration (or amount) per unit time." Do NOT omit units — the unit mark is almost always awarded independently and costs you a mark if skipped.

### Collision Theory

For a chemical reaction to occur, reactant particles must:
1. **Collide** with each other.
2. Have **sufficient energy** — at least equal to the **activation energy (Ea)**.
3. Have the **correct orientation** when they collide.

Only collisions meeting all three conditions are **successful collisions**. A reaction goes faster when the frequency of successful collisions per second increases.

**Activation energy (Ea)** is the minimum energy colliding particles must possess for the collision to result in a reaction. If the kinetic energy of the colliding particles is below Ea, the particles simply bounce apart — no reaction occurs.

**M22 Q4b (3 marks, Criterion B) — Worked example:**
> *"A student has a catalyst in two states: a solid and a solution. Formulate a hypothesis to test which state will give the greatest increase in rate of reaction. Use collision theory in your answer."*
>
> Model answer: "If the catalyst is in solution, then the rate of decomposition will be higher, because catalyst particles dispersed in solution can collide more frequently with reactant molecules, increasing the number of successful collisions per second."
>
> Mark scheme: (1) solution catalyst identified; (2) more / higher frequency of collisions; (3) scientific justification using collision theory. ORA accepted for solid catalyst.

> Do NOT write "more energy" as your only explanation — the mark scheme requires the collision frequency link AND the energy (Ea) condition for full marks.

### Effect of Temperature

Higher temperature → particles move faster (greater kinetic energy) → collide more frequently → a **greater proportion** of those collisions have kinetic energy ≥ Ea → more successful collisions per second → **rate increases**.

The critical phrase examiners award top marks for is:
**"a greater proportion of molecules have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy."**

Writing only "particles move faster → more collisions" earns partial credit. The link to activation energy is required for full marks at higher mark allocations.

| Temperature | Effect on particles | Effect on rate |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Increases | More kinetic energy; more frequent collisions; greater proportion ≥ Ea | Rate increases |
| Decreases | Less kinetic energy; fewer collisions; smaller proportion ≥ Ea | Rate decreases |

**M21 Q2e (2 marks, Criterion A) — Worked example:**
> *"Suggest why the rate of decomposition of CO₂ increases the closer the exoplanet is to the star it orbits."*
>
> Mark scheme: "The temperature is higher closer to the star [1] → at higher temperatures the rate of the reaction increases [1]." Both parts required — the temperature cause AND the rate consequence.

**N23 Q4c (2 marks, Criterion C) — Worked example:**
> *"Predict how the toast would appear different if the toaster temperature was lowered."*
>
> Mark scheme: "The colour would be less brown [1] — lower temperature gives a slower rate of the Maillard reaction [1]."

> Do NOT say only "higher temperature = faster reaction" — the mark scheme rewards the full particle-level chain: KE increases → more collisions → greater proportion ≥ Ea → rate increases.

### Effect of Concentration

Higher concentration → more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions between reactant particles → more successful collisions per second → **rate increases**.

The phrase **"per unit volume"** distinguishes concentration from total amount — it shows you understand what concentration actually means at the particle level.

**N21 Q2e (3 marks, Criterion A) — Worked example:**
> *"Using information from the video and your knowledge of collision theory, explain how concentration affects the rate at which the teeth are whitened."*
>
> Mark scheme: "More (hydrogen peroxide) particles are present at higher concentrations [1] → there are more successful collisions with stain particles [1] → the teeth are whitened more quickly / rate of reaction is faster [1]."

**N24 Q6 (3 marks, Criterion C) — Worked example:**
> A graph shows NaCl concentration versus mass lost by an iron nail after 7 days. As concentration increases, mass loss increases linearly.
>
> Mark scheme for evaluating the hypothesis: "Concentration increases AND mass loss increases [1] — linear relationship [1]." Higher concentration → more ions per unit volume → more collisions → faster corrosion.

> Do NOT say "the solution is stronger" — say "higher concentration = more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → faster rate." "Stronger" is NOT accepted by mark schemes.

### Effect of Surface Area

Smaller particle size → larger total surface area → **more particles are exposed** at the surface and available to collide with reactant particles → more frequent collisions → **rate increases**.

The word **"surface area"** must appear explicitly in your answer. The mark scheme in M18 Q5e specifically awards one mark just for using the terms *surface area* and *rate of reaction* correctly.

**M23 Q1d (3 marks, Criterion A) — Worked example:**
> *"Explain why powdered coal should be used rather than lumps of solid coal in power plants."*
>
> Mark scheme: (1) "Particles of powdered coal have a greater **surface area** than when using lumps" [1]; (2) "Rate of reaction is faster" [1]; (3) "Energy is released more quickly" [1]. All three are separate marks — no one point implies the others.

**M18 Q5e (3 marks, Criteria C/D) — Worked example:**
> *"Using your answer from part d, suggest why the limestone produced more CO₂ in the same time as the crushed oyster shell."*
>
> Mark scheme: (1) "Limestone particles are smaller than crushed oyster shell" (ORA) [1]; (2) "Rate of reaction is greater for limestone" (ORA) [1]; (3) correct use of the terms **surface area** AND **rate of reaction** as a dedicated mark [1].

**N20 Q3g (3 marks, Criterion A) — Worked example:**
> Biodegradable plastic pellets of different particle sizes were buried and tested over 50 days. Sample A degraded fastest, C intermediate, B slowest. List the samples in order of increasing particle size.
>
> Mark scheme: "A — C — B [1]. A had the fastest rate of biodegradability [1]. Rate of reaction increases with decreasing particle size / the smaller the particles, the higher the surface area and the faster the reaction [1]."

> Do NOT write only "smaller particles react faster" — write: "smaller particles → greater surface area → more particles exposed at the surface → more frequent collisions → faster rate." The word "surface area" must appear; "size" alone is not accepted.

### Effect of Catalysts

A catalyst **increases the rate of reaction** by providing an **alternative reaction pathway** that has a **lower activation energy** — and it does this **without itself being used up (consumed)** in the reaction.

This is a **3-part answer** for any 3-mark question. Each part is marked independently.

| Part | What to write | Why it matters |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | Speeds up / increases the rate of reaction | The functional outcome |
| 2 | Lowers the activation energy / provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea | The mechanism — energy profile link |
| 3 | Is not used up / not consumed / not permanently changed | Distinguishes catalyst from reactant |

For 2-mark questions, any two of the three parts earn full marks. For 1-mark questions, part 1 alone ("speeds up the reaction") is sufficient.

**N24 Q2d (3 marks, Criterion A) — Worked example:**
> *"Explain how a catalyst works to increase the rate of methanol production."*
>
> Mark scheme: (1) "Providing an alternative pathway" ✓; (2) "With a lower activation energy" ✓; (3) "Without itself being used up" ✓. All three are independent marks.

**M16 Q3c (2 marks, Criterion A) — Energy profile diagram:**
> *"Using the graph, outline how catalysts work."*
>
> Mark scheme: (1) "A catalyst gives a lower energy path for the reaction" [1]; (2) "The activation energy / energy barrier is lower OR provides an alternative reaction pathway OR so more molecules have enough energy to react" [1].

**N20 Q4a (2 marks, Criterion A):**
> *"Outline the function of a catalyst."*
>
> Mark scheme: "A chemical which speeds up the rate of a reaction / lowers the activation energy [1] — without itself being used up [1]." (WTTE)

**M21 Q2f (2 marks, Criterion A):**
> *"Outline the role of a catalyst in a chemical reaction."*
>
> Mark scheme: "Catalysts increase the rate of a reaction [1] — and any one of: reaction has lower activation energy / reaction takes place by an alternative path / the catalyst is not used up [1]."

> Do NOT write only "the catalyst lowers activation energy" for a 3-mark answer — you also need to say it speeds up the rate AND is not consumed. The mark scheme treats each component independently.

### Measuring Rate of Reaction

Four practical methods appear across MYP sessions. You must know how each method works and what it directly measures.

| Method | How it works | What you measure | Sessions |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Gas syringe / gas collection** | Gas produced (CO₂, O₂, H₂) is collected in a syringe or over water; volume read at time intervals | Volume of gas (cm³) vs time (s) | M17, M18, M22, N20 |
| **Mass loss** | Reaction vessel sits on a balance; gas escapes so total mass decreases over time | Mass (g) vs time (s) | N24 Q5 design questions |
| **Colour change / indicator** | A coloured reactant is consumed or a product forms; time until colour change is visible is recorded | Time for visible change (s or min) | M22, N18 |
| **Turbidity (cloudiness)** | A precipitate forms and the solution becomes cloudy; a cross drawn under the beaker disappears when turbid enough | Time for cross to disappear (s) | General method — appears in hypothesis questions |

**M18 Q5d (2 marks, Criterion C) — Worked rate calculation from graph:**
> The graph shows CO₂ volume vs time for limestone and crushed oyster shell reacting with HCl over the first 60 seconds.
>
> Rate = total volume of gas produced ÷ total time taken
> - Limestone: 80 cm³ ÷ 60 s = **1.33 cm³ s⁻¹** (accept ± 0.03)
> - Crushed oyster shell: 17 cm³ ÷ 60 s = **0.28 cm³ s⁻¹** (accept ± 0.03)

**M22 Q4e (3 marks, Criterion C) — Average then rate:**
> Three trials of O₂ collected in 30 s: 44 cm³, 44 cm³, 44 cm³. Average = 44 cm³.
>
> Rate = 44 ÷ 30 = **1.47 cm³ s⁻¹** (accept 1.5). Unit: cm³ s⁻¹ [unit mark awarded separately — always write it].

For gas collection, a **gas syringe** is the preferred equipment choice in design questions. In N17 Q3b (1 mark), students had to select one piece of equipment to collect data; the accepted answers were "thermometer or temperature probe" (because that experiment tracked temperature change). In M18 Q3d, a gas syringe connected to a conical flask is the model apparatus for CO₂ collection.

> Do NOT forget to write units after every rate calculation — the unit mark is always awarded separately and can be the difference between 3/4 and 4/4.

### Rate Calculations from Graphs

The **gradient** (slope) of a volume-vs-time or concentration-vs-time graph equals the **rate** at that point in the reaction.

$$\text{Gradient} = \text{rate} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta\text{(volume or concentration)}}{\Delta\text{time}}$$

- **Steep gradient** = fast rate (early in the reaction when reactant concentration is highest)
- **Shallow gradient** = slow rate (as reactants are used up)
- **Flat section (gradient = 0)** = reaction has stopped (reactants exhausted)
- **Average rate over a period** = total change ÷ total time (not the instantaneous gradient at a point)

**M24 Q6c (2 marks, Criterion C) — Worked example:**
> A graph shows vitamin C concentration (μg cm⁻³) for CO₂-treated orange juice over 21 days. Concentration drops from 375 to 350 μg cm⁻³.
>
> Rate = Δ[vitamin C] ÷ Δtime = (375 − 350) ÷ 21 = 25 ÷ 21 = **1.19 μg cm⁻³ day⁻¹**
>
> Mark scheme: (1) Δ[C] = 25 ± 5 μg cm⁻³ [1]; (2) rate = 25/21 = 1.19 μg cm⁻³ day⁻¹ [1]. Units required.

**M17 Q6c (2 marks, Criterion C) — Identifying anomalous data on a graph:**
> A graph of CO₂ volume vs time (CaCO₃ + HCl) shows one trial. The data point at 110 s does not fit the expected trend.
>
> Mark scheme: (1) "Data value at 110 s identified" [1] — units required; (2) estimated corrected value of **21 cm³** [1] — read from the trend.

> Do NOT pick the two most extreme data points to calculate a gradient if an outlier lies between them — always use the line of best fit. Do NOT state the anomalous point without giving its time coordinate.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Catalyst 3-mark structure** — Examiners always award three independent marks: (1) speeds up rate; (2) lowers Ea / alternative pathway; (3) not used up. Never write only one or two of these for a 3-mark question — each is a separate mark.
- **Surface area wording** — The word "surface area" must appear explicitly. "Smaller pieces" or "powder" alone gains at most 1 mark. M18 Q5e specifically awards a dedicated mark for "correct use of terms surface area **and** rate of reaction."
- **Temperature phrasing** — Full-mark answers include the phrase "greater proportion of molecules have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy." Stopping at "more collisions" misses the Ea link and loses the top mark.
- **Concentration phrasing** — "More particles per unit volume" is accepted. "More particles" alone is WTTE and usually scores. "Stronger solution" is NOT accepted.
- **Rate calculation units** — The unit mark is always awarded separately. Missing units in a 4-mark rate calculation still earns 3/4 but needlessly drops one mark.
- **CON rule** — A correct statement followed immediately by a contradicting one scores 0 for that marking point. E.g. "rate increases because of more collisions, but activation energy is raised" — the collision point scores 0.
- **WTTE** — Mark schemes explicitly say WTTE for catalyst definitions and collision theory explanations. You do not need to copy exact wording if the science is correct.
- **ORA** — Most factor questions accept the opposite direction with correct reasoning. If asked why rate increases with temperature, you can also argue why it decreases with lower temperature and earn the mark.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Catalyst lowers activation energy" alone for a 3-mark question | Must also state: speeds up rate AND is not consumed — three independent marks |
| "Smaller particles react faster" without mentioning surface area | Write: "smaller particles → greater surface area → more particles exposed → more collisions → faster rate" |
| "Higher temperature = more energy" without linking to Ea | Write: "greater proportion of molecules have energy ≥ activation energy → more successful collisions" |
| "More concentration = stronger solution" | Write: "higher concentration = more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → faster rate" |
| Forgetting units in rate calculations | Always state cm³ s⁻¹ / molecules min⁻¹ / μg cm⁻³ day⁻¹ — the unit is a separate mark |
| Naming an outlier without its coordinates | Identify by exact time value (e.g. "the data point at 110 s") then give estimated corrected value |
| Calculating rate using two extreme points that span an anomaly | Draw and use the line of best fit; take Δy/Δx from the trend, not raw data points |
| Writing "rate changes" in a hypothesis without direction | Must state "rate increases" or "rate decreases" — direction is required for hypothesis marks |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Catalyst 3-part answer** — Single most tested concept (M16, M21, M22, M23, N20, N24). Memorise and practise: speeds up rate + lowers Ea/alternative pathway + not used up. Write it out from memory without prompting until it is automatic.
2. **Surface area explanation chain** — Appears in M18, M23, N20: "smaller particles → greater surface area → more particles exposed at surface → more frequent collisions → faster rate." The word "surface area" must appear.
3. **Temperature full chain with Ea** — M21, N20, N23: learn the complete chain ending in "greater proportion of molecules with energy ≥ activation energy → more successful collisions → faster rate."
4. **Rate calculation from data** — Practise vol/time (M18, M22) and gradient/Δy÷Δx (M24, N20): always show: (1) equation or method, (2) substitution, (3) value, (4) units as four separate steps.
5. **Concentration — collision theory chain** — N21, N24: "more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → more successful collisions → faster rate." Three chained points.
6. **Measuring methods** — Know all four methods (gas syringe, mass loss, colour change, turbidity) and be able to describe the apparatus and what is measured for each — design questions (M18 Q3d, N24 Q5b, M22 Q5) ask for this.
7. **Graph skills: outlier identification and gradient** — M17, M24: practise naming the anomalous point by its exact time/concentration coordinate; practise reading Δy/Δx off a line of best fit rather than raw data.
8. **Hypothesis formulation for rate investigations** — M22, N17, N20: If/Then/Because structure; named IV; direction of change in rate; collision theory reasoning in the "because" clause.
