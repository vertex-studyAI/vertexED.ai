# Atomic Structure

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Subatomic Particles

Every atom is made of three subatomic particles. Protons and neutrons sit together in the **nucleus** at the centre of the atom. Electrons occupy **shells** (energy levels) that surround the nucleus.

| Particle | Location | Relative charge | Relative mass |
|----------|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Proton | Nucleus | +1 | 1 |
| Neutron | Nucleus | 0 | 1 |
| Electron | Shells (outside nucleus) | −1 | negligible (~1/1836) |

In a **neutral atom**, the number of protons equals the number of electrons — the charges cancel out exactly.

**N18 Q1d (3 marks):** "State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in a ⁵⁹₂₇Co²⁺ ion." → Protons = **27**; Neutrons = 59 − 27 = **32**; Electrons = 27 − 2 = **25** [1 each].

> Do NOT say electrons = 27 for Co²⁺ — the 2+ charge means 2 electrons have been lost, so electrons = 27 − 2 = 25.

---

### Atomic Number, Mass Number, and Nuclear Notation

The **atomic number (Z)** is the number of protons in the nucleus. It uniquely defines the element — if Z changes, you have a different element.

The **mass number (A)** is the total count of protons and neutrons in the nucleus. Electrons are not included because their mass is negligible.

**Nuclear notation** places both numbers beside the element symbol:

```
ᴬ
 X     where A = mass number (top-left), Z = atomic number (bottom-left)
ᶻ
```

For example: ²⁰⁸₈₂Pb means lead with mass number 208 and atomic number 82.

**M17 Q7d (2 marks):** "Determine the number of protons and neutrons in ²⁰⁸₈₂Pb." → Protons = **82**; Neutrons = 208 − 82 = **126** [1 each].

> Do NOT write bare numbers like "82, 126" without labelling — the mark scheme explicitly rejects unlabelled pairs. Always write "protons = 82; neutrons = 126."

---

### Calculating Neutrons (A − Z)

Since mass number = protons + neutrons, rearranging gives:

**Neutrons = A − Z**

This is the most frequently tested calculation in atomic structure. It applies to any neutral atom or ion — the ion charge does not affect proton or neutron count.

**N16 Q3e (1 mark):** "Calculate the number of neutrons in Mg-24." → Protons of Mg = 12; neutrons = 24 − 12 = **12** [1].

> Do NOT use the average atomic mass (12.31 for Mg) when the question names a specific isotope like "Mg-24." Use 24 − 12 = 12. Using the average atomic mass here scores 0.

---

### Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the **same element** that have the **same number of protons** but **different numbers of neutrons** (and therefore different mass numbers).

Because isotopes have the same atomic number, they are the same element and sit in the same position on the periodic table. Their chemical behaviour is identical because chemistry is governed by electrons, and isotopes have the same electron configuration.

**N22 Q1c (2 marks):** "Outline the meaning of the term isotope." → Atoms of the same element; same number of **protons**; different number of **neutrons** (/ different mass numbers) [2: 1 mark for same protons, 1 mark for different neutrons].

> You must state BOTH "same protons" AND "different neutrons/mass number" to get full marks. Saying only "same element, different mass number" without mentioning protons/neutrons explicitly may get only 1/2.

**N17 Q1g (2 marks):** "Suggest whether isotopes of an element react the same way. Justify." → Yes; isotopes have the **same electron configuration** (same number of electrons / same outer-shell electrons); chemical reactions depend on electrons [2: 1 for yes, 1 for justification].

---

### Electron Configuration (Shell Notation)

Electrons occupy shells around the nucleus. Shells fill from the inside out, and each shell has a maximum capacity:

| Shell | Maximum electrons |
|-------|------------------|
| 1st (innermost) | 2 |
| 2nd | 8 |
| 3rd | 8 (at MYP level) |

The electron configuration is written in shell notation separated by commas or full stops — both are accepted. For example, sodium (Z = 11) has configuration **2,8,1**.

Key configurations to memorise:

| Element | Z | Configuration |
|---------|---|--------------|
| H | 1 | 1 |
| He | 2 | 2 |
| Li | 3 | 2,1 |
| C | 6 | 2,4 |
| N | 7 | 2,5 |
| O | 8 | 2,6 |
| F | 9 | 2,7 |
| Ne | 10 | 2,8 |
| Na | 11 | 2,8,1 |
| Mg | 12 | 2,8,2 |
| Al | 13 | 2,8,3 |
| Si | 14 | 2,8,4 |
| Cl | 17 | 2,8,7 |
| Ar | 18 | 2,8,8 |

**N23 Q3a (1 mark):** "State the electron configuration of aluminium." → **2.8.3** [1].

**M25 Q3f (1 mark):** "State the electron configuration of oxygen." → **2.6** [1].

> Comma and full-stop notation are both accepted. The order must be correct — you cannot write 8,2 for magnesium.

---

### Ions: Cations and Anions

When an atom gains or loses electrons it becomes an **ion** — an atom with a net electric charge. The number of protons does NOT change when an ion forms; only the electron count changes.

- **Cation** (positive ion): the atom has **lost** electrons → fewer electrons than protons.
  - Electrons = Z − charge (e.g. Co²⁺ with Z = 27: electrons = 27 − 2 = 25)
- **Anion** (negative ion): the atom has **gained** electrons → more electrons than protons.
  - Electrons = Z + charge (e.g. Cl⁻ with Z = 17: electrons = 17 + 1 = 18)

**M18 Q2a (2 marks):** "State the number of protons and neutrons in a ²⁴Mg²⁺ ion." → Protons = **12**; Neutrons = 24 − 12 = **12** [1 each]. (Electrons are not asked; the ion charge does not affect protons or neutrons.)

**N21 Q8a (1 mark):** "Write the symbol for the lithium ion." → **Li⁺** [1].

> The most common error: forgetting to subtract the ion charge when finding electrons. For any cation X^(n+), electrons = Z − n. For any anion X^(n−), electrons = Z + n. Protons and neutrons are always read directly from the nuclear notation regardless of ion charge.

---

### Group and Period

The periodic table is arranged so that an element's position directly encodes its electron configuration.

- **Period** = the horizontal row = the number of occupied electron shells. Sodium is in Period 3 because it has 3 occupied shells (2,8,1).
- **Group** = the vertical column = the number of outer-shell electrons (for main-group elements, Groups 1–2 and 13–18). Chlorine is in Group 7 (or 17) because it has 7 outer electrons (2,8,7).

This relationship works in reverse too: knowing an element's group and period lets you write its electron configuration instantly.

**M21 Q1b (2 marks):** "State the group and period of silicon." → Group **4** (accept 14); Period **3** [1 each].

**N24 Q3b (2 marks):** "State the group of nitrogen and the period of argon." → N: Group **5** (accept 15); Ar: Period **3** [1 each].

> Group numbers can be given as the traditional short form (e.g. Group 4) or the IUPAC long form (e.g. Group 14) — both are accepted. Period is always a single number; do not say "row."

---

### Relative Atomic Mass (Weighted Average)

Most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes in nature. The **relative atomic mass (Ar)** is the weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes, taking their abundance into account. It is measured relative to ¹²C = 12.

Because it is a weighted average across a mixture of isotopes, the relative atomic mass is usually not a whole number (e.g. Cl ≈ 35.5).

When a question asks about a **specific isotope** (e.g. "Cl-35"), use the exact mass number, not the average atomic mass.

**M16 Q9c (2 marks):** "Five calcium isotopes are: ⁴⁰Ca, ⁴²Ca, ⁴³Ca, ⁴⁴Ca, ⁴⁶Ca. Calculate the average number of neutrons." → Average mass number = (40 + 42 + 43 + 44 + 46) / 5 = 43; Average neutrons = 43 − 20 = **23** [2: 1 mark for correct method, 1 mark for answer].

> Do NOT confuse "relative atomic mass" (a weighted average based on abundance) with "average mass number" (a simple mean). The M16 question uses a simple mean because no abundance data is given. If abundance percentages are provided, you must weight accordingly.

---

### Transition Metals

Transition metals occupy the **d-block** of the periodic table, which spans **Groups 3–12** (also called the d-block or "middle block"). In Period 4, the transition metals include Fe (iron), Co (cobalt), Ni (nickel), Cu (copper), and Zn (zinc).

Key features of transition metals tested at MYP level:
- They are found between the s-block (Groups 1–2) and the p-block (Groups 13–18).
- Many form coloured compounds and can have variable oxidation states (not required for atomic structure but useful context).

**N18 Q1b (1 mark):** "Select the block of the periodic table where cobalt is found." → **Transition metals** (d-block) [1].

**M21 Q1c (1 mark):** "Identify the region of the periodic table where iron is found." → **Transition metals** (d-block) [1].

> "d-block" and "transition metals" are both accepted. Do NOT say "middle of the periodic table" without the term "transition metals" — this is too vague.

---

### Noble Gases (Full Outer Shell → Unreactive)

Noble gases occupy **Group 18** (also called Group 0). Their defining feature is a **full outer electron shell**: helium has 2 electrons (full first shell); neon, argon, and the rest have 8 outer electrons (full second/third shell).

Because their outer shell is already full, noble gases have **no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons** — so they do not form chemical bonds under normal conditions.

Answering a "why don't noble gases react?" question requires **three separate marking points**:
1. State the electron configuration (e.g. Ar: 2,8,8).
2. State that it has a full/complete outer shell.
3. State that there is no need to share, gain, or lose electrons to become stable.

**N24 Q3c (3 marks):** "Explain why argon does not form compounds." → Electron configuration **2,8,8** [1]; has a **complete/full outer shell** [1]; **no need to share, gain, or donate electrons** to become stable [1].

**M21 Q1d (4 marks):** "Explain why hydrogen forms H₂ but helium does not form He₂." → H has 1 outer electron; needs 2 to complete shell; shares electron with another H to form covalent bond [2]. He has full outer shell (2 electrons); no need to gain/share/lose electrons; stable configuration [2].

> Saying only "helium has a full outer shell" without adding "no need to bond" gets 1/2 marks, not 2/2. Both points are separate marks.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Labelling is mandatory:** When asked for both protons AND neutrons, always write "protons = X; neutrons = Y." Writing bare numbers like "82, 126" — **NOT accepted** (M17).
- **Isotope definition — both halves required:** "Same protons" alone OR "different neutrons" alone gets 1/2 marks. Both clauses together get 2/2 (N16, N22).
- **Ion electrons:** Must show the subtraction explicitly. For Co²⁺: electrons = 27 − 2 = 25. Writing 27 — **NOT accepted** (N18).
- **Average atomic mass vs specific isotope:** N16 Q3e — do NOT use 12.31 (the average Ar of Mg) for Mg-24 neutrons. Use 24 − 12 = 12. Wrong answer scores 0.
- **Noble gas inertness — three-point answer:** Must give (1) electron configuration, (2) full outer shell, and (3) no need to bond. Only two out of three = 2/3 marks (N24).
- **Reactivity comparisons (Na vs Li):** Three separate marking points — outer electron further from nucleus, weaker nuclear attraction, more electron shells. Need all three for 3/3 (N21).
- **ECF (Error Carried Forward):** Applied generously in multi-part questions — a wrong atomic number in part (c) can still earn full marks in part (d) if used consistently.
- **CON rule:** If your correct answer is contradicted by a second statement in the same response, the whole answer scores 0. Do not add extra statements you are unsure about.
- **"Transition metals" wording:** "D-block" is accepted; "middle of the periodic table" is not sufficient without naming the region.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing unlabelled proton/neutron answers (e.g. "82, 126") | Always label: "protons = 82; neutrons = 126" |
| Using average atomic mass for a specific isotope's neutrons | Use A − Z for the named isotope only (e.g. Mg-24 → 24 − 12 = 12, not 12.31) |
| Forgetting to adjust electron count for ions | Cation: electrons = Z − charge; anion: electrons = Z + charge |
| Isotope definition with only one clause | Must state BOTH "same number of protons" AND "different number of neutrons" |
| Saying noble gases are unreactive without explaining why | Must give: full outer shell + no need to gain/lose/share electrons (two separate marks) |
| Confusing group and period | Group = outer electrons (columns); Period = number of shells (rows) |
| Using old-style group numbers only (e.g. Group 4 for Si) | Both Group 4 and Group 14 are accepted for silicon — state one clearly |
| Saying "middle of the periodic table" for transition metals | Say "transition metals" or "d-block" |
| Claiming ion charge changes proton or neutron count | Ion charge only changes electron count — protons and neutrons are fixed by nuclear notation |
| Stopping at "full outer shell" for noble gas stability | Must also say "no need to share/gain/lose electrons to become stable" |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Practise reading nuclear notation (ᴬ_Z X) and extracting protons, neutrons, and electrons instantly — including for ions. Always label your answers.
2. Memorise the electron configuration of H through Ar and be able to state OR draw (shell diagram) for any of them on demand.
3. Drill the ion-electron rule: cation electrons = Z − charge; anion electrons = Z + charge. This is the single most commonly missed mark.
4. Learn the group = outer electrons, period = number of shells relationship so you can identify any element's position from its config and vice versa.
5. Write the isotope definition in full every time: "same number of protons, different number of neutrons (different mass numbers)." Never abbreviate.
6. Build a three-point noble gas answer: (1) give config, (2) say "full outer shell," (3) say "no need to bond." Practise writing all three unprompted.
7. Know which elements are transition metals (Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn) and that they are in the d-block, Period 4.
8. For relative atomic mass calculations: identify whether the question wants a weighted average (with abundances) or a simple mean (equal mixture) — and always subtract Z at the end to convert from mass number to neutrons.
