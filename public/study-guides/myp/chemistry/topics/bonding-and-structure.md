# Bonding and Structure

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding occurs between a metal and a non-metal. Electrons are **transferred** from the metal atom to the non-metal atom. This creates oppositely charged ions (cation + anion), which are held together by **electrostatic attraction** in a giant ionic lattice.

The full 4-step explanation the mark scheme rewards — one mark per step:

1. The metal atom **loses electrons** and forms a positive ion (cation).
2. The non-metal atom **gains electrons** and forms a negative ion (anion).
3. An ionic bond is formed.
4. **Electrostatic attraction** between the oppositely charged ions holds the lattice together.

> Do NOT say electrons are "shared" — that is covalent. Do NOT say "donated" — say "transferred" or "lost/gained."
> Do NOT call ionic compounds "molecules" — they are lattices.

**Properties of ionic compounds:**

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| High melting point | Many strong electrostatic attractions between ions; a lot of energy needed to break the lattice |
| Conducts electricity when molten or dissolved | Ions are free to move and carry charge |
| Does NOT conduct when solid | Ions are fixed in lattice positions; cannot move |
| Often soluble in water | Polar water molecules attract and surround the ions |
| Brittle | Shifting layers bring like-charges adjacent; repulsion shatters the crystal |

**N22 Q2g (4 marks) — worked example:**
"Explain how iron and chlorine bond to form iron(III) chloride."
→ Iron loses electrons / forms positive ions [1]; Chlorine gains electrons / forms negative ions [1]; forming an ionic bond [1]; because of electrostatic attraction between the oppositely charged ions [1].

> Do NOT say "iron donates electrons to chlorine" — say "iron loses electrons; chlorine gains electrons."

### Covalent Bonding

Covalent bonding occurs between two **non-metal** atoms. Each atom contributes one (or more) electrons to form a **shared pair** — a covalent bond. Both atoms achieve a full outer shell (noble gas configuration). Covalent bonds can be single (one shared pair), double (two shared pairs), or triple (three shared pairs).

**Why H₂ forms but He does not — M21 Q1d (4 marks):**
- H has one electron in its outer shell — it is electronically unstable.
- H atoms share electrons to achieve a full outer shell of 2 (duplet / noble gas configuration).
- He already has a full outer shell of 2 electrons.
- So He has no need to share, gain, or lose electrons — it does not bond.

> Do NOT say "He is stable because it is balanced." Say "He has a full outer shell / noble gas configuration."
> WTTE is applied: "complete duplet," "stable electron shell," and "noble gas configuration" are all accepted. "Balanced" is NOT accepted.

**Types of covalent structure:**

| Type | Description | Examples | Properties |
|------|-------------|----------|------------|
| Simple molecular | Discrete molecules; weak intermolecular forces between them | H₂O, CO₂, NH₃, CH₄, O₂ | Low melting/boiling point; non-conducting; may be soluble |
| Giant covalent (macromolecular) | Network of atoms all bonded covalently; no discrete molecules | SiO₂ (silicon dioxide), diamond | Very high melting point; insoluble; non-conducting (except graphite) |

**N18 Q2e–f — SiO₂ (silicon dioxide) worked example:**
- Type of bonding: **covalent** (electrons shared between silicon and oxygen atoms).
- Soluble in water? **Insoluble** — because it has a giant covalent/macromolecular structure.

> Do NOT say just "covalent" for SiO₂ when asked about its high melting point or insolubility — say "giant covalent structure" or "macromolecular structure." Many strong covalent bonds must be broken; this requires a large amount of energy.

### Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding occurs in pure metals and alloys. Metal atoms lose their outer-shell electrons, forming a regular lattice of **positive metal ions** (cations) surrounded by a **sea of delocalised electrons**. The bond is the electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and the delocalised electrons.

**Properties and their explanations:**

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| Conducts electricity (solid and liquid) | Delocalised electrons can move freely and carry charge |
| Good thermal conductor | Delocalised electrons transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the lattice |
| Malleable and ductile | Layers of ions can slide over each other; delocalised electrons maintain attraction throughout |
| High melting point | Strong electrostatic attraction between positive ions and electron sea requires large energy to break |

**M19 Q3c — Teflon pan vs metal pan (4 marks):**
- Teflon (PTFE): **covalent** bonding (C–F bonds; non-metal atoms sharing electrons).
- Metal pan: **metallic** bonding.
- Advantage of Teflon: strong C–F covalent bonds mean no ions are released into food; non-stick, non-reactive.
- Advantage of metal: delocalised electrons allow high thermal conductivity — transfers heat efficiently.

> Both bond types must be stated to earn the bonding mark. "Covalent and metallic" must both appear.

### Bonding Type → Properties Comparison

| Property | Ionic (e.g. NaCl) | Simple covalent (e.g. CO₂, H₂O) | Giant covalent (e.g. SiO₂) | Metallic (e.g. Fe, Al) |
|----------|--------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------|------------------------|
| Melting point | High | Low | Very high | High |
| Conducts electricity (solid) | No | No | No (except graphite) | Yes |
| Conducts electricity (liquid/dissolved) | Yes | No | No | Yes |
| Solubility in water | Often soluble | Variable | Insoluble | Insoluble |
| Malleable / ductile | No (brittle) | N/A (often gas/liquid) | No (brittle) | Yes |
| Structural unit | Lattice of ions | Discrete molecules | Giant network | Lattice of positive ions + e⁻ sea |

### Lewis Structures (Dot-and-Cross Diagrams)

A Lewis structure shows only the **outer-shell (valence) electrons** of every atom in a molecule. Both **bonding pairs** (shared electrons between atoms) and **lone pairs** (non-bonding electrons) must be shown.

**Drawing rules:**
1. Count the total valence electrons for all atoms.
2. Place the least electronegative atom in the centre (H always on the outside).
3. Connect atoms with single bonds first (1 shared pair each).
4. Fill remaining electrons as lone pairs, starting with outer atoms.
5. If the central atom lacks a full octet, convert lone pairs to double/triple bonds.
6. Check: every atom except H should have 8 electrons around it (octet rule); H has 2.

**Mark scheme rules for Lewis structure questions (N23 Q1c, M18 Q1c):**
- All bonding pairs AND all lone pairs must be shown — missing lone pairs lose marks.
- Correct bond type required — single vs double bonds are discriminating marks.
- For drawn questions: each feature (central atom position, double bonds, lone pairs per oxygen) is a separate marking point.

> Do NOT draw single bonds in O₂, CO₂, or ethene — these require double bonds and it is almost always the discriminating mark.

**All molecules tested across past papers (1-mark select and 2–3-mark draw):**

| Molecule | Formula | Bonds on central atom | Lone pairs on central atom | Lone pairs on outer atoms | Notes |
|----------|---------|-----------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|-------|
| Water | H₂O | 2 × O–H single | 2 lone pairs on O | — | V-shape |
| Hydrogen sulfide | H₂S | 2 × S–H single | 2 lone pairs on S | — | Same pattern as H₂O |
| Ammonia | NH₃ | 3 × N–H single | 1 lone pair on N | — | Pyramidal |
| Methane | CH₄ | 4 × C–H single | 0 | — | Tetrahedral; no lone pairs anywhere |
| Oxygen | O₂ | O=O double | 2 lone pairs on each O | — | Double bond required |
| Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | 2 × C=O double | 0 on C | 2 lone pairs on each O | Linear; [O=C=O] |
| Hydrogen peroxide | H₂O₂ | O–O single + O–H single on each end | 2 lone pairs on each O | — | 3-bond framework |
| Methanoic acid | HCOOH | C=O + C–O + C–H | — | Lone pairs on both O atoms | 2–3 mark draw; both oxygens need lone pairs |
| Ethene | C₂H₄ | C=C double + 2 × C–H per carbon | 0 | — | Double bond required |
| Calcium chloride | CaCl₂ | Ionic compound | — | 8 electrons around each Cl⁻ | Shown as Ca²⁺ and two Cl⁻ ions |

**N23 Q1c — CO₂ drawn Lewis structure (3 marks):**
- Carbon in the centre with oxygen either side [1]
- Both double bonds correct (dots and crosses) [1]
- Correct arrangement of lone pairs around each oxygen atom [1]

> Do NOT draw lone pairs on the carbon in CO₂ — carbon has zero lone pairs in this molecule (it uses all 4 valence electrons in the double bonds).

**M25 Q3g — O₂ outlined bonding (2 marks):**
"Outline the bonding that is found in oxygen molecules."
→ Covalent bonding [1]; electrons are shared between atoms [1].
> Accept "shared pair of electrons." Must say "shared" — NOT "transferred."

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Ionic bonding "explain" questions:** Must give all four steps — transfer of electrons, charges formed on each ion, ionic bond formed, and electrostatic attraction. Omitting any single step loses a mark.
- **"Transfer" vs "share":** The word choice discriminates ionic (transfer) from covalent (share). Writing the wrong word triggers the CON rule and cancels all other correct marks.
- **Giant covalent vs simple covalent:** Just saying "covalent" for SiO₂ when explaining high melting point or insolubility is insufficient — must say "giant covalent" or "macromolecular structure."
- **Lewis structures — lone pairs:** The most common lost mark is omitting lone pairs on oxygen atoms in CO₂, H₂O, H₂O₂, and HCOOH. Every lone pair is a separate marking point.
- **Lewis structures — double bonds:** In O₂, CO₂, and ethene, drawing a single bond is wrong and costs the discriminating mark. Always draw O=O not O–O.
- **Noble gas inertness (N24 Q3c):** "Argon has configuration 2,8,8 — full outer shell — no need to gain, lose, or share electrons." WTTE accepted. Do NOT just say "it is inert" without explaining why.
- **Covalent bonding explanation:** Must link sharing to achieving a full outer shell. "They share electrons" alone is insufficient for a 3–4 mark question.
- **Metallic bonding properties:** "Conducts electricity" must be linked to delocalised electrons. "Has metal bonds" is not accepted.
- **CON rule:** Writing "covalent" for an ionic compound (or vice versa) will cancel all other correct marks in that question.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Electrons are donated in covalent bonding" | Donation = ionic. In covalent bonding, electrons are SHARED |
| "Ionic bonding = sharing electrons" | Ionic = TRANSFER of electrons; covalent = sharing |
| "Prokaryotes have no nucleus" | Wrong topic — but analogous error here: don't say "SiO₂ has covalent bonds" without adding "giant" when explaining insolubility |
| Drawing a single bond in O₂ or CO₂ | O₂ must show O=O (double bond); CO₂ must show O=C=O (two double bonds) |
| Forgetting lone pairs on oxygen atoms | Every O atom in H₂O, CO₂, H₂O₂, HCOOH has 2 lone pairs — always draw them |
| "He does not bond because it is stable" | Say "He has a full outer shell of 2 electrons — no need to share, gain, or lose electrons" |
| "Metals conduct because they have free electrons" | Say "delocalised electrons" — "free electrons" alone may not be accepted |
| "SiO₂ is insoluble because it has strong bonds" | Say "giant covalent/macromolecular structure" — bond strength alone is not the mark scheme phrasing |
| "Ionic compounds conduct electricity" | Only when MOLTEN or DISSOLVED (aqueous) — NOT when solid |
| Using "molecule" for ionic or giant covalent substances | Ionic substances are lattices; SiO₂ is a giant lattice — never say "a molecule of NaCl" |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Ionic bonding 4-step explanation** — practise writing it from memory: electron transfer → ions formed → ionic bond → electrostatic attraction. This is the highest-value bonding question type (N22 Q2g is 4 marks and appears every few sessions).
2. **Lewis structure selection** — appears in almost every session (18+ instances across all papers). Know the bond types and lone pair positions for all 10 molecules in the table above. Especially: O₂ (double bond), CO₂ (two double bonds, lone pairs on O only), H₂O₂ (lone pairs on each O), NH₃ (one lone pair on N).
3. **Covalent bonding explanation** — link sharing of electrons to achieving a full outer shell. Know why H₂ bonds but He does not (M21 Q1d is 4 marks).
4. **Giant covalent vs simple covalent properties** — learn the property distinctions in the comparison table. SiO₂ = giant covalent = high MP, insoluble.
5. **Drawing Lewis structures** — harder and worth more marks (N23 Q1c = 3 marks, M18 Q1c = 2 marks). Practise CO₂ and HCOOH especially: both involve double bonds and lone pairs.
6. **Metallic bonding properties** — link every property to delocalised electrons: conductivity, malleability, high MP. Use the exact phrase "delocalised electrons" in every answer.
