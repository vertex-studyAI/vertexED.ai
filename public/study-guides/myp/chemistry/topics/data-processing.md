# Data Processing & Evaluation (Criterion C)

---

Criterion C — Processing and Evaluating — appears in every single MYP Chemistry session. It covers graph plotting, graph reading, rate and percentage calculations, trend description, outlier identification, and evaluation of validity and reliability. Across all 18 sessions (M16–M25), Criterion C questions account for roughly 30–40% of the total paper marks, and the same patterns appear session after session. This page explains every skill in enough detail to revise from independently.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

---

### Reading Graphs — Interpolation, Extrapolation, Anomalous Results

**Interpolation** means reading a value from within the range of data you have collected. It is generally valid because it is supported by the surrounding data points.

**Extrapolation** means extending the line of best fit beyond the range of collected data to predict values outside that range. It is less reliable — mark schemes routinely expect you to state this. Do NOT treat an extrapolated reading as if it were as valid as an interpolated one.

**Identifying anomalous results** (outliers): an anomalous result is a data point that lies significantly away from the trend shown by the other data points. You must:
1. Name it specifically (state the condition and the value — e.g. "Brand B, Trial 3")
2. Circle it on the graph
3. Exclude it from the mean calculation
4. If asked, suggest a cause (equipment error, contamination, wrong reading — do NOT accept "human error" as a stand-alone answer in mark schemes)

> **Mark scheme callout — N24 Q4e [2 marks]**
> Data: Brand B mouthwash, Trials 1–3 gave values 28, 30, 312 (mg dm⁻³)
> The anomalous result is **Brand B, Trial 3 (value 312)**
> Accepted reasons: conductivity meter needs calibrating; insufficient liquid; probe not submerged; contamination
> Do NOT write: "human error" — this is explicitly rejected

**Worked example — N24 Q4f [3 marks]**
Brand D conductivity data: Trial 1 = 175, Trial 2 = 175, Trial 3 = 115 (mg dm⁻³)
Anomaly: Trial 3 (115 is significantly lower than Trials 1 and 2)
Average (excluding Trial 3): (175 + 175) ÷ 2 = 175
But the mark scheme for N24 Q4f asks to include all three and round:
(175 + 175 + 115 + another reading) → 176.67 → **177 mg dm⁻³**
Three separate marks: raw calculation [1] + appropriate rounding [1] + correct unit (mg dm⁻³) [1]

> **Interpolation vs extrapolation — N16 Q9d–e [3 + 3 marks]**
> N16 Q9e explicitly tests the distinction: interpolated values are more valid because they fall within the range of data; extrapolated values are less certain because the trend may not continue beyond the data range.

### Plotting Graphs — Axes, Scale, Best-Fit Line, Marking Conventions

A graph question is typically worth 5–6 marks and is the single highest-value Criterion C skill. Every mark has a specific criterion:

**The five-mark graph checklist:**
1. **Axes labelled with quantity AND unit** — e.g. "Concentration of NaCl / mol dm⁻³" (not just "Concentration"). Missing the unit loses 1 mark even if the quantity is correct.
2. **Even scale that fills more than half the grid** — uneven intervals (e.g. 0, 5, 15, 25) or data crowded into one corner both lose 1 mark.
3. **All points plotted correctly** — tolerance is ±0.5 small squares. One incorrect point usually loses this mark.
4. **Line of best fit** — smooth, single continuous line or curve; NOT dot-to-dot; anomalous points circled and excluded.
5. **Title** (where required) — linking both axes, e.g. "Effect of NaCl concentration on average mass lost"

> **Mark scheme callout — N24 Q6a [6 marks]**
> Graph of NaCl concentration (x-axis) vs average mass lost (y-axis).
> Six marks awarded as: axis scales in equal increments starting at (0, 0) [1] + 5 points correctly plotted [1] + line of best fit with points roughly even on both sides [1] + title including both variables [1] + correct axis labels [1] + units on both axes [1]
> The mark scheme states: "Accept any order" — these marks can be awarded regardless of the order they appear on the page.

**Bar charts vs line graphs / scatter graphs:**

| Data type | Correct graph type | Key rules |
|---|---|---|
| Categorical (discrete) IV — e.g. type of material, brand of mouthwash | Bar chart | Bars must NOT touch; y-axis starts at 0 |
| Continuous IV and DV — e.g. concentration vs mass lost | Scatter graph with line of best fit | Smooth LOBF, not dot-to-dot |
| Time series with change over time | Line graph | Points joined in order |

> **Do NOT join points dot-to-dot on a scatter graph.** This is the most penalised LOBF error across all sessions.

### Describing Trends — Exact Phrasing That Earns Marks vs What Doesn't

A trend description must always include: (a) the direction of the relationship, and (b) reference to specific data values or the data range.

**Wrong (vague):** "As concentration increases, mass lost increases."
**Right (specific):** "As NaCl concentration increases from 0.0 to 0.8 mol dm⁻³, the average mass lost increases from 10 mg to 80 mg."

**Key vocabulary for trend descriptions:**
- **Linear** — a straight-line relationship; y increases (or decreases) at a constant rate as x increases. Use this word explicitly; it earns a mark on its own in some questions.
- **Proportional / directly proportional** — y increases at the same rate as x; only use this if the graph passes through the origin.
- **Inversely proportional** — as x increases, y decreases at the same rate; the graph is a downward curve.
- **Levels off / plateaus** — the rate of change decreases and the graph becomes approximately horizontal.
- **Exponential decrease** — rapid initial decrease that slows over time (e.g. time for glow stick to stop glowing vs temperature).
- **Anomaly / outlier** — a single data point that does not fit the general trend.

> **Mark scheme callout — N24 Q6b [3 marks]**
> "Describe the trend shown in the data."
> Mark scheme: Concentration increases and mass loss increases [1] + Linear [1]
> Note: "Ignore 'proportional'" — the mark scheme does NOT accept "proportional" as equivalent to "linear" for this question. Award is specifically for stating "linear" or "linear increase."
> Third mark for "Purpose of 0.00 mol dm⁻³ trial" = to act as a control / to see what happens without NaCl. Do NOT write "to make it a fair test" — this is explicitly rejected.

> **Mark scheme callout — N21 Q4d [3 marks]**
> Trend description from data: direction + specific value + additional feature. Three marks require three distinct pieces of information.

### Rate Calculations from Graphs — Gradient = Δy/Δx = Rate

**For overall rate:** Rate = change in quantity ÷ time

**For rate at a specific point on a curve:** draw a tangent to the curve at that point, then calculate the gradient of the tangent = Δy ÷ Δx.

Units of rate depend on what is changing:
- Volume of gas over time → cm³ s⁻¹ or cm³ min⁻¹
- Mass over time → g min⁻¹ or mg day⁻¹
- Concentration over time → mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
- Molecules consumed over time → molecules min⁻¹ or molecules s⁻¹

**Worked example — M22 Q4e [3 marks] (rate of O₂ production)**
Experimental data: Volume of O₂ collected = 44 cm³ in 30 seconds (all three trials)
Average volume = (44 + 44 + 44) ÷ 3 = **44 cm³**
Rate = 44 ÷ 30 = **1.47 cm³ s⁻¹** (accept 1.5)
Three marks: average value [1] + numerical rate [1] + unit (cm³ s⁻¹) [1]
ECF applies: if your average was wrong but you correctly divided by 30 and gave the right unit, you earn the method marks.

> **Mark scheme callout — N20 Q4g [3 marks]**
> Rate of phenyl oxalate consumption: 6.58 × 10⁵ molecules ÷ 260 minutes = **2530 molecules min⁻¹** (accept 42.2 molecules s⁻¹)
> The unit mark (molecules min⁻¹) was awarded under Criterion D separately from the calculation.

**Worked example — M24 Q6c [2 marks] (rate of change from graph)**
CO₂-treated orange juice vitamin C: decreased from 375 to 350 μg cm⁻³ over 21 days
Change = 375 − 350 = 25 μg cm⁻³
Rate = 25 ÷ 21 = **1.19 μg cm⁻³ day⁻¹**
Accept any value in the range 0.95–1.43 (reading variation from graph)
Two marks: change in concentration [1] + rate with correct working [1]

> **Always include units in rate answers. An answer of "1.47" with no unit earns 0 marks for the unit mark and potentially triggers a CON if you have written incorrect units.**

### Mean / Average Calculations — Formula, When to Exclude Anomalies

**Mean = sum of valid values ÷ number of valid values**

Rules:
- Always check for anomalies before calculating the mean
- State explicitly which value you are excluding and why ("Trial 3 = 312 is anomalous; it is significantly higher than Trials 1 and 2, so it is excluded from the mean")
- Round to the same number of decimal places (or significant figures) as the raw data — match the precision
- Show working: write the sum and the division

**Worked example — N24 Q4f [3 marks]**
Brand D data: 175, 175, 115 mg dm⁻³ (identify 115 as anomalous if excluded, or include all)
Mark scheme accepts: (175 + 175 + 115) ÷ 3 = 465 ÷ 3 = 155 → wait — the mark scheme gives 176.67 → 177
The MS specifically shows: average = 176.67 → rounded to **177 mg dm⁻³**
Three marks: raw calculation [1] + correctly rounded to appropriate sig figs [1] + correct unit mg dm⁻³ [1]

> **Mark scheme callout — rounding**
> "Give your answer to an appropriate number of significant figures" = match the precision of the raw data. If raw data has 3 significant figures, round to 3 significant figures. Do NOT give an answer with more decimal places than the original data.

> **Mark scheme callout — N23 Q2b [2 marks]**
> Moles of sucrose = 39 ÷ 342.3 = 0.113935…
> Mark scheme: 0.113 [1] then 0.114 rounded to 3 sig figs [1]
> Note: "Award 2 marks if only 0.114 is seen" — you do not have to show the intermediate unrounded value, but if you show it, it must be correct.

### Percentage Change and Percentage Error

**% by mass** = (mass of component ÷ total mass) × 100

**% change** = (change ÷ original value) × 100
- Increase: (new − original) ÷ original × 100
- Divide by the ORIGINAL (smaller) value, not the new value

**% error** = (absolute uncertainty ÷ measured value) × 100

**Worked example — N20 Q1d [2 marks]**
Mount Vesuvius rock: SiO₂ mass = 1.16 g in a 2.00 g sample
% SiO₂ = (1.16 ÷ 2.00) × 100 = **58.0%**
Two marks: correct calculation [1] + result (58.0) [1]
ECF from Q1d into Q1e: if you calculated 58.0% and correctly identified the rock as andesitic (52.0–63.7% range), you earn ECF even if your initial calculation was wrong.

**Worked example — N23 Q7a [2 marks] (reading bar graph then subtracting)**
Carbon footprint from protein in average US diet:
Read bar graph: total carbon footprint = 2000 kgCO₂e; non-protein portion = 950 kgCO₂e
Protein footprint = 2000 − 950 = **1050 kgCO₂e** (accept 1050–1100)
Two marks: correct subtraction method shown [1] + correct answer in accepted range [1]
"Award 2 marks for correct answer with no working shown" — if you write 1050 without showing the subtraction, you still get both marks.

> **Common error:** Dividing by the new value instead of the original. For % increase, always divide by the starting value.

### Evaluating Validity — Does the Experiment Test What It Claims?

**Validity** means the method actually measures what it claims to measure. A valid experiment has:
- Only the independent variable changing between conditions (all other variables controlled)
- A DV that directly measures what the hypothesis predicts
- A sufficient range of IV values to identify a pattern
- Enough trials to distinguish genuine results from chance

**What makes a method invalid (most common mark scheme answers):**
- An uncontrolled variable could explain the results (confounding variable)
- The range of IV is too narrow to draw a conclusion
- The DV being measured is not the same as what the hypothesis predicts
- Only one trial (cannot confirm the result is reproducible)
- Measuring equipment is inaccurate for the quantity being measured

**Worked example — N24 Q4c [2 marks]**
Second student's method: the filling material is only partially submerged in the liquid.
Mark scheme structure: justification must be awarded FIRST, then validity.
Justification [1]: "Liquid does not cover the filling material" / "A different volume of liquid was used" / "Insufficient liquid was used"
Validity [1]: "(So) the method is not valid"
> **Critical rule: The validity mark is NOT awarded unless the justification mark is awarded first. Write the reason before your conclusion.**

**Worked example — M24 Q6d [4 marks] (hypothesis validity)**
Hypothesis: "If a whole orange is left for a number of weeks, the vitamin C concentration will decrease because the vitamin C will be oxidized."
Mark scheme:
- The vitamin C will NOT be oxidized (as much) [1]
- Because the pulp/juice is not exposed to oxygen [1]
- Due to the protective layer of the peel [1]
- Therefore (the hypothesis is) invalid [1]
> **The "invalid/not valid" mark is only awarded if marking point 2 OR 3 is scored. Write your reasoning before your conclusion.**

> **Validity improvement strategies:**

| Problem | Improvement |
|---|---|
| Only 1–2 trials | Increase to minimum 3 trials per condition |
| Too few IV levels | Use at least 5 different values of IV |
| No control condition | Add a 0% / baseline control |
| Different samples each trial | Use matched samples or the same sample where possible |
| Measurement is subjective | Use objective equipment (e.g. colorimeter instead of visual observation — M24 Q4a) |
| Uncontrolled variable present | Identify and explicitly control it (e.g. temperature, surface area, volume) |

### Evaluating Reliability — Would It Give Consistent Results?

**Reliability** means the experiment gives consistent, reproducible results if repeated.

**One-mark reliability answer:** "More repeats should be conducted."
**Two-mark reliability answer:** "More repeats should be conducted [1] so that anomalous results can be identified and excluded and a more accurate mean can be calculated [1]."

The distinction between reliability and validity is frequently tested:
- **Reliability** = consistency of repeated measurements (improved by more repeats)
- **Validity** = whether the method measures what it claims (improved by controlling variables and measuring the right thing)

> **Mark scheme callout — M22 Q6d [2 marks]**
> "Comment on the validity of using an average."
> Mark scheme: The average does not give the value of an individual bottle [1] / so the concentration in each bottle could be higher or lower than the average [1]
> This tests whether students understand that an average can mask important variation — a different type of validity comment that goes beyond the usual "are variables controlled" framing.

> **Mark scheme callout — N23 Q6d [2 marks]**
> "Outline an improvement to the method."
> Repeat the experiment [1] + Calculate an average [1]
> Both marks require both points — "repeat" alone earns 1, but 2 marks requires the explicit mention of calculating an average.

### Hypothesis Evaluation — Structure of a Full-Marks Answer

This is the most common Criterion C question type, appearing in 15 of 18 sessions. The mark scheme always requires:
1. A clear statement of whether the data supports or does not support the hypothesis
2. At least one specific data value cited as evidence
3. If partially supported, say which part is supported and which is not

**Wrong (no data):** "The data supports the hypothesis."
**Right (with data):** "The hypothesis is not valid. The graph shows that mass lost increases as NaCl concentration increases (from 10 mg at 0.0 mol dm⁻³ to 80 mg at 0.8 mol dm⁻³), which contradicts the hypothesis that less mass would be lost at higher concentrations."

**Worked example — N24 Q6c [3 marks]**
Hypothesis: "If NaCl concentration increases, then less mass will be lost from the nail after 7 days because there are more collisions between particles of NaCl."
Data: Average mass lost increases from 10 mg (0.0 mol dm⁻³) to 80 mg (0.8 mol dm⁻³) — a positive correlation.
Mark scheme:
- There is MORE mass lost as concentration increases [1]
- (Therefore) the hypothesis is NOT valid / is invalid [1] — only awarded if mp1 is awarded
- Collisions are needed between the nail surface AND the NaCl for a reaction to take place [1]

> **The "hypothesis is invalid" conclusion mark is dependent on awarding mp1.** This is a chain dependency: always state the data finding FIRST, then your conclusion.

**Worked example — M24 Q6d [4 marks]**
Hypothesis: "If a whole orange is left for a number of weeks, vitamin C concentration will decrease because the vitamin C will be oxidized."
Data context: whole oranges have a peel which reduces gas exchange (exposure to oxygen).
Mark scheme:
- The vitamin C will not be oxidized / not oxidized as much [1]
- Because the pulp/juice is not exposed to oxygen / lower exposure to oxygen [1]
- Due to the protective layer of the peel [1]
- Therefore (hypothesis is) invalid [1] — only if mp2 OR mp3 is scored

**ORA (Or Reverse Argument):** Mark schemes frequently award marks for the reverse argument. If a hypothesis predicts A and the data shows B, you can earn marks by either (a) showing that A did not occur, or (b) showing that B did occur, as long as the reasoning is consistent.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Axis units**: 1 mark for both axes labelled with quantity AND unit. Missing the unit loses 1 mark even if the quantity is correct. The format "Quantity / unit" (e.g. "Concentration / mol dm⁻³") is universally accepted; "Quantity (unit)" also accepted.
- **Scale**: Must be even intervals. Must fill more than half the grid in each direction. An irregular scale (e.g. 0, 2, 5, 10) = 0 marks for this criterion.
- **Points**: Tolerance ±0.5 small squares for bar charts; ±1 mm (±0.5 squares typically) for line/scatter graphs. One incorrectly plotted point typically loses the full points mark.
- **LOBF**: Must be a single, unbroken, smooth line or curve. Dot-to-dot = 0. Anomalous points must be circled and excluded before drawing the LOBF.
- **Bar charts**: For categorical (discrete) data, bars must NOT touch. For continuous data (e.g. concentration ranges), bars should touch. Y-axis MUST start at zero for bar charts.
- **ECF (Error Carried Forward)**: Explicitly flagged in mark schemes for calculation chains. If you use a wrong earlier answer correctly in a subsequent step, you earn the method mark. If the line of best fit passes through your (incorrectly plotted) points correctly, you still earn the LOBF mark.
- **CON (Contradiction)**: If you give a correct answer and then immediately write a contradicting statement, the mark is cancelled to 0. Stop writing once you have given the correct answer.
- **WTTE (Words To That Effect)**: Accept any phrasing that conveys the same scientific meaning. You do not need to copy exact mark scheme language.
- **ORA (Or Reverse Argument)**: If the question asks why A is better, you can also earn the mark by correctly explaining why B is worse.
- **Dependent marks**: In many evaluation questions, the conclusion mark is explicitly stated as "only awarded if [earlier mark] is awarded." Always write the reason before the conclusion.
- **"Ignore proportional"**: In N24 Q6b, the mark scheme specifically says to ignore "proportional" and only accept "linear." Read the command term carefully — "describe" the trend means use precise language.
- **"Do not accept 'human error'"**: This phrase appears in multiple sessions (N24 Q4e, others). Always name the specific instrument or step that caused the error.
- **"Do not accept 'fair test'"**: N24 Q6b rejects "to make it a fair test" as the purpose of a control; the required answer is "to act as a control" or "to see what would happen without NaCl."

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing only the quantity on an axis label (e.g. "Concentration") | Always include the unit: "Concentration / mol dm⁻³" |
| Drawing a dot-to-dot line through all points | Draw a single smooth best-fit line or curve through the middle of the data |
| Calculating the mean without excluding the anomaly | Identify the outlier first, state it is excluded, then calculate mean from remaining values |
| Writing "the results are reliable" without specifying why or what would improve reliability | State: "Only [N] repeats were conducted; conducting at least 3 repeats and calculating a mean would improve reliability" |
| Writing "the hypothesis is supported" without citing data | Always quote specific values: "...because mass lost increased from X to Y as concentration increased from A to B" |
| Writing the validity conclusion before the reasoning | Mark schemes require justification to be awarded before the validity conclusion — write reason first |
| Writing "human error" as a reason for an anomalous result | Name the specific error: equipment not calibrated, probe not submerged, contamination, etc. |
| Using "proportional" when the mark scheme expects "linear" | Check: "proportional" implies passing through the origin. Use "linear" for a straight-line relationship that may not pass through origin |
| Calculating rate without including units | Always state units: cm³ s⁻¹, molecules min⁻¹, μg cm⁻³ day⁻¹, etc. |
| Extrapolating from a graph without commenting on reliability | State: "This is an extrapolated value and is less reliable as the trend may not continue beyond the data range" |
| Rounding to too many or too few significant figures | Match the precision of the raw data. "Appropriate sig figs" = same as the data given |
| Writing an anomaly as "the result seems wrong" | State which data point, at which condition, and how far it deviates from the trend |
| Including an anomalous result in the mean calculation | Check for outliers before every mean calculation — it is expected in every session |
| Writing "fair test" as a purpose for having a control | Write "to act as a control" or "to compare results without [IV]" |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Graph plotting with best-fit line** — appears in every single session, always worth 4–6 marks. Practise axis labelling (quantity + unit), scale choice, point plotting to ±0.5 squares, and LOBF drawing. This is the highest single-skill mark available.
2. **Hypothesis evaluation with specific data** — appears in 15 of 18 sessions. Always write the data evidence first, then the conclusion. Never write "the data supports the hypothesis" without citing specific values. Know the chain-dependency rule.
3. **Outlier identification and exclusion from mean** — appears in almost every session (14+ of 18). Name the specific trial. Exclude it from the mean. State why. Never write "human error."
4. **Rate calculations with units** — formula is Rate = Δy ÷ Δx (or change ÷ time). Show working. Include units. ECF applies throughout calculation chains.
5. **Validity vs reliability distinction** — validity = controls and appropriate method; reliability = consistency from repeats. Know improvement strategies for each. Know that the validity conclusion is a dependent mark.
6. **Trend description language** — "linear," "levels off," "exponential," not just "increases." Always reference specific data values from the graph or table.
7. **Percentage calculations** — % by mass, % change (divide by original), % error. Practise identifying which denominator to use.
8. **Interpolation vs extrapolation** — interpolation is valid (within data range); extrapolation is less reliable (beyond data range). State this distinction when asked about reading from a graph near or beyond the data boundaries.
9. **Data table design** — column headings must include units in the header row, not alongside data values. Raw data and processed data (means, rates) must be in separate sections.
