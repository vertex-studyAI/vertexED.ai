# Energy in Reactions

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Exothermic vs Endothermic

In an **exothermic reaction**, energy is released to the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings increases. The products have less energy than the reactants — the difference is released as heat. The sign of ΔH is **negative**.

In an **endothermic reaction**, energy is absorbed from the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings decreases. The products have more energy than the reactants. The sign of ΔH is **positive**.

| Feature | Exothermic | Endothermic |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Energy flow | Released to surroundings | Absorbed from surroundings |
| Temperature of surroundings | Increases | Decreases |
| Products vs reactants | Products at lower energy | Products at higher energy |
| Sign of ΔH | Negative (−) | Positive (+) |
| Real examples | Combustion, hand warmers, neutralisation, iron oxidation | Thermal decomposition of NaHCO₃, dissolving ammonium nitrate, photosynthesis |

**Energy profile diagram — exothermic:** Reactants start high; products end lower; the hump in between is the activation energy (Ea). ΔH is negative — the drop from reactants to products is shown as a downward arrow.

**Energy profile diagram — endothermic:** Reactants start low; products end higher. Ea is the height from reactant level to the peak. ΔH is positive — products sit above reactants.

**M16 Q1a (1 mark):** State the name given to a reaction in which heat is released. → exothermic

**M23 Q1a (1 mark):** Select the term for a reaction that releases energy. → exothermic

**M25 Q2a (1 mark):** Select the type of change that takes place when a hand is placed on a thermochromic T-shirt (the T-shirt absorbs energy). → endothermic

> Do NOT write "gives out heat" or "increases temperature" when the question asks for the scientific term — only "exothermic" will be awarded.

### Bond Breaking and Bond Forming

Every chemical reaction involves breaking bonds in the reactants and forming new bonds in the products. The direction of energy flow is fixed and must be stated explicitly:

- **Breaking bonds requires (absorbs) energy — it is endothermic.** Energy must be put in to pull atoms apart against the electrostatic forces holding them together.
- **Forming bonds releases energy — it is exothermic.** Energy is released when atoms come together to form new bonds.

The overall energy change (ΔH) is determined by the balance between these two steps:

| Step | Energy direction | Type |
|------|-----------------|------|
| Breaking bonds in reactants | Absorbs energy | Endothermic step |
| Forming bonds in products | Releases energy | Exothermic step |
| If forming > breaking | Net release | Exothermic reaction (ΔH negative) |
| If breaking > forming | Net absorption | Endothermic reaction (ΔH positive) |

This logic also explains why **exothermic reactions release heat** — more energy is released when new bonds form in the products than is needed to break the bonds in the reactants.

**N16 Q3b (2 marks):** State if the reactions in fireworks are endothermic or exothermic. Justify your answer. → [1] energy/heat is released (or: release more energy than they absorb); [1] so the reaction is exothermic. Note: second mark is only awarded if the first is awarded.

> Do NOT write "bond breaking releases energy." Bond breaking always ABSORBS energy. This is one of the most common errors in this topic and examiners explicitly look for the correct direction.

### Activation Energy

**Activation energy (Ea)** is the minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur. It is the energy barrier that must be overcome to start the reaction.

On an energy profile diagram, Ea is shown as the height from the reactant energy level to the peak (the transition state). It is always a positive value regardless of whether the overall reaction is exothermic or endothermic — every reaction has an activation energy.

- Higher Ea → fewer particles have enough energy → slower reaction
- Lower Ea → more particles have enough energy → faster reaction
- Raising temperature increases particle kinetic energy → more particles exceed Ea → faster reaction

Activation energy is NOT the same as the overall energy change (ΔH). ΔH is the difference between the reactant and product energy levels. Ea is the height from the reactant level to the top of the peak.

**M21 Q2e (2 marks):** Explain why the rate of a decomposition reaction increases the closer an exoplanet is to the star it orbits. → [1] temperature is higher / more kinetic energy; [1] more molecules have enough energy to overcome the activation energy / rate of reaction increases.

> Do NOT confuse Ea with ΔH. They appear on the same diagram but measure different things. Ea is always from the reactant level to the peak; ΔH is from reactant level to product level.

### Catalysts and Activation Energy

A **catalyst** is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself being consumed in the reaction.

Catalysts work by providing an **alternative reaction pathway** that has a **lower activation energy**. More particles now have enough energy to react, so the reaction is faster. The overall energy change (ΔH) is unchanged — catalysts affect rate, not thermodynamics.

On an energy profile diagram with a catalyst: the peak is lower (lower Ea), but the reactant and product energy levels remain exactly the same.

**The 3-part mark scheme answer — required for all 3-mark catalyst questions:**
1. Provides an alternative reaction pathway
2. With a lower activation energy (Ea)
3. Is not consumed in the reaction / is regenerated

| Marks available | Points required |
|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 mark | Lowers activation energy OR provides alternative pathway |
| 2 marks | Lower Ea AND alternative pathway (both needed) |
| 3 marks | Lower Ea + alternative pathway + not consumed/regenerated |

**M23 Q3b (3 marks):** Grey and blue hydrogen are produced in the presence of a nickel catalyst. Explain how a catalyst works in a chemical reaction. → [1] speeds up the rate of reaction; [1] by lowering the activation energy OR providing an alternative pathway; [1] without being used up.

**N24 Q2d (3 marks):** Explain how a catalyst works to increase the rate of methanol production. → [1] providing an alternative pathway; [1] with a lower energy (activation energy); [1] without itself being used up.

**M16 Q3c (2 marks):** Using the energy profile diagram showing catalysed and uncatalysed reactions, outline how catalysts work. → [1] catalyst gives a lower energy path; [1] Ea/energy barrier is lower OR provides an alternative reaction pathway OR alters the mechanism; so more molecules have enough energy to react.

> Do NOT write "a catalyst speeds up the reaction" alone for a 2- or 3-mark question. This earns at most 1 mark in some sessions and 0 in others. Always include activation energy and alternative pathway for full marks.

### Calorimetry — q = mcΔT

**Calorimetry** measures heat energy changes in reactions. The formula used in every MYP calorimetry calculation is:

> **q = mcΔT**

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical unit |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| q | Heat energy released or absorbed | Joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ) |
| m | Mass of water being heated | grams (g) |
| c | Specific heat capacity of water | 4.18 or 4.19 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ (the paper always states the value) |
| ΔT | Temperature change = T_final − T_initial | °C |

To convert J → kJ: divide by 1000. To compare fuels: calculate energy per gram = q ÷ mass of fuel burned.

**Full worked example — N16 Q1c (5 marks):** "The combustion of 1.00 g of ethanol releases 30.0 kJ. Calculate the mass of ethanol required to heat 400 g of water from 10.0 °C to 98.0 °C. c = 4.19 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹. Give your answer to 3 significant figures."

- Step 1 — ΔT = 98.0 − 10.0 = **88.0 °C**
- Step 2 — q = mcΔT = 400 × 4.19 × 88.0 = **147 488 J = 147.488 kJ**
- Step 3 — mass of ethanol = 147.488 ÷ 30.0 = **4.92 g** (3 s.f.)

Mark scheme: [1] attempt to substitute; [1] 147 488 J or 147.488 kJ; [1] division step; [1] 4.92; [1] unit g. ECF applies — if ΔT is wrong but subsequent method is correct, marks 3–5 can still be awarded.

**Follow-on — N16 Q1d (2 marks):** Identify whether ethanol or butane is the better fuel. Butane: molar mass 58 g mol⁻¹, releases 2877 kJ mol⁻¹. → [1] butane releases 49.6 kJ g⁻¹ (2877 ÷ 58); ethanol releases 30.0 kJ g⁻¹; [1] butane is the better fuel because it releases more energy per gram. ORA accepted.

> Do NOT forget the unit mark — in N16 Q1c it is awarded separately and can be lost independently of the numerical answer. Always write "g" after the final answer.

### Temperature Changes in Practical Contexts

Many exam questions place energy changes in a practical, investigative context — hand warmers, metal reactivity experiments, etching reactions. The core principle is simple:

- **Temperature rises → exothermic reaction** (energy released to surroundings)
- **Temperature falls → endothermic reaction** (energy absorbed from surroundings)

**Hand warmer (M16, context):** Iron powder reacts with oxygen in the presence of a catalyst and releases energy → exothermic → temperature of the hand warmer increases. This is the context for M16 Q1a. The reusable sodium ethanoate hand warmer in M16 Q2 releases heat on crystallisation (exothermic); to reuse it, you heat the pack so the crystals dissolve (endothermic).

**Metal reactivity from ΔT (N17 Q3):** When metals are added to copper sulfate solution, the more reactive the metal, the larger the temperature change (more energy released from the more vigorous displacement reaction). Mark scheme: "the larger the temperature change/rise, the more reactive the metal."

**HF added to water (M22 Q2f–2g):** Temperature rose from 20 °C to 39 °C → ΔT = +19 °C (mark scheme accepts 14–15 with ECF from incorrect thermometer reading) → reaction identified as exothermic because the temperature increased.

**M22 Q2g (1 mark):** Use the temperature change from part (f) to identify the type of reaction. → exothermic.

> Do NOT say the dependent variable in a calorimetry experiment is "temperature" — it must be "temperature change" or "change in temperature" (ΔT). A thermometer or calibrated temperature probe is required equipment; "ruler" or "pH probe" are not accepted.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Exothermic/endothermic (1 mark):** The mark is for the correct term. WTTE applies in description questions, but if the command term is "state the term" only "exothermic" or "endothermic" is accepted — not descriptions like "releases heat."
- **Catalyst questions (2–3 marks):** The three-part answer (alternative pathway + lower Ea + not consumed) is tested across 7 sessions. For 2-mark questions, use the first two points. Writing "speeds up reaction" alone earns at most 1 mark for a 2-mark question and 0 for a 3-mark explain question.
- **Calorimetry (N16 Q1c):** ECF applies throughout. The unit mark (g) is awarded separately and can be lost independently. Always write the unit.
- **Fuel comparison (N16 Q1d):** Must compare energy per gram (or per mole with explicit calculation). ORA accepted. "Butane is better because it is a fossil fuel" scores 0 — must reference energy data.
- **Bond breaking vs forming:** Examiners expect the explicit language: breaking bonds absorbs energy (endothermic), forming bonds releases energy (exothermic). "Bonds break and form" without energy direction = no mark.
- **Temperature change as evidence:** Temperature increase = exothermic. Temperature decrease = endothermic. M22 Q2g tests exactly this. The justification must state the direction of temperature change to earn the mark.
- **ΔH vs Ea:** These are frequently confused in diagram questions. Ea = height from reactant level to peak. ΔH = height from reactant level to product level. A catalyst lowers Ea but does NOT change ΔH.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing "releases heat" when the question asks for the term | The answer is one word: exothermic |
| "Bond breaking releases energy" | Bond breaking ABSORBS energy. Forming bonds releases energy |
| "A catalyst speeds up the reaction" for a 3-mark question | Must add "provides alternative pathway" + "lowers activation energy" + "not consumed" |
| "A catalyst lowers activation energy and changes ΔH" | Catalysts lower Ea only — the overall ΔH is unchanged |
| Forgetting the unit in q = mcΔT calculations | Unit mark is awarded separately; always write "g" or "J" as appropriate |
| Saying "temperature" is the dependent variable in calorimetry | The dependent variable is "temperature change" or ΔT |
| "The catalyst is used up in small amounts" | Catalysts are NOT consumed — they are regenerated at the end |
| Confusing Ea with ΔH on an energy profile diagram | Ea = reactant level to peak; ΔH = reactant level to product level |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Catalyst mechanism (highest frequency — 7 sessions).** Drill the 3-part answer until automatic: (1) provides an alternative reaction pathway; (2) with a lower activation energy; (3) without being consumed/regenerated. For 1-mark: just "lowers activation energy." For 2-mark: add "alternative pathway." For 3-mark: add "not consumed."
2. **Exothermic/endothermic identification (8 sessions).** Easy marks but only if you use the exact term. Read the command term carefully — "state the term" requires one word; "justify" requires a sentence linking temperature change to energy direction.
3. **Bond breaking = endothermic, bond forming = exothermic.** Write this distinction out repeatedly. It is the reverse of what many students assume and is explicitly penalised in mark schemes.
4. **Calorimetry q = mcΔT.** N16 Q1c is the only full 5-mark calorimetry calculation across all 18 sessions. Know the formula, the value of c (4.18–4.19 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ — use what the paper gives), how to calculate ΔT, and how to convert J to kJ. Practice the N16 worked example until you can do it from memory.
5. **Energy profile diagrams.** Be able to sketch and annotate: reactant level, product level, transition state peak, Ea as an arrow from reactant level to peak, ΔH as an arrow from reactant to product level. Know how a catalyst changes the diagram: lower peak, same reactant and product levels.
6. **Temperature change practicals (Criterion B/C context).** Know that temperature probe or thermometer is required. Insulated calorimeter reduces heat loss — always mention heat loss to surroundings as the main source of error. The dependent variable is always "temperature change," not "temperature."
