## 1. Key Concepts

### Homologous Series Overview

A **homologous series** is a family of organic compounds that share the same functional group, the same general formula, and similar chemical properties. Consecutive members differ only by CH₂. Knowing which series a compound belongs to — by spotting its functional group — is the single most tested skill in MYP Chemistry organic questions.

| Series | General formula | Functional group | Suffix | Example |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|--------|---------|
| Alkanes | CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ | None — only C–C single bonds | -ane | ethane (C₂H₆) |
| Alkenes | CₙH₂ₙ | C=C double bond | -ene | ethene (C₂H₄) |
| Alcohols | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH | –OH (hydroxyl) | -ol | ethanol (C₂H₅OH) |
| Carboxylic acids | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH | –COOH (carboxyl) | -anoic acid | ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) |
| Esters | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOCₘH₂ₘ₊₁ | –COO– (ester linkage) | -yl -anoate | butyl ethanoate |

**Alkanes are saturated** (only single bonds). **Alkenes are unsaturated** (contain at least one C=C). This distinction matters for naming, for combustion, and for polymerisation.

> **Do NOT** write "organic compound" when asked to name the class — you must identify the specific homologous series (e.g. "ester" or "alcohol"). "Organic compound" alone earns 0 marks (M16 Q4a).

**M19 Q2f (4 marks) — Worked example:** Four VOC molecules were given as structural formulae. Students had to select both the class AND the IUPAC name for each. Correct answers: ethyl ethanoate (ester), propan-1-ol (alcohol), pentanoic acid (carboxylic acid), butane (alkane). The mark scheme awarded 1 mark per correct class + name pair — both had to be right to earn the mark.

### Functional Groups

A **functional group** is the atom or group of atoms responsible for a compound's characteristic reactions. Identifying the functional group in a structural formula is the highest-frequency question type — it appears in virtually every session.

| Functional group | Symbol | Homologous series | How to spot it |
|-----------------|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Hydroxyl | –OH | Alcohol | An oxygen with one hydrogen, attached to a carbon chain |
| Carboxyl | –COOH | Carboxylic acid | A C=O (carbonyl) with an –OH on the same carbon — the combination makes –COOH |
| Ester linkage | –COO– | Ester | An oxygen bonded between two carbons, one of which also has a C=O: –C(=O)–O– |
| Carbon–carbon double bond | C=C | Alkene | Two carbons joined by a double bond in the chain |
| None | — | Alkane | Only C–C single bonds and C–H bonds; no other groups |

**How to identify in exam questions:** Start by looking for oxygen. If there is no oxygen: alkane or alkene — check for C=C. If there is oxygen: look at how it is bonded. –OH hanging off a chain = alcohol. –COOH at the end = carboxylic acid. –COO– bridging between two carbons = ester. Two oxygens both bonded to the same carbon as C=O and O– = ester linkage.

> **Do NOT** confuse –COOH (carboxylic acid) with –COO– (ester). The difference is one hydrogen atom. –COOH has the O–H still present; –COO– has lost the O–H because it has reacted with an alcohol.

**M23 Q1c (2 marks) — Worked example:** Two functional groups were listed: –OH and –COOH. Students had to identify the organic family for each. Mark scheme: –OH = alcohol; –COOH = carboxylic acid. Both required for full marks.

**M24 Q1b (1 mark):** The –OH group in glucose. Answer: alcohol / hydroxyl. "Hydroxyl group" accepted but "glucose functional group" is not precise enough.

**N22 Q6a (2 marks):** Identify functional groups in adenosine and caffeine from structural diagrams. Adenosine = alcohol (–OH); caffeine = alkene (C=C). One mark each.

### IUPAC Naming

IUPAC names are built in two steps: (1) count the carbons in the longest chain to get the prefix; (2) identify the functional group to get the suffix.

| Carbons in chain | Prefix |
|-----------------|--------|
| 1 | meth- |
| 2 | eth- |
| 3 | prop- |
| 4 | but- |
| 5 | pent- |
| 6 | hex- |
| 10 | dec- |

**Suffix rules:**
- Alkane: prefix + **-ane** → methane, ethane, propane, butane, pentane
- Alkene: prefix + **-ene** → ethene, propene
- Alcohol: prefix + **-anol** (or **-an-1-ol**) → methanol, ethanol, propan-1-ol
- Carboxylic acid: prefix + **-anoic acid** → methanoic acid, ethanoic acid, propanoic acid
- Ester: alcohol part + **-yl** + acid part + **-anoate** → butyl ethanoate, pentyl ethanoate

> **Do NOT** use common names in exams. "Formic acid" is not accepted — write "methanoic acid." "Acetic acid" is not accepted — write "ethanoic acid." The mark scheme explicitly rejects common names (M18 Q1b).

**M18 Q1b (1 mark) — Worked example:** "State the systematic name for formic acid (HCOOH)." Answer: methanoic acid. Formic acid = 0 marks.

**N20 Q3b (2 marks):** State the IUPAC names of CH₂=CH₂ and CH₃CH=CH₂. Answers: ethene (2 carbons + ene); propene (3 carbons + ene). One mark each.

**M21 Q3b (2 marks):** State the name AND class of C₅H₁₂. Answer: pentane (5 carbons, only single bonds) and alkane. Both required.

### Structural Formulae

A **structural formula** shows how atoms are connected. MYP Chemistry distinguishes between:
- **Full (displayed) structural formula:** every single bond is drawn as a line; no bonds may be implied or grouped.
- **Semi-structural formula:** atoms are grouped but bonds between groups may be implied (e.g. CH₃CH₂OH).

The mark scheme almost always requires the **full structural formula** when it says "draw the structural formula." Semi-structural formulae are explicitly rejected (N16 Q1a, N21 Q3f).

**Key structures to know by heart:**

| Compound | Full structural formula | Key bond to show |
|----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Ethane | CH₃–CH₃ (all C–H bonds drawn) | C–C single bond |
| Propane | CH₃–CH₂–CH₃ | C–C–C chain, all H attached |
| Butane | CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ | Four-carbon chain, all H |
| Ethene | CH₂=CH₂ | C=C double bond (must show both lines) |
| Ethanol | CH₃–CH₂–OH | –OH with O–H bond shown |
| Ethanoic acid | CH₃–C(=O)–OH | C=O AND O–H both drawn |
| Methanoic acid | H–C(=O)–OH | C=O AND O–H on the same carbon |

For a **Lewis structure**, you must also show lone pairs (pairs of dots on O and N atoms) in addition to all bonds.

> **Do NOT** write a condensed formula (e.g. CH₃COOH) when "full structural formula" is asked — every bond must be a drawn line. Do NOT omit the C=O or the O–H in carboxylic acid diagrams; both bonds must appear for the mark.

**N16 Q1a (2 marks) — Worked example:** Draw full structural formulae of butane and ethanol. Butane: four carbons in a chain with all C–H bonds (10 H atoms total). Ethanol: –CH₂–OH with the O–H bond explicitly drawn. The mark scheme states condensed formulae are not accepted.

**M17 Q2a (3 marks):** Draw structural formula of ethanoic acid; state the name of the functional group. The C=O bond and the O–H bond must both be visible. Functional group = "carboxyl" or "carboxylic acid" (both accepted).

### Combustion of Hydrocarbons

**Complete combustion** occurs when there is excess oxygen. The products are always CO₂ and water.

> General equation: Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

**Incomplete combustion** occurs when oxygen is limited. Products include carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon (soot) instead of CO₂.

**How to balance a complete combustion equation:**
1. Write the skeleton: CₙHₘ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
2. Balance C first: coefficient of CO₂ = n
3. Balance H next: coefficient of H₂O = m/2
4. Balance O last: coefficient of O₂ = n + m/4

| Compound | Balanced equation |
|----------|-------------------|
| Propane (C₃H₈) | C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O |
| Pentane (C₅H₁₂) | C₅H₁₂ + 8O₂ → 5CO₂ + 6H₂O |
| Ethane (C₂H₆) | C₂H₆ + 3½O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O (or multiply through by 2) |

> **Do NOT** forget to balance oxygen last — students most often lose marks by balancing oxygen first and getting it wrong after changing carbon and hydrogen coefficients.

**M23 Q1b (2 marks) — Worked example:** Balance the combustion of propane. C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O. One mark for correct products; one mark for correct coefficients.

**M21 Q3c (2 marks):** Balance complete combustion of C₅H₁₂. C₅H₁₂ + 8O₂ → 5CO₂ + 6H₂O.

### Esterification

Esterification is a **condensation reaction** between an **alcohol** and a **carboxylic acid** that produces an **ester** and **water**.

> Alcohol + Carboxylic acid ⇌ Ester + Water

The reaction is **reversible** (equilibrium). To maximise ester yield: remove water as it forms, use excess of one reactant, or store at low temperature.

**How to name the ester:**
- Take the alcohol name, change the suffix to **-yl**
- Take the carboxylic acid name, change the suffix to **-anoate**
- Ester name = [alcohol]-yl [acid]-anoate

| Alcohol | Carboxylic acid | Ester product |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| methanol | ethanoic acid | methyl ethanoate |
| ethanol | ethanoic acid | ethyl ethanoate |
| butanol | ethanoic acid | butyl ethanoate |
| pentanol | ethanoic acid | pentyl ethanoate |
| ethanol | methanoic acid | ethyl methanoate |

> **Do NOT** write "H₂O" when asked to name the small molecule lost — write "water." The mark scheme explicitly rejects "H₂O" (M16 Q4c).

> **Do NOT** forget to name **both** reactants when asked "what are the reactants of [ester name]?" You must give both the alcohol AND the carboxylic acid — giving only one earns 0 marks (N17 Q2e).

**M16 Q4c (1 mark) — Worked example:** "State the name of the small molecule lost in esterification." Answer: water. H₂O alone = 0 marks.

**N17 Q2e (3 marks):** Select structural formula of butyl ethanoate and state its two reactants. Structural formula: CH₃COO(CH₂)₃CH₃. Reactants: ethanoic acid AND butanol. Both required for the full mark.

**N17 Q2d (4 marks):** Explain why perfumes should be stored in cold dry conditions. Low temperature slows evaporation (and shifts equilibrium right, favouring ester formation). Absence of water prevents the reverse reaction (hydrolysis of the ester back to alcohol + acid).

### Polymerisation

**Addition polymerisation** uses alkene monomers. The C=C double bond in each monomer opens, and the monomers join into a long chain with no by-product.

> n(CH₂=CH₂) → –[CH₂–CH₂]ₙ– (polyethylene/polythene)

The monomer must be an **alkene** (unsaturated). The polymer is **saturated** — the double bond disappears.

> **Do NOT** say the monomer is an alkane — addition polymerisation requires the C=C double bond. If the monomer has no double bond, addition polymerisation cannot occur.

**Condensation polymerisation** involves two different monomers (e.g. an alcohol and a carboxylic acid) reacting together with loss of water at each step. This produces polyesters.

| Type | Monomer required | By-product | Example |
|------|-----------------|-----------|---------|
| Addition | Alkene (C=C double bond) | None | Polyethylene, polypropylene |
| Condensation | Alcohol + carboxylic acid (or diamine + dicarboxylic acid) | Water | Polyester, nylon |

**N19 Q8a (3 marks) — Worked example:** Select the functional group class in polymer monomers. Answers: ester (–COO–); alcohol (–OH); alkene (C=C). One mark each.

**M23 Q7a (1 mark):** Select the correct ester product formed in polyester synthesis (alcohol + carboxylic acid). Must show the correct ester linkage (–COO–).

### Crude Oil and Cracking

**Crude oil** is a mixture of hydrocarbons (mostly alkanes of varying chain lengths), formed from the remains of ancient marine organisms. It is separated by **fractional distillation**.

**Fractional distillation process:**
1. Crude oil is heated and vaporises.
2. Vapour rises up a fractionating column, which has a temperature gradient (hotter at the bottom, cooler at the top).
3. Different fractions condense at different heights, depending on their boiling point.
4. Shorter chains have lower boiling points → condense near the top. Longer chains have higher boiling points → condense near the bottom.

| Fraction | Chain length | Boiling point | Use |
|----------|-------------|--------------|-----|
| Petroleum gases | C₁–C₄ | Lowest | Fuel (LPG) |
| Petrol/gasoline | C₅–C₁₀ | Low | Car fuel |
| Kerosene | C₁₀–C₁₆ | Medium | Jet fuel |
| Diesel | C₁₄–C₂₀ | Medium-high | Lorry/bus fuel |
| Fuel oil | C₂₀–C₅₀ | High | Ships, power stations |
| Bitumen | C₅₀+ | Highest | Road surfaces |

**Cracking** is the decomposition of long-chain alkanes into shorter, more useful molecules. Long-chain fractions are in lower demand; cracking converts them into shorter alkanes and alkenes.

> Long-chain alkane → shorter alkane + alkene(s)

**How to deduce an unknown cracking product:** Balance the carbon atoms and hydrogen atoms on both sides. What is not accounted for on the right side must be the unknown fragment.

> **Do NOT** give only the name of an unknown cracking fragment — the mark scheme requires the **molecular formula**. "Pentane" alone is rejected; "C₅H₁₂" is required (N20 Q3c).

**N20 Q3c (1 mark) — Worked example:** C₁₀H₂₂ → C₂H₄ + C₃H₆ + X. Deduce the formula of X. Left side: 10 C, 22 H. Right side so far: 2+3 = 5 C; 4+6 = 10 H. Therefore X = C₅H₁₂. Do NOT write "pentane" alone.

**M18 Q6b (3 marks):** Outline the process of fractional distillation. Mark scheme key points: heated crude oil vaporises; rises up column; cools at different heights; different fractions condense at different temperatures/heights. All three points needed for 3 marks.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **"Water" not "H₂O"** — when asked to name the small molecule lost in esterification, the answer must be the word "water". "H₂O" alone is explicitly rejected (M16 Q4c).
- **"Ester" not "organic compound"** — naming the class of a compound requires the specific homologous series. "Organic compound" earns 0 marks (M16 Q4a).
- **Both reactants required** — when naming the reactants for an ester, both the alcohol AND the carboxylic acid must be given. Naming only one = 0 marks (N17 Q2e).
- **Molecular formula for cracking** — when deducing an unknown cracking fragment, write the molecular formula (C₅H₁₂), not just the name "pentane" (N20 Q3c).
- **All bonds in structural formulae** — when "full structural formula" is specified, every bond must be drawn as a line. Condensed formulae (CH₃COOH) and semi-structural formulae are rejected (N16 Q1a, N21 Q3f).
- **Systematic names only** — common names (formic, acetic, propanoic = acceptable systematic, but formic = rejected) are not accepted. Always use IUPAC (M18 Q1b).
- **Class + name both needed** — for classification questions, both the homologous series AND the IUPAC name must be correct to earn the mark (M19 Q2f).
- **CON risk** — stating the correct ester product but also incorrectly stating that –OH is retained = 0 marks. State the correct answer clearly without contradicting it.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing "H₂O" as the by-product of esterification | Write "water" — H₂O alone is rejected |
| Writing "organic compound" when asked for the class | State the specific homologous series: alkane, alkene, alcohol, carboxylic acid, or ester |
| Giving only one reactant for an ester | Name both the alcohol AND the carboxylic acid |
| Writing just "pentane" for an unknown cracking product | Give the molecular formula: C₅H₁₂ |
| Drawing a condensed structural formula when "full structural formula" is asked | Draw every single bond as a line; show all C–H bonds individually |
| Writing "formic acid" or "acetic acid" | Use systematic names: methanoic acid, ethanoic acid |
| Confusing –COOH (carboxylic acid) with –COO– (ester) | –COOH still has its O–H; –COO– has lost the O–H (it reacted with an alcohol) |
| Forgetting to show C=O in a carboxylic acid structural formula | Both C=O and O–H must be drawn for –COOH to be recognisable |
| Saying alkene monomers "contain C=C after polymerisation" | The C=C double bond opens during addition polymerisation; the polymer is saturated |
| Balancing oxygen before carbon and hydrogen in combustion | Always balance C first, then H, then O last |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Functional group identification** — given any structural formula, identify the organic class by spotting –OH (alcohol), –COOH (carboxylic acid), –COO– (ester), C=C (alkene), or nothing extra (alkane). Practise on at least 10 unfamiliar structures.
2. **Drawing full structural formulae** — practise drawing ethanol, ethanoic acid, methanoic acid, propane, butane, and ethene from memory, showing every single bond. Check that carboxylic acid diagrams show both C=O and O–H.
3. **IUPAC naming** — memorise the prefix table (meth/eth/prop/but/pent/hex) and the suffix rules. Always use systematic names; never write formic or acetic.
4. **Esterification** — know the formula (alcohol + carboxylic acid → ester + water), how to name the ester (alcohol-yl + acid-anoate), and that "water" (not H₂O) is the required answer for the by-product.
5. **Balancing combustion equations** — use the C-then-H-then-O method every time. Memorise C₃H₈ + 5O₂ and C₅H₁₂ + 8O₂ as reference points.
6. **Cracking and crude oil** — know the process of fractional distillation (vaporise, rise, cool, condense at different heights), why cracking is needed (excess long-chain fractions, demand for shorter chains and alkenes), and how to balance cracking equations to find unknown molecular formulae.
7. **Addition polymerisation** — know that alkene monomers with C=C join to form saturated polymers with no by-product; contrast with condensation polymerisation (water lost, two different monomers).
