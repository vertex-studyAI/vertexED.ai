# Electrochemistry

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Electrochemical (Galvanic) Cells

An electrochemical cell (also called a galvanic cell) converts **chemical energy into electrical energy** through a **spontaneous redox reaction**. Two different metals are placed in their respective ion solutions and connected by an external wire and a salt bridge or electrolyte-soaked separator. Electrons flow through the wire from the more reactive metal to the less reactive metal — this electron flow is the electric current.

| Feature | Anode | Cathode |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Process | Oxidation (loss of electrons) | Reduction (gain of electrons) |
| Terminal | Negative (in galvanic cell) | Positive (in galvanic cell) |
| Mass change | Decreases — metal dissolves into solution | Increases — metal ions deposit as solid |
| Memory aid | AN OX (ANode = OXidation) | RED CAT (REDuction = CAThode) |

**N16 Q7f (2 marks):** "Using ideas about redox reactions and transfer of electrons, outline the processes taking place at each electrode." → Anode: oxidation / electrons are lost. Cathode: reduction / electrons are gained. Award 1 mark for correct identification of oxidation and reduction in terms of electron gain or loss wherever seen.

**N16 Q7g (4 marks):** "Predict the change of mass observed at each electrode and justify." → Anode decreases because the metal dissolves into solution (oxidation). Cathode increases because metal ions from solution gain electrons and are deposited as solid metal. No ECF.

> Do NOT just name the electrode — always state the process happening (oxidation or reduction) AND what this means for electron transfer.

### Electrolysis

Electrolysis uses **electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction**. An external power supply forces electrons in the opposite direction compared to a galvanic cell. The electrode connected to the **positive terminal of the supply is the anode** (oxidation occurs); the electrode connected to the **negative terminal is the cathode** (reduction occurs).

Common applications: electroplating, purification of metals, extracting metals from molten compounds.

**Worked example — N24 corrosion context (electroplating with zinc):**
Iron nails plated with zinc are modelled as an electrochemical system. In the electrolytic plating cell:
- Cathode (iron nail, connected to −): Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Zn(s) — zinc deposits onto the nail
- Anode (zinc block, connected to +): Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ — zinc dissolves to replenish the solution

**M17 Q1f (2 marks — half-equation balancing):** Balance the half-reaction CrO₄²⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Cr₂O₃(s) + H₂O(l) → **2CrO₄²⁻ + 10H⁺ + 6e⁻ → Cr₂O₃ + 5H₂O**. 1 mark for correct coefficients on one side; 2 marks for all coefficients correct.

> Do NOT confuse electrolysis with a galvanic cell. In electrolysis: anode = connected to positive terminal of external supply. In a galvanic cell: anode = the negative terminal (electrons leave from here).

### Half-Equations

A half-equation shows only one half of a redox reaction — either the oxidation or the reduction — with electrons written explicitly.

**Rules for writing and balancing half-equations:**
1. Write the species being oxidised or reduced on each side.
2. Balance atoms other than H and O first.
3. Balance O by adding H₂O.
4. Balance H by adding H⁺.
5. Balance charge by adding electrons (e⁻) to the more positive side.

**Oxidation half-equation** (electrons on the right — lost by the species):

> Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

**Reduction half-equation** (electrons on the left — gained by the species):

> Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

**Overall cell equation** — multiply half-equations so electrons cancel exactly, then add:

> Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)

**N16 Q8d (4 marks):** Given half-equations Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s) and Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻, formulate the overall equation with state symbols. → **Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → 2Ag(s) + Cu²⁺(aq)**. Mark scheme: reactants and products all correct; no electrons shown in final equation; correct balancing; arrow (not equilibrium sign); correct state symbols.

**M17 Q1g (2 marks):** Identify why CrO₄²⁻ is reduced. → [1] Reduction. [1] Cr⁶⁺/Cr(VI) gains electrons / oxidation state of chromium decreases from +6 to +3.

> Do NOT leave electrons in the overall equation. Electrons must cancel completely. Do NOT use an equilibrium sign (⇌) in the overall cell equation — use a one-way arrow (→).

| Half-equation type | Electrons | Example |
|--------------------|-----------|---------|
| Oxidation | Right side — produced | Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ |
| Reduction | Left side — consumed | Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu |
| Overall (combined) | None — cancel out | Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu |

### Reactivity Series

The reactivity series ranks metals from most to least reactive based on their tendency to lose electrons (be oxidised). A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its compound.

| Increasing reactivity (top = least) | Metal |
|-------------------------------------|-------|
| Au | Gold |
| Ag | Silver |
| Cu | Copper |
| Pb | Lead |
| Sn | Tin |
| Ni | Nickel |
| Zn | Zinc |
| Mg | Magnesium |
| Na | Sodium |
| K | Potassium |

**How it predicts cell behaviour:**
- The **more reactive metal** in a cell is always **oxidised** (acts as the anode) — it has a stronger tendency to lose electrons.
- The **less reactive metal** is **reduced** (acts as the cathode) — its ions gain electrons and deposit.
- **Greater difference in reactivity → higher cell voltage.**

**N16 Q7b (1 mark):** State a suitable hypothesis. → "The bigger the difference in reactivity between the two metals, the higher the cell potential." Accept "further apart in the reactivity series."

**N17 Q3 (reactivity series / displacement):** Students measured temperature change when metals were added to copper sulfate solution. Metals that caused a greater temperature rise were placed higher in the reactivity series — order: Lead < Iron < Zinc < Aluminium < Magnesium. Mark scheme: order based on temperature rise; larger temperature change = more reactive.

> Do NOT say a metal is "stronger" or "weaker" — say "more reactive" or "less reactive." Always link reactivity to electron loss: "more reactive metals have a greater tendency to lose electrons and be oxidised."

### Products of Electrolysis

The products that form at each electrode during electrolysis depend on three factors:

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Nature of electrolyte** | Determines which ions are present and available for discharge |
| **Concentration of electrolyte** | At high Cl⁻ concentration, Cl₂ is discharged at the anode instead of O₂ |
| **Electrode material** | Inert electrodes (graphite/platinum) do not react; active electrodes (e.g. copper) dissolve at the anode |

**General rules for predicting products:**
- At the **cathode**: the least reactive metal ion (or H⁺ if no metal ions) is reduced and deposited.
- At the **anode**: if using an active (soluble) electrode, the electrode metal dissolves. If inert, the most easily oxidised anion is discharged (Cl⁻ if concentrated; OH⁻/O₂ if dilute).

**Electrolysis of copper sulfate solution with copper electrodes (basis of electroplating):**
- Cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) — copper deposits
- Anode: Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ — copper dissolves (replenishes Cu²⁺ in solution)
- Net result: copper transfers from anode to cathode; concentration of solution stays constant.

> Do NOT say "the anode gains mass" during standard electrolysis with a soluble anode — the anode loses mass as the metal dissolves. The cathode gains mass as metal deposits.

### Applications of Electrochemistry

**Electroplating**
A thin layer of a more attractive or protective metal is deposited on a cheaper base metal using electrolysis. The object to be plated is the cathode; the plating metal is the anode; the electrolyte contains ions of the plating metal.

Example: silver-plating cutlery (N24 context — cutlery made of cheap iron plated with zinc or silver using electrolysis).

**Rechargeable Batteries**
In a rechargeable battery (e.g. lithium-ion), the spontaneous discharge reaction (galvanic) is reversed during charging by applying an external current — the same electrodes swap roles between use and charge. The N16 Q9 lithium-ion data analysis question asks students to interpret charge/discharge graphs.

**Sacrificial Protection (Galvanising)**
Zinc is more reactive than iron, so zinc will be preferentially oxidised (sacrificial anode) when both are in electrical contact in an electrolyte. Even if the zinc coating is scratched, the iron beneath remains protected because zinc continues to oxidise in its place.

**N24 Q6c (2 marks):** "Explain why zinc-plated nails corrode less than unplated steel nails in NaCl solution." → [1] Zinc is more reactive than iron/steel. [1] Zinc oxidises preferentially (acts as the anode / sacrificial protection) — zinc is oxidised instead of iron, protecting the iron.

> Do NOT say zinc "stops" corrosion — say zinc "oxidises preferentially" or "acts as a sacrificial anode." Do NOT call this just "coating" — the protection works electrochemically even where the zinc is scratched away.

**N16 electrolyte / circuit questions:**

**N16 Q6a (1 mark):** "State the type of charged particles that move in the solution." → ions. Do NOT accept "positive ions" alone (both ions move) or "electrons" (electrons move in the wire, not the solution).

**N16 Q6b (2 marks):** "Describe what would happen if the filter paper was not soaked in electrolyte." → [1] ions cannot move through the solution / salt bridge is broken. [2] circuit is incomplete / no current flows. ECF from part a accepted; accept "no electricity can flow."

> Do NOT say "electrons flow through the electrolyte" — electrons flow in the external circuit (wire); ions carry charge through the electrolyte/solution.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Electrolyte question (N16 Q6a):** Answer is always "ions" — not "positive ions" (both positive and negative ions move), never "electrons" (electrons move in the wire, not the solution).
- **Half-equations:** Electrons must appear explicitly and on the correct side. Oxidation: electrons on right. Reduction: electrons on left. If electrons appear on the wrong side, the mark scheme applies CON — zero marks for that part.
- **Overall equation (N16 Q8d):** Must have no electrons remaining; must use → not ⇌; must include state symbols if asked. All four conditions can each be a marking point.
- **Electrode mass changes (N16 Q7g):** Both the prediction AND the justification are required. Justification must reference the chemistry: "anode dissolves" (not just "loses mass") and "metal is deposited" (not just "gains mass"). No ECF on this question.
- **Sacrificial protection (N24 Q6c):** Two separate marks — (1) zinc is more reactive than iron; (2) zinc oxidises preferentially / acts as sacrificial anode. Saying only "zinc coats the iron" gets zero.
- **Reactivity series hypothesis (N16 Q7b):** Must reference reactivity, not just "voltage is different." Accepted phrasing includes "further apart in the reactivity series" or "greater difference in reactivity."
- **Redox identification (M17 Q1g):** Accept any of: species gains electrons / oxidation state decreases / oxidation number decreases. All are equivalent phrasings.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Electrons flow through the electrolyte" | Electrons flow through the external wire; ions carry charge through the electrolyte |
| "Positive ions move to the anode" | Positive ions (cations) move to the cathode; negative ions (anions) move to the anode |
| "The anode is positive in all cells" | In a galvanic/electrochemical cell the anode is the negative terminal; in electrolysis the anode is connected to the positive terminal |
| "Zinc protects iron because it coats it" | Zinc protects electrochemically — even if the coating is scratched, zinc is still preferentially oxidised (sacrificial anode) |
| Leaving electrons in the overall equation | Electrons must cancel exactly — multiply half-equations first so electrons are equal, then add and cancel |
| Using ⇌ in overall cell equation | Use → (the overall reaction is not at equilibrium; it proceeds spontaneously) |
| "More reactive means it is the cathode" | More reactive metal is oxidised → it is the ANODE; less reactive metal is reduced → it is the CATHODE |
| "Cathode mass decreases" | Cathode gains mass (metal deposits); anode loses mass (metal dissolves) |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **N16 Q6, Q7, Q8 first** — these three questions together cover every major electrochemistry concept (electrolyte role, hypothesis and variables, redox at electrodes, electrode mass, concentration effects, overall equations). Work through all parts in order as your primary practice set.
2. **Half-equations and combining them (N16 Q8d, M17 Q1f)** — practise: (a) writing with correct electron placement, (b) balancing atoms and charge, (c) multiplying to cancel electrons, (d) writing the overall equation with state symbols and arrow.
3. **Anode vs cathode** — commit to memory: anode = oxidation = mass decreases = electrons lost = negative terminal (galvanic) = connected to + supply (electrolysis). Cathode = reduction = mass increases = electrons gained.
4. **Sacrificial protection (N24 Q6c)** — this is a newer pattern and likely to recur. Always use: "zinc is more reactive / zinc oxidises preferentially / zinc acts as sacrificial anode / protects iron."
5. **Reactivity series (N16 Q7b, N17 Q3)** — know the order (K > Na > Mg > Zn > Ni > Sn > Pb > Cu > Ag > Au), and be able to use it to predict which metal is oxidised, which is reduced, and whether the cell voltage will be higher or lower.
6. **Electrolyte role (N16 Q6a/6b)** — simple 1–2 mark questions that are easy marks: "ions" carry charge through the solution; removing the electrolyte breaks the circuit.
7. **Oxidation number changes (N20 Q7d, M17 Q1g)** — be able to assign oxidation states and state which species is oxidised (increase) and which is reduced (decrease). Connect this to electron transfer language.
