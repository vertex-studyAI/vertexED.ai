# Chemical Reactions

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Types of Reactions

Six reaction types appear repeatedly across all 18 sessions. For each one you must be able to state a definition, recognise it on sight, and write or balance a symbol equation.

**Combustion** — a fuel reacts with oxygen to release energy. Complete combustion produces only CO₂ and H₂O. Incomplete combustion occurs when oxygen is limited and produces carbon monoxide (CO) or soot (C). Combustion reactions are always exothermic.

| Type | Example equation | Products |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Complete (alkane — propane) | C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O | CO₂ and H₂O only |
| Complete (methane) | CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O | CO₂ and H₂O only |
| Complete (hydrogen) | 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O | H₂O only |
| Incomplete | 2C + O₂ → 2CO | CO (not CO₂) |

**M23 Q1b (2 marks):** Balance the combustion of propane: C₃H₈ + ? O₂ → ? CO₂ + ? H₂O.

Step 1: 3 C atoms → 3CO₂. Step 2: 8 H atoms → 4H₂O. Step 3: count O needed: (3×2) + (4×1) = 10 O atoms → coefficient 5 in front of O₂.

Answer: **C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O**

Mark scheme: reactants correct [1], products correct [1].

> Do NOT write a word equation when a symbol equation is asked for — 0 marks even if all products are correct.

---

**Neutralisation** — an acid reacts with a base (or carbonate) to form a salt and water. If a carbonate is the base, CO₂ is also produced. The type of acid determines the salt name (hydrochloric → chloride; sulfuric → sulfate; nitric → nitrate).

| Reactants | Products |
|-----------|---------|
| Acid + alkali/base | Salt + water |
| Acid + metal carbonate | Salt + water + CO₂ |
| Acid + metal oxide | Salt + water |

**Key equations:**

| Reaction | Balanced equation |
|----------|-----------------|
| Ethanoic acid + NaOH | CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O |
| HCl + Ca(OH)₂ | 2HCl + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O |
| H₂SO₄ + CaCO₃ | H₂SO₄ + CaCO₃ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂ |
| HCl + CaCO₃ | CaCO₃ + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) |

**M16 Q2d (4 marks):** The salt sodium ethanoate is formed when ethanoic acid reacts with a base. Write a balanced chemical equation.

Answer: **CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O**

Mark scheme: correct reactants [1], correct products [1], correctly balanced [1], state symbols correct [1].

> Do NOT accept "acid and base react together" without specifying the salt formula — the mark scheme requires the salt formula explicitly. Do NOT forget CO₂ when acid reacts with a carbonate — there are three products, not two.

---

**Precipitation** — two aqueous solutions are mixed and an insoluble solid (precipitate) forms. This is a double displacement reaction. The precipitate is formed when ions from each solution combine to make an insoluble compound.

General form: A⁺(aq) + B⁻(aq) → AB(s)

**M18 Q2e:** To remove only Ca²⁺ ions from hard water, add sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻):

> Ca²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → CaSO₄(s)

CaSO₄ is insoluble and forms a precipitate; MgSO₄ remains in solution — so only Ca²⁺ is removed by filtering off the precipitate.

Mark scheme: sulfate ion identified [1], CaSO₄ is insoluble [1], MgSO₄ remains soluble [1], filter to remove precipitate [1].

**M17 Q3d (precipitation in a conductivity titration):**

> Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)

As NaCl is added, Ag⁺ ions are removed as the insoluble precipitate AgCl — conductivity drops because fewer ions remain in solution.

> Do NOT say "a solid forms" without identifying it as insoluble — the mark scheme requires the word "insoluble" to earn the product mark.

---

**Decomposition** — a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. Triggered by heat (thermal decomposition) or a catalyst (catalytic decomposition).

| Reaction | Balanced equation |
|---------|-----------------|
| Thermal decomposition of NaHCO₃ | 2NaHCO₃(s) → Na₂CO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) |
| Catalytic decomposition of H₂O₂ | 2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) |
| Decomposition of CO₂ (MOXIE) | 2CO₂(g) → O₂(g) + 2CO(g) |

**M16 Q8a (3 marks):** Write the balanced chemical equation with state symbols for: carbon dioxide → oxygen + carbon monoxide.

Answer: **2CO₂(g) → O₂(g) + 2CO(g)**

Mark scheme: correct products [1], correctly balanced [1], state symbols all correct [1].

**N21 Q2c (3 marks):** Write the balanced equation with state symbols for the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide.

Answer: **2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g)**

Mark scheme: formulae of products correct [1], correctly balanced [1], state symbols correct [1].

> Do NOT omit the coefficient 2 in front of H₂O₂ — an unbalanced equation loses the balance mark even if products are correct.

---

**Redox (oxidation–reduction)** — involves the simultaneous transfer of electrons. Oxidation and reduction always occur together.

**OIL RIG:**
- **O**xidation **I**s **L**oss (of electrons) → oxidation state increases
- **R**eduction **I**s **G**ain (of electrons) → oxidation state decreases

| Species | Role |
|---------|------|
| Oxidising agent | Accepts electrons; is itself reduced |
| Reducing agent | Donates electrons; is itself oxidised |

Oxidation state rules:

| Rule | Value |
|------|-------|
| Free element (e.g., Fe, O₂) | 0 |
| Monatomic ion (e.g., Fe²⁺) | = charge |
| Oxygen in compounds | −2 (except peroxides: −1) |
| Hydrogen in compounds | +1 (except metal hydrides: −1) |
| Sum in neutral molecule | 0 |
| Sum in polyatomic ion | = charge of ion |

**M25 Q3c (2 marks):** Explain why Fe → Fe₂O₃ is an oxidation reaction.

> Iron goes from oxidation state 0 to +3 in Fe₂O₃. Iron loses electrons (each Fe atom loses 3 electrons). Therefore iron is oxidised.

Mark scheme: iron is oxidised [1], electrons are lost / oxidation state increases from 0 to +3 [1].

**N20 Q7d (3 marks):** Describe what happens to iron in Fe(s) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l) → FeCO₃(s) + H₂(g) in terms of redox.

> Oxidation number of iron increases from 0 to +2 → iron has lost electrons → iron has been oxidised.

Mark scheme: oxidation number increased [1], from 0 to +2 [1], iron has been oxidised [1].

> Do NOT say "iron gains oxygen" as a complete redox explanation — the mark scheme requires electron language ("loses electrons") or oxidation state numbers to score more than 1 mark. Always quote both the starting and ending oxidation state.

---

**Displacement** — a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound in solution. Only works if the displacing metal is higher in the reactivity series.

**Reactivity series (most → least reactive):** K, Na, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Ni, Sn, Pb, **H**, Cu, Ag, Au

The bigger the gap in reactivity, the larger the temperature change the reaction produces.

**N17 Q3 (displacement with copper sulfate):**

> Mg(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)

Magnesium is more reactive than copper → displaces copper from solution. Temperature rise is measured; larger rise = more reactive metal. Order from N17 data: Pb < Fe < Zn < Al < Mg.

> Do NOT predict a displacement reaction without first checking the reactivity series — if the added metal is less reactive than the metal in solution, no reaction occurs.

### Balancing Equations

Balancing is the most frequently tested skill — it appears in virtually every session (M16, M17, M18, M19, M21, M22, M23, M24, M25, N17, N18, N19, N20, N21, N22, N24 and more).

**Step-by-step method:**

1. Write correct formulas for all reactants and products. Do NOT change subscripts.
2. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
3. Add coefficients (whole numbers in front of formulas) to balance.
4. Start with the most complex molecule; leave O₂ and H₂O until last.
5. Check by counting all atoms again.
6. Add state symbols: **(s)** solid, **(l)** liquid, **(g)** gas, **(aq)** aqueous (dissolved in water).

**State symbol rules:**

| Substance | State | Symbol |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Ionic compound dissolved in water | Aqueous | (aq) |
| Gas | Gas | (g) |
| Metal or ionic solid | Solid | (s) |
| Water (liquid) | Liquid | (l) |
| Organic liquid (e.g., ethanol) | Liquid | (l) — NOT (aq) |

> Do NOT write (aq) for organic liquids like ethanol — (aq) means dissolved in water. Do NOT change subscripts to balance an equation — only change coefficients. Writing H₃O instead of H₂O to balance hydrogen is a fatal error.

**N18 Q7a (2 marks):** Balance the combustion of methane and add state symbols.

Answer: **CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)**

Mark scheme: reactants correct [1], products correct [1].

**N17 Q1b (4 marks):** Balance two equations for lead extraction.

> 2PbS + 3O₂ → 2PbO + 2SO₂
> 2PbO + C → 2Pb + CO₂

Mark scheme: 2 marks per equation (reactants correct + products correct). Do NOT accept "?" in place of a blank coefficient.

**M19 Q2b (2 marks):** Select coefficients to balance: (?) NO₂(g) + O₂(g) + (?) H₂O(l) → (?) HNO₃(aq).

Answer: **4NO₂(g) + O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) → 4HNO₃(aq)**

Mark scheme: any two coefficients correct [1], all three coefficients correct [2].

> Do NOT try to balance by adjusting only one coefficient at a time without checking all atoms — always recount every element after each change.

### Redox Reactions — Half-Equations

A half-equation shows only one half of a redox reaction — either oxidation or reduction — with electrons written explicitly. Half-equations are used in electrochemistry and when combining reactions involving different numbers of electrons transferred.

**Writing a half-equation — steps:**

1. Write the species being oxidised or reduced on the left; products on the right.
2. Balance atoms other than O and H first.
3. Balance O by adding H₂O to the side that needs oxygen.
4. Balance H by adding H⁺ to the side that needs hydrogen.
5. Balance charge by adding e⁻ to the more positive side.

**Oxidation half-equation (anode):**

> Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

Copper loses 2 electrons → oxidised.

**Reduction half-equation (cathode):**

> Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s)

Silver gains 1 electron → reduced.

**M17 Q1f (2 marks):** Balance the half-equation for reduction of CrO₄²⁻ to Cr₂O₃ in acidic solution.

Starting point: CrO₄²⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Cr₂O₃(s) + H₂O(l)

Step 1: Balance Cr → 2CrO₄²⁻ on left.
Step 2: Balance O → 8 O on left, 3 O in Cr₂O₃ → 5H₂O on right.
Step 3: Balance H → 10H⁺ on left.
Step 4: Balance charge: left has 2(−2) + 10(+1) = +6 and right is 0 → 6e⁻ on left.

Answer: **2CrO₄²⁻ + 10H⁺ + 6e⁻ → Cr₂O₃ + 5H₂O**

Mark scheme: 1 mark for correct coefficients on one side; 2 marks for all coefficients correct. Accept 10H⁺.

**M17 Q1g (2 marks):** Identify the reaction type and explain why CrO₄²⁻ is reduced.

> Reduction — because Cr⁶⁺ gains electrons (6e⁻) OR because the oxidation state of chromium decreases from +6 to +3.

Mark scheme: "reduction" [1], Cr⁶⁺ gains electrons OR oxidation state decreases from VI to III [1].

> Do NOT say "CrO₄²⁻ loses oxygen" as a complete explanation — this scores at most 1 mark. The full mark requires electron language or oxidation state numbers.

**Combining half-equations — N16 Q8d (4 marks):**

Half-equations: Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s) and Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

Multiply the silver equation by 2 to balance electrons, then add both equations:

Answer: **Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → 2Ag(s) + Cu²⁺(aq)**

Mark scheme: reactants and products correct [1], no electrons shown [1], correctly balanced [1], correct state symbols [1].

> Do NOT leave electrons in the final overall equation — they must cancel completely. Mark schemes deduct the balance mark if electrons appear in the final answer.

### Gas Tests

Gas tests appear in almost every session. You must state both the reagent AND the positive result for full marks — naming only the reagent without the result (or vice versa) is typically half marks.

| Gas | Test | Positive result |
|-----|------|----------------|
| CO₂ | Bubble gas through **limewater** (calcium hydroxide solution, Ca(OH)₂) | Limewater turns **milky / cloudy / white** |
| O₂ | Insert a **glowing splint** into the gas | Splint **relights** |
| H₂ | Bring a **lit splint** to the gas | Burns with a **squeaky pop** |

**M17 Q4b (3 marks):** Outline a test for carbon dioxide.

> Bubble the gas through limewater (calcium hydroxide solution). If CO₂ is present, the limewater turns milky/cloudy/white because calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is formed as a precipitate.

Mark scheme: calcium hydroxide solution / limewater named [1], bubbling/passing through method stated [1], turns white/cloudy/milky OR CaCO₃ precipitate forms [1].

**M22 Q4a (2 marks):** Describe the test for oxygen and the expected result.

> Insert a glowing splint into the gas — the splint relights if oxygen is present.

Mark scheme: glowing splint [1], relights [1].

> Do NOT use a lit splint for the oxygen test — the mark scheme explicitly states "do not accept a lit splint." Do NOT say "the splint glows brighter" — the required word is "relights." For CO₂, do NOT say "bubbles appear" as a positive result — that is not a positive test result. Do NOT name limewater as a test for H₂ — limewater tests CO₂ only.

### Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry covers two setups: galvanic cells (which generate electricity from a spontaneous redox reaction) and electrolysis (which uses electricity to force a non-spontaneous reaction). In both setups, the same rule applies: **anode = oxidation, cathode = reduction**.

**Memory aid: AN OX / RED CAT**
- **An**ode = **Ox**idation
- **Red**uction = **Cat**hode

**Galvanic (voltaic) cells** convert chemical energy into electrical energy. Two metals of different reactivity are placed in solutions of their ions, connected by a wire (electron flow) and a salt bridge (ion flow to maintain charge balance).

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Anode (negative terminal) | More reactive metal — oxidation; metal dissolves; mass decreases |
| Cathode (positive terminal) | Less reactive metal — reduction; metal ions deposit; mass increases |
| Cell voltage | Larger gap in reactivity series → higher voltage |

**N16 Q7f (2 marks):** Outline the processes at each electrode in a galvanic cell.

> Anode: oxidation / electrons are lost. Cathode: reduction / electrons are gained.

Mark scheme: anode = oxidation/electrons lost [1], cathode = reduction/electrons gained [1].

**N16 Q7g (4 marks):** Predict the change of mass at each electrode.

| Electrode | Change | Justification |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Anode | Decreases | Metal atoms are oxidised and dissolve into solution |
| Cathode | Increases | Metal ions are reduced and deposited as solid metal |

Mark scheme: 1 mark per electrode for mass change + 1 mark per electrode for justification.

**Electrolysis** uses electrical energy to force a non-spontaneous redox reaction in a molten or dissolved ionic compound.

| Electrode | Process | What happens |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Cathode (−) | Reduction | Cations gain electrons; metal deposited or H₂ evolved |
| Anode (+) | Oxidation | Anions lose electrons; Cl₂ or O₂ evolved |

> Do NOT confuse positive/negative terminal labels in galvanic cells vs electrolysis. The key rule is always the same: anode = oxidation, cathode = reduction — regardless of the setup. This topic links to the dedicated electrochemistry page for full detail on industrial electrolysis and electrode product selection.

### Equilibrium and Le Chatelier's Principle

A **reversible reaction** uses the double arrow ⇌ and can proceed in both directions. At **dynamic equilibrium**, the forward and reverse rates are equal and concentrations remain constant — but both reactions are still occurring.

**Le Chatelier's Principle:** If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, the equilibrium shifts in the direction that opposes (minimises) the change.

| Change applied | Direction of shift | Reasoning |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Increase concentration of reactant | Right (forward) | Reduces the excess reactant |
| Increase concentration of product | Left (reverse) | Reduces the excess product |
| Increase pressure (gases only) | Side with fewer moles of gas | Reduces the pressure |
| Increase temperature | Endothermic direction | Absorbs the added heat |
| Decrease temperature | Exothermic direction | Releases heat to compensate |
| Add catalyst | No shift | Speeds up both directions equally |

**M16 Q10c (4 marks):** Explain how adding acid affects the equilibrium HCO₃⁻(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂CO₃(aq) + OH⁻(aq).

> Adding acid increases H⁺ concentration. H⁺ reacts with OH⁻ ions, removing a product. The equilibrium shifts right to replace the OH⁻. [HCO₃⁻] decreases; [H₂CO₃] increases.

Mark scheme: H⁺ reacts with OH⁻ [1], equilibrium shifts right [1], [HCO₃⁻] decreases OR [OH⁻] decreases [1], [H₂CO₃] increases [1].

**N17 Q2d (4 marks):** Explain why opened perfume bottles are stored in cold, dry conditions (reaction: alcohol + carboxylic acid ⇌ ester + water).

> Cold: low temperature keeps the equilibrium shifted right; the ester is not broken down. Dry: avoids adding water (a product) to the equilibrium; equilibrium does not shift left; ester is not hydrolysed.

Mark scheme: low temperature keeps equilibrium to the right [1], avoids breakdown of ester [1], dry avoids adding water to equilibrium [1], keeps equilibrium towards the right [1].

**N23 Q1d–1e (2 + 3 marks):** The equilibrium for dissolved CO₂ in cola: CO₂(aq) ⇌ CO₂(g). When a cold can is taken outside on a warm day, temperature increases → dissolving CO₂ is exothermic → higher temperature favours the endothermic direction (right) → more CO₂ escapes as gas → drink becomes less fizzy.

Mark scheme: cola becomes less fizzy [1], CO₂ escapes / no longer at equilibrium [1], solubility of CO₂ decreases with temperature [1].

> Do NOT say "the catalyst shifts the equilibrium" — a catalyst does not change the equilibrium position; it only increases the rate at which equilibrium is reached. Mark schemes penalise this as a misconception. Always state the direction of shift (left/right or "towards products/reactants") AND the reason — stating only "equilibrium changes" scores 0.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Balancing — partial credit:** the mark scheme awards per side — reactants correct [1] and products correct [1]. Getting one side wrong loses that mark but may not forfeit the other. If the equation is fundamentally unbalanced, no balance mark is awarded.
- **State symbols:** (aq) means dissolved in water — do NOT write (aq) for organic liquids. H₂O is (l) at room temperature. The mark scheme ignores state symbols unless specifically requested; if requested, all state symbols must be correct to earn the mark.
- **Redox explanations:** "gains oxygen" or "loses hydrogen" are old-style definitions — accepted for 1 mark maximum. Full marks require electron language ("Fe loses 2 electrons") or oxidation state numbers with both starting and ending values stated.
- **Half-equations:** electrons must balance charge on both sides. If atoms are balanced but charge is wrong, the balance mark is lost. Electrons are written as e⁻ on the side where they are added.
- **Gas test — O₂:** must specify a **glowing** splint, not a lit splint. Mark schemes explicitly say "do not accept a lit splint."
- **Gas test — CO₂:** must name **limewater** or **calcium hydroxide solution** — "water turns cloudy" without naming the reagent scores 0 for the reagent mark.
- **Le Chatelier:** must state the **direction of shift** (left/right/towards products/reactants) AND the reason. "Equilibrium changes" alone scores 0. Temperature and concentration changes are most common.
- **Catalyst and equilibrium:** a catalyst does NOT change the equilibrium position or yield — it only speeds up both reactions equally. Any statement that a catalyst changes the equilibrium position triggers CON (contradiction) for 0 marks.
- **Neutralisation — salt formula:** the salt must have the correct formula with correct subscripts — a missing or incorrect subscript = 0 for that mark.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Using a lit splint to test for oxygen | Use a **glowing** splint — it **relights** if O₂ is present |
| "Limewater turns blue" for CO₂ test | Limewater turns **milky/cloudy/white** — blue is cobalt chloride (the water test) |
| "H₂ turns limewater milky" | H₂ burns with a **squeaky pop** — limewater tests for CO₂ only |
| Writing (aq) for ethanol or other organic liquids | Organic liquids are **(l)** — (aq) means dissolved in water |
| "The catalyst shifts the equilibrium to the right" | A catalyst does not change the equilibrium position — it only speeds up both directions equally |
| "Iron gains oxygen so it is reduced" | Gaining oxygen = **oxidised** (OIL RIG: loss of electrons = oxidation) |
| Leaving electrons in the overall cell equation | **Cancel electrons** when combining half-equations — they must not appear in the final equation |
| "Equilibrium shifts" without stating direction | Always state: shifts **left/right** or "towards reactants/products" — direction is required |
| Changing subscripts to balance an equation | Only change **coefficients** — subscripts are fixed by the compound's formula |
| Forgetting CO₂ when acid reacts with a carbonate | Acid + carbonate → salt + water + **CO₂** — three products, not two |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Practise balancing equations from every session** — combustion, acid + carbonate, metal + oxygen (4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃), and decomposition are the most common types. Do at least one from each session.
2. **Memorise OIL RIG and oxidation state rules** — redox questions appear in at least 12 of 18 sessions. Always quote both the starting and ending oxidation state numbers.
3. **Learn all three gas tests word-perfect** — reagent name + positive result. The O₂ splint detail (glowing, not lit) is a known mark scheme trap worth a dedicated review.
4. **Practise writing and balancing half-equations** — balance atoms first, then O (with H₂O), then H (with H⁺), then charge (with e⁻). Cancel electrons when combining into an overall equation.
5. **Drill Le Chatelier shifts** — for every change (concentration, temperature, pressure, catalyst), state the direction of shift and the reason. Temperature and concentration changes appear most frequently.
6. **Know anode = oxidation, cathode = reduction** — applies in both galvanic cells and electrolysis. Link to mass change at electrodes (anode decreases, cathode increases).
7. **Memorise the three acid + base reaction types and their products** — acid + alkali → salt + water; acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂; acid + metal oxide → salt + water.
