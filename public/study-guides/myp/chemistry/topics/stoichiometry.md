# Stoichiometry

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Mole Concept

A **mole** is the SI unit for amount of substance. One mole of any substance contains exactly **6.02 × 10²³ particles** (Avogadro's number, N_A) — whether those are atoms, molecules, or ions.

The mole bridges the microscopic world (individual atoms) and the macroscopic world (grams you can weigh on a balance).

**Core formula:**

> **n = m / M**
>
> where n = moles (mol), m = mass (g), M = molar mass (g mol⁻¹)

Rearrangements:
- m = n × M (find mass from moles)
- M = m / n (find molar mass from mass and moles)

**Step-by-step method:**
1. Identify what you are finding (n, m, or M).
2. Write the formula: n = m / M.
3. Substitute the known values — **always convert kg → g first** (×1000).
4. Calculate and round to the sig figs of the given data.
5. Write the unit: mol for n; g for m; g mol⁻¹ for M.

**Worked example — N19 Q2c (4 marks):**
A Slinky toy is made of 0.405 kg of iron. Calculate the number of moles of Fe. (M_Fe = 56 g mol⁻¹)

> Step 1 — Convert: 0.405 kg × 1000 = 405 g [1]
>
> Step 2 — Apply formula: n(Fe) = 405 / 56 = **7.2 mol** [1]
>
> (2 sig figs required — matches given data)

> **Mark scheme callout (N19):** If the student divides 0.405 by 56 without converting, they get 0.0072 mol — award 1 mark for method only. The kg→g conversion is a separate mark. This same trap appeared again in **N21 Q8b** (12 kg of Li). Always convert kg to g before using n = m/M.

### Molar Mass

The **molar mass** of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance, in g mol⁻¹. It is numerically equal to the relative molecular/formula mass (Mr) but carries the unit g mol⁻¹.

**How to calculate:**
- Look up each element's relative atomic mass (RAM) from the periodic table (always provided in exam).
- Multiply each RAM by the number of atoms of that element in the formula.
- Sum all contributions.

**Formula:**

> **M = Σ (RAM × subscript)** for each element in the formula

**Step-by-step — worked example, M17 Q2c (1 mark):**
Calculate the molar mass of C₆H₈O₂.

> M = 6(12) + 8(1) + 2(16) = 72 + 8 + 32 = **110 g mol⁻¹** [1]

**Worked example with a hydrated salt — M22 Q1e (3 marks):**
Calculate the mass of water in 5 mol of CaSO₄·2H₂O.

> Step 1 — Moles of H₂O: each formula unit contains 2 H₂O, so n(H₂O) = 5 × 2 = 10 mol [1]
>
> Step 2 — Molar mass of water: M(H₂O) = 2(1) + 16 = 18 g mol⁻¹
>
> Step 3 — Mass: m = 10 × 18 = **180 g** [1 — unit required for this mark] [1]

> **Mark scheme callout (M22):** The unit "g" is a separate, independent mark on step 3. Writing "180" with no unit loses that mark. Also note for hydrated salts: count *all* atoms including those in the water molecules — CaSO₄·2H₂O has 2 oxygen atoms from SO₄ and 2 more from each H₂O.

> **Mark scheme callout — "ignore units" vs unit required:** Relative molecular mass (Mr) questions say "ignore units" in the mark scheme (M21 Q2c, N21 Q3d, N24 Q1e). Mass calculation answers always require the unit "g" as a separate mark.

### Mole Ratios from Balanced Equations

Coefficients in a balanced equation represent **mole ratios** — not mass ratios. A ratio mark is almost always a separate, independent mark worth [1].

**Formula:**

> n(unknown) = n(known) × (coefficient of unknown / coefficient of known)

**Step-by-step method:**
1. Write the balanced equation (or use the one given).
2. Calculate moles of the given substance using n = m/M.
3. **State the mole ratio explicitly** — e.g. "AgBr : Ag = 1 : 1" — this is its own mark.
4. Scale to find moles of the target substance.
5. Convert to mass/volume as needed using m = n × M.

**Worked example — M17 Q2d (4 marks):**
Photographic film uses: 2AgBr → 2Ag + Br₂. Calculate the mass of AgBr needed to produce 2.0 kg of silver. (M_Ag = 108; M_Br = 80)

> Step 1 — Convert mass: 2.0 kg = 2000 g
>
> Step 2 — Moles of Ag: n(Ag) = 2000 / 108 = 18.52 mol [1]
>
> Step 3 — Mole ratio: AgBr : Ag = 2 : 2 = **1 : 1** [1]
> n(AgBr) = 18.52 mol
>
> Step 4 — Molar mass of AgBr: M(AgBr) = 108 + 80 = 188 g mol⁻¹ [1]
>
> Step 5 — Mass: m(AgBr) = 18.52 × 188 = **3480 g** [1 — sig figs independent mark]

> **Mark scheme callout (M17 Q2d):** The significant figures mark is fully independent of the calculation marks — you can receive it even if an earlier step is wrong. ECF applies: if a student uses the wrong M(Ag) in step 2, they still receive full marks for steps 3–5 provided the method is correct.

> **Mark scheme callout (M16 Q1b):** A correct final answer with NO working shown = maximum 2 out of 3 marks, explicitly stated in the mark scheme. Always show every algebraic step.

### Concentration

Concentration links the amount of solute (in moles) to the volume of solution it is dissolved in.

**Formula:**

> **c = n / V**
>
> where c = concentration (mol dm⁻³), n = moles (mol), V = volume in **dm³**

**Critical unit conversion:**

> cm³ → dm³: divide by 1000
> dm³ → cm³: multiply by 1000

1 dm³ = 1 litre = 1000 cm³

Concentration in mol dm⁻³ is also written mol/L or M (molarity).

**Step-by-step method:**
1. Convert volume to dm³ if given in cm³ (÷ 1000).
2. Calculate or identify moles of solute.
3. Apply c = n / V.
4. Units: always mol dm⁻³.

**Mark scheme note:** Concentration itself is rarely the final calculate target in these 18 sessions — it appears more often as given data feeding into a mole calculation. However, the cm³ → dm³ conversion is tested indirectly and is a common error source.

### Percentage Composition by Mass

The percentage by mass of an element (or compound) tells you what fraction of a substance's total mass comes from that component.

**Formula:**

> **% by mass = (mass of component / total mass) × 100**

For a pure compound:
> % element = (RAM × subscript / M_compound) × 100

**Step-by-step method:**
1. Calculate (or look up) molar mass of the whole compound.
2. Calculate mass contributed by the element: RAM × number of atoms.
3. Divide by total molar mass and multiply by 100.
4. Units: % (no unit mark — it is dimensionless).

**Worked example — N17 Q5e (1 mark):**
A sovereign coin contains 7.32 g of gold in an 8.00 g alloy. Calculate the % gold by mass.

> % Au = (7.32 / 8.00) × 100 = **91.5%** [1]
> (Accept values rounding to 91.5; mark scheme notes "accept 92.8%" — verify original MS)

**Worked example — N18 Q2d (3 marks):**
Roman glass contains 0.5% Fe₂O₃ by mass. Calculate the moles of Fe₂O₃ in a 100 g sample. (M_Fe = 56; M_O = 16)

> Step 1 — Mass of Fe₂O₃ in sample: 100 × 0.5/100 = 0.5 g [1]
>
> Step 2 — Molar mass: M(Fe₂O₃) = 2(56) + 3(16) = 160 g mol⁻¹ [1]
>
> Step 3 — Moles: n = 0.5 / 160 = **3.13 × 10⁻³ mol** [1]

> **Mark scheme callout (N18 Q2d):** Answer must be in standard form. No ECF from the second marking point (stated explicitly in the mark scheme) — unusually strict for this paper. Also note: if M(Fe₂O₃) was calculated incorrectly in Q2c, ECF does apply from that error into the n = m/M step, but not beyond.

### Limiting Reagent

When two or more reactants are combined, the **limiting reagent** is the one that runs out first. It determines the maximum amount of product that can be formed. The other reactant is in **excess**.

**How to identify the limiting reagent:**
1. Calculate moles of each reactant using n = m/M.
2. Divide each by its stoichiometric coefficient to get the "mole ratio value."
3. The reactant with the **smaller mole ratio value** is limiting.
4. Base all product calculations on the limiting reagent.

**Formula summary:**

> Mole ratio value = n(reactant) / coefficient in balanced equation
> Smaller value → that reactant is the limiting reagent

**Mark scheme note:** Limiting reagent does not appear as a standalone direct-calculate question in these 18 sessions, but the concept underpins several Criterion C questions where experimental data yields unequal reagent amounts. For a complete answer in those contexts, explicitly identify which reagent is limiting and state "based on n(X) as limiting reagent."

### Exam Checklist — 5 Steps for Every Stoichiometry Question

Apply this to every calculation question before writing your final answer:

1. **Units:** Are masses in grams? Convert kg → g (×1000) if needed.
2. **Molar mass:** Have you shown the sum (e.g. 2(56) + 3(16) = 160)? This is often a separate mark.
3. **Mole ratio:** Have you stated it explicitly from the balanced equation? It is almost always its own mark.
4. **Working shown:** Have you written n = m/M = value at each step? Correct final answer without working = maximum 2/3 marks (M16 Q1b).
5. **Final answer format:** Does the question ask for standard form? Are sig figs consistent with the given data? Is the unit written?

### Three Most Common Mark Scheme Traps

**Trap 1 — kg vs g:** Mass given in kg, molar mass in g mol⁻¹. Students divide without converting and lose all but the method mark.
> Sessions: N19 Q2c, N21 Q8b. Fix: ×1000 before n = m/M.

**Trap 2 — Standard form required:** Some questions explicitly require the answer in standard form (×10ⁿ). Writing 320 000 instead of 3.2 × 10⁵ can lose the final mark.
> Sessions: N18 Q2d (3.13 × 10⁻³ mol), N24 Q2c (3.2 × 10⁵ g). Fix: check whether the expected answer is very large or very small, and always check the mark scheme instruction.

**Trap 3 — ECF exceptions:** Normally ECF applies across all steps. But two questions explicitly switch it off:
> N18 Q2d — no ECF from the second marking point.
> N24 Q2c — no ECF at all; AND answer must be in grams, not kg. This is the strictest stoichiometry mark scheme in all 18 sessions.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Mole ratio is always a separate mark.** Never skip writing it out — e.g. "Fe : O₂ = 4 : 3" earns [1] on its own.
- **ECF is the default** in multi-step calculations. If you make an error in step 1, you still earn marks for correct method in steps 2–4.
- **Two explicit no-ECF exceptions:** N18 Q2d (no ECF from 2nd mark) and N24 Q2c (no ECF at all). Know these.
- **"Ignore units"** applies only to relative molecular mass (Mr) questions. All mass answers require "g."
- **Correct answer without working** is penalised. M16 Q1b explicitly awards max 2/3 for no working.
- **Significant figures:** match to the given data (usually 2–3). M25 Q1b explicitly rejects 0.40 (wrong rounding) while accepting 0.37.
- **Standard form** is required when the answer is very large (N24: 3.2 × 10⁵ g) or very small (N18: 3.13 × 10⁻³ mol). Look for this requirement before writing the final answer.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Using mass in kg directly in n = m/M | Always convert: kg × 1000 = g before dividing by molar mass |
| Writing only the final answer, no working | Show n = m/M = value at every step — no working = max 2/3 marks |
| Omitting the mole ratio step | State it explicitly: "X : Y = a : b" — it is almost always its own mark |
| Forgetting the unit "g" on mass answers | The unit is a separate independent mark in most mass calculations |
| Writing answer in kg when question says grams | Read the question units; N24 Q2c explicitly does not accept kg |
| Answer not in standard form | Check if the answer is very large/small; N18 and N24 require standard form |
| Counting atoms incorrectly in hydrated salts | Count ALL atoms including water molecules: CaSO₄·2H₂O has 4+4 O atoms total |
| "Relative molecular mass has units g mol⁻¹" | Mr is dimensionless — mark scheme says "ignore units" for Mr questions |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Master the three-step mole calculation chain (molar mass → n = m/M → mole ratio → target mass). This pattern appears in 10+ sessions and accounts for the majority of stoichiometry marks.
2. Drill the kg → g conversion reflex: every time a mass is given in kg, write "× 1000 = g" before anything else.
3. Practise writing the mole ratio as its own explicit line of working — it is almost always worth [1] and is frequently skipped under time pressure.
4. Know when standard form is expected: if the calculated answer exceeds ~10 000 or is smaller than 0.001, express it in standard form.
5. Learn the two no-ECF exceptions (N18 Q2d, N24 Q2c) so you know when you cannot rescue a wrong first step.
6. For hydrated salts, always count water molecules separately — moles of water = moles of salt × number of H₂O in formula.
