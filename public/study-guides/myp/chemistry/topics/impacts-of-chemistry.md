# Impacts of Chemistry (Criterion D)

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### How Criterion D Works

Criterion D (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science) is the only criterion in MYP Chemistry assessed through extended writing. Every session contains at least one large extended response (12–17 marks) plus several smaller D-labelled questions (1–8 marks). The total D mark across a session is typically 20–35 marks.

**What separates Criterion D from others:**

| Criterion | What is tested |
|-----------|---------------|
| A | Recall, definitions, explanations, calculations |
| B | Experimental design, hypothesis, variables |
| C | Data processing, graphs, error analysis |
| **D** | **Real-world impacts — who is affected, how, and what should be done** |

**Best-fit marking:** The examiner reads the whole response and matches it to the markband that best describes the overall quality. A response that is brilliant on one strand but thin on others will not reach the top band. Every active strand must be addressed.

**Generalised markband rubric (used across all 18 sessions):**

| Mark per strand | Descriptor |
|-----------------|------------|
| 0 | Not attempted or completely incorrect |
| 1 | Basic statement made; one impact implied; one option mentioned; conclusion present but trivial |
| 2 | Clear statement with some justification; two options compared OR one with supporting evidence; impact stated; appraisal with some justification |
| 3 | Statement with full justification using chemistry; both/all options compared; multiple impacts with evidence; appraisal with scientific reasoning |
| 4 | Full scientific justification; all options compared using data from the stimulus; all perspectives addressed; thorough justified appraisal |

**Strand names that appear across sessions:**

| Strand | What is assessed |
|--------|-----------------|
| Environmental | Pollution, ecosystems, land/water/air quality |
| Economic | Costs, employment, profitability, development |
| Social | Communities, health, quality of life, equity |
| Ethical | Fairness, rights, moral responsibility |
| Scientific/Efficiency | How well each option works; comparative data |
| Appraisal/Conclusion | Justified overall judgement — weighing all evidence |
| Sustainability | Long-term resource use; renewable vs non-renewable |

Not every strand appears in every question. The mark scheme specifies which strands are active for each question, but addressing more than required never loses marks.

**One-sided answers cannot reach the top band.** If the question asks you to evaluate options, you must address both/all of them. Listing only advantages, or ignoring the appraisal strand, caps your score at 2 out of 4 for the comparison strands.

**N17 Q7 (17 marks):** The environmental strand required "an environmental impact for both cars with supporting data" for 3 marks. Students who discussed only one car type were limited to 2/4 for that strand regardless of quality.
> Do NOT discuss only one option — compare all options that appear in the stimulus.

### Structuring an Extended Response

**The P-E-A-I paragraph framework** is the most reliable way to write any extended D response:

- **P — Point:** Make a clear claim about an impact ("Incineration of plastics releases CO₂ and toxic gases into the atmosphere")
- **E — Evidence:** Cite data from the stimulus or named chemistry ("According to Table 1, non-recycled plastics send 100% of waste to landfill")
- **A — Analysis:** Explain the chemical mechanism ("CO₂ is a greenhouse gas — it absorbs infrared radiation, trapping heat in the atmosphere")
- **I — Implication:** Who is affected, what trade-off exists, what should change ("This exacerbates climate change, disproportionately affecting low-income coastal communities through sea-level rise")

**Social + economic + environmental angles must all be addressed.** Examiners for 14–17 mark questions have separate strands for each. A response covering only environmental impacts cannot score above 8–9 marks regardless of quality.

**M16 Q11c (food additives, 12 marks):** The mark scheme awards a mark at the top band only for "a correct social or ethical impact on an individual AND a community" plus "a concluding appraisal linking all the issues discussed." Students who wrote about health effects but forgot community or economic dimensions were capped at 6–8.

**Model conclusion template (for any Discuss/Evaluate question):**

> "Overall, [Option X] is the most [criterion] choice because [specific chemical/scientific reason]. Although [Option Y] has the advantage of [counterpoint], this is outweighed by [reason linked to context]. Given [specific constraint from the question — e.g. high population with limited budget, coastal location], [Option X] is recommended."

**The appraisal strand is worth 2–4 marks in every extended response.** It is the most commonly dropped mark. Do not end your answer with a summary — end with a justified recommendation.
> Do NOT write "In conclusion, both methods have advantages and disadvantages." Say "Overall, Method X is recommended for this context because [named reason]."

### Plastics and Polymers

This is the most frequently examined Criterion D topic, appearing in M19 (green chemistry/plastic recycling), M25 (plastics for soap packaging), N19 (algae-based vs oil-based plastics), N21 (circular economy), N22 (sleep spray packaging), and M22 (paper production and storage).

**Key chemistry:**

| Feature | Addition polymers | Condensation polymers |
|---------|----------------|--------------------|
| Monomer type | Alkenes (contain C=C double bond) | Two different functional groups (e.g. diol + diacid) |
| By-product | None | Water (or HCl) |
| Examples | Polyethene, PVC, PTFE (Teflon) | Nylon, polyester, PLA |
| Biodegradable? | No (most) | Sometimes (PLA is bio-based and compostable) |
| Source | Crude oil (monomers from cracking) | Varies (PLA from corn starch; nylon from petrochemicals) |

**Biodegradable vs non-biodegradable:**

Non-biodegradable plastics (most addition polymers made from crude oil) persist in the environment for hundreds of years, fragmenting into **microplastics** that enter food chains. Fish, turtles, and seabirds ingest microplastics — the particles accumulate up the food chain. Biodegradable alternatives such as PLA (polylactic acid, from corn starch) break down in composting conditions, but require **industrial composting** — they do not decompose in ordinary landfill at useful rates.

**Circular economy vs linear economy (N21 Q9, 12 marks):**

| Model | Description | Economic implication | Environmental implication |
|-------|------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Linear | Take → Make → Dispose | Lower up-front cost; high waste disposal cost | Depletes finite resources; maximises landfill |
| Circular | Reduce → Reuse → Recycle → Recover | Higher initial investment; saves raw material cost long-term | Minimises waste; extends resource life |

**N21 Q9 (12 marks):** The resources strand required "a clear statement that the linear economy depletes finite resources and the circular economy extends them, with justification for both." Stating only that the circular economy is "better for the environment" earned 1/4 for that strand.
> Do NOT say "circular economy is more sustainable" without naming the mechanism — say "the circular economy recovers and reuses raw materials, reducing dependence on finite crude oil reserves."

**N19 Q9 (algae vs oil-based plastics, 13 marks):** The sustainability strand required a "relevant comment about sustainability for both plastics with justification for both" for 4 marks. Algae-based plastics are carbon-neutral (CO₂ absorbed during algae growth offsets emissions during breakdown), but require large areas of water and may compete with food production — this trade-off must be named for the top band.

**M25 Q7b (plastics for soap packaging, 16 marks — worked example):**

The question evaluated three plastic types (crude-oil-based non-biodegradable, plant-based non-biodegradable, plant-based biodegradable) across five strands. A full-marks response for the environmental strand: "Crude-oil-based PE packaging is produced from crude oil, a non-renewable resource, and generates CO₂ on combustion. Plant-based non-biodegradable plastic has low GHG emissions and uses locally available feedstocks — reducing transport emissions. Plant-based biodegradable plastic limits long-term environmental impact as it chemically breaks down to water and CO₂, but only under specific composting conditions — without industrial composting infrastructure, it behaves like conventional plastic in landfill." This earns 3–4/4 for the environmental strand by addressing all three types with supporting data from the stimulus table.

### Carbon Footprint and Fossil Fuels

This topic appears as the main D question in N17 Q6c (reducing carbon footprint, 12 marks) and N20 Q8 (CO₂ sink systems, 15 marks), and as supporting material in N17 Q7, N24 Q7a, and several smaller questions.

**Combustion of fossil fuels:**
> C₍ₙ₎H₍ₘ₎ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

CO₂ is a greenhouse gas: it absorbs infrared radiation emitted from Earth's surface and re-radiates it back, raising global temperatures (the **enhanced greenhouse effect**). Methane (CH₄) and nitrous oxide (N₂O) are more potent greenhouse gases per molecule but present in lower atmospheric concentrations.

**Carbon footprint** = total mass of CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e) produced by an individual, process, or country per unit time.

| Increases carbon footprint | Decreases carbon footprint |
|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Driving petrol/diesel cars | Switching to renewable energy (solar, wind) |
| Air travel | Using electric vehicles (only lower-carbon if electricity is generated renewably) |
| Burning coal for electricity | Insulating buildings |
| Deforestation (removes CO₂ sinks) | Planting trees |
| Livestock farming (methane from cattle digestion) | Eating less meat |

**N17 Q6c (12 marks):** The "impact on individual and society" strand required "a specific reference to the result of an activity of either an individual AND society on the carbon footprint with justification" for 3 marks. Many students mentioned individuals only — the mark scheme explicitly required both scales in the same answer.
> Do NOT discuss only individual actions — the strand requires individual AND societal examples, each with a named mechanism.

**N17 Q6b (graph reading, 2 marks):** The mark scheme accepted 0.44 °C as the temperature increase from 1980–2010. The unit mark (°C) was awarded separately. A common error was reading the change as 0.5 — the mark scheme accepted ± tolerance but required reading from the stimulus graph specifically.

**N20 Q8 (CO₂ sink systems — Ferrock, CDC, Desert conversion, 15 marks):** The carbon sink strand required explaining the chemical mechanism: "Ferrock reacts iron waste with CO₂ to form iron carbonate, permanently sequestering carbon." Generic "absorbs CO₂" received only 1 mark; the chemical name and reaction type were required for 2–3 marks.
> Do NOT say "absorbs carbon dioxide" alone — name the compound formed and the reaction type.

### Nuclear Chemistry

This topic appeared as both extended-response questions in N24 (Q7c — transport of radioactive waste, 8 marks; Q8 — disposal methods, 12 marks) and in supporting questions in other sessions.

**Nuclear fission:** A neutron collides with a large nucleus (e.g. uranium-235), causing it to split into two smaller nuclei (e.g. barium-141 and krypton-92), releasing energy and additional neutrons. These neutrons trigger further fissions — a **chain reaction**. The energy released per kg of fuel is far greater than any chemical combustion reaction.

**Radioactive waste:** Fission by-products are radioactive isotopes with long half-lives. They emit ionising radiation (alpha, beta, gamma) that damages living cells and DNA. Waste must be stored until it decays to safe levels — which may take thousands of years for high-level waste.

**Risks vs benefits of nuclear power:**

| Benefits | Risks |
|----------|-------|
| No CO₂ emissions during operation — low carbon footprint | Radioactive waste with very long half-life |
| High energy density — large power output from small fuel mass | Risk of catastrophic accident (Chernobyl, Fukushima) |
| Reliable baseload power — not weather-dependent | Uranium is a non-renewable resource |
| No air pollution (no SO₂, NOₓ) | Public opposition — NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) effect |

**Three disposal methods compared (N24 Q8):**

| Method | Environmental risk | Economic cost | Key mark scheme phrases |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Sea dumping | Leakage from corroding containers contaminates ocean ecosystems and food chains | Lower transport costs but high ongoing risk | "depth of sea would have to be checked"; "impact on marine life" |
| Underground storage | Minimal if geology is stable; multiple barrier layers (clay, rock) prevent seepage | High initial construction cost; cheaper long-term | "can be stored safely for hundreds of thousands of years"; "several barrier layers" |
| Space disposal | Waste permanently removed from Earth | Very high launch costs; catastrophic if rocket fails | "no hazard to life on Earth" (advantage); "nuclear debris could fall if rocket explodes" (disadvantage) |

**N24 Q8 (12 marks):** The space disposal strand required both an advantage AND a disadvantage, each with support, for 4 marks.
> Do NOT list only "waste is removed from Earth" — the mark scheme requires both the advantage and the consequence of rocket failure, each with supporting reasoning, for 4/4.

**N24 Q7c (transport of radioactive waste, 8 marks):** The precautions strand required "a precaution for three methods of transport" for 3 marks on that component. Students who discussed only road and rail transport, omitting river or air, were limited to 2/3 for precautions.
> Do NOT cover only one or two transport methods — address each method named in the question for maximum marks.

### Water and Sustainability

Water treatment appears as the main D question in M19 Q8 (copper removal, 17 marks), M21 Q8 (water purification technologies, 13 marks), and M24 Q9b (desalination, 14 marks).

**Key processes to compare:**

| Process | How it works | Efficiency | Cost | Environmental note |
|---------|-------------|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Precipitation | Adding alkali to precipitate metal ions as hydroxides; filter off sludge | High for high concentrations | Low set-up cost | Produces large waste sludge — disposal problem |
| Electrochemical recovery | Electrolysis deposits metal at cathode; pure metal recovered | High; specific metals targeted | High energy + electrode replacement | No additional chemicals; no waste products |
| Chemisorption | Metal ions adsorb onto activated surface (e.g. zeolite, activated carbon) | Very high at low concentrations (>99%); poor at high concentrations | High initial cost; adsorbent can sometimes be reused | Minimal waste |
| Photocatalysis | UV light + catalyst (e.g. TiO₂) breaks down contaminants | 100% Cu removal but very slow (>1 week) | Cheap catalyst; needs large UV-exposed area | Uses solar energy; no chemical waste |
| Reverse osmosis / nanofiltration | Pressure forces water through semi-permeable membrane; ions rejected | Very high | High energy (pumps) | Produces concentrated brine — needs careful disposal |
| Desalination | Distillation or reverse osmosis to remove salt from seawater | High | Energy-intensive — expensive in regions without cheap power | Brine discharge damages coastal ecosystems |

**M19 Q8 (17 marks — worked example for efficiency strand):**

A student wrote: "Chemisorption removes 99% of copper ions, making it highly efficient." → 1 mark.

For 3–4 marks, the response needed: "Chemisorption is highly efficient at low concentrations (removes >99%), whereas electrochemical methods are effective across a wider range of concentrations. However, chemisorption becomes inefficient at high initial copper concentrations — in this scenario, where the old factory has produced high copper levels, electrochemical recovery is therefore more appropriate." This earns 3–4/4 for the efficiency strand by comparing both methods with stimulus data and linking to the specific scenario.

> Do NOT say "Method X is more efficient" without quoting a percentage or data figure from the stimulus table.

**M24 Q9a (desalination, 3 marks):** The question asked why desalination was needed in a specific region. Accepted answers included: "freshwater scarcity due to low rainfall"; "high population density and growing demand"; "seawater is the only abundant water source." Answers that stated only "no freshwater" without a reason for scarcity received 1/3.

**M21 Q8 (water purification, 13 marks):** The environmental strand required separate environmental impacts for microfiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis — a student who discussed only two of the three technologies was limited to 3/4 for the environmental strand.
> Do NOT omit any technology listed in the question's stimulus — all must be compared for the top band.

### Model Answer for "Discuss/Evaluate" Questions

**Generic template for a 14-mark Evaluate question (e.g. M24 Q9b — desalination, or N17 Q7 — car types):**

**Paragraph 1 — Scientific/technical comparison:**
> "Method A works by [mechanism with named chemistry]. It achieves [efficiency figure from stimulus]. Method B works by [mechanism]. Compared to A, B [direct comparison with data]."

**Paragraph 2 — Environmental impacts:**
> "Method A has the advantage of [environmental benefit, named mechanism] but the drawback of [specific environmental cost — e.g. brine discharge/waste sludge/CO₂ emissions]. Method B produces [environmental impact]. Method A is/is not preferable environmentally because [comparative reasoning]."

**Paragraph 3 — Economic and social impacts:**
> "Method A has high/low initial costs because [reason]. This means it is/is not accessible for [type of community/country — e.g. low-income areas, developing nations]. Method B costs [comparison]. The social impact of [method] is [specific effect on health/access/employment]."

**Paragraph 4 — Appraisal (always last, always justified):**
> "Overall, Method [X] is recommended for [this context] because [specific chemical or contextual reason]. Although Method [Y] has [one genuine advantage], this is outweighed by [reason]. Given [specific constraint from the scenario], Method X is the most practical and sustainable choice."

**What earns top marks:**
- Named chemistry (specific compound names, equations, or chemical properties)
- Data from the stimulus ("According to Table 2, Method X removes 98% of copper ions…")
- Comparative language ("more efficient than," "unlike," "whereas")
- Multi-perspective coverage (environmental + economic + social/ethical)
- Justified final conclusion — not just a preference

**What loses marks:**
- "This is bad for the environment" — no named pollutant or mechanism
- Listing only advantages or only disadvantages
- Omitting the appraisal
- Self-contradiction (**CON** rule: a correct point negated by a contradicting statement scores 0)
- Bullet lists without development — a mark is awarded for a "statement with justification," not a bare fact

**N19 Q9 (13 marks) as a full worked example** — this question requires all four strands (sustainability, environmental, social, appraisal). A band-4 response for the social strand stated: "Oil-based plastic production creates jobs in the petrochemical industry and supports economies in oil-producing nations. However, the environmental damage caused by plastic waste in oceans — including microplastic ingestion — disproportionately affects fishing communities who depend on healthy marine ecosystems for their livelihoods, particularly in developing nations with limited waste management infrastructure." This earns 4/4 by naming two social groups (petrochemical workers; fishing communities), linking each to the chemistry (crude oil production; microplastic contamination), and acknowledging the trade-off.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Named chemistry is always required for top marks.** "Releases harmful gases" → 1 mark. "Releases CO₂ and SO₂ which contribute to the greenhouse effect and acid rain respectively" → 2–3 marks.
- **The appraisal strand is worth 2–4 marks in every extended response** and is the most frequently dropped mark. End every extended response with a justified recommendation, not a summary.
- **Data from the stimulus must be used in high-mark questions.** The top band descriptor in M19, M24, M25, N17, N20, N21, and N24 explicitly requires referencing stimulus data.
- **CON (contradiction) rule:** A correct statement negated by a contradicting statement earns 0 for that point. Commit to a position and do not reverse it.
- **ORA (or reverse argument):** "Algae-based plastics are more sustainable" earns the same mark as "oil-based plastics are less sustainable" — the direction of comparison does not matter.
- **WTTE (words to that effect):** The exact phrasing of impact descriptions is flexible — the underlying chemical concept must be correct.
- **One-sided responses are capped.** A response that only argues for one option on a Discuss or Compare question cannot reach the top band for comparison strands.
- **Bullet-point lists without development do not earn comparison or appraisal marks.** A mark is awarded for a "statement with justification" — not for a bare fact.
- **Confusing "discuss" and "evaluate":** Discuss = explore multiple sides. Evaluate = assess all sides and arrive at a justified conclusion with a stated recommendation.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "This is bad for the environment" | Name the specific pollutant, mechanism, and affected ecosystem: "CO₂ released contributes to the greenhouse effect, warming oceans and threatening coral reefs" |
| Listing only advantages of one method | Always address both/all options — compare directly using comparative language ("whereas," "unlike," "compared to") |
| Forgetting the appraisal/conclusion | Every extended response must end with a justified recommendation — write it even if rushed, it is worth 2–4 marks |
| Not referencing stimulus data | Explicitly cite tables and graphs: "According to Table 2, Method X removes 98% of copper ions…" |
| Self-contradiction (CON penalty) | Plan before writing; do not change position mid-answer |
| Treating Criterion D like Criterion A | D requires real-world application and multi-perspective analysis, not just factual recall |
| Vague economic claim | Specify who pays, why costs differ, and what the consequence is: "Electrochemical recovery has high energy costs, making it uneconomical for factories in regions without cheap electricity" |
| Ignoring social/ethical strand | Check the question bullet points — if ethics is listed, address it explicitly with a named example |
| Writing only one paragraph | Use P-E-A-I for each strand; one paragraph per dimension makes it easy for the examiner to award strand marks |
| "Biodegradable means it breaks down anywhere" | Biodegradable plastics such as PLA require industrial composting conditions — they do not decompose usefully in ordinary landfill |
| "Nuclear power produces no pollution" | Nuclear power produces no CO₂ during operation, but generates radioactive waste with a very long half-life that requires careful long-term storage |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the P-E-A-I paragraph structure and practise writing one paragraph per strand for any topic (plastics, water, carbon, nuclear, food).
2. Know the chemistry of all high-frequency topics: combustion equations; addition vs condensation polymers (include PLA); greenhouse gas mechanism; nuclear fission chain reaction; water treatment processes (precipitation, reverse osmosis, chemisorption, photocatalysis, desalination).
3. Practise writing appraisal paragraphs specifically — one sentence stating the recommendation, one sentence with the chemical or contextual justification, one sentence acknowledging the trade-off.
4. Build a "dimension bank" for any new topic: automatically ask — what is the environmental impact? The economic cost? The social effect? Who benefits and who is harmed? What is the ethical consideration?
5. Time management: 14–17 mark questions need 20–25 minutes; plan strands before writing (2 minutes planning saves 4 marks). Smaller D questions (4–8 marks) need 6–10 minutes — one sentence per mark, each with a reason.
6. Learn accepted vs non-accepted phrases: "bad for the environment" alone — NOT accepted; "releases CO₂ contributing to climate change" — accepted. "More efficient" alone — NOT accepted; "removes 98% of ions compared to 72%" — accepted.
7. Review the N24 nuclear waste question (Q8) and the N19 plastics question (Q9) as model extended responses — both cover all common strands and have detailed rubric breakdowns in their session files.
