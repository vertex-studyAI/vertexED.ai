# MYP Chemistry — Command Terms & Mark Scheme Conventions

_Compiled from all 18 exam sessions (M16–M25, excluding M20). Every example is cited from a real paper._

---

## 1. MYP Command Terms

### State
**Definition:** Give a specific name, value, or other brief answer without explanation or justification.  
**How to apply:** One sentence maximum. No "because" required. Copy a formula, name a substance, give a number with unit.  
**Examples:**
- M16 Q1a: "State the formula of the compound formed between magnesium and chlorine." — just write MgCl₂.
- M22 Q4c (WTTE): "State one property of period 3 metals that increases across the period." — one phrase accepted.
- N24 Q4d: "State whether the half-equation shows oxidation or reduction." — single word answer.

---

### Identify
**Definition:** Recognise and name something from information given.  
**How to apply:** Select from given data/diagram and name it. No explanation needed unless explicitly asked.  
**Examples:**
- M23 Q1c: "Identify the type of organic compound shown." (CON warning: if student names both molecules as alcohols, award 0.)
- N19 Q7a (WTTE): "Identify the trend in atomic radius across period 3."
- M25 Q3a (WTTE): "Identify the element that has the greatest electronegativity."

---

### Write (/ Write down)
**Definition:** Give a concise answer, often a formula, equation, or symbol.  
**How to apply:** Produce the required chemical notation. Balanced equations must have correct state symbols if asked.  
**Examples:**
- M17 Q1: "Write the balanced equation for the combustion of methane."
- N22 Q1: "Write the half-equation for the oxidation of bromide ions."
- M24 Q2: "Write the electron configuration of a calcium ion."

---

### Define
**Definition:** Give the precise meaning of a term or concept.  
**How to apply:** Use accepted scientific language. Mark schemes often show WTTE — your phrasing need not be word-for-word.  
**Examples:**
- M18 Q1: "Define the term 'isotope'."
- N20 Q4a (WTTE): "Define 'activation energy'."
- M21 Q3d (WTTE): "Define the term 'empirical formula'."

---

### Outline
**Definition:** Give a brief account or summary.  
**How to apply:** More than State but less than Explain. Cover the key steps without full mechanistic detail.  
**Examples:**
- M16 Q2b (WTTE): "Outline why a precipitate forms when aqueous silver nitrate is added to the solution."
- N17 Q2a (WTTE): "Outline the trend in ionisation energy across period 2."
- M19 Q4b (WTTE): "Outline the effect of increasing temperature on reaction rate."

---

### Describe
**Definition:** Give a detailed account of something observed or measured.  
**How to apply:** Include observable features, trends, quantities. For graphs: direction, rate of change, any plateau. For data: numbers with units.  
**Examples:**
- M22 Q6a (WTTE): "Describe the trend in boiling points of the halogens."
- N18 Q1: "Describe the structure of a sodium chloride crystal."
- M25 Q4c (WTTE): "Describe the change in pH when a strong acid is added to a buffer solution."

---

### Explain
**Definition:** Give a detailed account, including reasons or causes.  
**How to apply:** Link observation → reason → conclusion. Use "because" or "therefore". Two-part answers are common (observation [1] + reason [1]).  
**Examples:**
- M18 Q6b (ORA): "Explain why one of the molecules has a lower boiling point." — needed both the structural comparison and the intermolecular force reasoning.
- N21 Q1d (ORA): "Explain why sodium is more reactive than lithium." — must reference electron configuration and ionisation energy.
- M23 Q1d (WTTE): "Explain how increasing the surface area affects the rate of reaction." — mark scheme accepted multiple valid phrasings.

---

### Calculate
**Definition:** Obtain a numerical answer showing relevant working.  
**How to apply:** Show every step. If you get the wrong answer in step 1, ECF lets you still gain marks in later steps for correct method.  
**Examples:**
- M17 Q2d (ECF): Multi-step molar mass calculation — ECF applied throughout.
- M17 Q5c (ECF): "Calculate the concentration of HCl." — ECF from incorrect volume in earlier part.
- M18 Q3c/3d (ECF): Enthalpy calculation chain — each step carried forward.

---

### Determine
**Definition:** Obtain the only possible answer from given information.  
**How to apply:** Often paired with "from the graph" or "using the data". Show your reading/extraction method clearly.  
**Examples:**
- N19 Q2c (ECF): "Determine the rate of reaction from the graph at t = 20 s."
- M24 Q5b (ECF): "Determine the empirical formula of the compound."
- N22 Q2d (ECF): "Determine the number of moles of CO₂ produced."

---

### Predict
**Definition:** Give an expected result based on scientific reasoning.  
**How to apply:** State the prediction AND the reason. Mark schemes usually require both.  
**Examples:**
- M21 Q4c (WTTE): "Predict whether the reaction will be exothermic or endothermic and explain your answer."
- M25 Q3e (WTTE): "Predict the product of the reaction between propene and bromine water."
- N20 Q4d (WTTE): "Predict the effect of a catalyst on activation energy."

---

### Suggest
**Definition:** Propose a possible answer where there may be more than one correct response.  
**How to apply:** Any scientifically plausible answer is accepted. You will not be penalised for an unusual correct answer.  
**Examples:**
- M16 Q7b (WTTE): "Suggest why the anomalous data point should or should not be included."
- N17 Q3e (WTTE): "Suggest a reason for the difference in solubility."
- M21 Q7d (WTTE): "Suggest one advantage of using electrolysis to recycle aluminium."

---

### Compare
**Definition:** Give an account of the similarities and/or differences between two (or more) items.  
**How to apply:** Address both items explicitly. Comparative language required ("greater than", "whereas", "in contrast").  
**Examples:**
- M18 Q5e (ORA): "Compare the rate of reaction using limestone versus crushed oyster shell." — ORA accepted the reverse comparison.
- M22 Q4b (ORA): "Compare the effectiveness of a solid and an aqueous catalyst."
- N23 Q6a (ORA): "Compare the two methods of extracting copper."

---

### Evaluate
**Definition:** Make an appraisal by weighing up strengths and limitations.  
**How to apply:** Present evidence for and against; reach a conclusion. Used heavily in Criterion C and D. Extended answers expected.  
**Examples:**
- M19 Q8 (markband, Criterion D): "Evaluate the use of biofuels as an alternative to fossil fuels."
- N18 Q7b (markband, Criterion D): "Evaluate the environmental impact of using chlorine to disinfect water."
- M24 Q9b (markband, Criterion D): "Evaluate the social, economic, and environmental implications of nuclear energy."

---

### Discuss
**Definition:** Offer a considered and balanced review that includes a range of arguments, factors, or hypotheses.  
**How to apply:** Cover multiple perspectives. Conclusions should be supported with evidence. Common in Criterion D extended responses.  
**Examples:**
- N20 Q8 (markband, Criterion D): "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using hydrogen as a fuel."
- M17 Q8 (markband, Criterion D): "Discuss the impact of plastics on the environment."
- N21 Q9 (markband, Criterion D): "Discuss the use of nuclear power as a low-carbon energy source."

---

### Deduce
**Definition:** Reach a conclusion from given information using logical reasoning.  
**How to apply:** Show the logical chain from data to conclusion. Similar to Determine but with more emphasis on the reasoning process.  
**Examples:**
- M16 Q3b (WTTE): "Deduce the type of bonding present from the physical properties given."
- N19 Q6c (WTTE): "Deduce the formula of the salt formed."
- M21 Q5e (WTTE): "Deduce whether the reaction is a redox reaction."

---

### Justify
**Definition:** Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion.  
**How to apply:** State your answer AND the scientific reason. Mark schemes for "justify" questions often require both components.  
**Examples:**
- N24 Q4c: Conditional marking — "Do NOT award validity mark unless the justification mark is awarded."
- M23 Q4e (WTTE): "Justify the choice of indicator for the titration."
- N17 Q4f (WTTE): "Justify whether the reaction mixture is acidic, basic, or neutral."

---

### Design
**Definition:** Produce a plan, simulation, or model.  
**How to apply:** For experimental design (Criterion B), must include: research question, hypothesis, variables (IV, DV, CVs), method, materials, data collection, safety. Marked by markband rubric — see Section 3.  
**Examples:**
- M16 Q6f (markband, 16 marks, Criterion B): Full experimental design question.
- M25 Q6b (markband, 18 marks, Criterion B): Design an investigation into the effect of temperature on enzyme activity.
- N18 Q5 (markband, 19 marks, Criterion B): Design an experiment to investigate the factors affecting rate of reaction.

---

### Draw
**Definition:** Produce a diagram, graph, or structural formula.  
**How to apply:** For graphs: label axes with quantity and unit, appropriate scale, plot all points, draw best-fit line/curve. For structures: show all bonds and lone pairs if required.  
**Examples:**
- M22: "Draw a dot-and-cross diagram for a molecule of water."
- N23 Q4e (ECF): "Draw a graph of volume of gas produced against time."
- M18: "Draw the displayed formula of ethanol."

---

### Plot
**Definition:** Mark data points on a graph and draw a line of best fit.  
**How to apply:** Plot each point as a cross (×). Draw the best-fit line through the trend — do not dot-to-dot. Mark scheme usually gives [1] for correct plotting and [1] for correct line.  
**Examples:**
- M19 Q6a: "Plot the data in the table on the grid provided."
- N20 Q5c: "Plot a graph showing the volume of gas produced over time."
- M24: "Plot the cooling curve for the data given."

---

### Estimate
**Definition:** Obtain an approximate value.  
**How to apply:** Acceptable range is usually specified in the mark scheme. Show method. Do not over-calculate.  
**Examples:**
- M21 Q4b (ECF): "Estimate the rate of reaction at t = 5 minutes."
- N22 Q4f (ECF): "Estimate the enthalpy change from the graph."
- M25 Q4b (ECF): "Estimate the half-life of the radioactive decay from the graph."

---

### Analyse
**Definition:** Break down information into components and identify their characteristics.  
**How to apply:** Identify patterns, relationships, trends. More structured than "describe" — link what you see to the underlying science.  
**Examples:**
- N21 Q2e (ORA): "Analyse the data to determine which metal is more reactive."
- M23 Q5e (WTTE, ORA): "Analyse the chromatogram to identify the substances present."
- M24 Q4a (ORA): "Analyse the results to determine the order of reactivity."

---

### Formulate (Research Question)
**Definition:** Express a specific, focused question for investigation, usually in the form "How does [IV] affect [DV]?"  
**How to apply:** Include the independent variable, dependent variable, and the system/substance being studied. Mark scheme WTTE — exact wording not required.  
**Examples:**
- N16 Q5a: Research question required as part of Criterion B design (18 marks total).
- M17 Q3g: Criterion B 14-mark question required a formulated RQ.
- M18 Q3d: "Formulate a research question for an investigation into the effect of concentration on reaction rate."

---

### Label
**Definition:** Add appropriate labels to a diagram.  
**How to apply:** Use the terms specified or accepted equivalents. Arrows must point to the correct feature.  
**Examples:**
- M19: "Label the parts of the electrolysis cell shown."
- N17: "Label the axes of the graph."
- M25: "Label the activation energy and the enthalpy change on the energy profile diagram."

## 2. Mark Scheme Conventions

### WTTE — Will The Taxon Elect
**Meaning:** Accept any answer that conveys the same scientific meaning, even if the phrasing differs from the mark scheme.  
**When it appears:** Appears on most [1]-mark knowledge and explanation questions. Signals flexibility of language.  
**What it does NOT cover:** Scientifically incorrect statements; vague or incomplete answers.  

**Real examples:**
- M16 Q2b: "WTTE. Accept references greater than the solubility product constant." — any equivalent expression of Ksp exceeded.
- M16 Q7b: "WTTE. Do not accept 'the data should be included' without relating it correctly to the trend." — WTTE applies to phrasing, not to omitting key concepts.
- M17 Q4b: "Increase air flow or more oxygen or more air — WTTE." — three phrasings all accepted.
- M23 Q1d: WTTE on surface area and rate explanation — "more frequent collisions", "greater collision frequency", "more collisions per second" all valid.
- M25 Q4d (WTTE): Accepted "loses electrons", "is oxidised", "electron donor" as equivalent answers.

**Exam tip:** Write clearly but do not memorise exact phrases from textbooks. If your answer is scientifically correct, WTTE protects you.

---

### ORA — Or Reverse Argument
**Meaning:** The mark scheme gives one direction of comparison; ORA means the opposite direction is equally valid if correctly reasoned.  
**When it appears:** Comparison questions, rate-of-reaction comparisons, reactivity series questions, any "which is greater/faster/more" question.  

**Real examples:**
- M18 Q5e: "Limestone particles are smaller than crushed oyster shell [1] — ORA; rate of reaction is greater for limestone [1] — ORA." If a student argued oyster shell is larger and therefore slower, both marks awarded.
- M18 Q6b: "Molecules with the smallest mass or length will have the lowest boiling point [1] — ORA." Accept "molecules with the largest mass will have the highest boiling point."
- M22 Q4b: "ORA for solid catalyst" — if student argued that solution gives faster rate, accept the converse argument for solid being slower.
- N21 Q1d: "ORA" — sodium more reactive than lithium, OR lithium less reactive than sodium — either direction accepted.
- N20 Q7f (ORA): Comparison of two fuels — either direction of comparison accepted with correct reasoning.

**Exam tip:** If a comparison question gives you freedom to choose either substance as the reference point, pick the one you know better. ORA means you will not be penalised for choosing the "other" direction.

---

### ECF — Error Carried Forward
**Meaning:** If a student makes an error in an early part of a calculation or conclusion, they can still gain marks in subsequent parts if they correctly apply the method to their (wrong) earlier answer.  
**When it appears:** Any multi-step calculation chain; any question where a later part explicitly builds on an earlier answer.  
**Limit:** ECF does not apply if the carried-forward answer is physically impossible or chemically nonsensical.  

**Real examples:**
- M16 Q1e: "ECF from part 1d" — element identified in 1d used to answer 1e; if wrong element but correct ion charge reasoning, mark awarded.
- M17 Q2d: ECF from molar mass calculation — each subsequent step marked on its own merits using the student's value.
- M17 Q5c: "ECF applies throughout the calculation chain" — concentration of HCl calculated from student's own volume, even if volume was wrong.
- M18 Q3c/3d: Enthalpy calculation chain — Q and ΔH steps each carry ECF.
- M21 Q4b (ECF): Rate estimated from graph — if student's tangent was drawn incorrectly but gradient calculated correctly from it, mark awarded.
- M22 Q6b/6c (ECF): Stoichiometry chain — moles in 6b carried into 6c.
- N18 Q6a/6b (ECF): Two-step electrochemistry calculation — each step ECF from previous.

**Exam tip:** Always show your working. Even if you get step 1 wrong, a clearly shown correct method in step 2 using your step-1 answer will earn the ECF mark. Never skip steps.

---

### CON — Contradiction
**Meaning:** A student gives a correct answer but then immediately contradicts it in the same response. The contradiction cancels the mark — award 0.  
**When it appears:** Identification and naming questions; any question where a student adds an incorrect qualifier to a correct answer.  

**Real examples:**
- M23 Q1c: "CON: Both alcohols = award 0." — student correctly identified one organic compound as an alcohol, then also identified the second (which was not an alcohol) as an alcohol. The contradiction cancels the correct identification.
- N24: Listed as a general marking instruction applicable across the whole paper — any correct answer negated by a contradictory statement receives 0.

**Exam tip:** Do not hedge or list multiple answers hoping one is correct. If you give two contradictory answers, you score zero even if one was right. Write one clear answer.

---

### Sequential / Conditional Marking
**Meaning:** Certain marks can only be awarded if a prior mark was also awarded. The mark scheme explicitly states this dependency.  
**When it appears:** Multi-step experimental justifications; evaluation chains; any question where step N is only meaningful if step N-1 is correct.  

**Real examples:**
- M22 Q3b: "Third mark for pH probe vs indicator only if first two marks awarded." — method choice only credited if the variable identification was correct.
- N22 Q5a: "Do not award the third mark unless the first two marks are awarded." — conclusion only credited if evidence was correctly processed.
- M24 Q6d: "Award the 4th mark only if marking point 2 or 3 is scored." — numerical answer only credited if intermediate reasoning was shown.
- N24 Q4c: "Do NOT award validity mark unless the justification mark is awarded." — a conclusion without justification receives 0 for the conclusion mark.

**Exam tip:** In multi-part "justify/evaluate" questions, always give the reasoning before the conclusion. If you write the conclusion without the supporting argument, you may lose both marks.

## 3. Markband Rubrics

Criterion B (Inquiring and Designing) and Criterion D (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science) questions are marked using **markbands** — holistic best-fit rubrics, not point-by-point mark schemes.

### How markband marking works
The examiner reads the whole response and decides which band best describes the overall quality. A response does not need to satisfy every descriptor in a band to receive that band — the examiner exercises professional judgement. This is called **best-fit marking**.

---

### Criterion B — Inquiring and Designing (typically 14–19 marks)

A Criterion B question asks you to design a complete investigation. The markband assesses four components:

| Component | What examiners look for |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Research question / focus | How precisely is the investigation defined? |
| Variables | IV and DV identified? How many CVs listed? |
| Method | Is it followable? Would it actually produce useful data? Complete and replicable? |
| Data collection plan | Types of data collected, number of trials, averaging |
| Safety / ethics | Hazards identified? Safety measures linked to specific hazard? |

**Markband descriptors (universal pattern across all sessions):**

| Band | Score range | Variables | Method | Data plan | Safety |
|------|-------------|-----------|--------|-----------|--------|
| 0 | 0 | None | None | None | None |
| 1 | Low | Some implied | Attempt only; not complete | One type of data | Concern mentioned |
| 2 | Mid-low | IV or DV + one CV | Insufficient detail; partially followable | Several data types or 3 trials | Hazard identified; linked to risk |
| 3 | Mid-high | IV, DV + one CV clear | Followable; would produce relevant data | All data types + 3 trials | — |
| 4 | Max | IV, DV + two or more CVs | Complete; fully replicable; controls described | All data types + 3 trials + averaging plan | — |

**Real Criterion B questions:**
- M16 Q6f: 16 marks — design an investigation into a factor affecting solubility.
- M17 Q3g: 14 marks — design an investigation into the effect of concentration on rate of reaction.
- M18 Q3d: 17 marks — design an experiment to investigate enthalpy of combustion.
- M19 Q6a: 17 marks — design an investigation into enzyme activity.
- N16 Q5a: 18 marks — design an investigation into electroplating.
- N18 Q5: 19 marks — design an experiment into rate of reaction (highest mark total seen).
- M25 Q6b: 18 marks — design an investigation into enzyme activity.

**Key advice for Criterion B:**
1. State the research question first ("How does [IV] affect [DV] in [system]?").
2. Explicitly name IV, DV, and at least two CVs.
3. Write method as numbered steps — enough detail for someone else to follow.
4. State number of trials (minimum 3) and that you will calculate averages.
5. Identify the specific hazard and state what you will do to mitigate it.

---

### Criterion D — Reflecting on the Impacts of Science (typically 7–17 marks)

A Criterion D question asks you to reflect on the real-world, social, economic, or environmental implications of chemistry. Extended writing. The markband assesses:

| Component | What examiners look for |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Scientific content | Is the science correct and relevant? |
| Discussion breadth | How many different factors/perspectives are covered? |
| Use of evidence | Are claims supported with specific data or examples? |
| Structure | Is the response logically organised? |

**Markband descriptors (universal pattern):**

| Band | Score range | Scientific content | Discussion | Use of evidence |
|------|-------------|-------------------|------------|-----------------|
| 0 | 0 | None | None | None |
| 1 | Low | Statement implied | One factor for one system | No evidence |
| 2 | Mid-low | Statement explicit | One factor covering both systems | Choice stated but not justified |
| 3 | Mid-high | Statement with scientific explanation | Multiple factors, both perspectives | Choice with justification |
| 4 | Max | Full scientific explanation with data | All relevant factors with science | Well-justified conclusion with evidence |

**Real Criterion D questions:**
- M16 Q11c: 12 marks — impacts of using fossil fuels vs renewable energy.
- M17 Q8: 14 marks — discuss the impact of plastics on the environment.
- M17 Q9: 10 marks — discuss the use of bioplastics as an alternative.
- M18 Q6c: 8 marks — environmental impact of CFCs.
- M18 Q7: 14 marks — evaluate nuclear power as an energy source.
- M19 Q8: 17 marks — evaluate biofuels as an alternative to fossil fuels.
- N16 Q11: 17 marks — discuss sustainable chemistry / green chemistry principles.
- N18 Q7b: 14 marks — evaluate chlorine use in water disinfection.
- N20 Q8: 15 marks — discuss hydrogen as a fuel.
- M24 Q9b: 14 marks — evaluate nuclear energy (4 sub-dimensions: social, economic, environmental, political).
- M25 Q7b: 16 marks — evaluate the use of carbon capture and storage.
- N21 Q9: 12 marks — discuss nuclear power as low-carbon energy.

**Key advice for Criterion D:**
1. Open with a clear statement of the issue and your position.
2. Discuss at least two perspectives (e.g., economic benefits vs environmental costs).
3. Include specific examples and data — named substances, actual figures where possible.
4. Address more than one stakeholder or system.
5. Close with a justified conclusion — do not leave it open-ended.

## 4. Examiner Marking Notes

### "Allow" vs explicit mark scheme answers
Where the mark scheme says "Allow [alternative]", examiners credit the alternative answer even without WTTE being stated. Treat it the same as WTTE.

### "Do not accept"
Marks schemes sometimes explicitly list answers that sound plausible but are NOT credited. Examples:
- M16 Q7b: "Do not accept 'the data should be included' without relating it correctly to the trend."
- Any answer that is vague (e.g., "it affects the result") without specifying how.

### Units
Numerical answers almost always require the correct unit. If the mark scheme gives a value without explicitly saying "ignore unit error", a missing or wrong unit loses the mark.

### Significant figures
Most mark schemes accept ±1 significant figure unless stated otherwise. Clearly excessive rounding (e.g., 2 instead of 1.8) may lose a mark. Always match or exceed the precision of the data given.

### Equation balancing
Mark schemes for equation questions usually give [1] for correct formulae and [1] for correct balancing. State symbols are often a separate [1]. You can gain partial credit.
