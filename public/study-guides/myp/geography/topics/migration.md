# Migration

Covers: voluntary migration, forced migration, internally displaced people (IDPs), refugees, asylum seekers, urbanization movement, and the impacts of migration on communities.

## Key concepts

- **Voluntary migration**: People choosing to move for better opportunities — jobs, education, quality of life, family reunification.
- **Forced migration / forced displacement**: People compelled to leave their homes by conflict, persecution, natural disasters, or climate change.
- **Internally Displaced People (IDPs)**: People forced to flee their homes but who remain within their own country.
- **Refugees**: People who have crossed an international border to escape persecution, conflict, or violence and cannot safely return home.
- **Asylum seekers**: People who have applied for refugee status but whose claim has not yet been decided.
- **Push factors**: Conditions that drive people to leave (poverty, conflict, drought, persecution).
- **Pull factors**: Conditions that attract people to a destination (economic opportunity, safety, family).
- **Rural-urban migration**: Movement from countryside to cities — a major driver of rapid urban growth in developing countries (Dhaka, Bangladesh: 400,000 mostly poor migrants per year; Kampala, Uganda: predicted 1.6M → 3.9M by 2030).
- **Brain drain**: Skilled workers leaving developing countries for developed ones, depriving origin countries of human capital.
- **Dependency ratio**: The ratio of dependants (elderly + children) to working-age population. Migration of young adults can lower it (benefit) or raise it in origin countries (problem).
- **Remittances**: Money sent by migrants back to their home country — often a major source of income for developing nations.
- **Xenophobia**: Hostility or prejudice toward foreigners — a negative social consequence of migration.
- **Cultural assimilation / integration**: The process by which migrants adopt aspects of the host culture. Degree of integration is debated — some host communities feel migrants should fully assimilate; others value multiculturalism.

## Case studies

### Global forced displacement figures (N21 Q6 — newspaper infographic)
- **2008**: 42 million forcibly displaced worldwide
  - 26 million IDPs
  - 15.2 million refugees
  - 827,000 asylum seekers
- **2018**: 70.8 million forcibly displaced worldwide
  - 41.3 million IDPs
  - 25.9 million refugees
  - 3.5 million asylum seekers
- Increase in refugees: **10.7 million**
- Asylum seekers grew more than 4x (from 827,000 to 3.5 million)
- 84% of IDPs are in developing regions
- 55% of IDPs originate from Syria, Afghanistan, and South Sudan (2016 data)
- 97% of disaster-related displacements caused by climate-related disasters
- 20 new displacements per minute (2016)

### Syrian refugee migration to Germany
- Germany accepted 1 million refugees in the 2015–16 crisis
- Syrian refugees were predominantly young men (first wave)
- This increased Germany's economically active population, reducing the dependency ratio
- Benefit: filled labour shortages in Germany's ageing economy (Germany had a birth rate of 1.3 per woman)
- Negative: attacks on refugee shelters in Berlin — isolated but real xenophobic violence
- Syrian migrants have assimilated in many cases — many speaking German and working in German industries

### Rural-urban migration — Dhaka, Bangladesh (N21 Q5)
- Fastest-growing mega-city in the world
- Estimated 400,000 mostly poor migrants per year
- Rapid economic growth
- Challenge: lack of access to sewage and clean water → health issues (diarrhoea, water pollution)
- Streets and rivers used as toilets in some areas

### Rural-urban migration — Kampala, Uganda (N21 Q5)
- One of the fastest-growing cities in Africa
- Population: 1.6 million (2019) → predicted 3.9 million by 2030
- Driven by high birth rates AND rural-urban migration

### Internally Displaced People by country (N21 Q8 — Migration Matters infographic, 2016)
- China: 7.4 million IDPs
- Colombia: 7.4 million IDPs
- Philippines: 5.9 million IDPs
- Cuba: 1 million IDPs
- India: 2.4 million IDPs
- Syria: 824,000 IDPs (in 2016 data — note: subsequent years show much higher figures)
- Haiti: 46,691 IDPs

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **Two benefits required in N22 Q5**: Only explaining ONE benefit of migration caps Criterion A at 4 marks regardless of quality. Must cover two positive effects.
- **Named and unpacked examples**: For N21 Q9 (essay), "rural Chinese migrants boosted the economy by moving to cities and working in industries such as construction" earns more than vague references to "migrants help economies." Name the place + specific industry/mechanism.
- **Perspective-specific limitations**: For N21 Q8 (IDP map limitations), the examiner expects the student to explicitly name the user (MYP student / government official) AND explain WHY that specific user is disadvantaged by that specific limitation. A government official writing a report needs current data — so outdated data harms them differently than it would harm a student researching general trends.
- **Synthesis in conclusions**: Top-band essays (7–8 for Criterion D) require ideas to be combined in a conclusion that makes a clear judgement about the "to what extent" question.

### What examiners penalise
- **Essay language in speeches** (N22 Q5): Writing a formal essay when asked for a Diversity Week speech loses C2 marks. Must use first person, rhetorical questions, personal pronouns, emotive vocabulary.
- **One-sided essays** (N21 Q9): Covering only benefits of migration or only negatives caps Criterion D at 1–2 marks. Both perspectives must be genuinely developed.
- **Vague sources in research design** (N24 Q4): "Newspapers" or "statistics" alone = 0. Must specify type and relevance to the migration investigation.

### Perspective-specific source limitation (model comparison, N21 Q8)

## Common question types

1. **Data reading** (1 mark each): Read figures from an infographic (increase in refugees, type growing 4x). Quote the precise number — "10.7 million" not "about 11 million."
2. **Outline purpose/value of a source** (2 marks): Purpose = what it's for ("informs readers about forced migration"). Value = why that format/medium is useful for this audience ("a pictograph makes complex data easy to understand for newspaper readers").
3. **Explain limitation from a specific perspective** (3 marks): Must name the user, name the limitation, explain *why this user specifically is disadvantaged*. Generic limitations = 1 mark.
4. **Migration essay** (24 marks, N21 Q9): Two genuine perspectives (benefits + negatives), named and unpacked examples, synthesis conclusion. Benefits: economic (labour gaps, taxes, remittances), cultural diversity. Negatives: social tension, xenophobia, pressure on services, brain drain.
5. **Diversity speech** (18 marks, N22 Q5): Two positive effects of migration explained with examples. Speech format: opening statement, emotive language, first person, conclusion. Covering only one benefit = Criterion A max 4 marks.
6. **Research design** (12 marks, N24 Q4): RQ must name a specific migration + place (e.g. Syrian refugees to Germany). Justify = explain mechanism by which migration changes population structure.

## Key arguments (for "to what extent" essays on migration)

### N21 Q9: "Migration creates more benefits for societies than negative consequences"

**YES — benefits outweigh negatives:**
- Fills labour shortages: Syrian refugees to Germany → young, economically active → reduced dependency ratio → increased tax base → funded pensions for ageing German population
- Cultural diversity enriches host society through cuisine, arts, languages
- Remittances from migrants to origin countries fund development
- Rural-to-urban migrants in China boosted manufacturing and construction, driving economic growth
- Forced migrants bring skills and resilience to host communities

**NO — negatives are significant:**
- Xenophobia and social tension: attacks on refugee shelters in Berlin, Germany
- Pressure on housing, schools, hospitals in destination areas
- Cultural friction: some host community members feel migrants don't integrate (N22 Q5 stimulus perspective: "they don't try to fit in and many immigrants don't speak our language")
- Economic drain argument: "we don't have enough jobs for our own people" (N22 stimulus perspective 1)
- Brain drain from origin countries deprives them of skilled workers
- Forced migrants face trauma, exploitation, and poor living conditions

**Conclusion that gets top marks:** Migration creates significant economic benefits for host societies — particularly in ageing economies where labour gaps would otherwise harm living standards. However, social tensions and xenophobia are real negative consequences. The net balance depends on scale, type of migration, host society's political will to integrate migrants, and origin country's capacity to manage the resulting brain drain. Well-managed voluntary migration tends to produce more benefits; forced migration creates more complex challenges for both migrants and hosts.

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the forced displacement data** (N21 Q6): 42M → 70.8M total; refugees increased by 10.7M; asylum seekers grew 4x (827,000 → 3.5M). These exact figures appear in State and Select questions.
2. **Two benefits for diversity speeches** (N22 Q5): Always cover TWO positive effects of migration. One effect = capped at Criterion A 4 marks. Germany/Syrian refugees is the go-to example.
3. **Perspective-specific analysis**: Practice explaining why outdated or incomplete data harms a government official differently from a student researcher. The key is specificity about the user's needs.
4. **Essay balance**: Both sides must be genuinely developed (not just mentioned). Examples must be named AND unpacked (explain the mechanism, not just the fact).
5. **Speech format vs essay format**: Diversity Week speech needs opening statement, emotive language, rhetorical questions, first person. Essay needs formal language, intro/body/conclusion as separate paragraphs.
6. **Research question specificity** (N24 Q4a): "How has migration to Germany changed its population structure?" = 1 mark. "How has Syrian refugee migration to Germany changed its population structure?" = 2 marks (specific migration named).
