# Rivers and Coasts

## Key concepts

- **Erosion**: The wearing away of rock or soil by water, wind, ice, or other forces. In rivers: hydraulic action (force of water), abrasion (material scraping), attrition (material colliding), solution (chemical dissolving). In coasts: same processes plus corrasion and corrosion.
- **Hydraulic action**: The force of water itself crashing against a surface, compressing air in cracks.
- **Attrition**: Particles carried by the river collide with each other and break into smaller, rounder pieces.
- **Longshore drift**: The movement of sediment along a coastline at an angle, driven by waves approaching at an angle and backwashing straight back.
- **Flooding**: When a river or coastal system is overwhelmed and water spreads beyond its channel or shoreline.
- **River basin / catchment area**: The area of land from which a river collects water.
- **Sustainable management**: Strategies that protect communities and the environment while allowing their continued use — includes hard engineering (levees, sea walls, flood barriers) and soft engineering (afforestation, managed retreat, beach nourishment).
- **Levees / embankments**: Raised banks along a river channel to contain floodwater.
- **Water plaza**: An urban flood management strategy — a multi-use public space with sunken reservoirs that capture surface water runoff and release it gradually into the storm drainage system (Rotterdam example).
- **Agricultural run-off**: Fertilisers, pesticides, and animal waste that wash from farmland into rivers, causing water quality decline (eutrophication, dead zones).
- **E. coli**: A bacteria indicator of water pollution from agricultural run-off and sewage — used as a measure of river water quality.
- **Dams**: Structures built across rivers to create reservoirs, generating hydroelectricity and controlling water flow; significant downstream impacts on communities and ecosystems.

## Case studies

### Portchester, UK (M21 Q5)
- A coastal town in the United Kingdom where a river meets the ocean
- Features: city/grey buildings, farmland, water treatment plant, residential housing on coastal promontory, dock/pier, ocean, coral reef area
- **Identified threats in the exam**: Agricultural run-off into the river (from fertilisers, pesticides, animal waste); coastal erosion threatening residential areas
- **Management strategy used in mark scheme (4 marks)**: Restrict fertiliser application to minimal-impact varieties (as done in Germany — the Alster River example); raised walkways to protect coastal paths from tourist/foot traffic erosion
- Specific example: "The Alster River in Germany where the government has restricted fertilizer application to only include fertilizers which have minimal impact on the surrounding environment"
- Students who named a specific management strategy for two labelled threats and referenced the diagram scored 6 marks (Criterion A)

### Rotterdam, Netherlands — Benthemplein Water Plaza (M23 Q3)
- Rotterdam experiences heavy rainfall and flooding every year
- Investigation: urban planner assessing flood risk in Benthemplein area
- **Proposed solution**: "Water Plaza" — 3 sunken reservoirs that can hold 1,730,000 litres of rainwater; released gradually into storm water drainage system; also used as recreational space
- **Data collected**: Interviews with local council (other water storage areas), resident interviews, 3 weeks of rainfall data, soil permeability samples, government meteorology predictions
- Weakness identified: not all stakeholders consulted (businesses not included); limited sample of interviews
- Strength: mixed primary and secondary data; phase structure allows review before finalising design

### Mekong River — Cambodia/Laos (M24 Q2)
- Context: People of Cambodia concerned about health of the Mekong River — water level decreasing, more polluted, fish stocks declining
- **Laos has built over 60 dams on the Mekong River** with plans for more; sells electricity to neighbouring countries
- **Key data**: 60 million people across 6 countries rely on the Mekong; river level dropped to lowest in 100 years; lack of water → fewer fish, more algae; Cambodia farmers experience erratic flow as Laos stores/releases water
- Investigation by students in Pakse (Laos) and Stung Treng (Cambodia): cross-border collaboration to understand impacts
- Limitation: students did not test water quality (e.g. oxygen levels); water-deprived areas can create dead zones
- Strength: data collected from two locations (Laos + Cambodia) provides comparative perspective

### Mississippi River — St Louis (M24 Q3 example)
- Mark scheme example for research question formulation
- RQ example: "To what extent would levees on the Mississippi River prevent flooding in St Louis?"
- GIS mapping data shows communities most at risk from flooding along the river's path
- Levees increase the volume of water the river can hold, preventing overflow into surrounding communities

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **Two threats covered** (M21 Q5): To access 6 marks on Criterion A, the presentation must address management strategies for *two* identified threats, not just one.
- **Diagram references** (M21 Q5): The persuasive presentation must explicitly reference the labelled diagram (using named locations, grid references, or features marked A and B). Without map reference, maximum is capped lower.
- **Causal chain for physical processes** (M24 Q1b): Simply naming a process = 1 mark. Full explanation requires: process → mechanism → threat to community. Example: "Erosion undercuts riverbanks → bank becomes top-heavy and collapses → threatens farmland close to the river as fertile land is washed away."
- **Specific water quality data** (M24 Q2): Noting that the investigation didn't test oxygen levels, or that water-deprived areas create dead zones, earns 3 marks for limitations (not just "didn't test water quality").
- **Cross-boundary perspective**: Mekong case shows that upstream dams in one country (Laos) directly damage downstream communities in another (Cambodia) — this is the geographic insight that earns high marks in evaluation questions.

### What examiners penalise
- **Generic research questions** (M24 Q3a): "To what extent would levees protect communities?" = 1 mark only (no location). Must specify both place AND strategy for 2 marks.
- **Vague secondary sources** (M24 Q3c): "Websites" or "newspaper articles" = 0 marks. Must specify type and relevance: "GIS mapping data showing flooding on the Mississippi" = 1 mark.
- **Presentation style vs essay style** (M21 Q5): This is a criterion-assessed communication task. Writing like an essay when asked for a persuasive presentation loses Criterion C2 marks. Must use first person, rhetorical questions, emotive vocabulary, direct audience address.

### Evaluate structure for rivers questions (M21 Q2, M23 Q3, M24 Q2)
All three evaluate questions follow the same 3+3+2 structure:
- Strengths (max 3): one well-explained strength with reasoning
- Limitations (max 3): one well-explained limitation with reasoning
- Appraisal (max 2): an overall judgement that weighs positives against negatives (can appear anywhere, not just the conclusion)

## Common question types

1. **State physical processes** (2 marks): Name two valid processes. No explanation needed. Accept: erosion, flooding, longshore drift, attrition, hydraulic action.
2. **Explain one physical process threatening communities** (4 marks): Choose one process, give the full causal chain (mechanism → community impact). One process developed fully > two processes mentioned briefly.
3. **Evaluate an action plan** (8 marks): Strengths + Limitations + Appraisal. See research-methods.md for the full pattern.
4. **Formulate RQ + justify** (6 marks, M24 Q3a–b): RQ must name a place AND a specific strategy AND connect to the SoI. Justify means explain why investigating it helps the local council protect the community/environment.
5. **Persuasive presentation with diagram** (18 marks, M21 Q5): Major communication task. Two threats + two management strategies + explicit diagram references + persuasive language + key/legend + concluding statement.

## Key arguments (for essay-type questions)

### For "to what extent does sustainable management protect communities and the environment?"

**Sustainable management DOES protect:**
- Hard engineering like levees, sea walls, and water plazas contain floodwater and prevent property damage
- Rotterdam's Benthemplein approach stores 1.73 million litres — prevents flooding while also creating recreational space (multi-use = sustainable)
- Restricting fertiliser use (Alster River, Germany) reduces agricultural run-off and restores water quality for downstream communities
- Cross-border cooperation (Mekong: Cambodia + Laos) can manage shared resources more fairly

**Sustainable management is LIMITED:**
- Upstream dam-building (Laos on Mekong) shows one country's "development" can devastate another's river health — requires international governance to be effective
- Agricultural run-off is difficult to control — farmers resist restrictions; enforcement is costly
- Hard engineering (levees) can increase flood risk downstream and encourages development in flood-prone areas
- Climate change is making rainfall more intense — existing flood infrastructure may be insufficient

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the physical processes and their mechanisms**: Be able to explain hydraulic action, attrition, longshore drift, and erosion with a causal chain reaching community impact.
2. **Rotterdam Water Plaza**: Know the key data (1.73 million litres, 3 reservoirs, recreational use) and understand it as an adaptation strategy.
3. **Mekong River**: Know the upstream/downstream dynamic (Laos dams → Cambodia water decline), the 60 million people figure, and the investigation's strengths/limitations.
4. **Portchester management strategies**: Know at least two threats (agricultural run-off and coastal erosion) and specific management responses (fertiliser restrictions, raised walkways).
5. **M21 Q5 is a high-stakes communication task**: Practice writing in persuasive presentation style — first person, audience-directed, rhetorical questions, emotive vocabulary, with explicit diagram references.
6. **RQ formulation rule**: Always include location + specific strategy/focus + connection to SoI for 2 marks.
