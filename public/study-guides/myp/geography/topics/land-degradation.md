# Land Degradation

## Key concepts

- **Land degradation**: The decline in the overall quality of land, including its soil, vegetation, and productivity — caused by both natural and human processes.
- **Soil degradation**: The decline in soil quality — loss of structure, fertility, and organic matter. Commonly caused by human activities.
- **Desertification**: The process by which fertile land becomes desert-like — caused by overgrazing, deforestation, drought, and over-farming. Particularly severe in arid/semi-arid regions.
- **Overgrazing**: When too many animals graze on land, removing vegetation cover, leaving soil exposed to erosion by wind and water.
- **Deforestation**: Removal of trees — exposes soil to erosion, disrupts nutrient cycling, reduces water retention.
- **Agricultural activities**: Intensive farming using heavy machinery, monocultures, and chemical inputs can compact soil, remove nutrients, and cause waterlogging.
- **Over-exploitation of vegetation for domestic use**: Collecting firewood, building materials, and other plant resources at unsustainable rates.
- **Soil erosion**: The washing or blowing away of the topsoil layer — removes nutrients, reduces fertility, leads to further degradation.
- **Sahel region**: The arid savannah south of the Sahara Desert, crossing 11 countries, severely affected by desertification. The semi-arid transition zone between the Sahara and wetter sub-Saharan Africa.
- **Reforestation / afforestation**: Planting trees to restore degraded land — restores soil structure, reduces erosion, and can sequester carbon.
- **Great Green Wall**: An African Union initiative (started 2007) to restore 100 million hectares of degraded land across 11 Sahel countries by 2030 — a mix of trees, grasslands, and plants spanning 8,000 km long and 15 km wide.
- **Arable land**: Land suitable for growing crops. Decline in % arable land is a key indicator of land degradation severity.

## Case studies

### Asia and Australia land degradation (M21 Q1 — donut chart)
- **Asia total degraded**: 370.3 million hectares
  - Overgrazing: 118.8 million ha (largest cause)
  - Agricultural activities: 111.5 million ha
  - Deforestation: 96.7 million ha
  - Over-exploitation of vegetation for domestic use: 42.3 million ha
  - Bio-industrial activities: 1.0 million ha
- **Australia total degraded**: 87.5 million ha
  - Overgrazing: 78.5 million ha (dominant cause at ~90%)
  - Agricultural activities: 4.8 million ha
  - Deforestation: 4.2 million ha
- **Key comparison**: Asia has 4x more total degraded land; overgrazing is dominant in both but far more extreme in Australia proportionally

### The Sahel region and the Great Green Wall (N22 Q1–2)
- The Sahel is the arid savannah south of the Sahara, crossing 11 countries
- **45% of Africa's land has been degraded**
- Causes of degradation in the Sahel: climate change, changing weather patterns, overgrazing, population growth, unsustainable farming practices
- **Human factors**: overgrazing, population growth, agriculture/over-farming
- **Natural factors**: extreme weather, drought, changing weather patterns
- Note: "unsustainable practices" alone is NOT accepted as a human factor — must be specific (overgrazing, population growth, agriculture)

### Great Green Wall Project (N22 Q1–2)
- Started 2007; African Union initiative
- Goal: restore 100 million hectares of degraded land across the Sahel by 2030
- Create 10 million jobs in rural areas
- The wall is 8,000 km long and 15 km wide
- Uses a mixture of trees, grasslands, and plants
- By 2022: approximately 15% completed
- By 2019: 503 hectares restored across Ghana, Mali, and Chad

### Green Beyond NGO — Mali action plan (N22 Q2)
- NGO working in Mali since 2007, focused on the Great Green Wall Project
- **Yearly results (2019 data)**: 891 people trained; 6,300 hectares reforested; 6,000 hectares land restored; 165,203 trees planted in one year
- **Action plan evaluated**: Visit 2 villages, interview 1 person per village; analyse % arable land and % population in extreme poverty (2020 to present)
- **Strength**: Uses both economic (% in extreme poverty) and social (% arable land) indices — balanced review of project aims
- **Limitation**: Only 2 people interviewed from 2 villages — sample size too small; people within villages have varying experiences → limited representation of project's community impact

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **Specific overgrazing mechanism** (M21 Q2): The full causal chain earns 4 marks: "Overgrazing removes plant cover → soil exposed → water and wind erode topsoil → nutrients removed → structure and fertility weakened." Each step adds marks. Simply saying "overgrazing causes soil erosion" = 1–2 marks.
- **Named examples enhance but are not required** for 4-mark explain questions (M21 Q2). An example can support the explanation but the mechanism must be there regardless.
- **Both economic AND social indices** are a strength in NGO evaluations (N22 Q2): Reviewing % arable land (environmental) and % in extreme poverty (economic) together = a balanced review. Stating only one dimension = limited strength.
- **Sample size critique with specific numbers** (N22 Q2): "Only 2 interviews from 2 villages is not enough" earns 1 mark. "2 interviews from a project involving hundreds of villages means the data is not representative of the project's impact" earns 2–3 marks.

### What examiners penalise
- **Multiple answers for Identify questions** (M21 Q1a, Q1b): The mark scheme explicitly states that if more than one answer is given, the response is incorrect and scored 0. For Identify questions with one required answer — write ONE answer only.
- **"Unsustainable practices"** is not a specific human factor (N22 Q1a mark scheme explicitly rejects it). Accepted: overgrazing, population growth, agriculture, over-farming.
- **Generic appraisals** in evaluate questions: "Overall, the investigation was good" = 1 mark. "Although the sample size is small, the use of both economic and social indicators means the investigation can still provide useful data on the project's impact" = 2 marks (references a specific aspect).

### Overgrazing causal chain (model answer for M21 Q2)
1. Animals eat plant cover over the soil (overgrazing removes vegetation)
2. Without plants to keep soil in place, the topsoil is exposed
3. Wind and water wash away the topsoil layer (soil erosion begins)
4. Nutrients in the topsoil are removed with erosion
5. The structure and fertility of the soil weaken
6. Less fertile soil reduces agricultural productivity → desertification can follow

## Common question types

1. **Identify from a graph** (1 mark each): Read the cause/region directly from a donut chart or bar graph. Write ONE answer only — multiple answers = 0.
2. **Explain one human activity causes land degradation** (4 marks): Overgrazing is the easiest to develop in full detail. Use the causal chain above.
3. **Evaluate an NGO action plan** (8 marks): Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2). Focus on: sample size (too small?), data types used (balanced economic and social?), timeframe (sufficient?). See research-methods.md for full structure.
4. **Human vs natural factors** (1 mark each): Know the distinction — overgrazing and population growth are human; drought and extreme weather are natural.

## Key arguments

### "To what extent is land degradation a human-caused problem?"

**Human causes dominate:**
- In Asia, 3 of top 4 causes are human (overgrazing 118.8M ha, agricultural activities 111.5M ha, deforestation 96.7M ha)
- In Australia, overgrazing (a human activity) accounts for ~90% of all degraded land (78.5/87.5 million ha)
- In the Sahel, population growth drives overgrazing and over-farming — the root driver is human pressure on land
- The Great Green Wall Project itself acknowledges the need for human-driven restoration — implicit acknowledgement that human activity caused the problem

**Natural factors also contribute:**
- Drought and changing weather patterns in the Sahel reduce vegetation cover independently of human activity
- Climate change (increasingly human-driven but also natural variability) creates longer dry seasons
- In the Sahel, changing rainfall patterns mean even well-managed land may degrade without drought mitigation

**Synthesis:** Human activities are the primary driver of land degradation globally (especially overgrazing and agriculture), but they interact with natural conditions. In semi-arid regions like the Sahel, drought and extreme weather amplify the damage of human activities — making both human and natural factors necessary to address.
