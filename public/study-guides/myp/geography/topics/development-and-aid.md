# Development and Aid

Covers: development indicators, HDI, international aid, inequality between and within countries, development goals (SDGs), and the relationship between aid and globalization.

## Key concepts

- **Development**: The process by which a country improves economic, social, and political well-being. Measured through indicators like GNI per capita, HDI, life expectancy, literacy rate.
- **HDI (Human Development Index)**: A composite index calculated from three indicators:
  1. **GNI per capita** (economic)
  2. **Education Index** (e.g. adult literacy rate, years of schooling)
  3. **Life expectancy at birth** (health)
  - Ranges from 0 to 1; closer to 1 = more developed
- **GNI (Gross National Income) per capita**: The average income per person in a country, including income from abroad — a key economic development indicator.
- **Adult literacy rate**: The percentage of adults who can read and write — an education development indicator.
- **Aid**: Resources (money, goods, technical assistance) transferred from wealthier countries or organizations to poorer ones to support development.
- **Bilateral aid**: Aid given directly from one government to another.
- **Multilateral aid**: Aid given through international organizations (e.g. UN, World Bank).
- **NGO**: Non-Governmental Organization — independent charities and civil society groups that deliver aid (e.g. Green Beyond NGO in the Sahel; Oxfam; UNICEF).
- **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)**: The UN's 17 goals for 2030, covering poverty, hunger, health, gender equality, clean energy, climate action, and more.
  - Goal 1: No Poverty
  - Goal 5: Gender Equality
  - Goal 12: Responsible Consumption and Production
- **Globalization**: The increasing interconnection of countries through trade, communication, migration, and investment. Aid can be seen as a form of globalization — global interactions creating change for communities.
- **Dependency ratio**: Ratio of dependants (elderly + young) to working-age people. High dependency ratios strain economies.
- **Remittances**: Money sent home by migrants — can substitute for aid in some contexts.
- **Poverty line**: $1.90/day (World Bank extreme poverty threshold) or $2.15/day (updated threshold used in Basra data in M24).
- **Fair trade**: A trading partnership that gives farmers and producers in developing countries fairer prices and better conditions — linked to SDG Goal 5 (Gender Equality) as most agricultural workers are women.

## Case studies

### Australia–Laos aid relationship (N20 Q1–4)
- **Laos** (developing country): HDI = 0.601; GNI per capita = US$6,079; adult literacy = 84.1%
- **Australia** (developed country): HDI = 0.939; GNI per capita = US$43,560; adult literacy = 99%
- In 2019, Australia's aid programme focused on primary education access in Laos
- **Results**: 19,795 children enrolled in reading/hygiene programmes; increased girls in primary school; 850 classrooms improved; 390 teachers trained
- **Aid breakdown by category**: Education 65%, Infrastructure and Trade 17%, Agriculture 10%, Effective Governance 7%, Development Support 5%, Health 1%, Building Resilience 1%
- Aid helps Laos achieve the Sustainable Development Goals

### Chain of benefit — education aid (N20 Q2)
The mark scheme model answer traces a full development chain:
1. Aid invests in education → more children access school
2. Better qualifications → better-paid jobs
3. Higher incomes → increased tax revenue
4. Tax revenue reinvested → more schools and hospitals built
5. Long-term outcome: **increased life expectancy** (closing the loop on HDI)
This is the 4-mark answer structure for "explain one benefit of aid for communities."

### SDGs and sustainable trade (N23 Q5)
- Stimulus: WTO researcher report
- 70% of total workforce in global cotton production are women (FAO)
- ~60–80% of world's food is grown by women (Fairtrade.net)
- More women than men live on less than $1.90/day (UN Women)
- 1.3 billion tonnes of food wasted annually while 2 billion people go hungry (UNDP)
- Fair trade in coffee (Ethiopia example): promotes fairer pay → supports women who dominate agricultural workforce → contributes to Goal 5 (Gender Equality)
- Sustainable trade can also address Goal 1 (No Poverty) and Goal 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production)

### Basra, Iraq — urban inequality (M24 Q5–7)
- Population: 1.45 million (growing due to urbanization)
- **More than 46% of Basra's population lives on less than $2.15 per day** — high inequality
- Wealth inequality within a city is a development challenge alongside GDP/national indicators
- Located at confluence of two largest rivers in the region, flowing into Arabian Gulf — major seaport, oil-based economy
- Context: Development inequality within cities is as important as between-country comparisons

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **HDI third indicator**: Must know all three — GNI per capita, Education Index, AND **life expectancy at birth**. "GDP per capita" and "infant mortality rate" are explicitly rejected. Life expectancy is the answer.
- **Largest aid category**: Education (65%) — a straightforward 1-mark identify question. Do not be tempted to say health (only 1% in Laos case).
- **Aid chain of reasoning** (N20 Q2): Each link in the causal chain earns marks. A 4-mark response traces the whole chain from investment → qualification → income → tax → community reinvestment → HDI improvement. Stopping at "better jobs" = 2 marks.
- **Globalization framing in RQ justification** (N20 Q3b): For 4 marks, the justification must explicitly connect the research question to globalization. Identifying that Australian aid is a "global interaction" that changes a "local community" in Laos is the key link.
- **Stakeholder specificity** (N20 Q3c): "Students" = 0. "Students in a school in Laos" = 1 mark. "Government officials" = 0. "Laos government officials" = 1 mark. Must be specific to the Laos context.
- **Source specificity** (N20 Q3d–e): "A website" = 0. "A government website showing the amount of aid given to Laos on a yearly basis" = 2 marks. Must specify type AND subject.

### What examiners penalise
- **GDP instead of GNI**: A common error. HDI uses GNI (Gross National Income), not GDP. One mark for selecting "life expectancy at birth" — the test asks for the third indicator beyond GNI and Education Index.
- **Biased sources** (N20 Q4): The mark scheme highlights that an action plan relying only on secondary sources from Australia is biased — it doesn't include Laos perspectives. This is a key 3-mark limitation.
- **Evaluation-only at end of process** (N20 Q4): A plan that only evaluates at the final stage (no draft feedback loop) is a structural limitation — the investigation cannot improve mid-process.

### Action plan evaluation — key limitation in Laos aid study (N20 Q4)
The examiner's model 3-mark limitation: "The student has only identified secondary sources that come from Australia. Using only these sources means the people of Laos' perspective on the impact of aid has not been considered. This means that the investigation is biased and is not a reliable investigation into how aid has impacted health in Laos."

## Common question types

1. **Identify from infographic** (1 mark): Largest aid category, third HDI indicator. One word or phrase only.
2. **Explain one benefit of aid** (4 marks): Develop one benefit with a full chain of reasoning. Education aid = best example because it connects to HDI through jobs, income, tax, services, life expectancy.
3. **Research design — stakeholders, sources** (12 marks combined, N20 Q3): Stakeholders must be specific to the context. Sources must specify type AND subject.
4. **Evaluate action plan on aid** (8 marks, N20 Q4): Key issues: source bias (only one country's sources); evaluation timing (only at end); sample size if interviews are involved.
5. **SDG report** (18 marks, N23 Q5): A major content + communication task. Pick ONE SDG, explain how sustainable trade contributes to it, use stimulus data (FAO/UN Women statistics), produce a formal report with title, subheadings, and recommendations.

## Key arguments (for development and aid questions)

### "To what extent does international aid effectively reduce development inequality?"

**Aid IS effective:**
- Australian aid in Laos: 19,795 children enrolled, 850 classrooms improved, 390 teachers trained — measurable progress on education access
- Education investment has multiplier effects: better jobs → higher income → higher tax → better services → HDI improvement
- Aid targeted at specific SDGs can address root causes (gender equality in agriculture, poverty)
- Fair trade in coffee (Ethiopia) gives women farmers fair prices → reduces poverty → contributes to Goal 5

**Aid has LIMITATIONS:**
- Aid dependency: countries may become reliant on external funding rather than developing domestic revenue
- Tied aid / bilateral aid can serve donor country interests rather than recipient needs
- If sources are biased (only from donor country perspective), aid can miss local needs
- Short-term project aid vs long-term structural change — infrastructure and governance take decades

**Synthesis:** Aid can effectively reduce inequality when: it is well-targeted to development indicators that have multiplier effects (education), involves local stakeholders in its design, and is evaluated by both donor AND recipient perspectives.

### "How does globalization create change for communities?"

The N20 statement of inquiry ("Globalization can create change for communities and individuals") frames all the aid-in-Laos questions. Key arguments:
- **Positive**: Global aid flows → improved education → better-qualified workers → economic growth → HDI improvement; global trade connections reduce poverty (SDGs via fair trade)
- **Negative**: Global aid can create dependency; Western-designed programmes may not suit local contexts; globalization of trade can exploit developing-country labour
- **Nuanced**: The impact depends on the type of globalization (aid vs trade vs investment), the recipient's governance, and whether local people are genuine stakeholders rather than passive recipients

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the three HDI indicators exactly**: GNI per capita, Education Index (adult literacy/years of schooling), Life expectancy at birth. Not GDP. Not infant mortality.
2. **Laos data**: HDI = 0.601; GNI per capita = US$6,079; literacy = 84.1%; largest aid category = Education (65%). These appear in Identify questions.
3. **Aid benefit chain**: Investment → qualification → job → income → tax → services → HDI (life expectancy). Know the full chain for 4-mark questions.
4. **Stakeholder specificity rule**: Vague stakeholders (students, government) = 0. Specific (students in a school in Laos, Laos government officials) = 1 mark each.
5. **Bias in evaluation**: Single-country sources = biased investigation — always flag this as a limitation in action plan evaluate questions.
6. **SDG report format** (N23 Q5): Title + subheadings = 2 format marks (easy points). Content requires choosing ONE SDG and explaining the mechanism by which sustainable trade contributes to it. Use the FAO/UN Women statistics from the stimulus.
