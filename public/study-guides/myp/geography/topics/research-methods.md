# Research Methods

## Overview
Research methods questions appear in **every single MYP Geography exam session**. They always occupy the same structural position: an action plan **Evaluate** question (8 marks) followed by a multi-part research **design** block (formulate RQ + justify + list/outline sources/challenges + explain source). Together these questions are worth 20–24 marks in every paper — roughly one third of total marks.

**Understanding research methods thoroughly is one of the highest-leverage things to do for exam preparation.**

---

## Key concepts

### The research process
1. **Statement of inquiry** — the big idea/context (given in exam)
2. **Research question** — your specific, focused question (you formulate this)
3. **Data collection** — primary and secondary sources
4. **Analysis** — what the data shows
5. **Conclusion** — answer to the research question

### Primary vs secondary data

| | Primary data | Secondary data |
|-|-------------|----------------|
| **Definition** | Collected directly by the researcher | Already collected by someone else |
| **Examples** | Interviews, surveys, field observations, photographs taken by you, soil samples, traffic counts | Government statistics, newspaper articles, academic papers, satellite images, maps, GIS data |
| **Strengths** | Up-to-date, specific to your RQ, you control quality | Large datasets available, historical comparison possible, often free |
| **Limitations** | Time-consuming, small sample sizes, interviewer bias | May be outdated, may not match your exact RQ, unknown methodology |

## Designing a good research question

A good MYP geography RQ must be:
- **Specific**: names a place, group, or time period
- **Focused**: answerable through investigation (not too broad)
- **Connected to the statement of inquiry**: links local/global, cause/effect, or challenge/opportunity
- **Investigable**: there are sources you can actually use to answer it

| Bad RQ | Why it fails | Better RQ |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| "How does globalization affect people?" | Too vague, no place, no group | "How has increased trade in Myanmar increased the income of factory workers?" |
| "What is food security?" | Not investigable — just a definition | "How can Tajikistan reduce food imports to increase food security by 2030?" |
| "Is climate change bad?" | Too broad, not geographic enough | "To what extent has increased flooding frequency affected rice farmers in Bangladesh?" |
| "How does tourism affect places?" | No named place; no specific impact type | "To what extent has ecotourism preserved biodiversity on Table Mountain in Cape Town?" |
| "How has migration to Germany changed its population?" | Not focused — no named migration movement | "How has the Syrian refugee migration to Germany changed its population structure?" |

**The focus rule**: A RQ without a named location/specific group = maximum 1 mark, even if it is clearly worded and connected to the SoI.

## Evaluating an action plan (8-mark question)

### Three-part structure (always the same)

| Sub-criterion | Max marks | What it tests |
|---------------|-----------|--------------|
| **Strengths** | 3 | Quality of reasoning about what the plan does well |
| **Limitations** | 3 | Quality of reasoning about what the plan fails to do |
| **Appraisal** | 2 | Overall judgement weighing strengths against limitations |
| **Total** | 8 | |

### Markband pattern (same for all three sub-criteria)

| Marks | Command-term equivalent | What the response does |
|-------|------------------------|----------------------|
| 0 | — | Nothing worthy |
| 1 | State | Names one strength/limitation/appraisal without elaboration |
| 2 | Outline | Names one AND adds brief elaboration (one clause or 2–3 short sentences) |
| 3 | Explain | Names one AND adds detail AND gives a reason WHY it is a strength/limitation |

**For appraisal only**: 1 = brief overall judgement (can be vague); 2 = weighs positive AND negative and references a specific aspect of the investigation

### Key rules
- ONE well-explained strength OR limitation beats three shallow ones (quality rewarded, not quantity)
- If a student outlines **3 or more** strengths/limitations, they can be awarded 3 marks even if each is only briefly elaborated
- The appraisal can appear **ANYWHERE** in the response — it does not need to be a concluding statement
- Suggesting alternative methods counts as addressing limitations
- Do NOT double-count a point as both a limitation AND an appraisal

### Common strong limitations to look for in any action plan
- **Sample size too small**: e.g. only 5 farmers, 12 families, 2 village residents, 40 nursing home residents from a population of millions → unrepresentative
- **Source bias**: e.g. only using sources from one country/perspective (N20: all secondary sources from Australia only → biased about Laos)
- **Missing stakeholders**: e.g. only interviewing residents, not businesses; only interviewing one government official from one country (N19)
- **Data gaps**: e.g. map shows "no data available" for some districts (M22: 4 districts missing from Aceh province choropleth)
- **Outdated data**: e.g. using 2016 statistics in a 2021 report
- **Time limitations**: e.g. only one data collection period missing seasonal variation (N23: traffic count over 3 weeks misses seasonal patterns)
- **Missing data types**: e.g. no water quality testing alongside quantity testing (M24)
- **Vague RQ**: e.g. RQ doesn't refer to specific countries or location (N19: "water cooperation between countries" is too broad)

## Justifying a research question (4-mark sub-question)

When asked to **justify** why your RQ will help achieve a goal:
- Don't just re-state the RQ
- Explain what the investigation will FIND OUT
- Connect what it finds out to the GOAL
- Show how it relates to the statement of inquiry

**Formula**: *"My RQ focuses on [specific aspect]. This will show [what data/findings]. This helps [stakeholder] understand [SoI concept] because [reason]."*

## Sources and how to explain their value

### Types of sources used in investigations
- Government statistics (population, income, crop production, demographic data)
- Newspaper / news articles (recent events, perspectives)
- Academic papers / reports (detailed analysis, historical data)
- Maps (spatial distribution)
- GIS maps (multiple data layers, spatial patterns over time)
- Photographs (visual evidence of change)
- Interviews (primary — lived experience)
- Surveys / questionnaires (primary — wider sample)
- Satellite imagery (change over time, deforestation monitoring)
- Census data (population structure, demographic trends)
- Topographic maps (elevation, relief, infrastructure location)

### Explaining value of a source (2–4 marks)
Structure: **What the source contains → How that specifically helps answer the RQ → Why that matters**

Example (M25 Q3d context):
*"Poverty level statistics for workers in Myanmar will help me answer my RQ because they show changes in income over time. By comparing income before and after trade increased, I can determine whether globalization has benefited factory workers. This directly answers the research question about whether increased trade improves lives."*

### Explaining limitation of a source (2–3 marks)
Structure: **What the source lacks → Why that absence is a problem for the specific RQ → Consequence**

Example:
*"Government poverty statistics may not capture informal workers who make up a large proportion of factory employees. This means my data will underrepresent the most vulnerable workers, leading to a conclusion that overstates the benefits of globalization."*

| 4a | 2 | Formulate | Formulate a clear and focused RQ connected to SoI: "The extraction of natural resources can have a great impact on human and natural environments." |
| 4b | 4 | Justify | Justify the relevance of your RQ to the SoI |

**Key N19 action plan evaluated**: Interview with Ethiopian Minister of Water and Energy; secondary research on water management (UNwater.org); discussing with stakeholders. **Limitation**: RQ ("to what extent can cooperation lead to fair water distribution?") too general — no specific countries named.

## Recurring patterns across all sessions

### The evaluate structure never changes
Every evaluate question (Q2 or Q3) uses the same 3+3+2 = 8 mark structure:
- Strengths (0–3) + Limitations (0–3) + Appraisal (0–2)
- Appraisal can appear ANYWHERE
- One well-developed point beats multiple shallow ones

### RQ formulation mark scheme is identical every year
- 1 mark = clear OR focused (but not both); or connected to SoI but lacking specificity
- 2 marks = clear AND focused AND connected to SoI
- Named location/group/time = what makes it "focused"

### Justify mark scheme progression (1–4)
- 1 = states a simple connection (one sentence)
- 2 = outlines — one sentence with two clauses
- 3 = describes — one argument in detail, no comprehensive reasons
- 4 = justifies — one argument in detail WITH reasoning AND link to SoI/goal

### Source value/limitation pattern
- 1 = states (bare fact)
- 2 = outlines (state + brief elaboration)
- 3–4 = explains (detail + reasoning + connection to specific user/RQ)

## Examiner tips (consolidated from all mark schemes)

1. **RQ needs two anchors**: Named place + specific group/time/strategy. One anchor = 1 mark only.
2. **One well-developed point beats three shallow ones** — consistently stated across every mark scheme.
3. **Appraisal anywhere**: The overall judgement in evaluate questions can appear anywhere in the response — examiners identify implicit weighing, not just concluding sentences.
4. **Specificity in source names**: "Statistics" = 0. "Government statistics on tourist numbers in Vietnam" = 1 mark. "Government statistics on tourist numbers in Vietnam showing trends from 2010–2023" = 2 marks.
5. **Stakeholder specificity**: "Government" or "Students" = 0. "Laos government officials" or "Students in a school in Laos" = 1 mark.
6. **Outline = state + one elaboration**: A single sentence with two clauses is typical for 2-mark outline questions. Don't over-write.
7. **Q4d in N24 (overcome challenge)**: New sub-type — must directly link solution to the specific challenge named in Q4c.
8. **"List" questions**: 1 mark per valid item. No explanation needed or rewarded. Save thinking time for Outline/Explain questions.
9. **Evaluate structure tip**: Write strengths first, then limitations, then appraisal. But remember the appraisal can go anywhere.
10. **Suggesting alternatives = limitations**: If you say "they should have interviewed businesses too," that counts as identifying a limitation.
