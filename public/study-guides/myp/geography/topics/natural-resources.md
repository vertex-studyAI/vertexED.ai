# Natural Resources

Covers: energy resources (renewable and non-renewable), water as a resource, resource scarcity, extraction impacts, and sustainable resource management.

## Key concepts

- **Natural resources**: Materials and substances occurring in nature that can be exploited for economic gain.
- **Renewable resources**: Resources that can replenish naturally on a human timescale — solar, wind, geothermal, hydropower.
- **Non-renewable resources**: Resources that cannot be replaced once used — coal, oil, natural gas, minerals.
- **Resource scarcity**: When the supply of a resource falls below demand, caused by: population growth, over-exploitation, environmental degradation, unequal distribution, or geopolitical factors.
- **Extraction**: The process of removing resources from the natural environment (mining, drilling, logging, fishing).
- **Sustainable extraction**: Removing resources in a way that allows natural systems to replenish — includes reclamation, reduced waste, filtered wastewater, selective extraction methods.
- **Biodiversity loss**: The extraction and processing of minerals is responsible for more than 80% of the world's biodiversity loss (UN report, cited in M22 Q9).
- **Hydroelectric power (HEP)**: Electricity generated by using flowing water to turn turbines — a renewable energy source but with significant environmental trade-offs (dam construction alters river flow, sediment transport, fish migration).
- **Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD)**: Africa's largest hydroelectric dam on the Nile — generates electricity for Ethiopia while raising concerns about downstream water supply for Sudan and Egypt.
- **Fossil fuels**: Coal, oil, and natural gas — the primary sources of global energy and the main driver of GHG emissions. Still widely used despite renewable alternatives.
- **Water footprint**: The total volume of freshwater consumed in producing goods and services.
- **Groundwater / aquifer**: Underground water reserves extracted by wells — can be depleted if extraction exceeds recharge rate (water deficit).
- **Desalinization**: Converting salt water to fresh water through filtration — energy-intensive but can supplement freshwater supply in water-scarce regions (Jordan, Middle East).

## Case studies

### Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) (N19 Q3)
- Africa's most important source of hydroelectric power when complete
- Intended to solve Ethiopia's energy crisis
- Location: Blue Nile River, Ethiopia
- **Egypt's concern**: Receives little annual precipitation; depends on the Nile for **90% of its water supply**
- **Sudan**: Also increasing Nile water use
- Ethiopia, Sudan, and Egypt agreed to cooperate in using their shared natural resource (the Nile River)
- Statement of inquiry (N19): "Natural resources can be shared more fairly through cooperation"
- Key tension: Ethiopia's development need (electricity) vs Egypt and Sudan's water security need

### Renewable vs non-renewable energy classification (N19 Q1)
- **Non-renewable**: Coal, Oil
- **Renewable**: Geothermal, Solar, Wind
- Common exam format: drag-and-drop classification from a given list

### Resource scarcity — model explanation (N19 Q2)
The mark scheme builds a 4-mark answer on population growth:
1. Population growth → greater demand for food (state)
2. → pressure on land in Sub-Saharan Africa (example + outline)
3. → deforestation and overgrazing → desertification (describe)
4. → **reducing the productivity of the land** (explain — the reasoning why this creates scarcity)
Key rule: Without a named example, maximum = 3 marks. With a named location (Sub-Saharan Africa, specific resource), full 4 marks available.

### Cadiz Mine, Australia — sustainable gold extraction (N24 Q5 example)
- Mark scheme example location: Cadiz Mine, Australia; resource = Gold
- Sustainable method 1: Wastewater filtration and purification to remove impurities before returning water to the environment
- Students can use any valid location and resource — the mark scheme accommodates different contexts
- Speech must explain TWO ways of sustainable extraction, with reasoning for why each is sustainable

### Caspian Sea — natural gas extraction (N19 Q4 example)
- Mark scheme example for research question formulation
- Natural gas extraction in Caspian Sea → pollution → threatens caviar production (sturgeon)
- Demonstrates how extraction impacts both marine ecosystems and local economies
- Used to illustrate a "focused" research question: specifies resource (gas), location (Caspian Sea), and specific impact (pollution threatening caviar)

### Minerals and biodiversity loss (M22 Q9 context)
- UN report: extraction and processing of minerals responsible for **more than 80% of world's biodiversity loss**
- Sustainable extraction methods (e.g. controlled mining, reforestation, wildlife corridors) can directly reduce this impact
- However, economic pressures in developing nations mean unsustainable extraction continues even with regulation
- Other drivers of biodiversity loss: urbanisation, agriculture, climate change, pollution

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **Named example for scarcity question** (N19 Q2): Without a named place or specific resource = maximum 3 marks. Adding "Sub-Saharan Africa" or "the Sahel" earns the 4th mark. The principle: locate your argument geographically.
- **Mechanism for scarcity**: Don't just say what causes scarcity — explain the causal chain. Population growth → land pressure → overgrazing → desertification → **reduced land productivity** (this reasoning is what earns 4 marks).
- **Both perspectives on GERD** (N19 Q3): A good evaluate response considers: Ethiopia's right to electricity/development; Egypt and Sudan's water security concerns; the value of multilateral cooperation (UN Water); the risk of biased interview data (only one government minister interviewed).
- **Sustainable extraction TWO ways** (N24 Q5): Criterion A caps at lower marks if only one sustainable method is explained. Must explain two clearly.
- **Speech format** (N24 Q5): Community audience requires greeting, body, conclusion/closing argument. Persuasive language (first person, emotive vocabulary) assessed separately under C2.

### What examiners penalise
- **Single-source bias in action plans** (N19 Q3): Interviewing only the Ethiopian Minister means only one stakeholder perspective. Limitation = bias toward Ethiopia; doesn't capture Egypt's/Sudan's viewpoint.
- **Vague data collection methods**: "List" questions for methods are 1 mark each — no explanation needed. But "Outline" questions for usefulness require elaboration (state + one specific reason).
- **Generic RQs**: A research question without a named location earns 1 mark for formulate. Must specify place AND impact/focus for 2 marks.

## Common question types

1. **Classification** (2 marks, N19 Q1): Drag-and-drop renewable vs non-renewable. Know: Renewable = solar, wind, geothermal, hydro. Non-renewable = coal, oil, gas, minerals.
2. **Explain one scarcity factor** (4 marks, N19 Q2): Develop one factor with full causal chain. Named example required for 4th mark.
3. **Evaluate action plan** (8 marks, N19 Q3): Standard strengths/limitations/appraisal structure for GERD water management investigation.
4. **Research design** (12 marks, N19 Q4): RQ + justify + list methods + outline usefulness + outline challenge.
5. **Sustainability essay** (24 marks, M22 Q9): "To what extent is sustainable extraction essential for reducing biodiversity loss?" Requires two perspectives + synthesis.
6. **Persuasive speech on sustainable extraction** (18 marks, N24 Q5): TWO methods explained with geographic specificity. Community audience requires persuasive language.

## Key arguments (for essay-type questions)

### M22 Q9: "To what extent is sustainable extraction of resources essential for reducing biodiversity loss?"

**YES — sustainable extraction IS essential:**
- Minerals/resource extraction responsible for 80%+ of biodiversity loss (UN report)
- Sustainable methods (controlled mining, reforestation, wildlife corridors, selective logging) directly reduce habitat destruction
- Renewable energy reduces demand for fossil fuel extraction — reduces land disturbance
- Without sustainable extraction, the root cause of biodiversity loss continues unchecked
- Examples: sustainable forestry certifications (FSC), marine protected areas around fishing zones, bioremediation of mining sites

**COUNTERARGUMENT — sustainable extraction alone is insufficient:**
- Other major drivers of biodiversity loss: urbanisation, intensive agriculture, climate change, pollution (not only extraction)
- Economic pressures in developing nations mean unsustainable practices continue even when regulations exist
- Corrupt or weak governance in resource-rich developing countries undermines extraction standards
- "Sustainable extraction" often means less damage, not zero damage — some biodiversity loss continues
- Suggests: sustainable extraction is necessary but not sufficient; must be combined with habitat protection, climate action, and governance reform

**Conclusion:** Sustainable extraction is essential — it directly addresses the largest single driver of biodiversity loss (>80%). However, it must be combined with regulation, enforcement, and addressing other drivers (agriculture, urbanisation, climate). The extent to which it reduces biodiversity loss depends on whether sustainable standards are genuinely enforced globally.

### "Should natural resources be shared more fairly through cooperation?"

**YES (cooperation works):**
- GERD: Ethiopia, Egypt, and Sudan agreed to cooperate — shows international willingness to share a resource
- UN Water organization provides policy frameworks and monitoring for shared water resources
- Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam negotiations reduced tension between countries
- Shared resources like the Nile create mutual dependency — cooperation is in all parties' interests

**NO / complications:**
- Countries have conflicting development needs (Ethiopia needs electricity; Egypt needs water)
- Power imbalances make "fair" cooperation difficult — larger/wealthier countries have more leverage
- Voluntary agreements can break down without enforcement mechanisms
- A single stakeholder interview (one government minister) produces biased data — real cooperation requires all voices

## Revision priorities

1. **Renewable vs non-renewable classification**: Coal, Oil = non-renewable. Solar, Wind, Geothermal = renewable. This appears as a classification question in N19 — know all five examples.
2. **GERD case study**: Ethiopia depends on the Nile for electricity; Egypt depends on Nile for 90% of water. Three-country cooperation (Ethiopia, Sudan, Egypt). Africa's largest HEP project.
3. **Scarcity causes**: Know at least two causes with mechanisms — population growth (most common in mark schemes) and over-exploitation. Named example required for full marks.
4. **Sustainable extraction TWO methods**: For N24 Q5-style questions, know specific sustainable methods (wastewater treatment, selective extraction, reforestation, wildlife corridors) with explanations of why each is sustainable.
5. **Essay**: UN statistic (80% of biodiversity loss from extraction) is a powerful opening. Counter with: extraction is the biggest but not only driver; enforcement matters.
6. **Research question rule**: Specifying location + resource type + specific impact = 2 marks. Vague = 1 mark.
