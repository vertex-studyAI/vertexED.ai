# Natural Hazards

## Definition
A **natural hazard** is a naturally occurring physical event that has the potential to cause loss of life or property. When it affects a community, it becomes a **natural disaster**.

**Key distinction**: A hazard only becomes a disaster when it interacts with a vulnerable population. The same magnitude earthquake can kill thousands in one country and dozens in another — the difference is vulnerability, not the hazard itself.

---

## Types of natural hazards

| Type | Examples | Key geographic factors |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| **Tectonic** | Earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis | Plate boundaries; Ring of Fire |
| **Hydrological** | Flooding, drought | River systems, rainfall patterns, land use |
| **Meteorological** | Hurricanes/cyclones, tornadoes, blizzards | Ocean temperatures, atmospheric conditions |
| **Climatological** | Wildfires, heatwaves | Temperature, drought, wind, vegetation type |
| **Biological** | Disease outbreaks, locust swarms | Vectors, climate, population density |

---

## Tectonic hazards — plate boundaries

| Boundary type | Also called | Movement | Hazard | Example |
|---------------|-------------|----------|--------|---------|
| **Conservative / Transform** | Transform | Plates slide past each other | Earthquakes | California (Pacific + North American plates) |
| **Destructive / Convergent** | Subduction zone | One plate sinks under another | Earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis | Philippines, Japan, Indonesia |
| **Constructive / Divergent** | Spreading ridge | Plates move apart | Volcanic activity | Iceland (North American + Eurasian plates) |

## Factors affecting severity of impact

### Physical factors
- Magnitude/intensity of the event
- Duration (prolonged drought vs sudden earthquake)
- Proximity of settlements to hazard zone
- Time of day (nighttime earthquake → more casualties asleep)

### Human factors (most important for MYP exams)
- **Income / development level**: HICs have better infrastructure (earthquake-resistant buildings), early warning systems, trained emergency services, insurance, faster recovery
- **Governance**: well-governed countries enforce building codes, maintain disaster preparedness plans
- **Population density**: more people in hazard zone = higher potential casualties
- **Education and awareness**: communities that know what to do (evacuation routes, drills) survive better
- **Age and vulnerability**: elderly, children, disabled people face higher risk during evacuation

---

## Income and natural disasters (key exam argument)

### How income INCREASES severity for poorer communities
- **Infrastructure**: buildings in LICs often lack earthquake-resistant design; collapse kills thousands
- **Warning systems**: HICs have sophisticated monitoring networks; LICs rely on international alerts (slower, less localised)
- **Emergency services**: LICs have fewer trained personnel, less equipment
- **Recovery**: HICs rebuild quickly (insurance, government funds); LICs take years or decades
- **Food security**: disasters destroy harvests in LICs where agriculture is primary income → cascading food crisis

### Counterarguments (income is NOT the only factor)
- Geography: coastal vs inland, plate boundary proximity
- Governance: some MICs have poor governance despite wealth (e.g. Venezuela)
- Type of hazard: some hazards are unpredictable regardless of income
- Community resilience: some poor communities have strong traditional knowledge of hazard response
- Inequality within HICs: Hurricane Katrina (2005), USA — wealthy HIC, but poor urban communities in New Orleans worst affected; over 25,000 displaced to Superdome

## Technology and natural hazards (key argument — N19)

### YES, technology reduces impacts in HICs
- **Early warning systems**: Japan seismic text alerts — people move to safety before shaking arrives
- **Earthquake-resistant design**: shock absorbers, cross-bracing, base isolation in buildings
- **Search and rescue robots/drones**: deliver aid to inaccessible areas
- **Satellite monitoring**: Wildfire Alert app (Portugal) — maps fire locations in real time for residents
- **Prediction systems**: seismographs, tiltmeters, gas sensors for volcanic eruptions

### LIMITATIONS of technology even in HICs
- **Hurricane Katrina (New Orleans, 2005)**: USA has advanced warning systems but poor urban communities could not respond → socio-economic inequality within HICs limits effectiveness
- **California**: even with earthquake warnings, people told to "duck, cover and hold" → if unprepared, casualties still occur
- **Education may matter more than technology**: knowing what to do when warnings sound is essential
- **Access**: elderly residents, those without smartphones, or low-income communities may not access tech-based warnings

**Conclusion for N19 essay**: Technology reduces impacts significantly in HICs, but is most effective when combined with education, governance, and community preparedness. Not all residents of HICs benefit equally.

## GIS (Geographic Information Systems) and hazard management

GIS allows multiple data **layers** to be overlaid on a map:
- Population density
- Tectonic plate boundaries
- Volcanic eruption history / earthquake magnitude
- Infrastructure (roads, hospitals, ports)
- Elevation/topography

### Value of GIS for tectonic hazard management (M23 Q5 — Tonga)
- Creates layers to identify vulnerable areas based on topography and land use
- Allows evacuation routes to be planned before disasters
- Measuring tool enables accurate distance calculations from volcanoes to critical infrastructure
- Can identify areas at highest risk for targeted investment

### Limitation of GIS for tectonic hazard management
- Expensive technology — particularly limiting for LIC governments after a disaster
- After Tonga eruption: US$43.7 million damage, 85,000 people affected — government needs recovery funds, not new GIS systems
- Requires trained staff to operate and interpret data
- GIS maps are only as good as the data put in (if data is outdated, analysis may be wrong)

## Flood management: Rotterdam Water Plaza (M23 Q3)

**Location**: Benthemplein, Rotterdam, Netherlands (city experiences heavy rainfall + annual flooding)

**Solution**: "Water Plaza" — 3 sunken areas acting as surface water reservoirs + recreational space
- Can hold 8,500 bathtubs / 1,730,000 litres of water from surrounding streets
- Water released into storm drainage system after rainfall subsides

**Investigation strengths**:
- Collects both primary (interviews with residents, soil samples, rainfall data) and secondary data
- Multiple data types: quantitative (rainfall, soil permeability) + qualitative (interviews)
- Water plaza addresses both functional need (flood management) AND community use (recreational space)

**Investigation limitations**:
- Not all stakeholders consulted — businesses in area were not interviewed
- Without business input, plan may face opposition delaying implementation

## Case studies from past papers

### 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami — Banda Aceh, Indonesia (M22 Q1)
- **Cause**: Indo-Australian and Eurasian plates collided under Indian Ocean; fault ruptured 30km below surface
- **Date**: 26 December 2004
- **Impact on Banda Aceh**: tsunami wave hit at 30 metres high; force equivalent to 60 hurricanes; 100,000 houses destroyed; one third of critical infrastructure destroyed along the coast; mass displacement
- **Aerial imagery**: before/after comparison showed total coastal destruction
- **Key exam use**: state two impacts; explain how income disparities worsen impacts

### Philippines — GIS mapping of tectonic activity (M21 Q6/Q7)
- **Source**: GIS-Education interactive maps with layered data
- **Layers**: tectonic plate boundaries; volcanic eruption history (1991–2019); earthquake magnitude; population density
- **Key volcanoes**: Pinatubo (20+ eruptions), Canlaon (11–19 eruptions)
- **Earthquake data**: red dots = >6.5 magnitude earthquakes; blue = 5–6.4 magnitude
- **Location X**: northern Philippines — lower population density, more volcanic activity
- **Location Y**: central Philippines — higher population density (>400/km² near Manila/Cebu), fewer volcanoes
- **Exam Q7**: Justify investing in earthquake-proof structures at X or Y — need data from source
- **Key insight**: population density data (e.g. >400/km² near epicentres at X) justifies investment there

### Wildfire Alert app — Portugal (N19 Q8)
- **Hazard type**: Climatological (wildfire)
- **App features**: satellite images, maps of fire locations, user-generated photos, "last updated: 3 hours ago"
- **Value for local residents**: shows fire locations so residents can decide whether to evacuate; easy to read (maps, satellite images, photos)
- **Limitations for local residents**:
  - Not updated in real time (3-hour gap)
  - Does NOT show wind direction → residents cannot know where fire will spread
  - Does NOT show fire movement direction
  - Requires internet/phone access — excludes less tech-literate or older residents

### Tonga Volcanic Eruption / Tsunami (M23 Q5)
- **Event**: Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai volcano, December 2021 – January 2022; 1.2m tsunami hit capital Nuku'alofa
- **Location**: 170 islands in Pacific Ocean; 36 inhabited
- **Impacts**: 600 structures damaged; transport, power, water, communications infrastructure affected; volcanic ash harmed agriculture and fishing; ~85,000 people affected; US$43.7 million damage
- **Management challenge**: delivering aid to affected islands after communications destroyed
- **GIS value**: mapping critical infrastructure (hospitals, police stations, ports) and hazard zones helps plan aid delivery and evacuation

### California vs Iceland — tectonic activity (N20 Q5)
| | California | Iceland |
|-|-----------| --------|
| **Plate boundary** | Conservative (Transform) — Pacific + North American plates | Divergent (Constructive) — North American + Eurasian plates |
| **Main hazard** | Earthquakes | Volcanic activity |
| **GDP per capita** | US$60,359 | US$70,056 |
| **Population density** | 408/km² | 3.3/km² |
| **Best management strategy** | Earthquake-proof buildings (high GDP makes this viable; high density makes evacuation impractical) | Lava diversion channels, evacuation (low density makes evacuation feasible) |

### Hawaii volcanic eruption (M25 Q5)
- **Hazard type**: Volcanic / tectonic
- **Location**: Big Island, Hawaii (USA — HIC)
- **Event**: 2018 eruption; lava flows destroyed hundreds of homes
- **NGO response**: "Going Harek" used color-coded lava map showing damaged vs undamaged buildings
- **Map value**: shows spatial distribution of damage; allows NGO to prioritise aid delivery; identifies safe zones vs danger zones
- **Map limitations**: static snapshot; no street names; doesn't show population distribution; doesn't show if residents already evacuated

### Lahaina Wildfire, Hawaii (M25 Q6)
- **Hazard type**: Climatological (wildfire)
- **Location**: Lahaina, Maui, Hawaii
- **Event**: 2023 — deadliest US wildfire in over a century; driven by Hurricane Dora winds
- **Analysed from perspective of an 80-year-old resident**
- Strategies involving communication/alerts limited for elderly (may lack smartphones, hearing impairment)
- Evacuation drills limited for elderly (mobility difficulties; real wildfire moves faster than drills prepare for)

### Haiti vs Chile (2010 earthquakes — comparison)
- Both experienced major earthquakes within weeks
- Haiti (LIC): ~316,000 deaths, widespread building collapse, cholera outbreak
- Chile (MIC/HIC): ~525 deaths, buildings held, rapid emergency response
- Same hazard, vastly different outcomes → income and governance were key

## Mark scheme patterns

### For "explain value/limitation of a source" (2–4 marks)
- **Value**: State what the source shows → explain HOW that helps the specific user achieve their specific goal
- **Limitation**: Name what the source LACKS → explain WHY that absence is a problem for THIS specific user
- Always tie to the named perspective: "For a resident in the Philippines..." or "For the NGO delivering aid..."

### For analyse questions (8 marks — M25 Q6)
- Must cover BOTH strategies (or both sides)
- Must maintain the specified perspective throughout (e.g. elderly resident)
- Must reach an analytical conclusion — not just description
- Conclusion must weigh effectiveness, not just summarise

### For technology/HIC essay (18 marks — N19 Q9) — Criterion A + D
- **Criterion A**: geographic knowledge (correct HIC examples, technologies, mechanisms)
- **Criterion D**: critical thinking — must acknowledge WHERE technology FAILS (Hurricane Katrina), not just list positives
- Upper band (7–8 on Criterion A, 9–10 on Criterion D) requires synthesis — examples unpacked to support argument; clear conclusion on "to what extent"

### For "to what extent" essays (24 marks)
- Must argue a position AND acknowledge counter-arguments
- Must use at least one named disaster example
- Appropriate geographical terminology throughout
- Clear, justified conclusion required
- **M21 Q8 (secondary vs primary effects)**: 7–8 marks on Criterion D requires "considerably balanced" perspectives — one side with weak treatment of other = max 5–6

### For the tectonic management email (N20 Q5b — 16 marks total)
- Criterion A: correct cause chain for boundary type (Conservative → plates stick → pressure → slip → earthquake OR Divergent → magma rises → eruption) + named management strategy with justification
- Criterion C: convincing email register (subject line, greeting, sign-off, persuasive language)
- Subject line counts toward C1 Format marks

## Q7-style essay framework: "To what extent does income influence disaster severity?"

### Essay structure
1. **Introduction**: Define key terms; state your position (income is a major but not sole factor)
2. **Point 1 — YES income influences severity**: infrastructure quality, warning systems, recovery speed
3. **Evidence**: Haiti vs Chile comparison; or Bangladesh flooding vs Netherlands flooding
4. **Point 2 — OTHER factors also matter**: governance, geography, hazard type, population density
5. **Counterpoint evidence**: Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans — wealthy USA, but poor urban communities worst affected
6. **Conclusion**: Income strongly influences severity, particularly RECOVERY time, but vulnerability is multifactorial

### Key vocabulary for this essay
- **Vulnerability**: degree to which a community is susceptible to harm
- **Resilience**: ability to recover from a disaster
- **Development gap**: income inequality between HICs and LICs
- **Multiplier effect**: disaster worsens poverty → longer recovery → next disaster hits harder
- **Building codes**: regulations requiring earthquake/fire-resistant construction
- **Tectonic plates**: moving sections of Earth's crust; boundaries = earthquake/volcano zones
