# Globalization

## Definition
The process by which the world is becoming increasingly interconnected as a result of trade, culture, and communication. Local and global processes become interdependent, creating both challenges and opportunities.

**MYP Statement of Inquiry** (M25): *"Local and global processes are interconnected as a result of globalization, creating challenges and opportunities."*

---

## Key concepts to know

### Positive impacts
- Increased tourism
- Access to internet and technology
- Cheaper goods (due to global supply chains)
- Cultural exchange (appreciating other cultures, learning languages)
- More employment opportunities (e.g. factory jobs via multinational companies)
- Increased trade and economic growth for developing countries
- Aid and development funding flows (e.g. Australia to Laos — see N20)

### Negative impacts
- **Environmental**: increased carbon emissions (shipping, aviation), deforestation for cash crops, ocean pollution from cargo transport; fast fashion generating millions of tonnes of textile waste annually
- **Social**: exploitation of low-wage workers in factories (poor conditions, no unions); cultural homogenization (local traditions lost); dumping of poor-quality goods on developing-country markets
- **Economic**: dependency on imports (food insecurity if supply disrupted); tax avoidance by multinationals; race to the bottom on wages
- **Inequality**: benefits flow disproportionately to wealthy nations and corporations

### Trade and exchange
- Countries specialize in goods they can produce cheaply → exports
- Global supply chains: one product may be manufactured across 5+ countries
- Free trade agreements reduce tariffs → cheaper imports → can harm local industries
- **Fast fashion** supply chain: clothes produced cheaply in South/Southeast Asia → shipped to HICs → worn few times → discarded (or shipped back to LICs as "second-hand")

### Fast fashion specifically
- Clothing **sales** rose sharply 2000–2015; clothing **use** (times worn per item) fell over same period (Source B, N22 — Ellen MacArthur Foundation data)
- 15 million items of used clothing shipped to Kantamanto Market, Accra (Ghana) every week
- 40% of these items are too poor quality to sell → end up as landfill at Korle Lagoon, Accra
- 9,090 tailors/creators work at Kantamanto daily (positive: employment created)
- ~4 million tonnes of unwanted textiles shipped globally per year
- Environmental cost: ~81% of all textiles end up as landfill; worth US$9.6 billion wasted

### Sustainability of trade
- **Social sustainability**: trade creates jobs (e.g. Kantamanto tailors); raises wages; garment industry employment in Bangladesh/Cambodia
- **Environmental unsustainability**: fast fashion carbon footprint; textile waste; shipping emissions; secondary markets in developing nations receive dumped goods
- SDG connection: Fairtrade and ethical sourcing models attempt to ensure women workers paid equally and communities benefit (see N23 Q5 on SDGs and sustainable trade)

## Mark scheme insights

### For "Identify positive impacts" (2 marks)
- 1 mark per impact, need two
- Must name the impact clearly (not just single words like "tourism")
- Accepted: increased tourism, internet access, cultural appreciation, language learning, cheaper goods

### For "Explain a negative consequence" (4 marks)
- Structure: **Impact → Negative consequence → WHY it is negative (cause/mechanism)**
- Example 4-mark response: *"Increased tourism causes more pollution. Many modes of transport rely on fossil fuels. Because tourism has become cheaper globally, tourism's overall carbon footprint has increased significantly."*
- One consequence, well-developed = full marks
- Don't list multiple consequences — depth beats breadth

### For formulating a research question (2 marks)
A good RQ must be:
1. Clear and specific (not vague)
2. Focused on a particular place or group
3. Connected to the statement of inquiry

Bad RQ: "How does globalization affect people?" (too vague, no place/group)
Good RQ: "How has increased trade in Myanmar increased the lives of factory workers?" (place + group + connected to global interconnection)

### For the N22 essay "To what extent is trade environmentally and socially sustainable?" (24 marks)
- MUST address **both** social AND environmental sustainability to reach 5–6 or 7–8 on Criterion A
- MUST have **two balanced perspectives** for Criterion D 5–6 and 7–8 (not just one-sided)
- Fast fashion case study (Kantamanto, Accra) is the key evidence source
- Structure: social sustainability YES → environmental sustainability NO → synthesis + conclusion

### For the N23 SDGs report (18 marks)
- Pick ONE SDG and focus on it throughout (not all three)
- Must engage with the WTO statistics given in Source A
- Format marks (C1) require a title + subheadings as separate paragraphs
- High marks require specific geographic examples and named countries, not just general claims

### For justify questions (4 marks)
- State your position first
- Then give ONE well-developed reason (not three shallow ones)
- Show the link between your RQ and the stated goal
- Model: *"RQ focuses on X. This will show Y because Z. This helps [stakeholder] understand [SoI concept]."*

## Case studies / examples worth knowing

### Fast fashion: Accra, Ghana (N22)
- **Kantamanto Market**: world's largest second-hand clothing market; receives 15 million items/week from HICs
- **Korle Lagoon, Accra**: landfill site where 40% of incoming clothing ends up (too low quality to sell)
- Positive: 9,090 tailors employed at Kantamanto
- Negative: textile waste, water/soil pollution at Korle Lagoon
- Government official perspective: needs reliable statistics to make policy arguments

### Aid and globalization: Australia–Laos (N20)
- Australia's 2019 aid programme focused on primary education in Laos
- 19,795 children enrolled; 850 classrooms improved; 390 teachers trained
- Laos HDI: 0.601; Australia HDI: 0.939 — aid bridges development gap
- Statement of inquiry: "Globalization can create change for communities and individuals"

### Sustainable trade and SDGs (N23)
- SDG 5 (Gender Equality): 70% of global cotton production workforce = women; more women than men live on <$1.90/day
- SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption): 1.3 billion tonnes of food wasted annually while 2 billion are hungry
- SDG 1 (No Poverty): Fairtrade promotes fairer wages for workers in supply chains
- Example: Ethiopian coffee and Fairtrade certification — economic empowerment of women workers

### Other supply chain examples
- **Myanmar / Bangladesh garment industry**: globalization created factory jobs but conditions often exploitative; low wages, long hours
- **China manufacturing**: "world's factory" — globalization moved manufacturing jobs from HICs to LICs
- **Tajikistan food imports**: globalization made food cheaper but created dependency (see [[food-security]])
