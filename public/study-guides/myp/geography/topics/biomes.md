# Biomes

## Definition
A **biome** is a large naturally occurring community of flora (plants) and fauna (animals) occupying a major habitat. Biomes are defined by climate, vegetation, and the organisms that live there.

---

## Key biomes to know for MYP

| Biome | Climate | Key features | Human threats | Innovations |
|-------|---------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Tropical rainforest** | Hot, wet year-round | High biodiversity, dense canopy, fast nutrient cycling | Deforestation (farming, logging), mining, oil/gas extraction | Satellite monitoring (PRODES in Brazil), reforestation drones, REDD+ carbon credits, selective logging |
| **Savanna** | Hot, seasonal rainfall | Grasslands with scattered trees, fire-adapted | Overgrazing, bush burning, poaching, land-use change | Wildlife conservancies, GPS-tracking of animals |
| **Deserts** | Hot/cold, very dry | Extreme temperatures, sparse vegetation | Oil/gas extraction, desertification from climate change, pollution | Solar farms (low ecological footprint), drip irrigation |
| **Temperate forests** | Moderate, four seasons | Deciduous trees, high soil fertility | Logging, urban expansion, acid rain | Selective logging, reforestation, National Parks |
| **Tundra / Arctic** | Very cold, permafrost | Low shrubs, mosses, lichens | Oil pipelines, permafrost melting (climate change), drilling | Environmental impact assessments, protected areas |
| **Ocean/Marine / Aquatic** | Varies | Coral reefs, open ocean ecosystems | Overfishing, plastic pollution, coral bleaching, coastal development | Marine protected areas (MPAs), coral reef seeding, sonar mapping |
| **Coral reefs** | Warm shallow water | High biodiversity | Warming temperatures (bleaching), coastal development, overfishing | Coral farming, heat-resistant coral species development |
| **Grasslands** | Temperate/tropical | Open land, grasses, periodic fire | Overgrazing, conversion to farmland, land-use change | Conservation grazing management, rewilding |

## Human threats to biomes — key categories (N23 Q6)

The exam gives students six threat categories to choose from. Know all six:

1. **Over-exploitation** — unsustainable fishing, hunting, resource extraction beyond natural replenishment
2. **Pollution** — industrial smokestacks (air pollution affecting ecosystems), littering, chemicals
3. **Deforestation** — logging machinery clearing forest for timber or farmland
4. **Land-use change** — cities expanding into biomes; large-scale industrial farming replacing natural ecosystems

For **justify** questions: pick the ONE threat most relevant to your chosen biome, explain the mechanism, and state a clear conclusion ("this is the greatest threat because...").

## Scientific and technical innovations: harm vs protection

### Innovations that HARM biomes
- **Industrial agriculture**: pesticides kill pollinators; monocultures reduce biodiversity; fertiliser runoff creates algal blooms in rivers/coastal ecosystems
- **Mining and drilling**: open-cast mining clears forest; oil spills destroy marine and coastal biomes; noise pollution affects wildlife behaviour
- **Genetically modified organisms (GMOs)**: risk of cross-pollination with wild species; may reduce genetic diversity in wild plant populations
- **Dams and irrigation**: alter river flow; destroy wetland habitats; fragment fish migration routes
- **Climate change technology side-effects**: rare earth metal mining for solar panels/batteries can damage ecosystems

### Innovations that PROTECT biomes
- **Satellite and remote sensing**: real-time monitoring of deforestation (e.g. Brazil's PRODES system detects illegal logging within days); can track animal populations
- **DNA barcoding**: rapid identification of species, helps enforce wildlife trafficking laws
- **Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)**: tech-enabled enforcement (drones, GPS), fish population recovery documented
- **Rewilding**: reintroduction of apex predators (wolves in Yellowstone → trophic cascade → river course changes)
- **Precision agriculture**: reduces chemical use, protects soil health and surrounding ecosystems
- **Carbon capture technology**: direct air capture + reforestation programs offset emissions harming biomes
- **Coral reef restoration**: coral farming + heat-resistant coral varieties being trialled in Great Barrier Reef
- **GIS and mapping**: identify deforestation patterns, plan conservation zones, monitor change over time (N23 Q4c notes GIS maps showing tourist/land-use numbers over 10 years)
- **Selective logging**: removes only certain trees, preserves canopy structure and biodiversity (N22 model RQ example: Sabah rainforests, Borneo)

## How protecting biomes reduces climate change (N22 Q4)

Key mechanism for the **forest biome** (especially Amazon):
- The **Amazon is a carbon sink** — it absorbs more CO₂ than it releases
- Reducing deforestation maintains carbon stores, keeping CO₂ out of the atmosphere
- Preventing increase in atmospheric CO₂ allows longwave radiation to escape → reduces further global warming
- Carbon sink argument works for **all forested biomes** (temperate, boreal, tropical)

For **marine biomes**: kelp forests and mangroves also act as carbon sinks; coral reefs support fisheries that feed billions

## Case studies from past papers

### Amazon Rainforest, Brazil (M21 Q3/Q4)
- **Context**: MYP class investigating how deforestation has changed lives of indigenous people in the Amazon
- **Research question evaluated**: "To what extent do changes in the natural environment impact human identities?"
- **Key threats**: deforestation making way for farmland; access to water decreased for many tribes; soil degradation impacting food production; destruction of culturally significant sacred areas
- **GIS maps used**: showed indigenous communities (white circles), deforestation (orange), oil and gas development (gold)
- **Investigation limitation**: language barrier between students and indigenous communities — families may not speak Portuguese/English, making interviews unreliable
- **Sample size issue**: only 12 families from 4 villages → limited data but covers different village experiences

### Coral Triangle (N23 Q7)
- **Location**: 6 million km² area covering Philippines, Malaysia, Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, Timor-Leste
- **Biodiversity status**: the world's centre of marine biodiversity
- **Human threats**: 85% of Coral Triangle reefs are threatened (vs global average of 60%); 79% decline in reef fish reproduction
- **Fishing**: global demand for tuna increasing; yellowfin tuna = overfished; bigeye tuna = fully exploited; skipjack = not overfished
- **Human dependence**: 120 million people sustained by the Coral Triangle; 2.25 million are fishers who depend on healthy seas
- **Source**: infographic from www.savingcoralreefs.org for World Reef Day, 2020
- **Source limitation**: infographic does not cite all sources → marine biologist cannot evaluate credibility of data

### Borneo/Sabah Rainforest (N22 Q3 example)
- **Research question (model)**: "Can we use selective logging technology to reduce deforestation in The Sabah rainforests?"
- **Threat**: unsustainable logging — large areas cleared → soil erosion → land degradation → biodiversity loss
- **Management**: selective logging preserves canopy, maintains soil structure, limits biodiversity loss
- **Challenge**: collecting primary data from a large rainforest area is physically difficult

### Climate change and biomes (N22 Q4)
- Exam asks to explain how protecting ONE biome could reduce ONE cause of climate change
- Model answer uses **Amazon as carbon sink** (see mechanism above)
- Other valid answers: protecting ocean biomes prevents loss of phytoplankton (oxygen/carbon cycle); protecting tundra prevents permafrost thaw and methane release

## Mark scheme insights

### For the M25 podcast script (18 marks)
This is a LANGUAGE + CONTENT question (Criterion A). Examiners assess both:
1. **Geographical knowledge**: correct explanation of how innovations harm/protect biomes; accurate reference to 2 specific biomes; appropriate terminology
2. **Communication style**: podcast language — engaging, appropriate for a general student audience, uses rhetorical questions, analogies, concise sentences

**What a high-scoring response looks like**:
- **Title**: specific and engaging, e.g. "Tech vs Nature: Are We Saving or Destroying Our Biomes?"
- **Opening**: hook the audience — rhetorical question or surprising statistic
- **Body**: explain 2+ innovations clearly; reference named biomes with specific detail; balance harm AND protection (or take a clear position)
- **Closing statement**: return to the big question; leave audience with something to think about
- **Language features**: rhetorical questions ("But at what cost?"), analogies, varied sentence length, direct address ("you might be wondering...")

**Common mistakes**:
- Writing like an essay instead of a podcast script — no audience engagement
- Naming biomes without explaining HOW the innovation affects them
- Only covering harm OR protection without acknowledging the other side
- Using overly technical language without explaining it to a general audience

### For "Evaluate the action plan" questions (8 marks — M21 Q3, N22 Q2)
Three-part structure: Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2) = 8 marks. Key notes:
- ONE well-explained strength beats three listed strengths
- Appraisal can appear ANYWHERE in the response — not just as a conclusion
- Language barrier (for Amazon study) and sample size (only 12 families; only 2 villages in N22) are strong limitations

### For "Justify greatest threat" (4 marks — N23 Q6)
- Must name the activity AND the biome AND give reasons AND a clear conclusion
- The conclusion does NOT need to appear at the end
- Examples helpful (Amazon/Coral Triangle) but not mandatory

### For Coral Triangle source analysis (3 marks — N23 Q7b, Q7c)
- VALUE: what specific data does the source contain → how does THAT help the specific user (marine biologist) with THEIR specific task?
- LIMITATION: what does the source lack → why does that absence matter specifically for the marine biologist's research?
- Key limitation: infographic doesn't cite all sources → biologist cannot verify credibility

### For the biomes essay (24 marks — N23 Q8)
**Sustainable management vs economic development** — key argument:
- **Sustainable management is more important**: biomes provide essential ecosystem services; loss is often irreversible; biodiversity collapse threatens all economic activity including fishing and farming; Coral Triangle — 120 million people depend on healthy seas
- **Economic development is also important**: reduces poverty; short-term trade-off may be necessary; development can fund future conservation
- **Balanced conclusion**: both are important — but without biomes, there is no economy; sustainable development is the long-term solution. Students may conclude they are equally important.

## Key vocabulary
- **Biodiversity**: variety of life in an area
- **Ecosystem services**: benefits humans get from biomes (clean water, oxygen, food, climate regulation)
- **Trophic cascade**: chain reaction through food web when one species is removed or added
- **Deforestation**: removal of trees at large scale
- **Rewilding**: restoring an ecosystem to a more natural state, often by reintroducing species
- **Carbon sink**: ecosystem (forest, ocean) that absorbs more carbon than it releases
- **Selective logging**: removing individual trees rather than clear-cutting; preserves canopy and soil
- **By-catch**: unintentionally caught species during commercial fishing; disrupts food chains
- **Over-exploitation**: using a resource faster than it can naturally replenish
