# Food Security

## Definition
**Food security** exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social and economic access to sufficient, safe and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life.

**Food insecurity** = when this access is threatened.

**Water security** is closely related — without reliable water access, agricultural production and food security collapse together. Jordan's water crisis (M22) is a key adjacent case study.

---

## Key concepts

### Causes of food insecurity
- **Import dependency**: relying on other countries for food → vulnerable to supply disruptions (war, trade disputes, pandemics)
- **Poverty**: inability to afford food even when available
- **Underdeveloped agriculture**: poor technologies, low investment, harsh terrain
- **Climate vulnerability**: drought, flooding destroy harvests
- **Conflict**: disrupts farming and supply chains
- **Natural disasters**: floods, earthquakes, volcanic ash destroys harvests (links to [[natural-hazards]])
- **Land degradation**: overgrazing, desertification, soil erosion reduce agricultural productivity

### Ways to increase food security
- Invest in agricultural technology (better seeds, irrigation, fertilizers)
- Diversify crops grown domestically
- Reduce import dependency
- Improve rural incomes (so farmers can invest in their land)
- International food aid (short-term solution only)
- Sustainable farming practices
- Water management (essential — see Jordan case study)

### Primary vs secondary research on food security
- **Secondary data**: caloric intake statistics, import/export data, malnutrition rates, crop types — useful for understanding scale of problem
- **Primary data**: farmer interviews — useful for understanding lived experience, farming practices, income; but small sample sizes are a major limitation

### Sustainable trade and food security (N23 connection)
- SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production): 1.3 billion tonnes of food wasted every year while 2 billion people go hungry
- SDG 1 (No Poverty): Fairtrade links trade sustainability to food security — better wages → better food access
- SDG 5 (Gender Equality): 60–80% of world's food is grown by women; women more likely to live in extreme poverty → closing gender gap in agriculture improves food security
- Sustainable trade models (Fairtrade, ethical sourcing) help ensure farmers receive fair prices → they can invest in production → food security improves

## Case study: Tajikistan (M25 Q2)

**Key facts**:
- Location: Central Asia (landlocked)
- ~60% of population employed in agriculture
- 47% live on less than $1.33/day — highest childhood malnutrition in Europe & Central Asia
- Imports cover 75% of food needs
- Main crops: wheat, cotton; terrain is mostly mountainous

**The problem**: Heavily reliant on food imports. War or instability in exporting countries can threaten food supply. Government wants to increase domestic food production and food security.

**Research question posed (M25 Q2)**: *"How can Tajikistan reduce their reliance on other countries to increase their food security?"*

**Action plan stages**:
1. Research sub-questions [Launched]: Is Tajikistan overly reliant? Why are farming technologies poor? Which foods imported? What % population vegan?
2. Secondary data collection [In progress]: caloric intake, crops grown/exported, farming practices, causes of malnutrition
3. Primary data [Not started]: Interview 5 farmers about practices and income
4. Presentation to government [Not started]: Secondary data + farmer interview findings

**Evaluating this action plan**:

*Strengths*:
- Uses both primary (farmer interviews) and secondary data → triangulation
- Focuses research sub-questions tightly on the research question
- Action plan is staged — systematic approach

*Limitations*:
- Only 5 farmers interviewed out of a population where 60% are farmers → tiny, unrepresentative sample
- Stage 4 presentation based only on secondary data and small interview sample → conclusions may not be valid
- No plan for collecting data on government food policy or import agreements
- Sub-question about vegan population percentage is irrelevant to the core RQ — wastes research effort

*Overall evaluation*: The plan has a logical structure but the primary data collection is severely limited. Interviewing only 5 farmers from a population where hundreds of thousands farm means the findings cannot be generalised. The government may make policy decisions on very thin evidence.

## Case study: Jordan — water security (M22 Q6/Q7/Q8)

**Context**: Jordan is a country in the Middle East. Does not have enough fresh water to meet the needs of its population. Extracts 46% of water from groundwater sources.

**Key data (Source B — groundwater map)**:
- Jordan has 12 groundwater basins; most are in **deficit** (extraction exceeds sustainable amount)
- Largest deficit: Amman Zarqa Basin: -78.5 MCM (Million Cubic Metres)
- Other significant deficits: Dead Sea Basin (-33 MCM), Rifthe Side Wadis (-31.7 MCM), Azraq (-28.5 MCM)
- Only two basins are sustainable (positive): **Hammad** (+6.1 MCM) and **Sarhan** (+3.3 MCM)
- Source: www.allthatiswater.org

**Exam Q6a**: Jordan's water extraction is sustainable in the **Hammad and Sarhan** basins.
**Exam Q6b**: Origin of Source B = **allthatiswater.org** (exact URL required)

**Proposed solution: Desalinization (Q7/Q8)**
- Process: salt water input → pressure → membrane filter → fresh water output; contaminants separated out
- **Resident against**: energy cost too high; fossil fuels needed contribute GHGs to environment; climate change makes Jordan's situation worse, not better
- **Resident FOR (Q7 — justify disagreement)**: water is essential for health and industry; groundwater no longer reliable; cost of desalinization plant is better than having no water at all
- **Limitation of desalinization diagram (Q8)**: doesn't show WHERE plants will be located (distance from residents' homes) → residents can't assess whether it will serve their area

**Connection to food security**: Water is the foundation of agriculture. Jordan's water deficit means:
- Farmers cannot irrigate crops reliably
- Food production is constrained
- Increased food imports required → higher food insecurity (same pattern as Tajikistan)

## Globalization and food insecurity

Globalization creates **import dependency**:
- Cheaper imported food can undercut local farmers → local agriculture shrinks
- When global supply chains are disrupted (war, pandemic, trade policy), countries heavily dependent on imports face sudden food crises
- Example: Tajikistan imports 75% of food → highly vulnerable to any disruption in supply from trading partners
- Example: Jordan groundwater depletion + food import dependency = compound vulnerability

But globalization can also **improve food security** if:
- Aid flows from HICs to LICs increase agricultural investment (e.g. Australia → Laos: N20)
- Sustainable trade practices (Fairtrade) ensure farmers earn enough to invest in production
- Technology transfer helps LIC farmers access better seeds, irrigation, market information

## Mark scheme insights (M25 Q2 — 8-mark evaluate)

### Structure for an 8-mark evaluate
You need THREE components: **Strengths + Limitations + Overall appraisal**

Each component has its own markband (0–3 for strengths, 0–3 for limitations, 0–2 for appraisal = total 8).

**Strengths markband**:
- 1 mark: states one strength
- 2 marks: outlines one strength (one sentence with elaboration) — if 3+ strengths outlined → 3 marks
- 3 marks: explains one strength with clear reasoning

**Limitations markband**:
- 1 mark: states limitation ("Only five farmers are interviewed")
- 2 marks: outlines limitation with context ("Only five farmers of a population where 60% are employed as farmers")
- 3 marks: explains — adds why this is a problem ("...so the sample will not represent the problems and the consultant will not gain sufficient information")

**Appraisal markband**:
- 1 mark: brief appraisal/judgment ("Overall, the investigation was unsuccessful")
- 2 marks: outlines appraisal — weighs positive AND negative points; makes overall judgement with reasoning

**Key examiner notes**:
- Suggesting alternative methods counts as addressing limitations
- Appraisal can appear ANYWHERE in the response (not just conclusion)
- Quality rewarded over quantity — one well-developed point > three weak ones

## Mark scheme insights (M22 Q7 — justify alternative perspective, 4 marks)

The student MUST present an alternative perspective to the quoted resident (who opposes desalinization). Agreeing with the resident earns 0.

Model 4-mark response: *"Another person might think that it is worth spending the money to provide freshwater to meet the needs of the population and industry of Jordan. The residents can no longer rely on groundwater, and so the cost of desalinization plants is far better than having no water at all."*
