# Tourism

Covers: overtourism, impacts of tourism on communities and natural environments, sustainable tourism management, and ecotourism.

## Key concepts

- **Tourism**: The activity of travelling for leisure, business, or other purposes to a destination outside one's usual environment.
- **Overtourism**: When the number of tourists at a destination exceeds the capacity of the environment, infrastructure, or community to absorb them sustainably — leading to environmental degradation, loss of quality of life for residents, and damage to the destination's appeal.
- **Sustainable tourism**: Tourism that meets the needs of tourists and host communities while protecting natural and cultural environments for future generations.
- **Ecotourism**: Tourism that focuses on natural environments and promotes conservation and community benefits — often used as a counter to mass tourism.
- **Carrying capacity**: The maximum number of visitors an environment can sustain without unacceptable damage.
- **Cultural tourism**: Tourism centred on the cultural heritage, traditions, and way of life of a community (e.g. Kaikōura's Māori-owned tourism).
- **Visitor fee / entry fee**: A charge levied on tourists to fund conservation and limit visitor numbers (Galapagos Islands: US$100 entry fee).
- **Tourist guide requirement**: A management strategy requiring tourists to be accompanied by an approved local guide — controls behaviour, protects sensitive areas, creates local employment.
- **Invasive species**: Non-native plants or animals introduced to an ecosystem by tourists — can devastate local biodiversity.
- **Cultural impact of tourism**: Tourism can revitalize culture (crafts, traditions, languages gain commercial value) but can also erode it (tourists' values and spending patterns alter local behaviour, dress, lifestyle).
- **Mass tourism**: Large-scale commercial tourism to popular destinations — often associated with environmental and cultural damage.

## Case studies

### Galapagos Islands, Ecuador (N19 Q5, M23 Q6–8)
- Location: 1,000 km from mainland Ecuador — volcanic islands
- Known for diverse, unique wildlife found nowhere else
- **Tourist numbers**: ~180,000 (2007) → ~300,000 (2018) → 271,238 (2019)
- **Economic impact**: ~US$143 million/year in revenue from international tourism; creates local jobs
- **Entry fee**: All visitors pay US$100 on arrival — funds conservation
- **Negative impacts**: tourists bring invasive plant and animal species harming ecosystems; more hotels and increased urbanization; tourists create **~230,550 kg of waste per year**
- **M23 management options**: (1) Increase the US$100 entry fee; (2) Require each tourist group to be accompanied by an approved local guide
- Marine reserve established in 1998 to protect biodiversity — limits visitor access to certain areas
- Government and key stakeholders must decide next steps (M23 context)

### Kaikōura, New Zealand (N23 Q1–4)
- Region in New Zealand; topographic map used in exam (1:250,000 scale)
- Original inhabitants: Māori; **90% of tourism activities owned and operated by Māori**
- Many tourist activities focus on Māori culture and the spiritual connection between land and people
- Increasing tourist numbers causing damage to natural environment at South Bay and South Bay Lookout Walk
- **Option A**: Charge a fee + set maximum daily visitor numbers for South Bay and Lookout Walk
- **Option B**: Build additional car park and viewing platform at grid reference 4702
- Investigation: local tourist operators deciding between Option A and Option B (8-week decision process)
- **Phase 1**: Interview tourist operators in neighbouring towns that charge entry fees; interview Kaikōura residents about car park opinions; 3-week traffic count survey
- Limitation of traffic count: only 3 weeks, doesn't capture seasonal variation

### Mount Everest, Nepal (N19 Q5)
- Thousands of tonnes of rubbish and human waste left each year by trekkers
- Example of overtourism causing environmental degradation in a remote natural environment

### Great Barrier Reef, Australia (N19 Q5)
- Tourism contributing to loss of coral through physical disturbance, pollution, and anchor damage
- Example of overtourism threatening a marine UNESCO World Heritage Site

### Mediterranean coast (N19 Q5)
- Pollution from plastics and waste causing severe damage to sea and marine wildlife
- Example of overtourism impact on beach and coastal environments

### Nepal — cultural impact (M22 Q5)
- Tourism revitalised culture: traditional local arts and crafts celebrated, demand from tourists keeping traditional skills alive
- Negative: mountain communities (e.g. Pokhara-Ghanchuk) — teenagers' fashion, behaviour, and lifestyle changing due to tourist influence; elders concerned about cultural identity being lost
- "Residents' spending patterns and behaviour now dictated by the cultural traits of tourists"
- "Traditional fashion, behaviour, and lifestyle of teenagers from mountain communities severely affected"

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

**N19 Q5 (petition):**
- The key insight: Criterion A (knowledge content) = only 6 of 18 marks. The remaining 12 marks assess format (title, caption = C1), language (persuasive, direct, emotive = C2), and organization (C3).
- Named specific examples from the paper context: Galapagos, Everest, Great Barrier Reef, Mediterranean
- Management solutions with explanation: "In the Galapagos Islands, a marine reserve was created in 1998 to protect biodiversity. It limits the numbers allowed to visit and restricts tourists to certain areas."
- Persuasive petition language: direct, emotive, first-person where appropriate

**M22 Q5 (blog post):**
- Both positive AND negative impacts of tourism on culture required
- Specific community named (Nepal; mountain communities; Pokhara-Ghanchuk)
- Blog format: informal first-person, rhetorical questions, subheadings, conversational tone
- "Advice for future travellers" = a required element for C1 format marks
- Mark scheme example (4 marks): "Tourism has revitalised the culture of host communities in Nepal. This is important because the skills required for these could have been lost without demand from tourism. However, the traditional fashion, behaviour, and lifestyle of teenagers from mountain communities have been severely affected by tourism."

**M23 Q7 (Galapagos management justification):**
- Either option (fee increase OR compulsory guide) is valid — the mark scheme accepts both
- 6 marks requires in-depth arguments AND comparison ("Option 2 is better than Option 1 because…")
- Using source data: "US$100 entry fee," "creates jobs for locals," "230,550 kg of waste" — all from the stimulus

**N23 Q2 (Kaikōura map reference):**
- Map reference REQUIRED for full marks: must name locations, grid references, or map features
- Without explicit map reference, maximum is 3 marks (not 4)

### What examiners penalise
- **Blog as essay** (M22 Q5): Essay language loses C2 marks. Must use informal, first-person, blog-appropriate style.
- **Petition as essay** (N19 Q5): Petition/interview format requires persuasive, audience-directed language. Academic essay = low C2.
- **Only one management option in Galapagos question** (M23 Q7): Justified responses that don't compare with the other option score lower (3–4 marks, not 6).
- **No map reference in Kaikōura question** (N23 Q2): Maximum 3 marks without explicit reference to the topographic map.

## Common question types

1. **Identify/state from source** (1 mark each): Origin of video (exact website name), purpose of source, coastal map feature. Must be precise.
2. **Outline value/limitation** (2 marks each): One value OR one limitation of a tourism source for a specific user. Must be specific to this user's needs.
3. **Justify management option** (6 marks): Choose ONE option, argue for it, AND compare it against the other option. Use source data. Both valid choices.
4. **Explain positive impact with map reference** (4 marks): Name the impact, give the mechanism, and explicitly reference the map (location name, grid reference, or feature).
5. **"To what extent" essay on sustainable tourism** (24 marks): Two perspectives on whether tourist numbers are the greatest threat. Other threats must be acknowledged for higher bands.
6. **Communication tasks** (18 marks, N19 Q5, M22 Q5): Content (6 marks) + format (2 marks) + language (6 marks) + organization (4 marks). Language and format = 12 marks. Write in the correct genre (petition, blog).
7. **Research design on sustainable tourism** (12 marks, N23 Q4): RQ needs named location + type of impact/benefit. Justify = explain why understanding this tourism interaction matters.

## Key arguments (for "to what extent" essays)

### M23 Q8: "An increase in tourist numbers is the greatest threat to sustainable tourism."

**AGREE — tourist numbers ARE the greatest threat:**
- Physical carrying capacity exceeded: Galapagos — 300,000 tourists/year + 230,550 kg of waste/year damages ecosystems directly
- Invasive species introduced by tourists threaten unique biodiversity that tourism depends on
- Mass tourism on Mediterranean coast: plastics and waste damage marine life
- Overtourism in Everest: tonnes of rubbish, human waste — threatens the pristine mountain environment
- Tourist numbers increasing globally, amplifying all negative impacts over time
- The very attraction (nature, culture) is destroyed by the volume of visitors — a self-defeating cycle

**DISAGREE — other factors are the greater threat:**
- **Governance failures**: Lack of enforcement of existing regulations; corruption; weak tourism planning — tourist numbers are manageable with proper governance
- **Local poverty**: Communities dependent on resource extraction (fishing, farming) that damage ecosystems even without tourists
- **Climate change**: Threatens ecosystems independently of tourism — coral bleaching, sea level rise affecting coastal destinations
- **Economic monoculture**: Over-dependence on tourism revenue creates fragility (COVID-19 devastated tourism-dependent economies) — a structural problem, not just a numbers problem
- **Inadequate infrastructure**: Poor waste management systems mean even moderate tourist numbers cause damage

**Conclusion:** Tourist numbers are a significant threat but their impact is mediated by governance, infrastructure, and management. With proper carrying capacity limits (entry fees, guide requirements), the damage from tourist numbers can be controlled. The greatest threat is the combination of high numbers AND poor governance. Sustainable tourism requires visitor management AND local governance reform.
