# Urban Environments

Covers: urban growth, megacities, city challenges (housing, pollution, services), sustainable urban development, and quality of life in cities.

## Key concepts

- **Urban growth**: The increase in the proportion of a country's population living in urban areas, or the expansion of cities in size and population.
- **Urbanization**: The process by which an increasing proportion of a country's population lives in towns and cities.
- **Rural-urban migration**: The movement of people from the countryside to cities, driven by push factors (poverty, lack of services, drought) and pull factors (jobs, education, infrastructure).
- **Megacity**: A city with a population of 10 million or more.
- **Slum / informal settlement**: Areas with inadequate housing, limited access to clean water, sanitation, and services — often populated by rural-urban migrants without resources to afford formal housing.
- **Quality of life (QoL)**: A measure of well-being covering: health, work, financial wellbeing, quality of environment, personal safety, social relationships, emotional wellbeing, belonging to a community.
- **Sustainable urban development**: Urban planning that meets present needs without compromising future generations — balancing economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection.
- **Green infrastructure**: Parks, urban forests, green roofs — improve air quality, mental health, flood management, and urban cooling.
- **Congestion**: Overcrowding of roads and public spaces — a major challenge in rapidly growing cities.
- **Urban heat island**: Cities are warmer than surrounding areas due to heat-absorbing infrastructure, reduced vegetation, and heat-generating human activity.
- **Land use zoning**: Planning policy that designates different areas for specific uses (residential, industrial, commercial, agricultural) — affects where population growth and economic development occur.
- **CBD (Central Business District)**: The commercial and business heart of a city — high property values, offices, retail.
- **GDP per capita**: Gross Domestic Product divided by population — a measure of economic output per person; used to compare levels of development between cities and countries.

## Case studies

### Dubai, UAE (N21 Q5)
- 12th-busiest airport in the world
- Over 15 million visitors in 2018
- Attracted by growing economy
- Example of rapid, wealthy urban growth attracting global migrants and tourists

### Singapore (N21 Q5)
- Global financial centre; second-largest port in the world
- Highest quality of life in Asia despite high population density
- Example of a megacity successfully managing urban growth through planning

### Kampala, Uganda (N21 Q5)
- One of the fastest-growing cities in Africa
- Population: 1.6 million (2019) → predicted 3.9 million by 2030
- Driven by high birth rates AND rural-urban migration
- Rapid growth challenges: pressure on housing, services, infrastructure

### Dhaka, Bangladesh (N21 Q5)
- Fastest-growing mega-city in the world (at time of paper)
- Estimated **400,000 mostly poor migrants per year**
- Rapid economic growth alongside persistent urban poverty
- Challenge: lack of access to sewage and clean water → health issues (diarrhoea, water pollution)
- Streets and rivers used as toilets in some areas
- **Management solution from mark scheme**: Local government building communal toilets → reduces water pollution → improves cleanliness → reduces health issues → reduces diarrhoea

### San Jose, Costa Rica (N21 Q5)
- One third of Costa Rica's population lives here
- Growth plan based on sustainable development
- Example of a city that has explicitly prioritized sustainability in its urban planning

### Las Vegas, USA (N21 Q5)
- "Entertainment Capital of the World"
- Population: 2 million+ inhabitants
- Over 42 million visitors in 2018
- Example of a HIC city with rapid growth driven by tourism and services

### Basra, Iraq (M24 Q5–7)
- Population: 1.45 million (growing due to urbanization)
- More than **46% of Basra's population lives on less than $2.15 per day** — significant urban poverty
- Major seaport — Iraq's main seaport; major trade centre; oil-based economy
- Located where two largest rivers in the region meet and flow into the Arabian Gulf
- Trade exports 2022: context of economic importance
- **Area X development decision**: choice between expanding the industrial area vs building more housing
  - In favour of housing: population growing, over 46% in poverty, increased demand for housing due to urbanization
  - In favour of industry: Basra profits from oil; seaport creates trade opportunities; agricultural land and CBD provide some employment
  - Map features: Industrial area, Canal (western edge), River (beside Area X), Low-income housing, Agriculture land, CBD, High-income housing, Sports arena

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward

**N21 Q5 (urban growth speech):**
- **Specific named city + specific problem + specific solution**: "Rapid urban growth has led to slums in Dhaka with 400,000 people migrating per year. One solution is for the local government to build communal toilets to reduce water pollution." This is the 4-mark (adequate) content response — descriptive but with a named solution.
- **Reasoning for WHY the solution helps**: "The solution minimises the amount of human waste in the waterways of Dhaka, which is the main cause of health issues there." This moves from description to explanation and earns 5–6 marks.
- **Persuasive speech language**: Rhetorical questions, emotive vocabulary, personal pronouns, direct address to the audience (urban planners). Writing like an essay loses C2 marks.

**M24 Q6 (Basra city planning):**
- **Both opinions must be referenced**: Mentioning only ONE opinion caps the score at 3/6 marks. Must engage with both "build more housing" AND "expand industrial area" — even if you only support one.
- **Using source data**: Citing "46% of population in poverty" from Source B, or the map features from Source A, earns higher marks. Vague statements without source reference score lower.
- **Justified conclusion**: The 6-mark response makes a clear decision and justifies it with multiple source-based reasons.

**M24 Q7 (megacity essay):**
- **Two quality of life indicators**: The question explicitly requires considering at least two QoL indicators. Addressing only one limits access to higher markbands.
- **Both perspectives**: Both "megacities decrease QoL" AND "megacities can improve QoL" must be developed, not just mentioned.
- **Named megacity examples**: Tokyo, Mumbai, Dhaka, New York — any named megacity with specific QoL data earns more than generic statements.

### What examiners penalise
- **Speech as essay** (N21 Q5): If it reads like an academic essay, loses C2 language marks. Must be explicitly addressed to the "International Urban Design Conference" audience.
- **Only one opinion in Basra question** (M24 Q6): Engaging with only one city planner's view = 3 mark cap.
- **One QoL indicator in essay** (M24 Q7): Must address at least two indicators (e.g. health + financial wellbeing). Failure to meet this requirement limits access to upper markbands.

## Common question types

1. **Source value/limitation** (2–4 marks, M24 Q5a–b): One value or limitation of an aerial map for city planners. Must be specific to this map and these planners' needs — not generic map praise.
2. **Justify a development decision** (6 marks, M24 Q6): Choose a development option AND engage with the opposing view. Use data from the source. 6 marks requires in-depth reasoning with source references.
3. **Urban growth challenges speech** (18 marks, N21 Q5): Major communication task. Name a specific city, describe a specific challenge, explain a specific solution. TWO or more examples earn higher content marks.
4. **Megacity QoL essay** (24 marks, M24 Q7): "To what extent" question. Two+ QoL indicators. Two genuine perspectives. Named megacity examples. Synthesis conclusion.

## Key arguments (for "to what extent" essay questions)

### M24 Q7: "To what extent does living in a megacity decrease the quality of life of societies?"

**YES — megacities decrease QoL:**
- **Health**: Air pollution from traffic and industry (China's megacities — Beijing particulate matter); spread of disease in dense informal settlements (Dhaka diarrhoea outbreaks)
- **Personal safety**: Higher crime rates in some megacities; inequality between wealthy and poor areas creates insecurity
- **Quality of environment**: Noise pollution, lack of green space, heat island effect, waste management challenges
- **Financial wellbeing**: Despite economic activity, 46% of Basra residents earn less than $2.15/day — urban wealth is unequally distributed
- **Social relationships**: Anonymity in megacities can reduce community cohesion

**NO — megacities improve QoL:**
- **Work and financial wellbeing**: Megacities concentrate economic activity — better job opportunities than rural areas (pull factor for 400,000 migrants/year to Dhaka)
- **Health services**: Megacities have better access to hospitals, clinics, specialised healthcare
- **Belonging to a community**: Cultural diversity, arts, restaurants, sports — Singapore's quality of life is highest in Asia despite density
- **Infrastructure**: Better public transport, schools, electricity, clean water access than rural areas (in many cases)
- **Social relationships**: Diverse communities; more opportunities for cultural exchange

**Conclusion that gets top marks:** The effect of megacity life on quality of life depends heavily on: (1) which QoL indicator you measure, (2) where you live within the city (wealthy vs slum area), and (3) the level of planning and governance. Megacities can simultaneously increase economic QoL while decreasing environmental QoL. On balance, rapid unplanned growth in developing-world megacities tends to decrease QoL for low-income residents, while planned megacities in HICs (Singapore, Tokyo) can maintain or improve it. The "to what extent" conclusion should acknowledge this variation.
