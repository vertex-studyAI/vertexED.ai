# Water Security

Covers: water scarcity, groundwater management, water deficits, sustainable water supply strategies (desalinization, dams, conservation), and the politics of shared water resources.

## Key concepts

- **Water security**: Reliable access to sufficient quantities of acceptable quality water for health, livelihoods, and economic activity — without unacceptable risk of disruption.
- **Water scarcity**: When water demand exceeds available supply — can be physical (not enough water) or economic (infrastructure/finances prevent access to existing water).
- **Groundwater**: Water held underground in porous rock formations (aquifers) — extracted via wells. Replenished slowly (decades to centuries).
- **Aquifer**: An underground layer of permeable rock or sediment that holds water. Over-extraction leads to a water deficit.
- **Water deficit**: When water extraction exceeds the sustainable recharge rate — represented as a negative figure (MCM = Million Cubic Metres).
- **Water surplus**: When recharge exceeds extraction — represented as a positive MCM figure.
- **Desalinization**: The process of removing salt from seawater to produce freshwater. Method: pressure forces saltwater through a semi-permeable membrane (reverse osmosis), separating water molecules from salt and other contaminants.
  - Advantages: creates freshwater from an effectively unlimited source (the sea)
  - Disadvantages: very high energy consumption; requires fossil fuels (unless powered by renewables); produces brine waste; expensive infrastructure
- **Dams / reservoirs**: Store water for controlled release — supports drinking water, irrigation, and HEP. Upstream dams can deplete downstream water supply (key Mekong / GERD issue).
- **Water management**: Strategies to ensure sustainable water use — includes conservation, recycling, pricing mechanisms, international agreements.
- **Virtual water**: Water embedded in the production of goods — importing food-heavy crops from water-abundant regions "saves" water in water-scarce countries.
- **Nile River**: Shared by 11 countries; particularly contested between Ethiopia (upstream, building GERD), Sudan, and Egypt (Egypt depends on Nile for 90% of freshwater).

## Case studies

### Jordan — groundwater crisis (M22 Q6)
- Jordan is in the Middle East and does not have enough fresh water to meet population needs
- **46% of Jordan's water extracted from groundwater sources**
- **Source B** (M22): Map showing groundwater basins and their extraction deficit (2019 data)
  - Amman Zarqa Basin: **-78.5 MCM** (largest deficit; darkest shading)
  - Dead Sea Basin: -33 MCM
  - Azraq Basin: -28.5 MCM
  - Rifthe Side Wadis: -31.7 MCM
  - Disi Basin: -18 MCM
  - Yarmouk Basin: -14 MCM
  - **Hammad Basin: +6.1 MCM** (sustainable — positive)
  - **Sarhan Basin: +3.3 MCM** (sustainable — positive)
- Only Hammad and Sarhan basins have sustainable extraction (positive value = recharge > extraction)
- Source created by www.allthatiswater.org

### Jordan — desalinization debate (M22 Q7–8)
- Government investigating desalinization to supplement fresh water supply
- **How desalinization works (Source C diagram)**: salt water input → pressure applied → semi-permeable membrane filter → water molecules pass through → fresh water output; salt and contaminants separated out
- **Anti-desalinization resident's view** (quoted in exam): "The energy needed is too high; expensive; climate change is already affecting us; using fossil fuels to run desalinization will contribute more GHGs; must look at other sources."
- **Pro-desalinization perspective** (examiner's expected counter-argument): "Worth spending the money to provide freshwater to meet population and industry needs; can no longer rely on groundwater; cost of desalinization is better than having no water at all."
- Limitation of desalinization diagram (Source C) for Jordan residents: "Does not show where the desalinization plants will be located, such as distance from residents' homes."

### Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) — water management (N19 Q3)
- Africa's largest source of HEP when complete
- Blue Nile, Ethiopia
- **Egypt**: little annual rainfall; **depends on the Nile for 90% of its water**
- Sudan: also increasing Nile water use
- Ethiopia, Egypt, Sudan agreed to cooperate to share the Nile resource
- **Investigation evaluated**: Consultant's action plan to help three countries reach agreement
  - Interview with Ethiopian Minister of Water and Energy (primary data)
  - Further research via UN Water website
  - Research question: "To what extent can cooperation between countries lead to fair distribution of water?"
- Limitation: Only one government minister interviewed — biased toward Ethiopia; doesn't represent Egypt's/Sudan's perspectives
- Strength: Interview questions are mainly relevant to the investigation; government minister has access to official data

## Common question types

1. **Select from map** (1 mark): Identify sustainable basins (Hammad and Sarhan — positive MCM values). Know what a positive vs negative MCM means.
2. **State source origin** (1 mark): Exact website name or organization name. Learn these from the source headings in past papers.
3. **Explain value of source** (4 marks): One value in detail + reasoning about why it helps the specific user. Not generic map praise.
4. **Justify alternative perspective** (4 marks): Always argue the OPPOSITE of the quoted viewpoint. Provide reasons/evidence for the counter-view.
5. **Outline limitation** (2 marks): One limitation + brief elaboration. "The diagram does not show where plants will be located" → "so residents don't know how far they'd need to travel."
6. **Evaluate action plan** (8 marks, N19 Q3): GERD water management — standard Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2) structure.

## Key arguments (for water security questions)

### "Is desalinization an effective solution to water scarcity?"

**YES — desalinization IS effective:**
- Produces freshwater from seawater (effectively unlimited ocean supply)
- Jordan can no longer rely on groundwater (most basins in deficit) — desalinization offers a new supply source
- Technology improving — costs declining over time; some plants now powered by solar energy
- Already used successfully in Gulf states (UAE, Saudi Arabia) and Israel

**NO — desalinization has major limitations:**
- Very high energy consumption — typically powered by fossil fuels → contributes to GHG emissions → worsens climate change → exacerbates water scarcity (ironic cycle)
- High capital cost to build plants — prohibitive for poor nations
- Produces concentrated brine waste — can damage marine ecosystems if disposed in the ocean
- Fixes symptoms (insufficient freshwater) but not root causes (population growth, poor conservation)

### "How can water be shared more fairly through international cooperation?"

- GERD example: Ethiopia, Sudan, and Egypt agreed to cooperate — international agreements possible
- UN Water organization provides frameworks, monitoring, and reporting
- Upstream development (Ethiopia building 60+ dams on Mekong; GERD on Nile) harms downstream users — international law needed to regulate
- Voluntary agreements can fail — power imbalances mean larger/stronger countries can ignore commitments
- Fair sharing requires: binding international law, independent monitoring, compensation mechanisms for downstream damage
