# Climate Change

## Key concepts

- **Enhanced Greenhouse Effect**: The natural greenhouse effect amplified by human emissions. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, CFCs) trap outgoing longwave radiation, warming the surface beyond natural levels.
- **Carbon footprint**: Total greenhouse gases emitted by an individual, business, or country — a theoretical measurement used to compare emissions.
- **Mitigation**: Reducing the *causes* of climate change (e.g. reducing GHG emissions through energy-efficient transport, carbon taxes, renewable energy).
- **Adaptation**: Reducing the *effects* of climate change (e.g. building coastal defences, planting drought-resistant crops, raising buildings on stilts).
- **Carbon sink**: A biome (e.g. the Amazon rainforest) that absorbs more CO₂ than it emits, removing it from the atmosphere.
- **IPCC**: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change — the scientific body that synthesises research on climate change.
- **Paris Agreement (2015)**: A global agreement aiming to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels; voluntary, so countries can withdraw (e.g. the USA under Trump).
- **Tipping points**: Irreversible thresholds beyond which climate systems cannot recover (referenced by UN Secretary General at COP27, 2022).
- **COP (Conference of the Parties)**: Annual UN climate summits where countries negotiate commitments; COP27 was in 2022.
- **1.5°C vs 2°C warming**: The difference matters enormously — at 2°C, flood risk increases by 170% (vs 100% at 1.5°C), coral reef loss rises from 70% to 99%, and heat wave exposure jumps from 14 million to 2 billion people.
- **Vulnerability**: The extent a location is negatively affected by climate change and whether it can adapt. Varies by country — Pacific island nations and African nations tend to show Very High vulnerability.

## Case studies

### Global temperature trend (N21 Source A)
- Scatter graph showing yearly average global temperature change 1960–2019
- Temperatures at or below 0°C difference in early 1960s; rising to approximately +1.0°C by 2019
- Overall trend: consistent increase with some fluctuation year-to-year

### 1.5°C vs 2°C impacts infographic (M23 Source A)
- Flood risk: 100% increase at 1.5°C → 170% increase at 2°C
- Heat waves: 14 million exposed at 1.5°C → 2 billion exposed at 2°C
- Sea level rise: 48cm (1.5°C) → 56cm (2°C), affecting 69 million vs 49 million people
- Coral bleaching: 70% of reefs lost at 1.5°C → 99% at 2°C
- Biodiversity: 6% insects / 8% plants at 1.5°C → 18% insects / 16% plants at 2°C

### Carbon footprint by country (N20 Source D — Global Footprint Network, 2017)
- USA: 4.5% of world population but 15.6% of world carbon footprint
- China: 19.9% of world population, 27.2% of world carbon footprint
- Rest of world: 47.1% of population, 33.4% of carbon footprint
- Context: Doughnut chart comparing population share vs carbon footprint share for 10 countries

### Rotterdam Water Plaza — Benthemplein (M23 Q3)
- Rotterdam experiences heavy rainfall and flooding each year
- "Water Plaza" proposed: 3 sunken reservoirs capable of holding 1,730,000 litres of water from surrounding areas
- Water is released into city's storm water drainage system after storage
- Example of climate adaptation — managing flood risk caused by increasing rainfall

### Tuvalu — sea level rise (M23 Q4)
- Low-lying atoll experiencing sea level rise
- Flood barriers in use on the coast
- Used as example of a location where climate adaptation (flood barriers) effectiveness can be investigated

### Spain climate vulnerability (N24 Q6–7)
- Spain rated Medium vulnerability on 2021 global map
- Barcelona: higher population density (16,000/km²), coastal flooding risk from low elevation and coastal position, 7 million+ tourists (2018), exports 6.33 billion Euros
- Madrid: inland, water stress risk, fire risk, lower coastal flooding risk, 5,400/km², 5.6 million tourists (2019)
- Climate change risks depend on the specific type (flood vs water stress vs fire)

### Paris Agreement (N20 Q8)
- 2015 global climate agreement
- USA withdrew under Trump — demonstrates weakness of voluntary agreements
- International climate policy has historically been voluntary, so countries are not required to join or meet targets

## Mark scheme insights

### What examiners reward
- **Mechanism not just naming**: For mitigation questions, explaining *how* the strategy reduces GHG emissions (e.g. "energy-efficient transport reduces CO₂ → less longwave radiation trapped → slower warming") earns more than just listing the strategy name.
- **Counterarguments for "to what extent" essays**: One-sided essays cap at 1–2 marks on Criterion D. The N20 Q8 and N24 Q8 essays both require genuinely different perspectives.
- **Synthesis in conclusions**: The upper bands (5–6 for Criterion D) explicitly require "ideas combined to form a clear and coherent conclusion." Simply writing "in conclusion, both sides have good points" scores nothing. You must weigh and judge.
- **Specific data**: For graph-reading questions (N21 Q1), referencing actual data values from the source earns the second mark. "Temperatures increased" = 1 mark; "Temperatures increased from approximately 0°C to +1.0°C" = 2 marks.
- **Source-specific evaluation**: For Q6c/6d (N20) and Q6a–c (N24), generic praise ("the graph is clear") scores 1 mark only. You must identify what specifically that graph shows and why it helps or fails the specific user.

### What examiners penalise
- Using carbon footprint as the only measure without noting it is theoretical and doesn't account for all GHGs (methane, CFCs) — this is the N20 Q8 counterargument
- Confusing mitigation and adaptation — exam questions frequently set these as alternatives to justify
- Graphs with only one year of data (limitation of N20 Source D) — a student investigating change over time would be hindered

### Mitigation vs adaptation: which is "better"?
The mark scheme example responses for N20 Q7 consistently use mitigation as the higher-scoring choice because it addresses the root cause. However, adaptation can also score full marks if the mechanism is explained (how it reduces impact rather than just describing what it does). The formula: "Strategy X reduces [specific GHG/warming mechanism] because [causal chain] → therefore [temperature/impact effect is reduced]."

## Common question types

1. **Graph reading** (1–2 marks): Read a climate data graph and state the trend with data. Always quote specific values.
2. **Explain one cause** (4 marks): Pick ONE human activity/cause and develop it with full causal reasoning. The 4-mark answer requires the whole chain: activity → GHG emission → greenhouse effect mechanism → warming.
3. **Evaluate an action plan** (8 marks): Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2). See research-methods.md for full structure.
4. **Research design** (12 marks): Formulate RQ + justify + outline challenge + explain source. See research-methods.md.
5. **Justify mitigation or adaptation** (4–6 marks): Pick a side and explain the mechanism. Must explain *why* it's more effective, not just describe it.
6. **To what extent essay** (24 marks): Requires two+ perspectives, specific examples, synthesis, and a justified conclusion. Criterion A (content) + Criterion D (perspectives/thinking) + Criterion C (format/language).

## Key arguments (for "to what extent" essays)

### N20 Q8: "To what extent do you agree that countries' carbon footprint share should not exceed their population share?"

**YES (agree):**
- USA contributes 15.6% of carbon footprint but only 4.5% of population — disproportionate and unfair
- Without mandatory agreements, countries can withdraw (USA withdrew from Paris Agreement)
- Historical voluntary agreements have failed to enforce sufficient action
- HICs have the financial capacity to fund mitigation

**NO / counterargument:**
- Carbon footprint is only a theoretical measurement — doesn't account for methane, CFCs, or all GHGs
- Proportional population share targets don't address absolute emissions volume — "in 10 years proportions could be similar but actual emissions may have increased dramatically"
- Developing nations argue the right to industrialise; developed nations emitted most historically
- Some argue targets should be based on historical emissions or GDP, not population

**Conclusion that gets top marks:** A mandatory global agreement IS needed, but it must set absolute emission reduction targets (not just proportional) and include all greenhouse gases, with differentiated responsibilities based on historical emissions and development status.

### N24 Q8: "To what extent is it important that all countries respond to climate change?"

**YES (all must respond):**
- Climate change is global — emissions from one country affect all others
- Tipping points are irreversible (COP27: "climate chaos irreversible")
- Poorest, most vulnerable nations (Pacific islands, sub-Saharan Africa) emit least but suffer most
- UN Secretary General: "Humanity has a choice: cooperate or perish"

**NO / differentiated response:**
- Developed vs developing divide — wealthier countries emitted more historically and should bear greater burden
- India and China depend on coal for development; unequal burden-sharing would be unjust
- Some responses argue proportionality based on historical emissions, current GDP, and capacity to adapt

**Conclusion:** All countries must respond to some extent, but the scale and type of response must be differentiated — developed nations must lead and fund transitions in developing nations.

## Revision priorities

1. **Know the 1.5°C vs 2°C data** from the M23 infographic — examiners test this with Identify questions requiring precise figures (e.g. "170%").
2. **Master the greenhouse effect mechanism** (solar radiation in → greenhouse gases trap outgoing longwave radiation → enhanced warming) — this is the causal chain for every "explain" question about climate change.
3. **Mitigation vs adaptation**: Know at least 2 clear examples of each and be able to explain the mechanism, not just name the strategy.
4. **Paris Agreement weaknesses**: Voluntary, USA withdrew, proportional targets don't address absolute volume — these are the key counterarguments in essay questions.
5. **Essay structure for "to what extent"**: Must have introduction, balanced body paragraphs (two genuine perspectives), synthesis in conclusion. Format marks are free — don't lose them.
6. **Named examples**: Rotterdam (adaptation — flood management), Tuvalu (sea level rise), Spain/Barcelona/Madrid (differential climate risks), Amazon (carbon sink for biome protection).
7. **Carbon footprint limitations**: It's theoretical, only measures carbon not all GHGs — use this as a counterargument in N20-style essays.
