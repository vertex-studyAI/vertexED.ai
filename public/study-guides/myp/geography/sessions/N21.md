## Q3 — Climate change: Action plan evaluation (8 marks)

## Q5 — Urban growth: Persuasive speech (18 marks, Criterion A)

**Stimulus**: Video showing the development of six urban settlements over the past 35 years (Dubai UAE, Singapore, Kampala Uganda, Dhaka Bangladesh, San Jose Costa Rica, Las Vegas USA). Context provided as text:
- **Dubai, UAE**: 12th-busiest airport in the world, welcoming over 15 million visitors in 2018, attracted by the growing economy.
- **Singapore**: Global financial centre, second-largest port in the world; highest quality of life in Asia despite high population density.
- **Kampala, Uganda**: One of the fastest-growing cities in Africa; population predicted to grow from 1.6 million in 2019 to 3.9 million by 2030 due to high birth rates and rural-urban migration.
- **Dhaka, Bangladesh**: Fastest-growing mega-city in the world with rapid economic growth; estimated 400,000 mostly poor migrants per year.
- **San Jose, Costa Rica**: One third of Costa Rica's population lives here; growth plan based on sustainable development.

> - **YES migration creates benefits**: fills labour gaps (e.g. rural Chinese migrants to cities boosting construction/manufacturing); economic growth through taxes paid; cultural diversity; remittances to home countries
> - **COUNTERARGUMENT/NEGATIVE**: xenophobia and social tension (e.g. attacks on refugee shelters in Berlin/Germany); pressure on housing and services; brain drain from origin countries; forced migrants face trauma, exploitation
> - **CONCLUSION**: Migration creates significant economic benefits but social/cultural tensions are a real negative — the balance depends on scale, type of migration (voluntary vs forced), and host society's response. Strong essays make a nuanced judgement.

## Key mark scheme patterns (N21)

1. **Perspective questions require explicit referencing**: For Q8 (limitations from different perspectives), the examiner expects the student to explicitly mention the user/perspective (MYP student, government official) and explain WHY that specific user is disadvantaged — not just state the limitation in abstract.
2. **"Outline" = state + one elaboration**: Consistently used across Q1, Q7a, Q7b, Q4e — a single sentence with two clauses or two/three short sentences is typical for full marks on outline questions.
3. **Source analysis values vs limitations**: When asked for a value, explain why the FORMAT/MEDIUM helps (e.g. a pictograph helps a newspaper reach a wide audience). When asked for a limitation, explain why it fails the specific USER in their specific context.
4. **Formulate questions need two anchoring elements**: Time + place, or place + specific focus. Just "how can we reduce carbon emissions?" is too vague for 2 marks.
5. **Criterion A essays need both perspectives AND conclusion**: For upper bands (7–8), examiners look for: (a) multiple perspectives developed fully, (b) examples named and unpacked with detail, (c) a conclusion that makes a justified judgement. Naming an example without unpacking = 3–4 band maximum.
6. **Best-fit applies across all markbands**: If a response demonstrates strong strengths and moderate weaknesses, award the higher band. Do not penalise for errors.
7. **Command term discipline**: "State" = no explanation needed or rewarded; "Outline" = brief elaboration; "Explain" = full cause-and-effect reasoning; "Evaluate" = strengths + limitations + appraisal; "To what extent" = balanced argument + justified conclusion.
