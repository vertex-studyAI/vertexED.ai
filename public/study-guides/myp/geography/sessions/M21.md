## Q3 — Research methods: Amazon Rainforest (8 marks, Criterion B)

**Stimulus**: Source C — Interactive map showing how changes in the Amazon Rainforest are impacting indigenous communities. Map shows: Indigenous communities (white circles), Deforestation (orange), Oil and gas development (gold), Border of Amazon Rainforest. Inset photos show: "The rainforest is being removed to make way for farmland"; "Access to water had decreased for many tribes"; "Soil degradation is impacting food production"; "Trees have lost places that hold significant cultural value."

**Context**: MYP Year 5 class in Brazil studied the effect of human actions on the rainforest biome and the identities of the people who live there. They investigated how deforestation has changed the lives of indigenous people in the Amazon Rainforest. Action plan:

- **Statement of inquiry**: Change in the natural environment can impact human identities and relationships.
- **Research question**: To what extent do changes in the natural environment impact human identities?
- **Procedure**: Visit 4 villages shown on the map (Source C) over two weeks; interview different families to understand how deforestation has changed their daily lives; collect and analyse soil samples for fertility.
- **Sample size**: 12 families total (3 families from each of the 4 villages)
- **Primary data**: (1) Collect 5 soil samples from each village; (2) Ask open-ended questions: a) Do you eat different foods because of deforestation? b) Are there enough wild animals left to hunt? c) How has deforestation affected your children? d) How many sacred areas have been destroyed?
- **Secondary data**: (3) Maps showing deforestation in the Amazon Rainforest; (4) Statistics on deforestation from wildlife organisations.

**Task**: **Evaluate** the action plan.

**Markband — Strengths**:
| Marks | Descriptor | Notes / Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| 0 | Nothing worthy | — |
| 1 | Student **states** the strength(s) of the action plan | "The sample size of families is large enough to gather useful data." Only **one** strength needs to be mentioned to warrant 1 mark. A (1 mark) response is likely to be one sentence that refers to a single strength. |
| 2 | Student **outlines** the strength(s) of the action plan | "Interviewing 12 different families from 4 different communities will provide the students with a variety of data to analyse." Only one strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on with brief detail to warrant 2 marks. However, if a student outlines three or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks. |
| 3 | Student **explains** the strength(s) of the action plan | "Interviewing 12 families from 4 different communities will provide the students with a variety of data to analyse. **Different families from different villages might have varying experiences, so interviewing 12 families from four areas, each with unique insights into the issue, will give more valuable primary data.**" Only one strength needs to be mentioned and elaborated on in additional detail with reasoning to warrant 3 marks. If a student outlines three or more strengths, they can be awarded 3 marks. |

**Markband — Limitations**:

| 2 | Student **outlines** the limitation(s) of the action plan | "The action plan does not consider the possible language barrier between the students and indigenous communities. **The families might not be able to speak Portuguese or English.**" Only one limitation needs to be mentioned and elaborated on with brief detail (two clauses / two/three short sentences) to warrant 2 marks. ## Q5 — Rivers & coasts: Portchester persuasive presentation (18 marks, Criterion A)

**Stimulus**: Diagram (interactive) showing the community of Portchester, a town in the United Kingdom. 1. **Drag and drop** labels A and B onto the diagram to show the locations of **two** threats to the river and/or coastal system in Portchester.

3. Write a **script for a persuasive presentation** to **explain** the sustainable management strategies you would recommend to address the two threats to the river and/or coastal system you have identified.

In your script, you **must** include:
- examples from your MYP studies
- reference(s) to your labelled diagram

A conclusion which reflects on which effects pose the most risk is likely to be expected but not required. |
| 5–6 | Student provides **different** perspectives in an **analysis** and **synthesizes** information to make **clear** arguments | Two perspectives included with substantial development of these to show a good understanding and their implications, although they may not always be considerably balanced. Ideas on which effects pose the most risk (e.g. multiple primary effects or secondary over primary) may be combined to form a clear and coherent conclusion. The synthesis (combining ideas) could appear in the main body or in the conclusion. |
| 7–8 | Student provides **different** perspectives in a **detailed analysis** and **synthesizes** information to make **clear** arguments | Two perspectives included with thorough development of these to show excellent understanding and their implications. The two perspectives must be considerably balanced. Ideas on which effects pose the most risk are combined to form a clear, coherent and convincing conclusion. The synthesis could appear in the main body of the essay or in the conclusion. Students may conclude that they pose equal risk. |

> Key argument framework:
> - **Secondary > primary**: tsunamis, fires, disease outbreaks, economic disruption, displacement — these often affect far more people over a longer time than the initial shaking/eruption; example — 2011 Tohoku earthquake: primary effects killed thousands but the tsunami (secondary) devastated entire coastlines and the Fukushima nuclear disaster had decade-long consequences
> - **Primary > secondary**: immediate structural collapse causes the majority of direct deaths; secondary effects can be mitigated with better preparation; example — Haiti 2010 earthquake: primary structural collapse killed 200,000+
> - **COUNTERARGUMENT**: depends heavily on type of event (earthquake vs volcano vs tsunami), location, and level of development; secondary effects are harder to predict and prepare for, so in low-income countries they may be more devastating
> - **CONCLUSION**: both perspectives must be genuinely weighed; students can conclude either way — what matters is synthesis and a justified judgement

## Key mark scheme patterns (M21)

1. **One-word identify questions are strict**: Q1a and Q1b explicitly state that if more than one answer is given, the answer is considered incorrect (0 marks). Only write one answer for Identify/State questions.
2. **Quality over quantity**: For all extended-answer questions (Q2, Q3, Q4a–d, Q6c–d, Q7), one well-elaborated point beats multiple shallow ones. This is explicitly stated in almost every mark scheme note.
3. **Appraisal positioning (Q3)**: The overall judgement for Evaluate questions does NOT have to be a concluding statement — it can appear anywhere in the response. Examiners look for implicit or explicit weighing.
4. **Criterion B is additive (Q3)**: Strengths (max 3) + Limitations (max 3) + Appraisal (max 2) = 8 marks total. Students can score full appraisal marks while having weak strengths/limitations sections.
5. **Research question focus rule (Q4a)**: A RQ without a named location/specific focus = maximum 1 mark, even if it is clearly worded and connected to the SoI.
6. **Presentation language is assessed separately (Q5)**: Criterion A tests geography content; C2 tests persuasive/presentation language. A response can have excellent geography but poor presentation style and lose C2 marks. Use first person, rhetorical questions, emotive vocabulary.
7. **Q5 diagram labelling is mandatory for format marks**: C1 explicitly requires an appropriate key/legend. Students who write only a script without a key/legend can score maximum 1 mark on C1.
8. **Q7 justification — data specificity matters**: Citing population density figures ("more than 400 per square km") is what separates 4–5 marks from 6 marks. Use numbers from the source.
9. **Q8 Criterion D requires genuine balance**: 7–8 marks for Criterion D specifically requires "considerably balanced" perspectives. One perspective argued strongly with only superficial treatment of the other = 5–6 marks maximum.
10. **Best-fit model throughout**: Examiners match overall quality to the nearest markband; they reward correct points and do not penalise incorrect ones alongside correct ones.
