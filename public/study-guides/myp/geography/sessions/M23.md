## Q1 — Climate change: Source A infographic (2 marks)

**Stimulus**: Source A — a detailed infographic titled "What are the impacts of a 1.5°C and 2°C temperature change?" showing:

## Q3 — Research methods: Rotterdam flood management (8 marks)

**Stimulus**: Rotterdam is a city in The Netherlands that experiences heavy rainfall and is flooded each year. An urban planner is investigating how to manage the flood risk caused by increasing levels of rainfall, focusing on an area called Benthemplein in Rotterdam.

**The investigation includes**:
- **Goal**: To develop a strategy to manage the flood risk caused by increasing levels of rainfall in Benthemplein, Rotterdam.
- **Solution proposed**: Create a "Water Plaza" with surface water storage and recreational areas. 3 surface reservoirs can hold 8500 bathtubs or 1,730,000 litres of water from surrounding areas. Water is then released into the city's storm water drainage system.
- **Planning Stage — Phase 1 (8 weeks)**:
  1. Interview members of the local council to evaluate the success of other water storage areas in Rotterdam
  2. Interview residents of the area to determine how they currently use the space
  3. Use rainfall data to identify the highest level of rainfall and amount of rainfall storage needed
  4. Take soil samples to calculate the rate that water moves through the soil
  5. Use predictions from the government meteorology department on the highest rainfall in this location
- **Planning Stage — Phase 2 (4 weeks)**:
  1. Review data from phase one
  2. Sketch plans for the area
  3. Create digital imaging to show the solution

**Task**: **Evaluate** the urban planner's investigation.

> Note: Strengths and weaknesses of the investigation can include the methodology, outcome and planning of the investigation. Suggesting alternative methods can be interpreted as addressing limitations.

## Q5 — Natural hazards / GIS: Tonga volcanic eruption (18 marks, Criterion A)

**Stimulus**: An email from the Ministry of Disaster Response, Government of the Kingdom of Tonga, to a GIS company called SpatialCode.

**Email content**:
> "Dear SpatialCode, We have experienced the devastating effects of a volcanic eruption and resulting tsunami in Tonga. The volcano erupted here from December 2021 to January 2022. The eruption caused a 1.2 metre tsunami wave to hit our capital city, Nuku'alofa. In the aftermath of the volcanic eruption and tsunami, it was very challenging to deliver aid to those who needed it most, and this is something that we need to address in the future. Here is a link to relevant information about the eruption and its impacts. Please reply to this email to explain at least one strength and at least one limitation of using geographic information systems (GIS) to manage the impacts of tectonic events in Tonga. Yours sincerely, Ministry of Disaster Response, Government of the Kingdom of Tonga."

**Supporting source** (Ministry of Disaster Response website — three tabs):
- **Introduction**: Tonga is made up of 170 islands in the Pacific Ocean. People live on 36 of these islands. Map showing location of volcano and capital city Nuku'alofa.
- **Impacts**: Tsunami damaged 600 structures; infrastructure affected (transport, power, water, communications); volcanic ash harmed agriculture and fishing; approximately 85,000 people affected; US$43.7 million of damage caused.
- **The future**: Working with experts to monitor the volcano and identify areas most at risk. Map of Nuku'alofa critical infrastructure (hospitals, police stations, ports, power plants, telecommunications, roads, water storage).

**Task**: Reply to the email. Write a subject title for your email and reply explaining **at least one strength** and **at least one limitation** of using GIS to manage the impacts of tectonic events in Tonga.

> Note: If a response only refers to strengths or limitations, the maximum mark that can be awarded is 3.

## Key mark scheme patterns (M23)

1. **Quality over quantity — applies universally**: Across every developed-answer question, one well-elaborated point beats three superficial ones. Examiners consistently state "quality of response is what is rewarded, not the number of [strengths/limitations/values] referred to."
2. **Appraisals can be anywhere**: For Q3 (evaluate), the overall appraisal/judgement does not have to be a concluding statement — it can appear anywhere in the response.
3. **Either option is valid for Q7**: Justify questions with two options accept either choice provided the justification has depth. A "thorough" justification requires in-depth arguments referring to specific details.
4. **GIS question (Q5) requires both content AND format**: Students lose marks if they write a geography essay rather than a formal email. Subject title, salutation, formal sign-off, and first-person professional language are all assessed separately.
5. **"To what extent" essays (Q8) require multiple perspectives AND a synthesis**: Getting to the 5–6 band requires the synthesis/conclusion to weigh and combine ideas — not merely list two sides. The synthesis can appear in the body, not only at the end.
6. **Source evaluation questions have a ceiling if one-sided**: Q5 Criterion A caps at 3 marks if a student only discusses strengths OR only limitations — both sides are required.
7. **Best-fit model throughout**: If a response meets 2 of 3 requirements in a markband, examiners still award the mark. Examiners mark positively — rewarding what is correct, not penalising what is incorrect.
8. **Identify/State questions are strict**: Q1a requires the precise figure "170%". Q6a requires "www.galapagosdaily.com" or "Galapagos Daily". Vague responses get 0.
