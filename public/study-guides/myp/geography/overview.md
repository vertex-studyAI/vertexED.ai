## Exam structure patterns (across 11 sessions)

### The consistent skeleton (all sessions)

Every paper follows this three-section structure:

1. **Topic section 1** (Q1–Q2, ~6 marks): Short data-reading + explain/describe — tests factual recall of one topic
2. **Research evaluation section** (Q3, 8 marks): Evaluate a provided action plan — always Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2)
3. **Research design section** (Q4, 12 marks): Design your own investigation — always includes Formulate (2) + Justify (4) + 2–3 smaller sub-questions
4. **Extended task** (Q5, 18 marks, Criterion A): Write in a specific genre. Always criterion-marked. Always content + language + format.
5. **Topic section 2** (Q6–Q8, ~12–15 marks): Source analysis questions on a second topic, typically ending in a 6-mark justify question
6. **Essay** (final question, 24 marks, Criterion A): "To what extent" essay on the second topic

**Total marks**: typically 80–82 marks across all questions.

### Mark distribution
- Q1–Q2: ~6 marks (10%)
- Q3 (evaluate): 8 marks (10%)
- Q4 (research design): 12 marks (15%)
- Q5 (extended task): 18 marks (22%)
- Q6–Q8 (source analysis + justify): ~12–16 marks (15–20%)
- Final essay: 24 marks (29%)

**The extended task and essay together = ~42 of ~81 marks (~52%). This is where exams are won or lost.**

### Changes and evolution across sessions

- **2019–2020 period** (N19, N20): Format largely identical to recent sessions. N19 essay was 18 marks (not 24) — the first sign of 24-mark essays emerging by M20/M21 onward.
- **M21–present**: Essay consistently 24 marks. Criterion D (thinking critically — perspectives) assessed as a separate sub-criterion within the 24-mark essay in some sessions.
- **Research design section evolution**: N19–M22 used 4 sub-questions in Q4 (2+4+2+4 marks). More recent sessions (M23, N23, M24, N24, M25) have 4–5 sub-questions, with the new addition of "outline one challenge" (2 marks) and "list/outline sources" (2 marks each) becoming standard.
- **Q3 evaluate structure**: Consistently 3+3+2 = 8 marks throughout all sessions. No change.
- **Criterion structure**: Criterion A (content) + Criterion C (format, communication, organisation) assessed in Q5 and the essay throughout. Some sessions also assess Criterion D (thinking critically) separately in the essay. When Criterion D appears, it adds a perspectives/synthesis dimension explicitly.

## Command term patterns (confirmed across 11 sessions)

| Command term | Marks | How often | Key insight |
|-------------|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Identify / State / Select** | 1–2 | Every session | One word or one fact. Writing more scores no extra marks. Over-answering wastes time. |
| **Outline** | 2 | Every session | Fact + one brief elaboration in a single sentence or two short sentences. Hit this ceiling cleanly, then move on. |
| **Explain** | 4 | Every session | WHAT + WHY + consequence. Use "because", "this means that", "as a result". One cause developed with full reasoning beats three shallow ones. |
| **Evaluate** | 8 | Every session (Q3) | Always Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2). The appraisal can appear anywhere — not just in a conclusion. |
| **Formulate** | 2 | Every session (Q4a) | RQ must be (a) clear, (b) focused (named place/group/strategy), (c) connected to the SoI. Missing any one = 1 mark, not 2. |
| **Justify** | 4 or 6 | Every session (Q4b and a later source question) | One well-developed argument with reasons. For the 6-mark version, must engage with both options/perspectives for full marks. |
| **To what extent** | 24 | Every session (final essay) | Argue a position + develop counterargument + synthesise to a conclusion. Both perspectives must be genuinely developed. |
| **Describe** | 3–4 | Occasional (N19, N22 Q6b, N20) | Detail without comprehensive reasons required. WHAT, not WHY. |
| **Analyse** | 8 | M25 only (Q6) | Both sides + analytical conclusion required. |
| **Annotate** | 2 | N20 only (Q5a) | Label a diagram with specific technical terms. |
| **List** | 2 | M22, N19, N20 | One item per mark. No explanation needed or rewarded. |

**Universal command terms** (appeared in every or nearly every session): Identify/State/Select, Outline, Explain, Evaluate, Formulate, Justify, To what extent.

**Occasional command terms**: Describe, List, Analyse, Annotate.

## Key mark scheme rules (confirmed universal across all 11 sessions)

These rules appear explicitly in almost every mark scheme:

1. **Quality over quantity — the single most repeated rule**: One well-elaborated point with reasoning ALWAYS beats three shallow points. Stated explicitly in mark schemes from N19 through M25 without exception. Examiners reward depth.

2. **Best-fit model**: Examiners award the markband that best fits overall quality. If you meet 2 of 3 requirements in a band, you still get the mark. Examiners reward what is correct and do NOT penalise incorrect points alongside correct ones.

3. **Appraisal can appear anywhere**: For every evaluate question (Q3) across all 11 sessions, the mark scheme explicitly states the appraisal/overall judgement does NOT have to be a concluding statement — it can appear anywhere in the response.

4. **Depth triggers higher markbands**: The jump from 2→3 marks or from 3→4 marks is consistently about adding "because" or "this means that." One connective phrase can be worth a mark across all developed-answer questions.

5. **Vague responses score 0 for specific questions**: For identify/state questions with a single correct answer, vague or general responses score 0. "Websites" for a source = 0. "Government" for a stakeholder = 0. Must be specific.

6. **Examples mandatory only in Q5 and the essay**: For Q1–Q4, examples are helpful but never strictly required to reach full marks (unless the question explicitly asks for "an example from your MYP studies"). For Q5 (18-mark extended task) and the final essay (24 marks), examples from MYP studies are mandatory for the upper markbands.

7. **Both sides required for some questions**: For the Q5 extended task where you must discuss strengths AND limitations of GIS (M23), or where "at least one strength and one limitation" are specified, covering only one side caps the Criterion A mark at 3–4. Similarly, the final essay needs genuinely developed perspectives on both sides for upper Criterion D marks.

8. **Specificity in formulate questions**: A research question without a named place or specific focus = maximum 1/2 marks, confirmed in all 11 sessions.

### Rules appearing in newer sessions (M23–M25) not confirmed in earlier sessions

- **Q3c specificity rule** (M24): For identify/state 1-mark sub-questions within the research design section, "websites" or "government data" alone = 0 marks. Named, specific sources only.
- **Q6 both-options cap** (M24): For justify questions where two options are presented and you must engage with both, referring to only one option caps the response at half marks (3/6).
- **Criterion C format as separate paragraphs** (consistent M21 onward): For C1 format marks on essays and extended tasks, structural elements must appear as separate paragraphs. A response without paragraph breaks scores 0 on C1 format.

## Most common exam mistakes (from all 11 mark schemes)

1. **Identify/State questions**: Writing explanations when only a fact is needed — wastes time, earns 0 extra marks. One-word or one-clause answers are correct.

2. **Explain questions**: Only describing WHAT without explaining WHY — the jump from 2→4 marks requires causal reasoning. Every explain answer needs a "because" or "as a result."

3. **Evaluate questions (Q3)**: Covering strengths OR limitations but not both — misses 3 out of 8 possible marks. Or writing the appraisal only in a final sentence rather than embedding it naturally (this is a style issue, not a marking issue — but it often results in a vague appraisal).

4. **Formulate questions**: Writing a vague research question without specifying a place, group, or strategy — caps at 1/2 marks. "How does tourism affect the environment?" = 1 mark. "How does ecotourism affect biodiversity on Table Mountain, Cape Town?" = 2 marks.

5. **Extended task (Q5) genre errors**: Writing a geography essay instead of the required genre (email, speech, podcast, blog, etc.). Examiners assess communication style (Criterion C2) separately — essay-style responses lose these marks.

6. **Essay perspectives**: Arguing one side only in the "to what extent" essay. Without a genuine counterargument and synthesis, the Criterion D mark is capped at 1–2.

7. **Essay structure**: Writing a continuous block of text without introduction/body/conclusion as SEPARATE PARAGRAPHS. Criterion C1 format marks require physical paragraph breaks — confirmed in all sessions from M21 onward.

8. **Source analysis questions**: Describing what a source shows without explaining WHY it is valuable or limited to the SPECIFIC USER described in the question. A limitation for an 80-year-old is different from a limitation for a marine biologist — the perspective matters for full marks.

9. **Identify questions (multiple answers)**: In M21, the mark scheme explicitly states that writing MORE THAN ONE answer for a 1-mark identify question = 0 marks. Be precise.

10. **Q5 content cap**: In N22 and N24, writing only ONE benefit/example when TWO are required caps the Criterion A content mark at 4/6 marks regardless of quality.

## Topics to study by priority

Based on frequency data from 11 sessions of evidence:

### Tier 1 — Study first; these appear in every exam

**Research methods** (11/11 sessions)
- Always Q3 (evaluate an action plan) and Q4 (design your own investigation)
- Q3 structure is completely fixed: Strengths (3) + Limitations (3) + Appraisal (2)
- Q4 always starts with Formulate (2) + Justify (4) + smaller sub-questions on sources and challenges
- What to know: what makes an action plan strong vs weak; how to write a focused RQ; how to evaluate primary and secondary sources; what "challenges" look like in investigations
- Practice: write 3 different RQs on 3 different topics and justify each one

### Tier 2 — Very likely to appear; high marks when present

**Natural hazards** (7/11 sessions)
- When present, carries the most marks of any topic section: typically Q5 (18 marks) + Q6–Q7 (8 marks) + Q8 essay (24 marks) = 50 marks
- Topics tested: tectonic plate boundaries and causes, primary vs secondary effects, management strategies, vulnerability, income disparities and disaster impacts, GIS tools
- Key examples to know: Haiti/Chile 2010 earthquake comparison (income and severity), Japan earthquake management, Lahaina wildfire (Hawaii), 2018 Hawaii volcanic eruption, Tonga 2022 tsunami, Philippines tectonic activity, Indonesia 2004 tsunami
- Absent in 4 consecutive sessions (N21, N22, N23, N24) — statistically due to appear

**Climate change** (7/11 sessions, major in 4 sessions)
- Major section in N20, N21, M23, N24
- Topics tested: causes (human activities), greenhouse effect, temperature trend graphs, impacts (sea level rise, coral bleaching, biodiversity loss), mitigation vs adaptation, global agreements (Paris Agreement, COPs), carbon footprint
- Source analysis skills: reading choropleth maps of vulnerability, dual-axis graphs, doughnut charts
- Key arguments: developed vs developing country divide on responsibility; mandatory vs voluntary agreements; mitigation (addresses causes) vs adaptation (addresses effects)

**Biomes** (6/11 sessions)
- When present, nearly always carries a major question (18-mark extended task in M25 and N22 Q5; multiple questions in N23 Q6–Q8)
- Topics tested: biome names and characteristics, human threats (deforestation, overgrazing, pollution, land-use change), scientific/technological innovations protecting biomes, biome-climate change connection (carbon storage), sustainable management
- Key biomes to know in detail: Tropical rainforest (Amazon), Coral/aquatic biome (Coral Triangle), Grassland/savannah
- Both harmful and protective innovations must be covered for 18-mark questions

### Tier 3 — Appears in roughly a third of sessions; worth preparing

**Globalization** (4/11 sessions — N20, M22, N22, M25)
- Topics tested: positive and negative impacts, trade flows, fast fashion (resource use, labour), aid and development, cultural exchange
- Often appears in research design section as the SoI topic (not just as the main topic section)
- Linked to food security, development, and tourism

**Tourism** (4/11 sessions — N19, M22, M23, N23)
- Topics tested: overtourism impacts, sustainable tourism management, cultural impacts, ecotourism, economic benefits vs environmental damage
- Often involves source analysis of video/infographic data + justify which management option is better
- Key examples: Galapagos Islands (both M23 and N19), Kaikōura New Zealand (N23)

### Tier 4 — Lower frequency; prepare as time allows

**Migration / Forced migration** (3/11 sessions — N21, N22, N24)
- Voluntary and forced migration, push-pull factors, economic benefits, social tensions, population structures, ageing populations
- N24 linked migration explicitly to population change and demographic structures

**Natural resources** (3/11 sessions — N19, M22, N24)
- Sustainable extraction, biodiversity loss, energy classification (renewable vs non-renewable), water management

**Urban environments** (2/11 sessions — N21, M24)
- Urban growth challenges, megacities, quality of life indicators, city planning decisions

**Rivers and coasts** (2/11 sessions — M21, M24)
- Physical processes (erosion, flooding, longshore drift), sustainable management strategies, source analysis

**Land degradation** (2/11 sessions — M21, N22)
- Causes (overgrazing, deforestation, agriculture), effects, restoration projects (Great Green Wall)

**Food security / Water security / Development** (1–2/11 sessions each)
- Lower frequency but could appear; food security linked to globalization; water scarcity (Jordan, Mekong River)

## Synthesis: what makes an A-grade response?

### For Q3 (Evaluate, 8 marks) — the most predictable question
- **Strengths**: One specific strength + why it makes the investigation more reliable/valid/representative. Named detail from the stimulus ("The 40 interviews across 4 nursing homes...") beats vague praise.
- **Limitations**: One specific limitation + WHY it undermines the investigation. Connect to what the investigation is TRYING to achieve.
- **Appraisal**: One sentence that explicitly weighs (uses "although" or "however") a strength against a limitation. Can be embedded anywhere — not only at the end.

### For Q4a (Formulate, 2 marks)
- Checklist: (1) Clear — a reader immediately understands what is being investigated; (2) Focused — names a specific place, group, or strategy; (3) Connected — references the statement of inquiry's key concept.
- Model: "How has [specific intervention] affected [specific group/indicator] in [specific place]?"

### For Q5 (Extended task, 18 marks)
- **Content (Criterion A)**: Named examples, specific data, geographic vocabulary used correctly. Two examples or two aspects where required (e.g. two biomes, two management strategies, two benefits).
- **Format (Criterion C1)**: Include ALL required structural elements of the genre. For speeches: opening statement + body + closing statement. For emails: subject title + salutation + body + sign-off. For essays: intro + body + conclusion as SEPARATE PARAGRAPHS.
- **Language (Criterion C2)**: Write in the genre's natural register. Speeches and presentations use first-person, rhetorical questions, emotive vocabulary, audience-directed language. Emails use formal professional language. Reports use subheadings and formal register. Blog posts use informal first-person with engaging language.
- **Organisation (Criterion C3)**: Ideas build on each other logically. Use transitions ("Furthermore", "However", "As a result", "In contrast") within and between paragraphs.

### For the final essay (24 marks)
- **Criterion A (content)**: Named examples with unpacked detail. "The 2010 Haiti earthquake killed 200,000+ people due to poor building standards, whereas the same-year Chile earthquake of similar magnitude killed far fewer because..." — this is content that earns marks, not just "earthquakes kill more in poor countries."
- **Criterion D (perspectives/synthesis)**: Two genuine perspectives, each developed fully. A conclusion that synthesises both sides and makes a specific judgement. The synthesis can appear in the body, not only the conclusion.
- **Criterion C1 (format)**: Three separate paragraphs: introduction, main body, conclusion. This is marked mechanically — if the paragraphs are not visually separate, 0 marks on C1.
- **Criterion C2/C3 (language and organisation)**: Formal essay register throughout. Transitions that show cause-and-effect relationships. Ideas that build on each other, not a list.

### The universal A-grade move across all question types
> **State the thing → connect it to the specific context → explain the consequence → link to the broader principle.**
> This four-step chain is what separates 3-mark responses from 4-mark responses, and 6-mark responses from 8-mark responses, across all sessions.
