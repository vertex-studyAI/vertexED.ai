# MYP Biology Command Terms Reference

## Why Command Terms Matter

Command terms are the action words in exam questions that tell you exactly what the examiner wants. Understanding what each term requires is critical for scoring full marks. The difference between "State" and "Explain" can be 3+ marks, and using the wrong approach will cost you points even if your biology knowledge is correct.

**Key principle:** The command term dictates the depth and structure of your answer, not just the content.

---

## MYP Biology Assessment Criteria

MYP Biology exams are marked according to four criteria:

### Criterion A: Knowing and Understanding
Tests factual recall and conceptual understanding of biology content.

### Criterion B: Inquiring and Designing
Tests your ability to design investigations, identify variables, formulate hypotheses, and plan methods.

### Criterion C: Processing and Evaluating
Tests data analysis, graph plotting, calculations, pattern identification, and evaluation of results.

### Criterion D: Reflecting on the Impacts of Science
Tests your ability to discuss real-world applications, ethical considerations, and societal implications of biology.

## Command Terms: Official Definitions & Examiner Expectations

### State (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give a specific name, value, or brief answer without explanation.

**What examiners want:**
- A short, direct answer (often 1–5 words)
- No justification or reasoning required
- Specific factual recall

**What to avoid:**
- Writing long explanations (wastes time, earns no extra marks)
- Being vague or general when a specific term is needed
- Confusing "State" with "Explain"

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q1a):**
"State the name of one other hinge joint in the human body."
Mark scheme: Knee. Accept: ankle, knuckle. [1 mark]

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q1h):**
"State the name of this regulating process [blood sugar, temperature and water balance]."
Mark scheme: Homeostasis. [1 mark]

---

### Identify (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Recognize and name or select something from given information, data, graphs, or diagrams.

**What examiners want:**
- Pick out specific information from the stimulus material
- Name the item clearly
- Often paired with "Justify" for higher marks

**What to avoid:**
- Describing instead of naming
- Listing multiple options when only one is asked for
- Not using the data provided when it is the source for the answer

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q4b):**
"Identify the variables in this investigation."
- Independent variable: Light intensity [1]
- Dependent variable: Length / height of plant / stem [1]. Do not accept "plant growth" alone.
- Control variables (any 2): temperature of container / same type of plant / same growth medium / humidity / same volume of water [2]

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q4d):**
"Identify the type of data collected for the independent variable."
Mark scheme: Categorical data / qualitative data / discrete data / colour is not continuous. [1 mark]

---

### Select (1 mark typically)
**Definition:** Choose the correct answer from given options (multiple choice, drag-and-drop, dropdown).

**What examiners want:**
- Choose the right option from a provided list
- Quick factual check — no explanation needed unless paired with Justify

**What to avoid:**
- Not reading all options carefully
- Overthinking; these are usually direct recall questions
- Selecting more than one answer when only one is required

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q1a):**
"Select the term that is not another kingdom used to classify living things."
Options: Bacteria / Fungi / Plant / Virus
Mark scheme: Virus. [1 mark]

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q8a):**
"Select the name of the process by which water evaporates from plant leaves."
Options: osmosis / photosynthesis / respiration / transpiration
Mark scheme: Transpiration. [1 mark]

---

### Outline (2–3 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give a brief account or summary, including key points but without extensive detail.

**What examiners want:**
- Main points clearly stated
- Logical sequence if describing a process
- More than "State" but less than "Explain"
- Usually 2–3 distinct points

**What to avoid:**
- Writing only one point when multiple are required
- Going into excessive mechanistic detail (save that for "Explain")
- Not structuring points clearly

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q2a):**
"Outline how oxygen diffuses into the blood from the alveoli."
- From high concentration (in the lungs) to low concentration (in the blood) / down a concentration gradient [1]
- Through the (alveolar) membrane / wall / capillary [1]

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q1d):**
"Outline one similarity and one difference between aerobic and anaerobic cellular respiration."
- Similarity: both produce energy / ATP; both use glucose [1]
- Difference: aerobic requires oxygen / anaerobic does not; waste products differ (water and CO₂ vs lactic acid) [1]

---

### Describe (2–4 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give a detailed account including characteristics, features, or the sequence of events.

**What examiners want:**
- Clear description of what is happening, step by step if a process
- Include observable features or changes
- More detail than "Outline," but may not require full causal reasoning
- Reference to stimulus material (diagrams, graphs) where provided

**What to avoid:**
- Confusing with "Explain" — description tells WHAT, explanation tells WHY
- Skipping observable details visible in the stimulus
- Not using sequence language when describing a process

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q2b):**
"Describe how estrogen and progesterone work together to prepare the body for pregnancy."
- Estrogen thickens (uterus) lining [1]
- Progesterone maintains (uterus) lining [1]
- So that a fertilized egg / zygote can implant or survive [1]

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q2c):**
"Describe why bacteria on salted meat have changed their form."
- (Bacteria has) decreased in volume / shrunk / shrivelled [1]
- Water has left / (bacteria is) dehydrated [1]
- By osmosis [1]

---

### Explain (3–5 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give a detailed account of causes, reasons, or mechanisms; make relationships clear.

**What examiners want:**
- Cause-and-effect relationships with biological reasoning
- Clear logical sequence using linking language: "because," "therefore," "so," "which leads to"
- Link observations to underlying biology concepts
- Use of data from graphs or tables where provided

**What to avoid:**
- Just describing WHAT happens without explaining WHY
- Not using data or figures from the stimulus when it is provided
- Stating the obvious without scientific mechanism

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q2c):**
"Use data from the graph to explain the changes seen when exercising (3–10 min)."
- Increased pulse rate [1 — must reference data]
- Increased breathing rate [1 — must reference data]
- One correct data value used (e.g. "pulse rate increased from 75 to 120 bpm") [1]
- Faster cellular respiration / more energy released [1]
- Supplies cells/muscles with more O₂ / removes CO₂ faster [1]

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q5c):**
"Explain how the movement of water causes the plant fibres to increase in size as humidity increases."
- Any two increases in size: cells / vacuoles / cell walls / gaps / lamella [2]
- Water enters the fibres / cells by osmosis / diffusion [1]
- Larger difference between cell and environment / more rapid entry of water [1]

---

### Suggest (1–3 marks typically)
**Definition:** Propose a solution, hypothesis, or explanation using scientific knowledge. The answer may not be directly found in the sources — reasonable application of biology is required.

**What examiners want:**
- Reasonable scientific reasoning applied to a new or unfamiliar context
- Creative but scientifically sound thinking
- Usually accepts multiple valid answers (WTTE)

**What to avoid:**
- Giving unrealistic or non-scientific answers
- Being too vague ("it helps the organism" — NGE)
- Repeating information already given in the question

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q4f):**
"Suggest why the average stem lengths at 90% and 100% light intensity are the same."
Mark scheme: Maximum growth achieved at 90% / light is no longer a limiting factor / something else is a limiting factor. WTTE. [1 mark]

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q2e):**
"Suggest why synthetic HGH must be injected rather than taken in tablet form."
- Goes directly into the blood [1]
- Will not be digested or denatured [1]

---

### Discuss (3–6 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give a balanced account, presenting different perspectives, arguments, or factors.

**What examiners want:**
- Multiple viewpoints or factors
- Evidence or reasoning for each point
- Consideration of different outcomes, pros and cons, or contrasting examples
- Structured, balanced argument

**What to avoid:**
- Only giving one side of the argument
- Not using provided data, graphs, or tables as evidence
- Lack of structure or balance between the points made

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q1d):**
"Discuss the differences between aerobic and anaerobic cellular respiration."
- Aerobic requires oxygen and anaerobic does not [1]
- Aerobic releases more energy / ATP than anaerobic [1]
- Aerobic produces water (and CO₂) and anaerobic produces lactic acid [1]

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q6d):**
"Use data from the graph to discuss whether the results support the hypothesis."
- Hypothesis is valid / partially valid as extension increases from 0–2% / 0–5% NaOH [1]
- Between 2 or 5–10% the extension decreases; above 10% no clear pattern despite change in concentration [1]

---

### Evaluate (4–6 marks typically)
**Definition:** Make a judgment based on criteria; weigh up evidence and reach a conclusion.

**What examiners want:**
- Assessment of strengths and weaknesses or competing options
- Specific evidence cited in support of each assessment
- A clear conclusion with justification
- Consideration of multiple factors

**What to avoid:**
- Not providing a clear judgment or conclusion
- Ignoring key evidence from the stimulus
- One-sided evaluation that fails to consider alternatives

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q3g):**
"Outline whether the company's decision to run the chamber at 85% light intensity is supported by data."
- 85% allows maximum germination for Species Z / Species Z has optimum germination below 85% [1]
- Don't know optimum for Species Y (between 80 and 90) / 85% was not tested / close to optimum for Species Y AND uses less energy / lowers costs [1]

---

### Justify (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion.

**What examiners want:**
- Clear reasoning or evidence to back up a stated answer
- Often follows "Select" or "Identify" — explain WHY your choice is correct
- Use of data, scientific principles, or logical argument

**What to avoid:**
- Justifying the wrong choice (if paired with Select, wrong selection = 0 for both marks)
- Not using the data or evidence provided
- Circular reasoning ("it's correct because it is")

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q1d):**
"Using the DNA sequences, justify which two species are most closely related."
Mark scheme: Lion and Jaguar — sequences most similar (8 out of 10 bases are the same). [1 mark]

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q4c):**
"Justify the student's prediction [more light → longer stem] using scientific reasoning."
- Increase in light causes an increase in photosynthesis [1]
- Produces more glucose which is used for growth [1]

---

### Calculate (1–3 marks typically)
**Definition:** Obtain a numerical answer, showing the working.

**What examiners want:**
- Correct formula or mathematical method
- Clear working shown step by step
- Final answer with correct units and appropriate significant figures
- ECF (Error Carried Forward) often applies

**What to avoid:**
- Not showing working — you lose method marks even if the answer is correct
- Wrong or missing units
- Rounding errors or inconsistent significant figures compared to the data

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q4b):**
"Calculate the missing average soil pH for Blue hydrangea. Show your working."
- Working: (5.21 + 5.54 + 5.32) / 3 = 5.356... [1]
- Answer: 5.36 (to 3 sig figs) [1]. Award 2 marks if 5.36 seen directly.

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q4d):**
"Measure the stem length for Trial 4 at 30% light intensity and calculate the average."
- Measurement: 45 mm [1]
- Average: 42 mm [1]. ECF applied.

---

### Plot / Present (3–6 marks typically)
**Definition:** Produce a graph, chart, or data display from given data.

**What examiners want:**
- Axes labelled with variable names AND units
- Appropriate, even scale (equal increments; starting at 0 where appropriate)
- Points plotted accurately (within ±0.5 grid square)
- Line of best fit or bars drawn correctly as appropriate
- Title and key if needed

**What to avoid:**
- Incorrect or missing axis labels or units
- Uneven scale intervals
- Points plotted inaccurately
- Joining scatter points that should not be joined
- Missing title or key where required

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q4c):**
"Plot the average soil pH data in a bar graph."
- y axis labelled "(average) soil pH" [1]
- x axis labelled "colour (of flower)" [1]
- Equal intervals on y axis starting at zero [1]
- Two bars correctly plotted [1]
- All four bars correctly plotted [1]. ECF from calculated mean.

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q5b):**
"Plot a graph to show the effect of temperature on increase in average stem length."
- y axis scale has even increments [1]
- Both axes labelled with variable name and unit [1]
- Four or more data points plotted correctly [1]

---

### Formulate (2–3 marks typically)
**Definition:** Create or construct a research question or hypothesis.

**What examiners want:**
- Research question: must include both independent variable and dependent variable, phrased as a question
- Hypothesis: "If [IV change], then [DV change], because [scientific reasoning]"
- Clear link between variables
- Testable and falsifiable

**What to avoid:**
- Missing IV or DV in research question
- No scientific reasoning in hypothesis (direction of change alone is not enough)
- Wrong format — hypothesis must not be a question

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q4a):**
"Formulate a research question for this investigation [humidity vs fibre strength]."
Mark scheme: How does the humidity affect the mass needed to break the fibre? [1 mark] — must include IV (humidity) and DV (mass/breaking point).

---

### Design (15–19 marks typically — extended Criterion B response)
**Definition:** Plan or create a detailed experimental investigation.

**What examiners want (holistic marking across multiple sub-criteria):**

#### Variables (up to 4 marks):
- IV: clearly stated and specific to the context. Do NOT accept vague terms (e.g., "pH" alone — must be "soil pH")
- DV: clearly stated and measurable. Do NOT accept "plant growth alone" — must specify height, mass, number of leaves, etc.
- Two CVs: each qualified and specific. Do NOT accept "keeping equipment the same" or unqualified "temperature"

#### Hypothesis (required in some papers):
- Testable prediction linking IV and DV with direction of change
- Scientific reasoning provided

#### Equipment (up to 2 marks):
- Appropriate and specific to the investigation
- Linked to measuring the DV and controlling CVs
- Do NOT re-state equipment already given in the question

#### Method (up to 4 marks):
- How to manipulate the IV (explicit values or range)
- How to measure the DV (technique and units)
- How to control CVs
- Sufficiently detailed to be followed by another person

#### Data Collection (up to 3 marks):
- Minimum 5 values/variations of the IV (stated explicitly)
- Minimum 3 repeats/trials
- Plan to calculate averages

#### Safety (up to 2 marks):
- Specific to THIS investigation (linked to an identified hazard)
- Justified — not generic ("wear a mask", "tie hair back" are NOT acceptable unless genuinely relevant)

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q6):**
"Design an investigation to test how changing soil pH affects plant growth."
- IV: Soil pH. Do NOT accept pH alone, or pH of solutions.
- DV: Length / height / mass of plant. Do NOT accept "plant growth alone."
- CVs must be qualified: "temperature of growing container," "light intensity," not "temperature" or "light" alone.
- Method must state how to vary the IV, how to measure DV, and how to maintain CVs.
- Data: at least 5 pH values (between 3.5 and 10), at least 3 trials, plans to calculate averages.
- Safety: must be linked to acid/alkaline solutions specifically.

**Common mistakes:**
- Vague or reversed IV/DV
- CVs not qualified or not realistic
- Method lacks explicit IV values
- Safety considerations too general
- Missing or weak hypothesis

---

### Predict (2–4 marks typically)
**Definition:** State an expected outcome based on given information or scientific principles.

**What examiners want:**
- Logical prediction using evidence, trends, or biological concepts
- Extension of observed patterns
- Reasoning to support the prediction

**What to avoid:**
- Guessing without scientific basis
- Not explaining the reasoning behind the prediction
- Ignoring trends visible in provided data or graphs

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q7e — Suggest used for prediction-type):**
"Suggest two impacts of climate change on bird migration patterns."
- Breeding sites move (further north) / migration happens at different times [1]
- Food not available at the same time each year / some may not need to migrate anymore [1]

---

### Compare and Contrast (2–4 marks typically)
**Definition:** Give an account of similarities AND differences between two or more items.

**What examiners want:**
- At least one clear similarity with evidence
- At least one clear difference with evidence
- Both items addressed for each point (not just describing one)

**What to avoid:**
- Describing only one item
- Listing similarities without differences or vice versa
- Vague comparisons without specific reference to data

**Mark scheme example (M25, Q3c):**
"Identify a similarity and a difference in germination between species A and species B."
- Similarity: both species had seeds that germinated / both increase with light intensity / maximum germination at 100% for both. ORA. [1]
- Difference: always more seeds germinated for A than B at all intensities / species B cannot germinate without light / all seeds germinated at 100% for A only. [1]

---

### Deduce (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Reach a conclusion from information provided using reasoning.

**What examiners want:**
- Logical reasoning from data, diagrams, or patterns
- Clear conclusion stated explicitly

**What to avoid:**
- Guessing instead of reasoning from the evidence
- Not stating the conclusion clearly

**Mark scheme example (M23, Q3e):**
"State the relationship between lactose tolerance and milk consumption."
Mark scheme: The higher the % lactose tolerance, the higher the milk consumption. ORA. [1 mark]

---

### Determine (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Obtain the only possible answer from data or a calculation.

**What examiners want:**
- A definitive answer drawn from the evidence
- Clear reasoning or working shown

**What to avoid:**
- Giving a range when a specific answer is required
- Not showing how the answer was obtained

**Mark scheme example (N24, Q5e):**
"Conclude the optimum pH for Zoysia grass growth."
- pH 7 [1]
- This is the pH giving the most blades and the greatest (average) height [1]

---

### Annotate (1–2 marks typically)
**Definition:** Add brief notes or labels to a diagram or image.

**What examiners want:**
- Labels pointing to specific structures
- Brief, accurate descriptions at each label point
- Linked to the correct location in the diagram

**What to avoid:**
- Labels not connected to the correct structure
- Vague or incorrect annotations
- Missing the structures specified in the question

---

## Mark Scheme Conventions

### WTTE — Words To That Effect
The mark scheme answer is a model — synonyms, paraphrases, and equivalent phrasing are accepted provided the biological meaning is the same.

**Example (M25, Q3b):**
"Suggest a reason for conducting the experiment at 0% light intensity."
MS: "Used as a control experiment." WTTE accepted: "To see if plants germinate without light." Both score 1 mark.

**Practical implication:** Write clearly and accurately — do not try to memorise exact MS wording. Correct biology expressed in your own words is fully credited.

---

### ORA — Or Reverse Argument
The logically reversed statement is accepted as an alternative answer.

**Example (M23, Q3e):**
"State the relationship between lactose tolerance and milk consumption."
MS: "The higher the % lactose tolerance, the higher the milk consumption."
ORA: "The lower the % lactose tolerance, the lower the milk consumption." Both are credited.

---

### ECF — Error Carried Forward
If a student makes an error in an early part of a question, they can still earn marks in later parts if their subsequent working is correct based on their (incorrect) earlier answer.

**Example (N24, Q4b–4c):**
Student calculates average pH as 5.40 instead of 5.36.
- Loses the calculation mark in Q4b.
- BUT if they correctly plot 5.40 on the bar graph in Q4c, they earn the plotting mark. ECF is applied.

**Practical implication:** Always show your working and continue with your answer even if you are unsure of an earlier result. You can still earn the remaining marks.

---

### CON — Contradiction
If a student writes a correct answer and then immediately contradicts it with an incorrect statement, the mark is NOT awarded.

**Example (M23, Q3c, notes):**
"State the meaning of phenotype."
Student writes: "The observable characteristics of an organism. Phenotype is the same as genotype."
Result: 0 marks. The second sentence directly contradicts the first (CON).

**Practical implication:** Do not over-write answers. Once you have stated a correct point, stop. Adding incorrect information can cost you the mark.

---

### NGE — Not Good Enough
The answer is partially correct but lacks the required precision or specificity to be awarded the mark.

**Example:**
"Explain why high SA:V ratio is beneficial to a cell."
Student writes: "It has a high ratio." This is NGE — the student has just restated the question rather than explaining the benefit.

**Practical implication:** Always complete the thought. "The high SA:V ratio means more surface area is available for diffusion relative to cell volume, so materials move in and out faster" is sufficient.

---

### Max [N]
Only N marks are available from a list of bullets, even if the student lists more correct points.

**Example (M25, Q2e):**
"Suggest two additional pieces of information to increase validity of blood sample comparison [max 2]."
MS accepts: age / fitness / stress / medical history / medication / ethnicity.
If the student lists 5 correct items, only the first 2 are credited.

**Practical implication:** Read the question carefully. If it says "suggest two," write exactly two answers. More does not earn more marks.

---

### Sequential marks (mp2 requires mp1)
Some questions specify that a later marking point can only be awarded if an earlier marking point has already been earned.

**Example (N24, Q3b):**
"Discuss if front limb structure is a good characteristic to identify mammals."
MS note: "Only award mark 3 if another mark is awarded."
Mark 3 (concluding statement) depends on earning at least one earlier mark.

**Example (N24, Q5d — Analyse):**
MS note: "Do not award mark 3 if mark 2 is not awarded."

**Practical implication:** Build your answer sequentially. State the observation first, then explain, then conclude. Do not jump straight to conclusions.

---

### "Do not accept" rules
The mark scheme explicitly lists responses that must NOT be credited, even if they seem reasonable.

**Common "Do not accept" rules across all sessions:**

| Context | Do NOT accept | Reason |
|---------|--------------|--------|
| DV in design questions | "Plant growth" alone | Must specify what is measured: height, mass, leaf count, etc. |
| IV in design questions | "pH" alone | Must specify context: "soil pH" |
| CVs in design questions | "Keeping equipment the same" | Not a scientific variable |
| Safety in design | Generic precautions (e.g. "wear a mask") unless genuinely relevant | Not specific to the investigation hazard |
| Anaerobic respiration (human) | Ethanol as a product | Lactic acid only in human cells |
| Microorganism questions | "Kills" the microorganism | Cooling only reduces / slows activity |
| Q stems already given | Restating information given in the question | No new information added |
| Genetics | "Gene" when "allele" is required | CON if incorrect terminology used |

## Criterion B Markband Descriptors (Inquiring and Designing)

Criterion B design questions are marked holistically using markbands. The mark awarded reflects the overall quality of the response against each sub-criterion, not a tally of individual points.

### Variables sub-criterion (typically up to 4 marks)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Explicitly states an appropriate IV or DV (not both) |
| 2 | States both IV and DV appropriately |
| 3 | States IV, DV, and one qualified CV |
| 4 | States IV, DV, and two qualified CVs |

### Method sub-criterion (typically up to 4 marks)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Method is linked to IV or DV but detail is insufficient to collect relevant data |
| 2 | Method is linked to both IV and DV but is incomplete |
| 3 | Method is linked to IV and DV and could be followed by another person |
| 4 | Method linked to IV and DV, includes detail on CVs, and could be repeated reliably |

### Data collection sub-criterion (typically up to 3–4 marks)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Any reference to different variations of the IV |
| 2 | At least 5 variations of the IV or at least 3 trials |
| 3 | At least 5 values of the IV AND at least 3 trials |
| 4 | At least 5 stated IV values AND at least 3 trials AND plans to calculate averages |

### Safety sub-criterion (typically up to 2 marks)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Relevant safety precaution stated (linked to the investigation) |
| 2 | Relevant safety precaution with justification linked to a specific hazard |

---

## Criterion D Markband Descriptors (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science)

Criterion D extended responses are marked holistically across multiple sub-sections. Each sub-section has its own markband, usually 1–4 marks.

### Generic sub-section markband (used in N24 Q8, M25 Q8, M23 Q9)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | A simple statement relevant to the sub-topic |
| 2 | Two statements, OR one statement with further support |
| 3 | Two statements with further support for one of them |
| 4 | Two statements with further support for both of them |

### Concluding appraisal (typically 2 marks)

| Mark | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | A concluding opinion is stated |
| 2 | A concluding appraisal with justification that includes specific detail |

**Key rules for Criterion D:**
- Every bullet point in the question must be addressed. Missing any section caps your total mark for that section.
- Statements alone (1 mark) are not sufficient for high marks — each point needs support, reasoning, or an example.
- Specific detail scores over general claims: "Reduces CO₂ by 61% compared to flight" beats "is better for the environment."
- The concluding appraisal must be justified — a bare opinion ("I think X is best") earns only 1 mark.

## Common Command Term Mistakes by Criteria

### Criterion A (Knowledge and Understanding)
**Error:** Confusing "State" with "Explain."
- State = short answer, no reasoning required
- Explain = detailed reasoning with cause-and-effect chain

**Error:** Being vague when specificity is required.
- "State a characteristic of living organisms" → "Growth" scores. "They do things" does not.

**Error:** Using the wrong technical term.
- "Gene" instead of "allele" in a genetics question triggers CON and scores 0.

### Criterion B (Inquiring and Designing)
**Error:** Not qualifying variables.
- "Temperature" scores 0 as a CV → "Temperature of the growing container" scores 1
- "Light" scores 0 → "Light intensity (in lux)" scores 1

**Error:** Reversed IV and DV.
- IV = what YOU deliberately change
- DV = what you MEASURE as a result of the change

**Error:** Vague DV.
- "Plant growth" scores 0 → "Height of the plant stem in mm" scores 1

**Error:** Generic safety.
- "Wear gloves" scores 0 unless genuinely relevant to the investigation hazard
- "Wear protective gloves when handling acidic soil solution to avoid skin irritation" scores 1

### Criterion C (Processing and Evaluating)
**Error:** Not showing working for calculations.
- Always show your working, even for simple calculations — you earn method marks even if the final answer is wrong

**Error:** Missing units or wrong significant figures.
- Match the sig figs used in the data table
- Always include units on calculated answers and graph axes

**Error:** Irregular axis scale on graphs.
- Intervals must be equal and evenly spaced — not e.g. 0, 2, 5, 10, 20

### Criterion D (Reflecting on Impacts)
**Error:** One-sided discussion.
- "Discuss and Evaluate" requires multiple perspectives — pros AND cons, or at least two different considerations

**Error:** No concluding appraisal.
- A missing conclusion caps the entire Criterion D response

**Error:** Generic statements.
- "It's good for the environment" scores 0
- "Green roofs reduce urban flooding by absorbing rainfall, reducing strain on drainage systems" scores 1

## Quick Reference: Marks vs. Depth

| Marks | Typical depth | Example command terms |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Single fact, value, or name | State, Select, Identify (simple), Justify (simple) |
| 2 | Two points OR one point + justification | State (x2), Outline, Suggest, Compare (simple) |
| 3 | Three points OR a sequence of reasoning | Outline, Describe, Explain (simple), Discuss (simple) |
| 4–6 | Detailed explanation OR multiple balanced factors | Explain, Discuss, Plot, Evaluate, Predict |
| 7–10 | Complex multi-part question | Multiple sub-questions with varied command terms |
| 12–19 | Extended response — multiple sections assessed independently | Design (Criterion B), Discuss and Evaluate (Criterion D) |

## Examiner's General Marking Instructions

1. **Mark positively:** Award marks for what the student knows and can do. Do not deduct marks for incorrect information unless it creates a direct contradiction (CON).

2. **Each marking point on a separate line:** Each mp is worth 1 mark unless stated otherwise. A tick is placed at the exact point in the response where the mark is earned.

3. **WTTE applies throughout:** Accept equivalently phrased answers unless the meaning is changed or contradicted.

4. **"And" vs. "or" in mark schemes:**
   - **"and" in bold** = both parts required for the mark to be awarded
   - **"or" in bold** = alternative acceptable answers (either alone scores)

5. **Holistic marking for extended responses:** Questions using markbands are marked on best-fit — the overall quality is matched to the nearest descriptor. Do not add up sub-points mechanically.

## Criterion-Specific Tips

### Criterion A Questions
- Focus on accuracy and correct scientific terminology
- Recall facts precisely — the mark scheme usually requires a specific term
- Do not over-explain for "State" or "Identify" questions

### Criterion B Questions (Variables and Design)
- Always qualify your variables (be context-specific)
- Think: "Could someone else follow this method exactly and get the same results?"
- State at least 5 specific IV values and plan for 3 repeats
- Safety must name the specific hazard and explain why it is a risk

### Criterion C Questions (Data and Graphs)
- Check units and significant figures against the data table before answering
- Label all graph axes with variable name AND unit
- Use a ruler for drawing bars and lines
- Show all working for calculations, even simple ones
- Apply ECF — always continue answering even if you made an earlier error

### Criterion D Questions (Impacts)
- Think beyond the biology: ethics, economics, politics, environment
- Use specific examples and data from the stimulus material
- Balance your discussion — address at least two different perspectives per sub-section
- Always include a concluding appraisal with justification and specific detail

## Final Tips for Exam Success

1. **Read the command term first** — it tells you the structure and depth required before you think about the biology content.

2. **Check the marks** — they directly indicate how many distinct points you need to make.

3. **Use the stimulus material** — diagrams, graphs, and tables are provided for a reason. Reference them explicitly with data values.

4. **Manage your time** — do not write a 10-line answer for a 1-mark "State" question.

5. **For extended responses**, address every bullet point in the question. A missing section caps your mark for that sub-criterion.

6. **Show your working** for all calculations, even if you are confident the answer is correct.

7. **Use scientific terminology** correctly and consistently — incorrect use of a key term (e.g. gene vs. allele) can result in CON and zero marks for that question.

8. **Structure multi-mark answers** with clear bullet points or numbered steps — this makes it easy for examiners to award each marking point.

9. **Do not contradict yourself** — adding an incorrect second sentence after a correct first sentence scores zero (CON).

10. **For Criterion D**, your conclusion must be justified with specific detail, not just a statement of opinion.

**Remember:** The command term is your roadmap. Follow it precisely, and you will give examiners exactly what they are looking for.
