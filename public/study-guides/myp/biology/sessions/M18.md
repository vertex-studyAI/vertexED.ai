### Question 8 (5 marks) — Terrace Farming vs Vertical Farming

**Command term:** Compare and contrast / Justify

**Stimulus:** Video/images showing terrace farming (making steps at right angles to the steepest slope, cut out on the uphill side, uses waste soil as a bank on the downhill side; helps reduce soil erosion and flooding by absorbing heavy rainfall; problem: ground can absorb too much rainwater leading to saturation; unmaintained terraces can lead to mudslides and increased soil erosion, particularly in sandy soils or on extremely steep terrain) and vertical farming (growing crops on several floors or using the outside walls of buildings; all waste water is recirculated through the irrigation system).

Informational text: "For many thousands of years, humans have been changing their natural environment to make it easier or more efficient to farm... Terrace farming involves making steps at right angles to the steepest slope consisting of a channel dug out on the uphill side. The waste soil is used to form a bank on the downhill side. Terrace farming allows the use of land that is too hilly or steep for conventional farming methods. It helps to reduce soil erosion and flooding by absorbing heavy rainfall. In some situations, the soil quality is reduced because nutrients are leached from the soil. If terracing is not maintained properly, the ground can absorb too much rainwater leading to saturation. Unmaintained terraces can lead to mudslides and increased soil erosion, particularly in sandy soils or on extremely steep terrains."

**Question:** Compare and contrast how the needs of the crops have been met in landscape terracing and vertical farming. Justify your answers.

**Mark scheme — any five points from the following list (Criterion D):**

**Similarities (must be explicitly linked):**
- Both types of farming maximize space for growing crops
- Both types of farming improve light available for crop growth
- Both types of farming improve the water supply to crops (Accept references to flooding or drainage only when linked to plants or crops)

**Differences:**
- *Light:* terracing relies on natural light *and* light in vertical farming can be controlled
- *Water:* terracing relies on climate *or* is not controlled *and* vertical farming reuses waste water *or* is controlled
- *Soil:* terracing reduces soil erosion *and* vertical farming has no soil erosion
- *Nutrients:* terracing relies on nutrients in soil *or* nutrients can be depleted *and* nutrients can be controlled in vertical farming

**Total: 5 marks (Criterion D)**

## Key Mark Scheme Patterns

1. **WTTE (Words to That Effect):** Used throughout — accept synonymous phrasing, not just exact wording.
2. **ORA (Or Reverse Argument):** Accepted where the converse argument is scientifically valid.
3. **"Do not accept 'gene'"** — Question 1d specifically requires "allele" or correct genotype notation.
4. **"Do not accept 'environment'"** — Question 1c requires specific reference to colour/lichen/bark.
5. **Criterion A** (Knowledge and Understanding): Qs 1a, 1b, 1c, 1d, 2a, 2b, 3a, 3b, 3c, 3d, 5a, 7a, 7b
6. **Criterion B** (Inquiries): Qs 4a, 4b, 4c, 6
7. **Criterion C** (Processing): Qs 4d, 5b, 5c, 5d, 5e, 5f, 5g
8. **Criterion D** (Reflection): Qs 1d (part), 7b (part), 8, 9
9. **Linked marking:** In 5f, the justification mark is only awarded if correctly linked to the stated improvement.
10. **Multiple-select questions:** Q7a — 2 marks only if all four correct factors *and no others* are selected.
11. **Research question format (4b):** Must include the independent variable (pH) AND the dependent variable (rate/time of colour change); "Why" questions not accepted.
12. **Hypothesis validity (5e):** Partial validity is expected — the hypothesis holds at low concentrations but not at high concentrations due to enzyme saturation (all active sites occupied).
13. **Markband design questions (Q6, Q9):** Holistic rubric assessing multiple components; scores are not summed per row but judged against overall descriptors.
