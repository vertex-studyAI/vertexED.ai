### Q1c (2 marks)

**Topic**: Ecology / Food webs
**Full question**: Identify a food chain including a caterpillar and four other organisms.
**Stimulus**: Food web diagram showing: Plants, Caterpillar, Field mouse, Pied flycatcher, Tawny owl, Weasel, Frog, Dragonfly — with directional arrows showing feeding relationships. Drag-and-drop response: five boxes in a horizontal chain with arrows between them.
**MS note**: Mark scheme unavailable. Any valid 5-organism chain passing through Caterpillar drawn from the food web. e.g. Plants → Caterpillar → Pied flycatcher → Weasel → Tawny owl. Award 1 mark per valid sequential pair, max 2.

---

### Q1d (2 marks)

**Command term**: Suggest
**Topic**: Ecology / Predator-prey relationships / Adaptation
**Full question**: Pied flycatchers mainly eat insects, including caterpillars and adult butterflies. Their eggs hatch at the time in the year at which most caterpillars are present in the ecosystem. Suggest two reasons why pied flycatchers' eggs hatch at the same time as the peak in caterpillar population.
**Stimulus**: Text description.
**MS note**: Mark scheme unavailable. Likely accepted answers: (1) ensures maximum food/energy availability for newly hatched chicks; (2) reduces competition for food with adult birds / improves survival/growth rate of chicks; (3) evolutionary advantage — individuals whose eggs hatch at peak food time have greater reproductive success.

---

### Q1e (3 marks)

**Command term**: Suggest
**Topic**: Ecology / Climate change / Population dynamics
**Full question**: Over the past 20 years, the timing of the peak in caterpillar population has changed. It is now earlier in the year, as shown in the graph below. Some scientists suggest that the timing of the peak has moved because of climate change. Suggest why climate change may have caused the peak in caterpillar population to move earlier in the year.
**Stimulus**: Line graph — two bell curves showing population size (y-axis) over time March–May (x-axis). Orange curve labelled "2000" peaks in late April; blue curve labelled "2020" peaks in mid-March/early April, shifted ~2–3 weeks earlier.
**MS note**: Mark scheme unavailable. Likely accepted answers: warmer temperatures earlier in spring cause earlier leaf burst/plant growth; caterpillars hatch earlier in response to warmer conditions/earlier food availability; temperature triggers developmental timing; climate change causing earlier spring overall.

---

### Q1f (4 marks)

**Command term**: Predict
**Topic**: Ecology / Food webs / Climate change impacts
**Full question**: Pied flycatchers migrate from Africa to Europe to reproduce every spring. The timing of pied flycatchers' migration has not changed, even though the timing for the peak in caterpillar population has changed. Use the food web to predict the consequence of this mismatch in timing on the forest ecosystem.
**Stimulus**: Same food web diagram from Q1c (provided again alongside a text-entry box).
**MS note**: Mark scheme unavailable. Extended response expected. Likely chain of reasoning: pied flycatcher population decreases (insufficient food for chicks) → organisms preyed upon by pied flycatchers (caterpillars, other insects) increase in number → knock-on effects up and down the food web (e.g. plants decrease due to more caterpillars; tawny owls / weasels that fed on pied flycatchers decrease). Accept any logical cascade with evidence from the food web diagram.

**Context stimulus**: Transport is responsible for about 25% of the carbon dioxide (CO₂) released globally each year. In wealthier countries, where people travel frequently, transport can have a big impact on an individual's carbon footprint. - a description of how the proposed route may result in a reduced carbon footprint for the region
