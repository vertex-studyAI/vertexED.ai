## 3. Criterion Patterns

### Criterion A — Knowing & Understanding
**What it tests**: Factual knowledge, definitions, application of biological concepts to unfamiliar stimuli.

**Typical marks**: 20–30 marks across 4–8 sub-questions, worth 1–6 marks each.

**Common command terms**:
- "State" / "Identify" (1–2 marks): literal recall, no explanation needed
- "Define" (1–2 marks): precise textbook definition
- "Outline" (2–3 marks): brief account covering main points
- "Explain" (3–5 marks): mechanism + reasons + direction of effect
- "Describe" (2–4 marks): what is happening, not why

**Mark scheme for Criterion A**:
- Point-scored: each accepted point = 1 mark up to the stated maximum
- WTTE applies: synonyms and paraphrases accepted
- Contradiction rule: if a student writes a correct point then contradicts it, the mark is NOT awarded
- ECF: if a student uses a wrong value from part (a) correctly in part (b), carry-forward credit given

**Low vs high band distinguishers**:
- Low marks: vague answers ("the cell grows"), direction stated without reason, no units on numerical answers
- High marks: mechanism stated step by step, correct biological terminology, link cause to consequence (e.g., "increased temperature → enzyme denaturation → active site shape changes → substrate cannot bind → reaction stops")

---

### Criterion B — Inquiring & Designing
**What it tests**: Ability to design a valid, controlled experiment from a given scenario.

**Typical marks**: 13–21 marks. Uses a holistic markband rubric (not point-scored).

**Design markbands (indicative)**:
| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No response or completely irrelevant |
| 1–3 | Identifies some variables; hypothesis present but not testable; method incomplete |
| 4–6 | IV and DV stated; some CVs identified; hypothesis has direction; method has logical steps but lacks detail or controls |
| 7–9 | All variables identified with units; hypothesis is testable and justified; method is reproducible with clear controls; data table has headings and units |
| 10–12 | All above plus safety/ethical considerations; method explicitly states how CVs are controlled; data table structure allows valid analysis |
| 13–15+ | Exemplary throughout; pilot study mentioned; reliability addressed; clear link from hypothesis to method |

**Recurring design contexts (know these well)**:
- Photosynthesis rate: Elodea/Cabomba/duckweed in NaHCO₃; count bubbles per minute; IV = light intensity or colour/wavelength
- Enzyme activity: lactase breaking down lactose; glucose test strip; IV = temperature or pH; DV = rate/time for colour change
- Organism behaviour (choice chambers): woodlice, Daphnia, leeches choosing between conditions; IV = humidity, light, temperature
- Plant growth: seedling height/mass; IV = fertiliser concentration, light colour, CO₂ concentration
- Yeast respiration: CO₂ bubble rate; IV = sugar concentration or temperature
- Environmental stress: acid rain simulation; effect on seed germination (count germinated seeds / measure radicle length)

**What top-band design responses include**:
1. IV stated with the specific range of values to be tested (e.g., "light intensities of 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 lux")
2. DV stated with the unit and exactly how it will be measured (e.g., "number of oxygen bubbles produced per minute, counted visually")
3. At least 4–5 CVs listed, each with the specific method for controlling them (not just "keep temperature constant" but "using a thermostatically controlled water bath at 25°C")
4. Hypothesis: directional + justification from scientific knowledge (e.g., "As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis will increase because more photons are available to drive the light-dependent reactions")
5. Method: numbered steps, minimum 3 trials per condition for reliability, states the control setup
6. Data table: complete column headings with units, rows for each IV level, space for repeats
7. Safety: at least one genuine hazard with precaution
8. Ethics (if organisms involved): minimal harm, appropriate housing

### Criterion C — Processing & Evaluating
**What it tests**: Graph reading, calculation, trend description, conclusion drawing, evaluation of methodology.

**Typical marks**: 18–30 marks.

**Common command terms**:
- "Calculate" (2–3 marks): show working, include units
- "Describe the trend/pattern" (2–3 marks): direction + qualifier (e.g., "increases steeply then levels off")
- "Explain the relationship" (3–4 marks): cause-and-effect mechanism
- "Conclude" (2–3 marks): answer the hypothesis with reference to data
- "Evaluate the method" (4–6 marks): specific weaknesses + specific improvements

**Mark scheme for Criterion C**:
- Point-scored for calculations and graph reading
- ECF strongly applied: if a student uses an incorrect graph value but calculates correctly, full marks for the calculation
- "Describe" answers must include both direction AND detail (not just "increased" but "increased by 40% between 0 and 30°C")

**Evaluation question structure** (4–6 marks, common in every paper):
- Weakness 1 → Specific improvement 1 (paired marking: you need both to score)
- Weakness 2 → Specific improvement 2
- Weakness 3 → Specific improvement 3
- Vague weaknesses score zero: "the experiment was not accurate" = 0 marks
- Valid weaknesses: small sample size, single trial only, confounding variable not controlled, measuring instrument precision, subjective measurement

### Criterion D — Reflecting on Impacts of Science
**What it tests**: Extended essay on the social, ethical, environmental, or economic impacts of a biological topic. Requires balanced perspectives.

**Typical marks**: 9–17 marks. Holistic markband rubric.

**Typical essay topics across sessions**:
- Genetic modification / GMO crops and animals
- Vaccination programmes and herd immunity
- Antibiotic resistance and pharmaceutical policy
- Climate change and species extinction
- Stem cell research and cloning
- Organ transplantation and donation policy
- Human population growth and resource use
- Pesticide use and biodiversity
- Personalized medicine and genetic testing
- Conservation biology and rewilding

**Criterion D markbands (indicative)**:
| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | No response |
| 1–3 | One-sided; vague scientific references; no conclusion |
| 4–6 | Two perspectives present; some biological evidence; limited conclusion |
| 7–9 | Multiple stakeholders; biological evidence is accurate; balanced evaluation; clear conclusion |
| 10–12 | All above plus specific data or examples; addresses counterarguments; appraises the limitations of each view |
| 13–15+ | Exemplary: precise evidence, named studies/examples, nuanced conclusion that goes beyond "both sides have points" |

**What top-band Criterion D responses include**:
1. Opening: clear statement of the issue and why it is scientifically and socially significant
2. At least 3 distinct stakeholder perspectives (e.g., scientists, government, affected communities, industry, environment)
3. Each perspective supported by specific biological evidence (named pathogens, percentages, mechanisms)
4. Counterarguments explicitly acknowledged and addressed
5. A concluding appraisal that does NOT just summarise — it makes a reasoned judgement
6. Biological terminology used correctly throughout
7. Avoid: purely emotional arguments, vague statements like "it could harm the environment", unsupported claims

## 4. Common Question Types

### Type 1: Interpret a diagram or photograph
- "Label the structures indicated by arrows X and Y"
- "Explain the function of structure Z"
- Appears in Criterion A; diagrams of cells, organs, food webs, graphs
- Key skill: precise labelling with correct terms; explanations must include function, not just location

### Type 2: Explain a biological process from a novel scenario
- A stimulus (graph, data table, passage) introduces unfamiliar organism or context
- "Explain why the organism shows this response"
- Key skill: apply known mechanisms (e.g., enzyme kinetics, osmosis, nervous reflex) to the new context

### Type 3: Calculate using provided data
- Percentage change, rate of reaction, magnification, population size
- Key skill: show formula, substitution, answer with units
- ECF applies: wrong answer from a previous part can still earn calculation marks

### Type 4: Describe a trend in a graph
- Almost always present in Criterion C
- Must include: direction, magnitude, and turning point if present
- Example: "The rate increases from 20 to 45 units between 10°C and 40°C, then decreases sharply to zero at 60°C"

### Type 5: Full experimental design (Criterion B)
- Always present; see Criterion B section above
- Often preceded by a short stimulus about a real experiment to set the biological context
- Common trap: students state the hypothesis without justification, or list CVs without explaining how to control them

### Type 6: Evaluate a method
- "Suggest two improvements to the method described"
- Must always pair weakness with improvement
- Common valid improvements: increase sample size, add control group, use a more precise measuring instrument (name it), repeat experiment (state n), blind/double-blind protocol for surveys

### Type 7: Criterion D essay
- Prompt always includes a scenario or quote
- Requires extended response (typically 300–600 words for top band)
- Must demonstrate multi-stakeholder thinking and evidence-based reasoning

## 5. Mark Scheme Rules

### WTTE — Words To That Effect
Any synonym or accurate paraphrase of the expected answer is awarded the mark. You do not need to memorise exact textbook phrasing. Example: "broken down" and "hydrolysed" are both accepted for enzyme action.

### ORA — Or Reverse Argument
When a question asks for an explanation that could validly be argued in either direction, both directions are accepted. Commonly appears with osmosis questions (water moves into OR out of cell depending on setup) and homeostasis (negative feedback increases OR decreases the variable).

### ECF — Error Carried Forward
If a student makes an error in part (a) but uses that incorrect value correctly in part (b), full marks for part (b) are awarded. This is heavily applied in Criterion C calculations. Strategy: always show your working — even a wrong answer can earn ECF marks in the next part.

### CON — Contradiction
If a student states a correct point and then immediately contradicts it within the same answer, the mark for that point is NOT awarded. The contradiction cancels the correct statement. Example: "Water moves into the cell by osmosis because the water potential outside is higher, so water moves out" — the contradiction ("water moves out") cancels the mark.

### Sequential / chain-of-reasoning marks
Some questions require a chain of steps to score full marks. Each step in the chain must be present. Example (3 marks): "Insulin is released (1) → insulin causes cells to take up glucose (1) → blood glucose concentration falls back to normal (1)." Skipping step 2 means you score only 2/3 even if steps 1 and 3 are correct.

### "Max n" marking
If a question is worth 3 marks and the mark scheme lists 6 possible points, you can score any 3 of those 6. You do not need to write all 6. Strategy: write more points than the mark total to maximise your chance of hitting the accepted ones.

### Specific errors that cost marks
- Missing units on any numerical answer
- Saying "it" or "they" without a clear referent (ambiguous pronoun)
- "Bigger/smaller" without specifying what is bigger or smaller
- "Increase" without stating what is increasing
- Giving the correct numerical answer but no working for a calculation question (may lose method marks)

## 6. Revision Priorities by Tier

### Tier 1 — Certain (100% of sessions)
These topics appear in every single paper. Master them completely before revising anything else.

1. **Experimental design (Criterion B)**: Know how to write a complete design for photosynthesis, enzyme, behaviour, plant growth, and respiration experiments. Practice writing full designs from scratch in timed conditions.
2. **Data processing (Criterion C)**: Practice describing trends with precise language, calculating percentage change and rate, and writing paired weakness/improvement evaluation answers.
3. **Ecosystems**: Food web construction and interpretation, energy flow and loss between trophic levels, population dynamics, effects of removing a species, human impacts on ecosystems.

### Tier 2 — Very Likely (appeared in 13–17 of 18 sessions)
Master the core content; expect at least one significant question per paper.

4. **Cell biology**: Cell structure (prokaryote vs eukaryote), organelle functions, cell division (mitosis stages), cell specialisation, surface area to volume ratio
5. **Genetics**: Monohybrid crosses (Punnett squares), dominant/recessive/codominant inheritance, sex-linked traits, mutation types and causes, DNA structure
6. **Human body systems**: At minimum: digestion (enzymes + villi), circulation (heart, double circulation, blood components), nervous system (reflex arc, neuron types), immune response (antigens, antibodies, active vs passive immunity)
7. **Photosynthesis**: Word and symbol equation, limiting factors (light, CO₂, temperature), light-dependent and light-independent reactions (overview), chloroplast structure
8. **Sustainability/Climate change**: Carbon cycle, greenhouse effect, deforestation consequences, biodiversity loss, conservation strategies (in situ/ex situ)

### Tier 3 — Likely (appeared in 8–12 of 18 sessions)
Solid revision needed; likely to appear as part of a larger question.

9. **Enzymes**: Lock-and-key model, effect of temperature (optimum, denaturation), effect of pH, enzyme inhibitors (competitive/non-competitive), industrial applications
10. **Respiration**: Aerobic equation (word and symbol), anaerobic products in animals vs plants/yeast, comparison of aerobic and anaerobic, uses of ATP
11. **Transport systems**: Osmosis, diffusion, active transport; plant transport (xylem/phloem, transpiration); blood vessel types; heart structure
12. **Health & disease**: Types of pathogens (bacteria, virus, fungus, parasite), vectors, vaccination (active/passive, herd immunity), antibiotic resistance
13. **Homeostasis**: Blood glucose regulation (insulin/glucagon), thermoregulation (vasodilation/constriction, sweating, shivering), negative feedback concept

### Tier 4 — Possible (appeared in 5–8 of 18 sessions)
Review but do not over-invest time.

14. **Biotechnology/GM**: CRISPR concept, GM crops (pros/cons), selective breeding, cloning, stem cells
15. **Gas exchange**: Lung structure (alveoli adaptations), fish gills, stomata and leaf gas exchange
16. **Evolution**: Natural selection mechanism (variation, selection pressure, survival, reproduction, inheritance), evidence for evolution, speciation
17. **Reproduction**: Mitosis vs meiosis, sexual vs asexual reproduction, human reproductive system, hormones (FSH, LH, oestrogen, progesterone), IVF
18. **Classification**: Kingdoms/domains, binomial nomenclature, dichotomous keys

## 7. What Makes a Top-Band Response

### For Criterion B (Design)
The single most common reason students lose marks in Criterion B is writing a good hypothesis or good variable list but a weak method. The examiner gives holistic marks, so one weak element pulls the whole response down.

**Must-haves for top band**:
- **Hypothesis**: "[IV] will [increase/decrease] [DV] because [biological mechanism]." The mechanism must be correct and specific. Vague justifications ("because it is better") score zero.
- **Variables**: IV with specific levels tested (e.g., 5 temperatures: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50°C). DV with exact measurement method and unit. CVs: minimum 4, each with specific control method, not just "keep it constant."
- **Method**: numbered steps, states the control condition, specifies number of repeats (at least 3), specifies volume/concentration of all solutions, states how DV is measured (instrument + how).
- **Data table**: column 1 = IV levels, columns 2-4 = repeat trials, column 5 = mean; all headers with units in brackets.
- **Safety/Ethics**: one real hazard with a specific precaution (e.g., "dilute acid — wear safety goggles"); if animals used, state minimal harm principle.

**Common mistakes to avoid**:
- Writing "change the light intensity" instead of "use light intensities of 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 lux"
- Writing "measure the growth" instead of "measure plant height in cm using a ruler at 24-hour intervals"
- Writing "keep temperature constant" without saying how (water bath, thermostat)
- Missing units from any measurement
- Method that cannot be reproduced (missing quantities, concentrations, timings)

### For Criterion D (Extended Essay)
The most common reason students score in band 4–6 rather than 7–9+ is writing a one-sided argument or failing to include specific scientific evidence.

**Structure for top band** (approximately 400–600 words):
1. Introduction: state the issue clearly; give one piece of scientific context (statistic, mechanism, or definition)
2. Perspective 1 (benefits/one side): specific biological evidence; named example if possible
3. Perspective 2 (concerns/other side): specific biological evidence; acknowledge why people hold this view
4. Additional perspective (economic, ethical, or environmental): shows multi-stakeholder thinking
5. Counter-argument: explicitly take the strongest opposing point and address it
6. Conclusion: make a reasoned judgement — do not simply restate the perspectives; say which considerations matter most and why

**Specific evidence > vague claims**:
- Poor: "GM crops can harm the environment"
- Good: "Bt cotton produces Cry proteins toxic to bollworm larvae, reducing pesticide use by up to 50% in some studies, though non-target insects such as lacewings may also be affected"

**Language for top-band Criterion D**:
- "Evidence suggests that…", "While proponents argue…, critics counter that…"
- "On balance, the scientific consensus indicates…"
- "The long-term consequences remain uncertain because…"
- Avoid: "I think", "obviously", "everyone knows"
