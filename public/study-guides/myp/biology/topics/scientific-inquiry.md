# Scientific Inquiry — Criterion B: Inquiring and Designing

> **MOST IMPORTANT TOPIC.** Criterion B appears in every single session (M16–N25) and carries 13–21 marks depending on the session. This page covers everything needed to score at the top markband.

---

## 1. What Is Criterion B Testing?

Criterion B assesses whether you can **design a valid scientific investigation**. Every Criterion B question gives you a context (an organism, a substance, an environmental factor) and asks you to plan an experiment. The examiner is NOT checking your content knowledge — they are checking whether your experimental design is scientifically valid.

There are always several distinct strands marked:
1. **Research question / problem statement** — is it testable and focused?
2. **Hypothesis** — does it link IV to DV with a scientific explanation?
3. **Independent variable (IV)** — correctly identified and manipulated
4. **Dependent variable (DV)** — correctly identified and measurable
5. **Control variables (CVs)** — at least two, each with a method to keep it constant
6. **Method / procedure** — enough detail to replicate; equipment listed
7. **Sufficient data** — 5 IV levels + 3 repeats + plan to calculate mean
8. **Safety / Ethics** — specific to the investigation; ethics only when human/animal subjects

---

## 2. Research Question Format

### Rules
- Must be in the form **"Does [IV] affect [DV]?"** or **"How does [IV] affect [DV]?"**
- The IV and DV must both appear explicitly.
- **"Why does..."** — NOT accepted (not testable).
- The question must be falsifiable (you could get a "no" answer).

### Examples
| Acceptable | Not acceptable |
|-----------|----------------|
| How does light intensity (lux) affect the rate of oxygen production in *Cabomba*? | Why does light affect photosynthesis? |
| Does temperature (°C) affect the time taken for colour change in yeast? | What happens when temperature changes? |
| How does pH affect the number of bacterial colonies formed? | Does pH affect the rate? (rate of what?) |

---

## 3. Hypothesis Format — If / Then / Because

**Template:**
> If [the IV is changed in a specific direction]… then [the DV will change in a specific direction]… because [scientific mechanism linking IV to DV].

### Rules
- The "because" clause must contain **correct biology** — mark schemes explicitly check this.
- The direction of change must be stated (e.g. "increases", "decreases" — not just "changes").
- Vague language ("will be affected") is not awarded full marks.

### Worked Example (N22 — light intensity & photosynthesis)
> If light intensity is increased, then the rate of oxygen production will increase, because light provides the energy needed for the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis, splitting water molecules and releasing oxygen as a by-product.

### Worked Example (N20 — caffeine & heart rate)
> If caffeine concentration is increased, then the heart rate of Daphnia will increase, because caffeine is a stimulant that inhibits adenosine receptors, increasing the firing rate of the sino-atrial node equivalent.

## 4. Variables — The Most Common Source of Lost Marks

### Independent Variable (IV)
- Must be stated **specifically** — not vague.
- "pH" alone — **NOT accepted** (M18: must say "pH of the solution" or "pH adjusted using HCl/NaOH").
- "Light" alone — **NOT accepted** (must say "light intensity measured in lux").
- State the **range** you will use (e.g. 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 lux) — minimum **5 levels**.

### Dependent Variable (DV)
- Must be **measurable** — give the unit.
- "Rate" alone — **NOT accepted** (M18, M19, N22, M24, M25 all have this note).
- "Plant growth" alone — **NOT accepted** (M25: must say "stem length in mm" or "leaf area in cm²").
- "Rate of photosynthesis" — **NOT accepted** by itself; must say "rate of oxygen production (cm³/min)" or "number of bubbles per minute".
- "Time" alone — must say "time for colour change (seconds)" or "time until solution clears (s)".

### Control Variables (CVs)
- Minimum **2 CVs**, each with:
  1. The variable named specifically.
  2. The method to keep it constant.
- "Keeping the equipment the same" — **NOT accepted** as a CV (M24).
- "Same species" — accepted only if the experiment involves multiple organisms.
- "Same volume of solution" — accepted if volume is relevant.
- "Same temperature" — accepted only if temperature is NOT the IV.

### Worked CV examples
| CV | Method to control |
|----|-------------------|
| Temperature | Place all tubes in the same water bath set to 25 °C |
| Volume of solution | Use a measuring cylinder to add exactly 10 cm³ each time |
| Light source distance | Keep the lamp exactly 20 cm from the container throughout |
| CO₂ concentration | Use the same concentration of sodium hydrogen carbonate in each trial |

## 5. Method Requirements for Full Marks

A full-mark method must allow **complete replication** by a stranger. Include:

1. **Equipment list** with sizes/concentrations (e.g. "250 ml beaker", "0.1 mol/L HCl").
2. **Step-by-step procedure** — do not use bullet points alone; number the steps.
3. **How the IV is changed** — e.g. "prepare 5 solutions: pH 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 using a buffer solution".
4. **How the DV is measured** — e.g. "use a gas syringe to collect oxygen produced in 5 minutes".
5. **Evidence of sufficient data**:
   - At least **5 different IV values** (not just "a range of values").
   - At least **3 repeats** per IV value.
   - State you will "calculate the mean" of the repeats.
6. **Control variable methods** integrated into the steps.

**Common method errors (all from mark schemes):**
- Not stating how many repeats.
- Saying "test different concentrations" without listing them.
- Not explaining how the DV is recorded/measured.
- Only listing equipment without a procedure.
- Stating the IV range without 5 levels.

---

## 6. Sufficient Data Strand

This strand specifically awards marks for:
- Naming **at least 5 IV levels** (specific values, not just "a range").
- Naming **at least 3 repeats** per IV level.
- Stating the **plan to calculate mean** (or average).

Some sessions also add a **Justification of range** strand (N23, worth 2 marks):
- Explain WHY your chosen range is appropriate — e.g. "temperatures above 45 °C would denature yeast enzymes so the range 10–40 °C covers the active range".
- Also include a safety reason if appropriate — e.g. "temperatures above 60 °C create burn risk".

---

## 7. Safety and Ethics

### Safety
- Must be **specific to the investigation** — "be careful" or "wash hands" — **NOT accepted** (M24).
- Link the hazard to the investigation substance or procedure.
- Examples:
  - "Wear goggles as HCl is corrosive and may cause eye damage."
  - "Handle UV lamps with UV-protective glasses as UV radiation can damage the retina."
  - "CO₂ build-up in enclosed space — ensure good ventilation."

### Ethics (human or animal subjects only)
- Required when the investigation uses humans or animals.
- Examples: Daphnia (N20), leeches (N18), humans exercising (M17).
- Key points to include:
  - "Obtain **informed consent** from human participants."
  - "Minimize stress/discomfort to animals; return to habitat after."
  - "Keep Daphnia in their pond water; return immediately after observations."
  - "Participants have the right to **withdraw** at any time."

## 8. Markband Descriptors Across Sessions

Markbands vary in total marks (13–21) but the descriptors follow the same pattern:

| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| **0** | Does not reach the standard described below. |
| **1–3 / 1–4** | Identifies a problem but it is unclear or not focused; variables partially identified; method is incomplete or not replicable. |
| **4–7 / 5–8** | Research question is clear; some variables identified correctly; method is partially complete; safety mentioned but vague. |
| **8–11 / 9–12** | Research question is focused and testable; IV, DV, and at least 1 CV correct; method mostly complete; hypothesis includes a scientific explanation; safety is specific. |
| **12–16 / 14–21** | Research question is precise and focused; IV, DV, and 2+ CVs all correct with methods; detailed method allowing full replication; 5 IV levels, 3 repeats, mean planned; hypothesis has correct scientific explanation; safety specific; ethics addressed where required. |

## 10. Worked Full-Mark Response Examples

### Example 1 — N22 Q6 (15 marks): Photosynthesis light intensity

**Research question:**
How does light intensity (lux) affect the rate of oxygen production (cm³ per minute) in *Cabomba caroliniana*?

**Hypothesis:**
If light intensity is increased from 1000 to 5000 lux, then the rate of oxygen production will increase, because light provides energy for the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis. Greater light intensity increases the rate of photolysis (splitting water molecules), releasing more oxygen as a by-product, until another factor (CO₂ or temperature) becomes limiting.

**Variables:**
- IV: Light intensity — 1000, 2000, 3000, 4000, 5000 lux, adjusted by moving the lamp and measured with a lux meter.
- DV: Volume of oxygen produced (cm³) in a 5-minute period, measured using a gas syringe.
- CV 1: Temperature — all tubes placed in a water bath at 25 °C.
- CV 2: CO₂ concentration — 1 g sodium hydrogen carbonate dissolved in each 500 ml of water.
- CV 3: Length of *Cabomba* stem — use 10 cm lengths each time.

**Method (summary):**
1. Cut ten 10 cm stems of *Cabomba*; place one in a 500 ml beaker with 1 g NaHCO₃ dissolved in water.
2. Set water bath to 25 °C; place beaker inside.
3. Position lamp at distance to give 1000 lux (check with lux meter).
4. Connect gas syringe to tubing sealed around stem.
5. Allow 2 minutes to acclimatise; then collect gas for 5 minutes. Record volume.
6. Repeat steps 3–5 for 2000, 3000, 4000, 5000 lux (3 repeats per level using fresh stem each time).
7. Calculate mean oxygen volume for each light intensity.

**Safety:** Wear safety goggles as the glass equipment may shatter. Do not touch the lamp bulb as it becomes very hot; risk of burns.

---

### Example 2 — N23 Q5 (19 marks): Yeast & temperature with range justification

**Research question:**
How does temperature (°C) affect the rate of CO₂ production (cm³ per minute) by yeast in a 5% glucose solution?

**Hypothesis:**
If temperature is increased from 10 °C to 45 °C, then the rate of CO₂ production will increase up to approximately 35–40 °C, then decrease, because enzymes such as zymase control fermentation; increasing temperature increases enzyme-substrate collision frequency, raising the rate, until above the optimum temperature the enzyme's active site denatures, reducing CO₂ output.

**Range justification:** The range 10–45 °C is appropriate because: (a) below 10 °C yeast is metabolically inactive; (b) above 45 °C, yeast enzymes denature irreversibly; (c) temperatures above 60 °C create a burn hazard to the investigator.

**Variables:**
- IV: Temperature — 10, 20, 25, 35, 45 °C, controlled with water baths.
- DV: Volume of CO₂ collected in gas syringe (cm³) in 5 minutes.
- CV 1: Glucose concentration — 5% solution measured with a refractometer.
- CV 2: Volume of yeast suspension — 20 cm³ measured with a syringe each time.
- CV 3: pH of solution — use 7.0 buffer solution in each replicate.

**Sufficient data:** 5 IV levels × 3 repeats = 15 trials; mean calculated for each temperature.

**Safety:** Temperatures above 45 °C — wear insulated gloves when handling hot water baths; risk of scalding.

## 12. Common Mistakes — Don't Lose These Marks

| Mistake | Why it loses marks | Fix |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| "Rate" as DV | Too vague — not measurable | Add what is being measured and units: "rate of O₂ production in cm³/min" |
| "Why does..." RQ | Not testable | Use "How does... affect...?" or "Does... affect...?" |
| Only 3 IV levels | Insufficient data range | Always state minimum 5 specific values |
| "Keep equipment the same" as CV | Not a real variable | Name an actual variable (temperature, volume, concentration) |
| Vague safety: "be careful" | Not accepted | Link hazard to investigation: "HCl is corrosive — wear goggles" |
| Hypothesis without "because" | Loses scientific explanation marks | Always include a mechanistic explanation |
| Not stating 3 repeats | Data sufficiency strand lost | Explicitly say "repeat 3 times at each level, calculate mean" |
| Ethics omitted for animal subjects | Ethics strand lost | Always include ethics when using Daphnia, leeches, or humans |
| General CV not linked to method | Partially accepted only | Say HOW you will keep it constant (not just what it is) |
| Re-stating equipment from the question | Not awarded | Identify variables beyond what was already given |

## 13. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the **If/Then/Because** hypothesis template and practise writing one for each of the 10 common contexts.
2. Practise identifying **IV, DV, and 2 CVs with methods** for each context.
3. Always write **5 named IV values** — never say "a range."
4. Learn the **specific DV phrasings** accepted: "rate of O₂ production in cm³/min", "heart rate in beats per minute", "stem length in mm."
5. Write a method for the **N22 (photosynthesis)** and **N23 (yeast temperature)** questions under timed conditions.
6. Check you have included **3 repeats + mean** in every method answer.
7. When the investigation uses living organisms (Daphnia, bacteria, humans) — always add an **ethics sentence**.
