# Ecology and Ecosystems

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Levels of Organisation (Ecological)

| Level | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Individual | A single organism | One oak tree |
| Population | All individuals of one species in an area | All oak trees in a forest |
| Community | All populations of different species in an area | All plants + animals + fungi in the forest |
| Ecosystem | Community + the abiotic (non-living) environment | The forest including soil, water, light, temperature |
| Biosphere | All ecosystems on Earth | |

---

### Biotic and Abiotic Factors

**Biotic factors** (living): predation, competition, disease, food availability, symbiosis.

**Abiotic factors** (non-living): temperature, light intensity, pH, water availability, humidity, salinity, soil nutrients, wind speed.

---

### Food Chains and Food Webs

**Food chain:** Shows the direction of energy flow between organisms.
> Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk

- Arrows show the direction of energy transfer (from eaten to eater).
- **Producer:** An organism that makes its own food via photosynthesis (always a plant or alga). Always at the start of a food chain.
- **Primary consumer:** Eats the producer (herbivore).
- **Secondary consumer:** Eats the primary consumer.
- **Tertiary consumer:** Eats the secondary consumer.
- **Decomposer:** Breaks down dead organic matter (fungi, bacteria); recycles nutrients into soil. **Not shown in food chains** but ecologically important.

**Food web:** Multiple overlapping food chains in an ecosystem, showing more realistic feeding relationships.

**Mark scheme rules:**
- Arrows MUST point in the direction of energy flow (from prey to predator), not the other way.
- "Eaten by" describes the arrow direction — draw from plant to herbivore, not herbivore to plant.
- "Plants produce energy" — **NOT accepted.** Plants convert/trap light energy; they do NOT produce energy.

---

### Trophic Levels

| Trophic level | Name | Example |
|--------------|------|---------|
| 1st | Producers | Grass, algae, phytoplankton |
| 2nd | Primary consumers | Rabbit, grasshopper, zooplankton |
| 3rd | Secondary consumers | Fox, frog |
| 4th | Tertiary consumers | Eagle, shark |

---

### Energy Pyramids (Pyramids of Biomass/Numbers)

- **Pyramid of biomass:** Shows the total mass of organisms at each trophic level. Usually a true pyramid shape (decreases from bottom to top).
- **Pyramid of numbers:** Shows the number of organisms — can be inverted (e.g. many insects on one tree).
- Only about **10% of energy** is transferred from one trophic level to the next (the rest is lost as heat, movement, undigested material).
- This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 links — too little energy reaches the top.

**N20 Q1a / N19 Q1:** Identify trophic levels from a food web; calculate how much energy reaches level 3 if level 1 has 10,000 kJ → 10% passes each level → 10,000 → 1,000 → 100 kJ.

---

### Biodiversity

- **Biodiversity:** The variety of living organisms in an area — includes species diversity, genetic diversity, and ecosystem diversity.
- **Species richness:** The number of different species in an ecosystem.
- High biodiversity = more stable ecosystem (more alternatives in food web if one species is lost).

**M25 Q7–8 (bird tracking/biodiversity):** Biodiversity indices used to compare areas; key message: more species + more even distribution = higher biodiversity.

---

### Population Dynamics

- **Population growth** is affected by:
  - Birth rate and death rate.
  - Immigration (organisms moving in) and emigration (moving out).
- **Carrying capacity:** The maximum population size an environment can support given available resources.
- **Limiting factors:** Food, water, space, predation, disease.

**Predator-prey cycles:** When prey increases, predators increase (more food); when predators increase, prey decreases (over-predation); then predators decrease (less food) → cycle repeats.

### Human Impacts on Ecosystems

#### Deforestation
- Removal of forests for agriculture, logging, or development.
- Effects:
  - Loss of habitat → reduced biodiversity.
  - Loss of carbon sink → increased CO₂ in atmosphere.
  - Soil erosion (no roots to hold soil).
  - Disruption of water cycle (less transpiration).

**N24 Q7c (deforestation & carbon cycle):** 6-mark chain question — must trace how deforestation leads to increased atmospheric CO₂:
> Trees cut down → less photosynthesis → less CO₂ removed from atmosphere → burning/decomposing trees releases CO₂ → CO₂ increases in atmosphere → enhanced greenhouse effect.

#### Eutrophication
A 4-step chain that appears almost every other session:

**M22 Q3 (4-mark question) — learn this chain precisely:**

> 1. Excess fertiliser / nitrates / phosphates run off into water body.
> 2. Algae grow rapidly (algal bloom); block sunlight from reaching underwater plants.
> 3. Underwater plants die (no light for photosynthesis).
> 4. Decomposing bacteria multiply rapidly; decompose dead plants → deplete oxygen in water (BOD increases).
> 5. Fish and other aquatic organisms die due to lack of oxygen.

**Mark scheme notes:**
- Must say bacteria "deplete oxygen" — NOT "use up all the oxygen" (imprecise).
- "Algae block light" — must be stated explicitly; not just "algae grow."
- Step 4 must mention decomposers / bacteria AND oxygen depletion as two separate points.

#### Climate Change / Greenhouse Effect
- **Greenhouse gases:** CO₂, methane (CH₄), water vapour, nitrous oxide.
- **Enhanced greenhouse effect:** Human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation) increase GHG concentrations → more infrared radiation trapped → global temperature rises.
- Effects: sea level rise, habitat loss, species extinction, changed precipitation patterns, coral bleaching.

**N20 Q8 (17 marks, Criterion D):** Extended essay on climate change solutions — requires: evaluating solutions, linking to cause-and-effect, personal reasoned judgement.

#### Conservation
- **In situ conservation:** Protecting species in their natural habitat (nature reserves, national parks, marine protected areas).
- **Ex situ conservation:** Protecting species outside their natural habitat (zoos, seed banks, captive breeding).
- **Advantages of in situ:** Natural behaviours preserved; natural selection continues; self-sustaining.
- **Advantages of ex situ:** Protects critically endangered species; safe from poaching; controlled breeding.

### The Carbon Cycle

Key processes:

| Process | Effect on CO₂ | Notes |
|---------|--------------|-------|
| Photosynthesis | Removes CO₂ from atmosphere | Plants + algae; converts to glucose |
| Respiration | Adds CO₂ to atmosphere | All living organisms |
| Combustion (burning) | Adds CO₂ to atmosphere | Fossil fuels + wood |
| Decomposition | Adds CO₂ to atmosphere | Bacteria/fungi break down dead matter |
| Formation of fossil fuels | Stores carbon long-term | Compressed organic matter over millions of years |

**N20 Q7 (carbon cycle diagram):** Must correctly label photosynthesis (removing CO₂) and respiration/combustion (adding CO₂).

**M18 Q2 (carbon cycle):** Deforestation → less photosynthesis → less CO₂ removed; also burning trees directly releases stored carbon.

## 3. Case Studies to Know

### Eutrophication — The 4-Step Chain
Every 1–2 sessions this appears. Write it from memory:
1. Fertiliser nitrates/phosphates enter water (runoff).
2. Algal bloom — algae multiply; block sunlight.
3. Underwater plants die (no photosynthesis).
4. Decomposers (bacteria) multiply; deplete oxygen → fish die.

### Deforestation and Carbon Cycle (N24 Q7c)
Full 6-mark chain:
1. Trees removed → less photosynthesis.
2. Less CO₂ absorbed from atmosphere.
3. Trees burned/decomposed → CO₂ released.
4. CO₂ concentration in atmosphere increases.
5. More infrared radiation trapped (greenhouse effect).
6. Global temperature increases.

### African Savanna Food Web (N19 Q1)
Typical structure: Grass → Zebra → Lion; Grass → Elephant; Grass → Wildebeest → Cheetah; all connected in web. Decomposers (bacteria/fungi) recycle nutrients.

## 4. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Food chain arrows:** Always point from food source to consumer (direction of energy flow). Backward arrows = 0 marks.
- **Energy transfer:** 10% rule — only 10% of energy passes to the next trophic level.
- **Eutrophication:** Must mention decomposers (bacteria) AND oxygen depletion as separate points.
- **"Plants produce energy"** — NOT accepted; correct phrasing: "plants convert light energy into chemical energy" or "plants capture/store energy."
- **Conservation:** When comparing in situ and ex situ, must give reasons (not just names).
- **Carbon cycle:** Must distinguish which processes add and which remove CO₂.

---

## 5. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Drawing food chain arrow from predator to prey | Arrow goes FROM prey/plant TO predator: Grass → Rabbit → Fox |
| "Plants produce energy" | Plants convert/capture/store light energy as chemical energy |
| Eutrophication: skipping the oxygen depletion step | Must state: decomposers deplete dissolved oxygen → aquatic organisms die |
| Confusing community and ecosystem | Community = biotic only; ecosystem = community + abiotic environment |
| Saying "deforestation directly causes global warming" | Chain: less photosynthesis + combustion → more CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect → warming |
| Forgetting decomposers in nutrient cycles | Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) are essential for recycling nutrients back to soil |
