# Photosynthesis and Respiration

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Photosynthesis

**Word equation:**
> Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen

**Chemical equation:**
> 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (using light energy)

**Where it happens:** Chloroplasts, specifically in the **thylakoid membranes** (light reactions) and **stroma** (dark reactions / Calvin cycle).

**What is needed:**
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — from the air via stomata.
- Water (H₂O) — absorbed through roots.
- Light energy — absorbed by chlorophyll (the green pigment in chloroplasts).

**What is produced:**
- Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — used for energy (respiration), growth, or stored as starch.
- Oxygen (O₂) — released as a by-product through stomata.

**Chlorophyll:** The green pigment that absorbs light energy. Absorbs mainly red and blue wavelengths; reflects green (which is why plants look green). Found in chloroplasts.

---

### Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis

A **limiting factor** is the factor that, when in short supply, restricts the rate of photosynthesis even if other factors are adequate.

| Factor | Effect when increased | Effect when at maximum | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|-------|
| Light intensity | Rate increases (up to a point) | Another factor becomes limiting | Measured in lux |
| CO₂ concentration | Rate increases | Another factor limits | Can use NaHCO₃ solution to increase CO₂ |
| Temperature | Rate increases (up to optimum) | Enzymes denature above optimum → rate drops sharply | Optimum ~25–35 °C for most plants |
| Water | Essential; deficiency = stomata close | Rarely limits in aquatic plants | |
| Chlorophyll (wavelength) | Red + blue absorbed; green reflected | | Some wavelengths not useful |

**M25 Q5 (enzyme denaturation):** At high temperatures, enzymes (e.g. RuBisCO in Calvin cycle) denature — active site changes shape — substrates can no longer bind → rate of photosynthesis falls to near zero. This is a permanent (irreversible) effect above a critical temperature (~40–45 °C in most plants).

**N22 Q5 (CO₂ and light as limiting factors):** When light is increased but CO₂ remains low, rate eventually plateaus because CO₂ is now limiting. Must identify the correct limiting factor from a graph.

### Measuring Photosynthesis in the Lab

**Indicator organisms:** Aquatic plants — Cabomba (M21), Elodea, Duckweed (M19).

**Common methods:**
1. **Count oxygen bubbles** — count bubbles per minute produced by an aquatic plant under different light intensities.
2. **Gas syringe** — collect oxygen gas and measure volume in cm³.
3. **Colour change of indicator** — hydrogencarbonate indicator changes from orange (normal CO₂) to yellow (high CO₂) or purple (low CO₂); less CO₂ in solution = more photosynthesis.
4. **Duckweed count** — number of fronds (leaves) over time as a measure of growth = proxy for photosynthesis rate (M19 Q5).

**N22 Q4 (variable identification):**
- IV = temperature (°C)
- DV = O₂ concentration — **not "rate of photosynthesis"** alone (must specify what is measured).
- Mark scheme: "oxygen concentration in solution (mg/L)" accepted; "rate" alone NOT accepted.

**M25 Q4 (plant growth experiment):**
- IV = light intensity (lux)
- DV = stem length (mm) — **"plant growth" alone NOT accepted** (must give specific measurable outcome).

## 2. Aerobic Respiration

**Definition:** The process of releasing energy (ATP) from glucose using oxygen.

**Word equation:**
> Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water (+ energy as ATP)

**Chemical equation:**
> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (+ ATP)

**Where it happens:** Mostly in the **mitochondria** (specifically the inner mitochondrial membrane and matrix).

**Products:**
- ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — the cell's usable energy currency.
- Carbon dioxide — exhaled.
- Water — excreted or used.

**Uses of energy (ATP):**
- Muscle contraction.
- Active transport.
- Protein synthesis.
- Maintaining body temperature.
- Cell division.

---

## 3. Anaerobic Respiration

**Definition:** The process of releasing energy (ATP) from glucose WITHOUT oxygen.

**Two types — critical distinction:**

| | In **animals / humans** | In **yeast / fungi** |
|--|------------------------|---------------------|
| Reactant | Glucose | Glucose |
| Products | Lactic acid | Ethanol + Carbon dioxide |
| ATP yield | Small amount (2 ATP) | Small amount (2 ATP) |

**Animal (human) word equation:**
> Glucose → Lactic acid (+ small amount of ATP)

**Yeast word equation:**
> Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide (+ small amount of ATP)

**N24 Q1d (aerobic vs anaerobic):** Key question — must use terms correctly:
- Aerobic = uses oxygen; produces CO₂ + H₂O + more ATP.
- Anaerobic = no oxygen; produces lactic acid (animals) OR ethanol + CO₂ (yeast/plants).

**M23 Q1d — CRITICAL mark scheme note:**
> **Do NOT accept "ethanol" as the product of anaerobic respiration in humans.** Ethanol is only produced in yeast. In humans, anaerobic respiration produces lactic acid. This is a frequently tested distinction.

## 4. Fermentation

**Fermentation** is anaerobic respiration in yeast — the breakdown of glucose to produce ethanol and CO₂.

**Practical applications:**
- **Brewing/winemaking:** Yeast ferments grape/grain sugar → ethanol (alcohol).
- **Bread making:** Yeast ferments sugar → CO₂ (makes bread rise; ethanol evaporates).
- **Biofuels:** Yeast ferments plant sugars → bioethanol as renewable fuel (N19 Q5).

**Zymase:** The enzyme in yeast responsible for fermentation. Named specifically in N19 mark schemes.

**N19 Q3/Q4 (yeast respiration):**
- Different sugars (glucose, fructose, sucrose, starch) were tested.
- Glucose was most readily fermented; starch slowest (needs to be digested first).
- CO₂ production measured as the indicator of fermentation rate.
- IV = type of sugar; DV = volume of CO₂ produced (cm³).

**N23 Q3 (UV radiation and yeast):**
- UV light damages yeast DNA → fewer functional enzymes (including zymase) → reduced CO₂ production.
- Variable identification for Criterion B strand: IV = UV exposure time; DV = CO₂ production rate (cm³/min).

## 5. Comparing Photosynthesis and Respiration

| Feature | Photosynthesis | Aerobic Respiration |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| Reactants | CO₂ + H₂O + light | Glucose + O₂ |
| Products | Glucose + O₂ | CO₂ + H₂O + ATP |
| Energy change | Stores energy | Releases energy |
| Location | Chloroplasts (plants only) | Mitochondria (all cells) |
| When | In light | All the time |
| Net gas exchange (day) | Absorbs CO₂, releases O₂ | Absorbs O₂, releases CO₂ |
| Net gas exchange (night) | None | Absorbs O₂, releases CO₂ |

**Common exam question:** "At what light intensity does the plant neither gain nor lose mass?" → This is the **compensation point** — where photosynthesis rate = respiration rate; net gas exchange = 0.

## 6. Enzyme Kinetics (Linked to Photosynthesis/Respiration)

Enzymes are biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions without being consumed.

- **Active site:** The specific region of an enzyme where the substrate binds.
- **Lock and key model:** Each enzyme is specific to one substrate (complementary shapes).
- **Denaturation:** When an enzyme loses its shape (active site changes) due to high temperature or extreme pH → substrate can no longer bind → reaction stops.
- **Optimum temperature:** The temperature at which enzyme activity is fastest (typically 35–40 °C for human enzymes).
- **Optimum pH:** Varies by enzyme (e.g. pepsin in stomach = pH 2; most plant enzymes = pH 7).

**M21 Q7 (enzyme substrate concentration):** As substrate concentration increases, rate increases until all active sites are occupied → rate plateaus (maximum velocity reached). Adding more substrate after this point does not increase rate.

## 8. Mark Scheme Insights

- **"Ethanol" in human anaerobic respiration** — **NOT accepted** (M23, N24). Always write "lactic acid" for humans/animals; "ethanol + CO₂" only for yeast.
- **"Plant growth" as DV** — **NOT accepted** (M25). Must specify: "stem length (mm)", "leaf area (cm²)", "number of leaves", "biomass (g)".
- **"Rate of photosynthesis" as DV** — **NOT accepted** without specifying what is being measured. Use "rate of O₂ production (cm³/min)" or "number of bubbles per minute."
- **"Mitochondria store energy"** — **NOT accepted** (M19). Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration / produce ATP.
- **Denaturation:** Must say the **active site changes shape** — not just "enzyme is destroyed."

## 9. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Humans produce ethanol in anaerobic respiration | Humans produce LACTIC ACID; ethanol is only in yeast fermentation |
| "Photosynthesis produces energy" | Photosynthesis stores/converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose) |
| "Limiting factor is always light" | Read the graph — if CO₂ is low, adding more light will not increase rate further |
| "Rate of photosynthesis" as DV | Must specify: "rate of O₂ production in cm³/min" |
| Aerobic respiration happens only during exercise | Aerobic respiration occurs continuously in all living cells |
| Denaturation = enzyme "killed" | Denaturation = active site changes shape; enzyme becomes non-functional |
| Confusing compensation point | Compensation point = light level where photosynthesis = respiration; net O₂ and CO₂ exchange = 0 |
