# Biotechnology

## Topic overview

Covers genetic modification, selective breeding, CRISPR, biofortification, in vitro meat, 3D bioprinting, and seed banks. Appears primarily under Criterion A (knowledge) and Criterion D (evaluation of impacts). Design questions (Criterion B) sometimes use biotechnology contexts.

---

## Key concepts

### Selective breeding vs genetic engineering

**Selective breeding** (N17 Q3c, M17 Q8d):
- Humans choose organisms with desired traits and breed them together over many generations
- Relies on existing genetic variation within a species
- Takes many generations to achieve the desired result
- Example: Belgian Blue cattle bred for muscle mass (M17 Q3 context)

**Genetic engineering** (N17 Q3c, M17 Q8d):
- Artificially altering the genome by inserting, deleting, or modifying genes
- Can introduce genes from a completely different species
- Produces results in a single generation

Similarities:
- Both select for desired traits
- Both aim to alter the genetic makeup
- Both rely on principles of heredity

Differences:
- Genetic engineering is much faster than selective breeding
- Genetic engineering can introduce traits from another species; selective breeding cannot
- Genetic engineering is an artificial process; selective breeding is considered more natural

Weakness of selective breeding (N17 Q3d):
- Reduction of the gene pool / genetic variation
- Low variation reduces the ability to adapt to environmental change
- Desired trait may have unintended negative effects on the species

### How genetic modification (GM) works

General process — inserting a gene into bacteria (N18 Q7e, N19 Q6b, N24 Q2d):

1. **Human gene extracted** from donor organism (e.g., human insulin gene; HGH gene)
2. **Plasmid DNA opened** using restriction enzymes
3. **New gene inserted** into the plasmid
4. **Plasmid transferred** into a bacterium
5. **GM bacteria reproduce**, making copies of the gene and producing the desired protein
6. **Extraction and purification** of the protein product

The correct sequence is frequently tested as a drag-and-drop ordering task. Note: step 1 always comes before step 3; reproduction (step 5) comes before extraction (step 6).

### GM bacteria for insulin production (N19 Q6b–d)

- In the 1920s, insulin was discovered; early production used pig pancreas
- Around 1980, human insulin was first produced by GM bacteria
- Now insulin is produced industrially at scale from GM bacteria

Individual impacts: insulin allows people with diabetes to respond to meals; improves quality of life
Community/social impacts: some religious groups may have concerns; some prefer animal-derived insulin
Economic impacts: industrialisation lowers production costs; more people can afford insulin
Appraisal: industrialisation has improved management of diabetes overall, though access remains inequitable

Ethical issue of pig-to-human pancreas transplant (N19 Q6e):
- Some religious groups may have concerns
- Some oppose using animals for human benefit
- DNA crossing the species barrier

### GM bacteria for Human Growth Hormone (HGH) (N24 Q2d–e)

- HGH treats growth disorders in children
- Synthetic HGH is produced using the same GM bacteria process
- Why inject rather than take as tablet: protein would be digested/denatured in the stomach; injection delivers directly to bloodstream (N24 Q2e)

### GM crops: American chestnut (M17 Q8e)

- American chestnut trees were largely wiped out by chestnut blight (a fungus introduced via Japanese chestnut imports)
- Scientists used biotechnology: extracted the resistance gene (oxo gene) from wheat and inserted it into American chestnut DNA
- The gene for blight resistance is transferred to offspring

Discuss and evaluate implications of changing the genome of chestnut trees (M17 Q8f):
- Advantages: restores ecosystem; provides food for wildlife; economic value of chestnut wood/fruit
- Disadvantages: unknown long-term effects; gene could spread to other species; loss of natural evolutionary pressure
- Environmental impact: potential ecosystem disruption; or positive — restores biodiversity
- Economic impact: benefits forestry industry; high cost of development
- Conclusion required

### Biofortified crops (M18 Q7d, N18 Q8)

**Biofortified crops** are genetically modified to contain additional nutritional value.

Examples (M18 Q7d):
| Crop | Added nutrient | Country | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maize | Beta-carotene | Zambia | 2012 |
| Millet | Iron | India | 2012 |
| Wheat | Zinc | Pakistan | 2013 |
| Sweet potato | Beta-carotene | Uganda | 2007 |
| Beans | Iron | Rwanda | 2012 |
| Cassava | Beta-carotene | Nigeria | 2011 |

**Golden rice**: GM rice containing beta-carotene in the grains. Beta-carotene is converted to vitamin A in the human body. Developed to address vitamin A deficiency, which causes blindness and weakened immunity.

Discuss and evaluate biofortified crops (N18 Q8) — required elements:
- Health impacts: reduces vitamin A deficiency; prevents blindness; individual benefit
- Environmental impacts: risk of cross-pollination with non-modified crops; GM crops may outcompete native varieties; potential habitat disruption
- Ethical impacts: people may be forced to eat GM food without choice; concerns about "playing with nature"
- Economic impacts: cost to develop and distribute; could reduce healthcare costs from nutrient deficiency; may reduce food security costs
- Recommendation/conclusion: weigh benefits (addressing global malnutrition) against risks (environmental, ethical)

### In vitro meat production (N17 Q7c)

Process:
- Stem cells extracted from a cow
- Cells divide in laboratory conditions producing millions of cells
- Muscle fibres formed; fat cells separated
- Turned into a burger or other meat product

Implications — intensive meat farming vs in vitro:
- Impacts of intensive farming: land use, methane emissions, animal welfare, water use, antibiotic use
- Strengths of in vitro: no animal slaughter; lower land/water use; potential for scalability
- Limitations of in vitro: high cost; laboratory infrastructure required; consumer acceptance; energy use; unknown long-term effects
- Environmental: farming → methane, deforestation; in vitro → lower emissions but energy-intensive
- Ethical: intensive farming raises animal welfare issues; in vitro raises questions about "natural" food
- Concluding appraisal required

### 3D bioprinting (N17 Q8)

- 3D printers use in vitro cell culture techniques to print biological tissue/organs
- Proposed as a source of body tissue for transplant patients (replaces need for a matching donor)

Advantages:
- No donor needed; eliminates waiting lists
- Tissue can be grown from patient's own cells (lower rejection risk)
- Could be available on demand

Disadvantages:
- Technically complex and expensive
- Long-term effects unknown
- Ethical questions about artificially created tissue
- Infrastructure required; not yet widely available

Concluding appraisal required: weigh immediate medical benefit against technical and ethical challenges.

### CRISPR gene editing (N23 Q1h)

- Gene editing technology that can identify and repair defective alleles by cutting out and replacing sections of DNA
- Can potentially cure untreatable genetic diseases (e.g., cystic fibrosis)
- Benefits: cure disease; reduce need for long-term medication
- Ethical considerations: long-term effects unknown; could be used for cosmetic changes not just medical; regulations vary between countries

### Seed banks

- Seed banks store seeds from a wide variety of plant species at low temperatures to preserve genetic diversity
- Purpose: insurance against extinction; enable future crop breeding; preserve wild plant genetic material
- Relevant to biofortification — access to wild crop varieties provides genes for biofortification projects
- Links to selective breeding: seed banks preserve genetic diversity needed for selective breeding programmes

## Mark scheme insights

1. **GM process sequence**: The order of steps is frequently tested. Genes are EXTRACTED before being INSERTED. Bacteria REPRODUCE before EXTRACTION of product. Restriction enzymes open the plasmid — this step is distinct from inserting the gene.
2. **Compare and contrast**: Must clearly label similarities AND differences. Simply listing one type of biotechnology is not enough.
3. **Evaluate questions**: Always need: pros, cons, environmental impact, economic impact, ethical consideration, AND a concluding recommendation. Missing any strand is capped in the markband.
4. **Biofortified crops table (M18 Q7d)**: 3 marks — data for ≥4 crops; correct columns (crop, nutrient, country, year); logical ordering in one column (e.g., chronological).
5. **In vitro meat (N17 Q7c)**: 16-mark question requires all five strands of the markband — intensive farming impacts, in vitro strengths and limitations, environmental, ethical, AND appraisal.
6. **Gene definition (N18 Q7c, N23 Q4f)**: "A section of DNA/chromosome that codes for a protein or heritable characteristic." Do NOT accept "DNA has genetic information" alone.
7. **Why inject HGH rather than tablet (N24 Q2e)**: Two linked reasons — (1) protein is digested/denatured in stomach; (2) injection enters blood directly.

## Common mistakes

- Confusing selective breeding (using existing variation within species) with genetic engineering (inserting genes from other species)
- Getting the GM sequence wrong — especially placing "bacteria reproduce" before "gene inserted"
- Writing only advantages OR only disadvantages in a discuss/evaluate question — must be balanced
- Omitting the concluding appraisal in extended response questions
- Saying "selective breeding introduces new genes" — it selects from existing genes, it does not introduce new ones
- Saying the statin "reflects" the active site — it BLOCKS the active site
- Describing golden rice as "containing vitamin A" — it contains beta-carotene, which the body converts to vitamin A

1. Memorise the 6-step GM process in correct sequence — it appears in some form in almost every exam
2. Know the compare/contrast framework for selective breeding vs genetic engineering (similarities AND differences)
3. Prepare a structured plan for Criterion D biotechnology essays: health impact → environmental impact → ethical impact → economic impact → conclusion
4. Know the biofortified crops examples (golden rice, iron-fortified beans, etc.) and why they were developed
5. Understand in vitro meat: the stem cell process AND the evaluate essay requirements
6. CRISPR: one benefit (cure disease) AND one ethical concern (unknown long-term effects; cosmetic use; regulatory variation)
