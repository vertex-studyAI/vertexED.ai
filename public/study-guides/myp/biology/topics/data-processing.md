# Data Processing — Criterion C Guide

## Topic overview

Criterion C (Processing and Evaluating) appears in **every single session**. It covers graph plotting, calculations (mean, rate, percentage), data description, evaluating validity and reliability, and line of best fit. This is the criterion with the most recurring mark scheme patterns and the most preventable dropped marks.

---

## When to use each graph type

### Bar chart
- Use when the **independent variable (IV) is categorical** (discrete, non-continuous)
- Examples: different species, different colours, different disinfectants, different food types
- N24 Q4c: "Colour of flower" is categorical → bar chart
- N24 Q4d: IV is categorical data / qualitative data / discrete data (accept any of these phrasings)
- Rules:
  - Bars must NOT touch (they represent distinct categories)
  - Y-axis MUST start at zero for bar charts
  - Equal intervals on the y-axis
  - Bars must be within ±0.5 squares of the correct height

### Line graph / scatter graph
- Use when **both variables are continuous** (numerical data that can take any value)
- A **line graph** joins points with straight lines between successive data points
- A **scatter graph** plots each data point independently; add a line of best fit
- Use a scatter graph (with line of best fit) when investigating a relationship between two continuous variables and you expect a trend with variation in data (e.g. N20 Q6a — caffeine vs heart rate)
- Use a line graph when time is the IV and you want to show change over time (e.g. N21 Q5b — antibiotic resistance over years)

### Which graph to use — decision guide:

| Situation | Graph type |
|---|---|
| IV is categorical (e.g. species, colour, food type) | Bar chart |
| IV and DV are both continuous; investigating a correlation | Scatter graph with line of best fit |
| IV is time; showing change over time | Line graph (connect points) |
| Comparing means across categories | Bar chart |
| Showing individual data points and a trend | Scatter graph |

## Axis labelling rules

Every axis must have:
1. **The variable name** — e.g. "Temperature" not just "T"
2. **The unit** — e.g. "/ °C" or "(°C)" — missing units is one of the most common single-mark losses
3. **Even intervals** — e.g. 0, 10, 20, 30... NOT 0, 5, 15, 25...
4. **A scale appropriate to the data** — data should fill at least half the graph area

Common unit errors from mark schemes:
- N21 Q5b: Resistance data plotted; y-axis should say "Resistance (%)" — missing "%" loses 1 mark
- M18 Q5c: "State the unit missing from the x-axis" → g dm⁻³ (accept g/dm³ or g/l)
- N19 Q5d: y-axis label requires mass of ethanol AND the unit /mg
- N20 Q4f: x-axis = Temperature AND °C; y-axis = Heart rate AND bpm — both the label and unit needed for 1 mark each
- M22 Q6d: Stomatal density answer must be rounded to 89 mm⁻²
- N23 Q4c: Axis labels must include units (ppm min⁻¹ for rate axis)

**Bar charts**: y-axis must START AT ZERO. Line graphs / scatter graphs may use a broken axis if data does not include zero, but must be clearly indicated.

## Plotting accuracy

- Tolerance: **±1 square** (for line graphs and scatter graphs) or **±0.5 squares** (for bar charts)
- Mark scheme language: "Two points plotted correctly (±1 mm / ±0.1 / ±0.5 squares)" = 1 mark; "All points correct" = 2 marks
- Common partial-credit pattern: 2 or 3 correct out of 5+ earns 1 mark; all correct earns 2 marks

Practise plotting from mark scheme data tables to develop precision.

## Calculating means

**Mean = sum of values ÷ number of values**

Significant figures rule: Round to a **sensible degree of accuracy** matching the raw data.

Examples from mark schemes:
- N22 Q4d: (262 + 261 + 259) ÷ 3 = 260.67 → rounded to **261** (3 sig figs, matching raw data) — full 2 marks only for correct rounded answer
- M24 Q4b: (219 + 217 + 218 + 215) ÷ 4 → average = **217** (rounded appropriately)
- N24 Q4b: (5.21 + 5.54 + 5.32) ÷ 3 = 5.3567 → **5.36** (2 decimal places to match raw data)
- M22 Q6d: Average count used, then stomatal density = count ÷ area → round to **89 mm⁻²** (2 sig figs)

Mark scheme tip: "Give your answer to an appropriate degree of accuracy" = match precision of raw data. "3 significant figures" = apply the rule literally.

## Calculating rates

**Rate = change in quantity ÷ time**

Examples:
- N20 Q4a: Heart rate (bpm) = heartbeats in 20 s ÷ 20 × 60 → e.g. 47 ÷ 20 × 60 = **141 bpm**
- N23 Q4b: Rate of CO₂ production = average CO₂ concentration ÷ time (ppm min⁻¹)
- N22 Q4b: Rate of transpiration = change in volume ÷ time (cm³ h⁻¹)
- N17 Q5b–c: Rate of osmosis = increase in mass ÷ time (g min⁻¹)

Always include the correct unit for rates:
- Heart rate → bpm (beats per minute)
- CO₂ production → ppm min⁻¹
- Transpiration → cm³ h⁻¹
- Osmosis → g min⁻¹

## Calculating percentages

**% increase = (new value − original value) ÷ original value × 100**

Examples:
- N20 Q6b: % increase between predicted heart rate and actual measured rate → 3 significant figures required
- M17 Q6d: % increase in pika body mass between lowest and highest altitude → answer to 3 sig figs
  - Working: 16.6 (difference) ÷ original value × 100 = 11.87%
- M18 Q1c: Calculate total protein needed daily → 10 g = 20%, so 100% = 50 g; unit mark required separately

**Significant figures rules**:
- "3 significant figures" = e.g. 11.9 NOT 11.87411 NOT 12 (12 has only 2 sig figs)
- N19 Q5g: Difference must be to **2 sig figs** — answer is 5.0 mg (not 5 mg)
- When the question says "appropriate degree of accuracy" — match the precision of the given data

## Describing trends — must reference data values

A common Criterion C error is vague trend descriptions that earn 0 marks.

**Wrong (vague)**: "The rate of resistance increases over time."
**Right (specific)**: "The percentage of resistant bacteria increased from 3.0% in 1999 to 8.9% in 2014, with a steeper increase after 2009."

Mark scheme patterns requiring data reference:
- N21 Q5e: "Explain the change in bacteria population during each stage" → must describe EACH stage with reference to graph features
- N22 Q5d: "Using data from the graph, explain the results" → must cite specific value: "the rate plateaus above 0.10% CO₂"
- M25 Q4h: "Comment on whether data supports the prediction" → must say "from 30 to 90% light intensity results support the hypothesis; beyond 90% they do not"
- M25 Q2c: Exercise graph → must reference specific numbers (e.g. "pulse rate increased from 75 to 120 bpm")
- N20 Q4d: "State the relationship" → "the quantity of oxygen decreases as altitude increases" (must state direction of BOTH variables)

Structure for describing a trend:
1. State the overall direction (increases / decreases / stays constant)
2. Reference the relevant data range or specific values from the graph/table
3. Note any turning points, plateaus, or anomalies

## Line of best fit rules

- Draw a smooth curve or straight line that passes **through the middle** of the data points
- Points should be **approximately equally distributed** above and below the line
- Do NOT join points dot-to-dot (that is a line graph, not a line of best fit)
- Do NOT force the line through the origin unless justified
- N20 Q6a: "Add a line of best fit to the graph" — LOBF must have points approximately equally above and below

Line of best fit is then used for:
- Reading off predicted values at a given x-value (N23 Q4d — predict rate at 20 minutes)
- Extrapolating trends beyond measured data

## Evaluating validity and reliability

### Key definitions

**Validity**: the method actually measures what it claims to measure; the results are a true reflection of the variable being tested

**Reliability**: results are consistent and reproducible; repeating gives similar results

### What makes results valid
- Control variables are properly controlled (only IV changes)
- DV is measured directly and accurately
- Method is appropriate for the research question
- Sufficient range of IV tested

### What makes results less valid / invalid
- Uncontrolled variables (confounding variables) could explain the results
- The range of IV is too narrow to draw a conclusion
- Only one trial (no repeats) — cannot confirm result is reproducible
- Different specimens/organisms used across trials (not comparable)
- Measuring equipment is imprecise
- The DV measured is not the same as what the hypothesis predicts

Examples from mark schemes:
- N20 Q4e: Data for Daphnia "not sufficient to draw valid conclusion" because: not enough trials (only one); not enough increments of temperature (only three); different Daphnia used for each temperature (so not comparable)
- M18 Q4d: Method not sufficient for relevant data because: range of pH not relevant to human body; only three pH values (not five); only one trial per condition
- M22 Q4e: Group 2 results more valid than Group 1 because: two significant figures (less rounding error); inclusion of a control (water, no dye)
- N22 Q4f: "Comment on validity of prediction" → results support from 20°C to 40°C but do not support above 40°C; or "insufficient data — only three temperatures tested"
- N21 Q4h (strengths and weaknesses of disinfectant method): weakness = only two trials; cannot identify outliers; hydrogen peroxide data was not reproducible

### Common validity improvement strategies

| Problem | Improvement |
|---|---|
| Only 1–2 trials | Increase to minimum 3 trials per condition |
| Too few IV levels | Use at least 5 different values of IV |
| No control condition | Add a 0% / baseline control |
| Different organisms each trial | Use the same organism (or matched organisms) |
| Measurement is subjective | Use objective measuring equipment (e.g. colorimeter, ruler, data logger) |
| Variables not controlled | Explicitly monitor and record CVs |

## The most common Criterion C errors

From recurring mark scheme exclusions and notes:

1. **Missing units on axes** — most common single-mark loss in every session
2. **Wrong graph type** — using a bar chart when data is continuous, or a line graph when IV is categorical
3. **Vague trend descriptions** — "the rate increases" without data values earns 0 marks
4. **Bar chart does not start at zero** — y-axis must begin at 0 for bar charts
5. **Uneven axis intervals** — e.g. 0, 5, 15, 25 instead of 0, 10, 20, 30
6. **Rounding to wrong precision** — too many or too few significant figures
7. **Line of best fit drawn as dot-to-dot** — must be a smooth curve/line through data
8. **Validity evaluated without evidence** — must cite specific data to support the evaluation
9. **Stating "the method is valid" or "invalid" without giving a reason** — the reason is always the mark
10. **Calculating rates without units** — rate answers must always have units (e.g. bpm, g min⁻¹)
11. **Calculating percentage increase from the wrong base** — always divide by the ORIGINAL (smaller) value
12. **Not identifying anomalous results (outliers)** — examiners expect acknowledgement of anomalous data before evaluating a conclusion

## Worked examples from mark schemes

### Example 1 — Calculating a mean (N22 Q4d)
Data: 262, 261, 259 for O₂ concentration at 40°C.
Mean = (262 + 261 + 259) ÷ 3 = 782 ÷ 3 = 260.67
Rounded to 3 sig figs = **261**
Mark scheme: 2 marks for 261; 0 marks for 260 or 260.67 (wrong precision)

### Example 2 — Calculating a rate (N20 Q4a)
Heartbeats in 20 seconds = 47
Heart rate in bpm = 47 ÷ 20 × 60 = **141 bpm**
Mark scheme: 1 mark for working shown; 1 mark for correct calculation; 1 mark for all three correct (141, 249, 288)

### Example 3 — Describing a trend with data (N21 Q5e, bacteria growth stages)
Stage 1 (slow growth): "Population grows slowly; plentiful resources but few bacteria to reproduce."
Stage 2 (rapid growth): "Population grows rapidly; plentiful resources; many bacteria reproducing."
Stage 3 (plateau): "No net population growth; as many bacteria dying as reproducing; limiting factors present."
Mark scheme: Do NOT accept "bacteria stop reproducing" for Stage 3.

### Example 4 — Evaluating validity (N22 Q4f)
Question: "Comment on the validity of the prediction that O₂ decreases as temperature increases."
Answer: "The results do NOT support the prediction from 20°C to 40°C (O₂ increases). The results DO support the prediction above 40°C. OR: There is insufficient data; at least five temperatures are needed to show a reliable trend."
Mark scheme: 1 mark each for the two parts.

### Example 5 — Identifying an anomaly (M21 Q5f)
Data: Trial 1 at 30°C = 2 bubbles; all other trials at 30°C = 6–7 bubbles.
Anomaly: Trial 1 at 30°C (value of 2)
What to do: Ignore/exclude the outlier when calculating the average.

### Example 6 — Calculating % increase (M17 Q6d)
Lower altitude body mass: ~140 g; higher altitude: ~156.6 g
Difference = 16.6 g
% increase = 16.6 ÷ 140 × 100 = 11.87%
To 3 sig figs = **11.9%**

### Example 7 — Stomatal density calculation (M22 Q6d)
Count per field of view: 7, 9, 8, 8, 10, 5, 10, 6, 8, 9 = total 80 stomata over 10 fields
Average count = 80 ÷ 10 = 8
Field area = 0.3 × 0.3 = 0.09 mm²
Stomatal density = 8 ÷ 0.09 = 88.9 → rounded to **89 mm⁻²**

## Revision priorities

1. Practise drawing bar charts and scatter graphs with correctly labelled axes and units — aim for zero dropped marks on presentation
2. Memorise the rate calculation formula and always include units in your answer
3. Use the "describe + data value + explain" structure for all trend description questions
4. Know the three-part validity evaluation: what the conclusion is, what evidence supports it, and what limits it
5. Always include a MINIMUM of 3 trials and 5 IV increments in any designed investigation (Criterion B) — this directly links to Criterion C data quality
6. Practise rounding to significant figures: 3 sig figs, 2 sig figs, appropriate decimal places
7. Learn the LOBF rules: smooth, equal distribution of points above and below, do not join dots
