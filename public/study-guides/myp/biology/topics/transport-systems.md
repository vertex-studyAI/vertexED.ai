# Transport Systems

## Topic overview

Transport systems covers both **plant transport** (xylem, phloem, transpiration, stomata, osmosis) and **animal transport** (heart and blood vessels, blood cells, gas exchange, alveoli). Appears under Criterion A (knowledge), Criterion C (data processing from transpiration experiments), and Criterion B (design of transpiration investigations).

---

## Key concepts

### Plant transport — xylem and phloem

**Xylem** (M17 Q8c, M18 Q7a, N18 Q7a):
- Transports water and mineral nutrients from roots to leaves
- Unidirectional (upward)
- Water enters roots by osmosis and moves up via xylem due to transpiration pull

**Phloem** (N17 Q1, M17 Q8c, N19 Q2d):
- Transports sugars (glucose/sucrose) produced by photosynthesis
- Bidirectional: from where sugars are produced (leaves) to where they are used (roots, growing tips)
- Process is called **translocation**
- N19 Q2d: "The roots cannot produce glucose but need it for growth. Outline how the roots obtain glucose." — Answer: sugar moves down phloem from where it is produced to where it is used; correct use of term translocation or phloem earns the Criterion D mark

**Nutrients taken up by roots** (M18 Q7a, N18 Q7a):
- Taken in by the roots
- By diffusion OR active transport
- Transported in the xylem / vascular tissue to the leaves
- Used for synthesis of organic molecules / amino acids / plant compounds / growth

Mark scheme note: "Xylem" or "vascular tissue" or "transport tissue" are all accepted for the vessel name.

### Transpiration

- **Transpiration** = loss of water vapour from the leaves through stomata
- Acts as a driving force pulling water up through xylem (transpiration pull)
- Rate of transpiration is affected by: light intensity, temperature, air movement (wind), humidity

**N17 Q1a–d** — transpiration basics:
- Turgid cell = filled with water; cell wall prevents bursting (N17 Q1b)
- Plasmolysed cell = severely dehydrated; plant wilts or goes floppy (N17 Q1c)
- Marram grass leaf adaptations reduce water loss (N17 Q1d):
  - Curled/rolled leaves: trap humid air near stomata → increases humidity → reduces evaporation gradient → less transpiration
  - Hairy inner surface: traps water vapour
  - Sunken/inner stomata: less exposed to wind
  - Waxy cuticle: impermeable surface reduces evaporation
  - Mark scheme requires: structure named → mechanism → outcome (reduced transpiration)

**M22 Q4 — transpiration investigation (17 marks)**:
- IV: colour of solution / food colouring added to water (M22 Q4a)
- Rate of transpiration = change in volume of water ÷ time (cm³ h⁻¹) (M22 Q4b)
- DV: volume/amount of water in beaker (measure initial and final volumes; record time)
- CVs: temperature of environment/water; species of plant; wind/air movement; dimensions of stem; surface area of leaves; light intensity (M22 Q4c)
- Sealing beakers improvement: prevents direct evaporation from beaker; ensures change in volume is due to transpiration only (M22 Q4g)

**M22 Q5 — potometer investigation design (17 marks, Criterion B)**:
- Potometer: device that measures rate of transpiration by tracking movement of an air bubble in a capillary tube
- IV: air movement (fan speed)
- DV: distance bubble moves per unit time (cm/min) OR rate of transpiration
- CV: temperature; light intensity; type of plant; leaf surface area
- Data: at least 5 fan speeds; at least 3 trials; calculate average; include a control (no fan)
- Hypothesis: if air movement increases, transpiration rate increases because increased evaporation from leaf surface removes water vapour faster (steeper concentration gradient)

### Stomata

**Structure and function** (M22 Q6, M25):
- Stomata are pores in the leaf surface, surrounded by guard cells
- Open in light (for CO₂ entry for photosynthesis); close in dark / dry conditions (to reduce water loss)
- Gas exchange occurs through stomata: CO₂ in, O₂ out (during photosynthesis); O₂ in, CO₂ out (during respiration)

**Stomatal density** (M22 Q6):
- Stomatal density = number of stomata ÷ area (stomata/mm²)
- More stomata = higher rate of gas exchange AND higher transpiration rate

Comparison of species (M22 Q6g):
- Sunflower (temperate): stomata on both surfaces because transpiration happens on both sides
- Water lily (floats on water): stomata only on the upper surface because lower surface is in contact with water → no gas exchange between stomata and water
- Pondweed (submerged): no stomata because transpiration does not occur underwater; gases diffuse through the epidermis

Why random sample (M22 Q6a): too difficult to sample all leaves; to ensure sample is representative and avoid bias

### Osmosis and plant cells

**Osmosis** = movement of water from a region of low solute concentration (high water potential) to a region of high solute concentration (low water potential) through a semi-permeable membrane, until equilibrium

- Turgid = cell full of water; cell wall prevents further expansion → provides structural support
- Flaccid = cell has lost water; turgor pressure decreases → plant wilts
- Plasmolysed = cell membrane pulls away from cell wall in very concentrated solution

**M24 Q7b** — dehydrated cell: more solute outside → water moves outward by osmosis
- Draw large arrow pointing out; small arrow pointing in (net outward movement)

**Transpiration pull**: Water evaporates from leaf → concentration gradient → water drawn up xylem → water absorbed from soil by osmosis through root hairs

### Animal transport — the heart and blood vessels

**Heart structure** (M25 Q2 context):
- Four chambers: two atria (receive blood) + two ventricles (pump blood)
- Left side: oxygenated blood → body
- Right side: deoxygenated blood → lungs
- Valves prevent backflow

**Blood vessels**:
- **Arteries**: carry blood AWAY from heart; thick, elastic walls; withstand high pressure (M21 Q8a)
  - Arteries have more elastic fibres and thicker/more muscular walls than veins (M21 Q8a)
- **Veins**: carry blood TOWARD heart; thinner walls; valves prevent backflow
- **Capillaries**: site of exchange; one cell thick; very small diameter

**Effect of exercise on heart and breathing rate** (M25 Q2c — 5 marks):
- During exercise (3–10 min): pulse rate increases (e.g. from 75 to 120 bpm); breathing rate increases (e.g. from 20 to 40 breaths/min)
- Reason: faster cellular respiration / more energy released
- Further: supplies cells/muscles with more oxygen; removes CO₂ faster; supplies glucose faster
- Data values MUST be referenced for full marks — e.g. "pulse rate increased from 75 to 120 bpm"

**High blood pressure (hypertension)** (M21 Q8a):
- Stress → temporary increase in blood pressure
- Arteries better withstand pressure than veins: thicker walls; more elastic fibres; more muscular

**Blood pressure medication** (M21 Q8c):
- Medication causes blood vessels to stop contracting / relax → vasodilation
- Blood vessel lumen increases in diameter → same blood volume flows through larger space → pressure drops
- Criterion D mark: correct use of vasodilation, dilate, lumen, volume

### Gas exchange and alveoli

**Diffusion of oxygen through alveoli** (M25 Q2a, N23 Q1f):
- Oxygen moves from high concentration (lungs/alveoli) to low concentration (blood)
- Through the alveolar membrane / capillary wall
- Do NOT say red blood cells are the destination — oxygen enters the blood first

**Alveoli structural adaptations** (N23 Q1f, M25 Q2):
- Large surface area (many alveoli) → more efficient diffusion
- One cell thick / short diffusion distance → efficient transfer
- Good blood supply / capillary network → maintains concentration gradient
- Moist surface → dissolves gases

**Red blood cell adaptations for gas transport** (N22 Q2e, N24 Q1b):
- No nucleus → more space for haemoglobin (which binds oxygen)
- Haemoglobin → binds to oxygen to transport it
- Biconcave disc shape → maximises surface area to volume ratio
- Small and flexible → can pass through capillaries

Mark scheme note: Feature must be linked to its function. "No nucleus" alone without linking to "more space/haemoglobin" earns only 1 mark per pair.

**White blood cells** (N24 Q1b, M25 Q2d):
- Produce antibodies OR fight infection / immune defence
- High white blood cell count may indicate active immune response to infection
- M25 Q2d: Victor has highest WBC → most likely to resist viral infection

**Blood clotting — platelets**: help seal wounds and prevent blood loss

## Mark scheme insights

1. **Transpiration rate calculation (M22 Q4b)**: Must identify BOTH initial and final volumes AND time. Rate = (V₁ − V₂) ÷ time. Missing any one of these components loses the mark.
2. **Red blood cell features (N22 Q2e)**: Mark as pairs — feature + explanation. Feature without explanation earns 1/2 marks available.
3. **Alveoli adaptations (N23 Q1f)**: Large surface area is ALWAYS worth a mark; second mark from: 1 cell thick / moist / good blood supply.
4. **Exercise graph (M25 Q2c)**: Must reference specific data values from the graph. "Pulse rate increased" earns 1 mark; "from 75 to 120 bpm" earns the data reference mark.
5. **Stomata distribution in water lily (M22 Q6g)**: Must explain WHY stomata are only on top — not just describe the distribution. Reason: lower surface is in contact with water; gas exchange between stomata and air, not water.
6. **Phloem and translocation (N19 Q2d)**: The Criterion D mark in this question is awarded for correctly using the term "translocation" or "phloem" — not just describing sugar movement.
7. **Potometer design (M22 Q5)**: A "control condition" (no fan/zero fan speed) is required for full data marks. Including the 0% condition and ≥5 different fan speeds are both needed.
8. **Artery vs vein (M21 Q8a)**: "More elastic fibres" is the key point. "Thicker walls" is also accepted. Do NOT accept "arteries carry oxygenated blood" as it is irrelevant to pressure.

## Common mistakes

- Confusing xylem (water/minerals upward) with phloem (sugars, bidirectional)
- Describing transpiration as just "water evaporating" without mentioning stomata or the driving force
- Not linking red blood cell features to their function (feature alone = half marks)
- Omitting data values when explaining exercise graph results
- Saying gas exchange occurs "between stomata and water" in pondweed — gases diffuse through the epidermis, not stomata
- Forgetting that osmosis requires a semi-permeable membrane (N20 MS — "through a semi-permeable membrane" is required)
- Writing "water concentration" instead of "water potential" (explicitly rejected by N20 MS)
- Describing stomata function as only for water — they are also the site of gas exchange (CO₂ and O₂)

## Revision priorities

1. Know the differences between xylem and phloem: direction, substance transported, and the driving process
2. Transpiration investigation: IV, DV, CVs, how to calculate rate, why seal the beaker
3. Red blood cell adaptations: three features (no nucleus, haemoglobin, biconcave shape) each linked to function
4. Alveoli adaptations: surface area, thin walls, moist surface, good blood supply — all linked to efficient diffusion
5. Exercise graph: practise reading data values and linking increased pulse/breathing to cell respiration needs
6. Stomata: role in gas exchange AND transpiration; distribution in different plant types; stomatal density calculation
7. Osmosis direction in cells: draw arrows showing net movement toward higher solute concentration
