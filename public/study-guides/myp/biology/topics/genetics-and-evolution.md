# Genetics and Evolution

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### DNA and Genes

- **DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid):** A double-stranded helix made of nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.
- **Base pairing rules:** A–T (adenine–thymine); G–C (guanine–cytosine). (N23 Q1b)
- **Gene:** A section of DNA that codes for a specific protein (or trait).
- **Allele:** A different version of the same gene (e.g. the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown and blue).
- **Chromosome:** A long molecule of DNA coiled around proteins (histones). Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
- **Genome:** The complete set of genetic information in an organism.

**Critical distinction:** "Gene" and "allele" are NOT the same. Using them interchangeably in a mark scheme answer triggers a **CON (contradiction)** = 0 marks for that section in many sessions.

---

### Dominant and Recessive Alleles

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Dominant | Allele whose phenotype is expressed even when only one copy is present. Written as capital letter (e.g. A). |
| Recessive | Allele whose phenotype is only expressed when two copies are present (homozygous). Written as lowercase (e.g. a). |
| Homozygous | Two identical alleles (AA or aa). |
| Heterozygous | Two different alleles (Aa). |
| Genotype | The alleles an organism has (e.g. Aa). |
| Phenotype | The physical characteristic expressed (e.g. brown eyes). |
| Carrier | Organism that is heterozygous for a recessive condition — does not show the phenotype but can pass the allele to offspring. |

---

### Punnett Squares

**Method:**
1. Identify the alleles for each parent.
2. Write parent genotypes (e.g. Aa × Aa).
3. Create a 2×2 grid; write one parent's gametes across the top and the other's down the side.
4. Fill in each box by combining the alleles.
5. Calculate the ratio of offspring genotypes and phenotypes.

**Worked example — M21 Q1d (dimples, dominant):**

Parents: Dd (dimples) × dd (no dimples)

|   | D | d |
|---|---|---|
| **d** | Dd | dd |
| **d** | Dd | dd |

Results: 50% Dd (dimples) : 50% dd (no dimples)
Ratio: 1 with dimples : 1 without dimples

**Worked example — N23 Q1d (cystic fibrosis, recessive):**

CF allele = c (recessive); normal = C
Parents: Cc (carrier) × Cc (carrier)

|   | C | c |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | CC | Cc |
| **c** | Cc | cc |

Results: 25% CC (normal) : 50% Cc (carrier) : 25% cc (CF)
Probability of child having CF = 1 in 4 = 25%

**Mark scheme rules for Punnett squares:**
- Must draw the grid (not just state the result).
- Must show the gametes.
- Ratio must be expressed correctly (e.g. 1:2:1 or 3:1).
- Percentage or fraction also accepted (25%, 1/4).

### Inheritance Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Autosomal dominant | One copy of dominant allele enough; affects males and females equally | Dimples (M21), Huntington's disease |
| Autosomal recessive | Two copies of recessive allele needed; carriers exist | Cystic fibrosis (N23), lactose intolerance (M23) |
| Codominance | Both alleles expressed; neither is dominant | ABO blood groups |
| Sex-linked | Gene is on X chromosome; usually recessive; more common in males | Colour blindness, haemophilia |

**M17 Q3 (hearing loss):** Genetic deafness in this context was autosomal recessive. Students needed to explain why two hearing parents could have a deaf child — both parents must be carriers (Aa × Aa → 1 in 4 chance of aa).

**N22 Q3 (sex determination):** Males = XY; Females = XX. Sperm determine the sex of the offspring (carry either X or Y). Eggs always carry X.

### Natural Selection

**Darwin's four requirements for natural selection to occur:**

1. **Variation** — individuals in a population show variation in their traits (often due to mutations or genetic recombination).
2. **Selection pressure** — an environmental factor that affects survival (e.g. predation, disease, temperature, food availability).
3. **Differential survival / "survival of the fittest"** — individuals with favourable adaptations are more likely to survive and reproduce.
4. **Inheritance** — favourable traits are passed to offspring; proportion of allele increases in the population over generations.

**Key phrases (from mark schemes across all sessions):**
- "Random mutation produces variation"
- "Better adapted individuals survive to reproduce"
- "Pass on favourable alleles to offspring"
- "Frequency of the allele increases over generations"

**M18 Q1 (peppered moths):** Before industrialisation — light moths survived better on light bark. After — dark moths had better camouflage on soot-darkened trees; light moths were predated more → dark allele increased in frequency.

**M19 Q3d (rabbits):** Myxomatosis — resistant rabbits survived, reproduced, passed resistance allele to offspring.

**M22 Q7 (bacteria that eat plastic):** Random mutation produced enzyme to digest PET; bacteria with this mutation out-competed others in plastic-polluted environments.

**N20 Q1d (natural selection general):** Must include all four stages — variation, selection pressure, survival, inheritance/allele frequency change. Missing "inheritance" step = lose 1 mark.

---

### Adaptation

**Structural adaptations:** Physical features (e.g. thick fur in arctic animals, long roots in desert plants).
**Behavioural adaptations:** Actions (e.g. migration, hibernation).
**Physiological adaptations:** Internal processes (e.g. camels produce very concentrated urine).

---

### Speciation

- **Speciation** is the formation of a new species from an existing one.
- Occurs when two populations of the same species become **reproductively isolated** (cannot interbreed).
- Isolation can be geographic (mountains, oceans) or behavioural.
- Over many generations, natural selection acts differently on each population → genetic differences accumulate → new species.

**M24 Q2 (killer whales):** Different orca populations may be becoming separate species due to differences in diet, habitat, and behaviour. Key concept: reproductive isolation → divergence → speciation.

---

### Extinction

- Extinction occurs when all members of a species die out.
- Causes: habitat destruction, hunting, invasive species, climate change, disease.
- **Mass extinction:** Loss of many species in a short geological time period.

---

### Genetic Modification (GM)

**Process:**
1. Identify the gene of interest in a donor organism.
2. Cut out the gene using **restriction enzymes**.
3. Insert the gene into a vector (usually a bacterial plasmid) using **ligase** enzymes.
4. Introduce the vector into the target organism.
5. Target organism expresses the new protein.

**M17 Q8 (American chestnut tree):** GM wheat gene inserted to give resistance to chestnut blight fungus. Benefit: restore species to forests; Concern: GM pollen may spread to wild relatives; ecological unpredictability.

**N18 Q7/Q8 (biofortified crops — Golden Rice):** GM rice with genes for beta-carotene production. Benefit: reduce vitamin A deficiency; Concern: may reduce biodiversity; farmers lose control of seed supply.

**N19 Q6 (insulin):** Human insulin gene inserted into bacteria → bacteria produce human insulin for diabetics. Example of a beneficial medical application.

**N25 Q4/Q5 (pesticide resistance):** GMO crops engineered to be resistant to herbicides or insects.

---

### Selective Breeding vs Genetic Engineering

| Feature | Selective breeding | Genetic engineering |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Method | Choose individuals with desired traits; breed them repeatedly | Directly insert genes from any organism |
| Speed | Slow — many generations | Fast — one step |
| Precision | Imprecise — many genes transferred | Very precise — single gene |
| Cross-species transfer? | No — same species only | Yes — any two species |
| Examples | Dairy cattle, wheat varieties | Insulin-producing bacteria, GM crops |

**N17 Q3c:** Students asked to compare selective breeding and genetic engineering — must explain at least two specific differences for full marks.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

- **Natural selection:** All four stages must be present for full marks. Simply saying "survival of the fittest" without explaining the mechanism = 1/4 marks.
- **Punnett squares:** Must show the grid. Correct answer without the diagram — partial credit only.
- **Gene vs allele:** Mixing them up (e.g. "the gene for blue eyes") — marked as CON in some sessions = 0 marks for that answer.
- **GM process:** Must mention restriction enzymes and ligase (or equivalent) in extended answers about the mechanism.
- **Dominant/recessive:** Correctly identify which is which from context clues before drawing the Punnett square.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing "survival of the fittest" without explanation | State: variation exists → better adapted individuals survive → reproduce → pass allele to offspring → frequency increases |
| Confusing gene and allele | Gene = section of DNA for a trait; allele = one version of that gene |
| Forgetting to show gametes on Punnett square | Always show parent gametes at the top and side of the grid |
| Stating wrong dominant/recessive | Read question carefully for who shows the trait and with what frequency |
| "GM introduces new alleles" | Technically a new gene from a different organism — be precise: "gene for insulin was transferred from human DNA into a bacterial plasmid" |
| Saying speciation = extinction | Speciation = new species formed; extinction = species dies out |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Practise drawing Punnett squares for dominant, recessive, and carrier crosses.
2. Memorise the 4 steps of natural selection with correct biological language.

5. Understand speciation: reproductive isolation → genetic divergence → new species.
6. For exam questions asking about "concerns" with GM — use ecological (gene flow, biodiversity) and ethical (consent, labelling, corporate control) arguments.
7. Know base-pairing rules cold: A–T, G–C.
