# Health and Disease

## Topic overview

Covers the causes of disease, how the body defends itself, medical and surgical interventions, and the links between lifestyle and non-communicable disease. Appears primarily under Criterion A (knowledge) and Criterion D (impacts/evaluation).

---

## Key concepts

### Pathogens and infection

- A **pathogen** is an organism (or virus) that causes disease or infection (N18 Q2a)
- Pathogens include bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites
- Cholera is caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, transmitted via contaminated water (N18 Q2c)
- Cholera outbreaks follow natural disasters because water/sewage infrastructure is damaged, leaving people without access to clean water (N18 Q2c)

### The body's defences

**Non-specific (physical) barriers:**
- Skin acts as a physical barrier; also uses pH, oils, and sweat to prevent infection (N18 Q2d)
- White blood cells: destroy/engulf pathogens or produce antibodies in the bloodstream (N18 Q2d)

**Specific immune response:**
- White blood cells (lymphocytes) produce specific antibodies against a pathogen (N18 Q2e)
- Memory cells remain after infection so a second exposure triggers a faster, stronger response (N18 Q2e)

### Vaccination and immunity

How vaccinations work (N18 Q2e — 5 marks):
1. Vaccine is a weakened or inactive form of the pathogen, or parts of it
2. Triggers specific white blood cells / lymphocytes
3. Immune system produces specific antibodies
4. Memory cells are retained
5. Subsequent infection: quicker response; person does not develop symptoms (or symptoms are mild)

Mark scheme tip: Do NOT use the word "immunity" to explain how immunity is achieved — describe the mechanism.

Advantages and disadvantages of vaccinating children (N23 Q7b):
- Advantage: can be included in regular vaccination schedule; guaranteed dose; develops immunity
- Disadvantage: supply issues in remote areas; does not cure disease in already-infected organisms; possible side effects

### Antibiotics and antibiotic resistance

- Antibiotics prevent bacterial growth or kill bacteria, allowing the body's immune system to fight infection (N21 Q5 context)
- Antibiotic resistance: some bacteria are no longer killed by certain antibiotics (N21 Q5 context)

How bacteria evolve resistance (N21 Q5a):
1. Variation exists: some individuals are naturally resistant
2. Antibiotic is added: sensitive bacteria are killed
3. Resistant individuals survive and multiply, passing on the resistance trait
4. New population is resistant; antibiotic no longer effective

Key MS note: Do NOT say bacteria become "immune" — use "resistant." Evolution through natural selection, not adaptation during a lifetime.

Most effective antibiotic: the one with the largest, clearest zone of inhibition (N21 Q5c — Trimethoprim selected)

### Cardiovascular disease (CVD)

**Atherosclerosis** (N21 Q7a):
- Too much cholesterol → fatty plaque builds up in arteries
- Artery becomes narrowed; blood flow to heart muscle is reduced
- Risk of coronary heart disease / heart attack increases
- High cholesterol caused by: unhealthy diet (salty, sugary, processed foods), physical inactivity, smoking, age (N21 Q7 context)

**Statins** (N21 Q7b):
- Enzymes in the liver convert substance A into cholesterol
- Statins block (inhibit) these enzymes / block the active site
- This prevents / reduces production of cholesterol in the liver
- Mark scheme tip: Do NOT say statins "reflect" the active site

Advantages of statins: effective long-term; easy to take (tablet form); lowers LDL cholesterol
Disadvantages of statins: must be taken regularly; possible side effects (headache, nausea, reduced vitamin D/hormones); does not address cholesterol caused by genetics alone

**Angioplasty and stents** (N21 Q7c):
- Balloon catheter inserted into blocked artery
- Balloon inflated: squeezes plaque against artery walls / widens the artery
- Stent (tube) left in place to keep artery open
- More blood can flow; heart does not need to pump as hard

Advantages of angioplasty: effective long-term; one-time procedure; short recovery time
Disadvantages: risk of complications (heart attack, infection, thrombosis); does not lower cholesterol levels

### Diabetes

- Diabetes is linked to diet and an inability to regulate blood sugar (N19 Q6 context)
- People with diabetes do not produce insulin or cannot respond to insulin (N19 Q6c)
- Fast-acting insulin is taken before meals to rapidly lower blood sugar as it rises after eating
- Blood sugar regulation is an example of **homeostasis** — the maintenance of a constant internal environment (N19 Q6a)
- GM bacteria are used to produce human insulin industrially (see also: Biotechnology topic)

### Cancer

- Cancer arises when normal cell division via mitosis becomes uncontrolled due to abnormal genetic changes (N22 Q8 context)
- Risk factors: smoking (lung cancer), alcohol (liver cancer), sun/UV exposure (skin cancer) (N22 Q8 context)
- Incidence rates increase with age: greater cumulative exposure to risk factors; more mutations accumulate over time (N22 Q8b)

Cancer data patterns (N22 Q8a):
- Both males and females: new cases increase with age then decline
- Difference: male cases higher from age 60–84; females slightly higher 0–59 and 85+
- Male cases peak at 65–74; female cases peak slightly earlier

Sun exposure: discuss and evaluate (N22 Q8c):
- Benefits: vitamin D production → calcium absorption and strong bones; good for mental health / SAD; enables outdoor activity
- Risks: increased risk of skin cancer; cataracts; sunburn
- Advice to minimise risks: use sunscreen; limit peak-hour exposure; wear protective clothing

### Lifestyle factors and individual vs society

N21 Q8 (15 marks, Criterion D) — key content required:
- Impact on quality of life: reduced ability to work; pain; reduced mobility; increased medical costs
- Economic advantages of healthy lifestyle: reduced healthcare costs; productivity gains; lower insurance premiums
- Economic disadvantages: cost of healthy food; gym memberships; time
- Individual responsibility: personal choices; informed decision-making
- Society responsibility: education; access to healthy food; regulation of food industry; healthcare funding
- Conclusion required

### Personalized medicine

N22 Q9 (14 marks, Criterion D):
- DNA sequencing used to determine which version of a disease a patient has
- Predicts how individual patients will respond to different drugs and doses
- Medical benefits: correct treatment; fewer side effects; early detection; benefits wider society by reducing costs from misdiagnosis
- Economic: DNA sequencing costs falling; significant upfront investment; could reduce long-term costs; inequality in access
- Ethical: data ownership and privacy; consent for genetic information storage; insurance/employer discrimination; using predictive data before illness manifests
- Conclusion: beneficial but requires regulation of data use, investment, and equitable access

### Mental health

- Stress is a lifestyle factor affecting mental and physical health (M21 Q8 context)
- Eustress (work stress) can lead to increased blood pressure, tiredness, lack of focus, trouble sleeping
- Long-term stress → hypertension → increased risk of heart attack or stroke
- Melatonin is suppressed by cortisol (stress hormone); low melatonin → difficulty sleeping (M21 Q10)
- Exercise improves mood and mental health; advantages include weight loss, improved fitness, reduced risk of heart disease; disadvantages include injury risk and compliance

## Mark scheme insights

1. **Atherosclerosis chain**: Must link cholesterol → plaque → narrowing → reduced blood flow → heart at risk. Partial chains lose marks.
2. **Statins**: Require two linked steps — enzyme blocked AND cholesterol production reduced. One step earns 1 mark.
3. **Vaccination**: Five-point process is commonly tested at 5 marks. Memory cells are the key "bonus" point. Do NOT say "weakened virus" as the only answer — must describe what the immune system does with it.
4. **Discuss/Evaluate questions**: Must include individual AND societal perspectives, economic arguments, AND a concluding statement. Missing any element is capped by the markband.
5. **Cancer question**: When asked to state similarity and difference from a graph, must use specific data values, not just "both increase."
6. **Sun exposure**: Balanced discussion required — credit is lost if only benefits or only risks are discussed.
7. **Antibiotic resistance**: Do NOT say bacteria "adapt" or become "immune." Use "resistant" and reference natural selection mechanism.

## Common mistakes

- Saying bacteria become "immune" to antibiotics instead of "resistant"
- Describing statins as "reflecting" the enzyme active site rather than blocking it
- Saying enzymes "die" when denatured (N21 Q3c — Do NOT accept "die")
- Writing "immunity" without explaining the mechanism (antibodies + memory cells)
- Omitting economic or societal dimension in Criterion D discuss questions
- Missing the conclusion in extended response questions
- Confusing correlation and causation when discussing lifestyle and disease risk
- Saying "the data supports the hypothesis" without quoting actual data values
