# Homeostasis

## Topic overview

Homeostasis is one of the highest-frequency topics in MYP Biology. It appears in almost every session and spans Criterion A (definition and mechanism), Criterion D (impacts), and sometimes Criterion B/C (experimental design around variables like temperature). Key sub-topics: definition, negative feedback, thermoregulation, blood sugar regulation (insulin/glucagon), osmoregulation, and kidney/dialysis.

---

## Key concepts

### Definition of homeostasis

- **Homeostasis** = the maintenance of a constant (stable) internal environment (N19 Q6a, N20 Q2a, N23 Q2c, M25 Q1h)
- WTTE accepted: "maintaining conditions within the body within a narrow range"
- Do NOT define homeostasis using only one named example (e.g. "blood glucose stays at X") — N23 MS explicitly rejects this

The body maintains:
- Body temperature (36.5°C–37.5°C) (N21 Q3)
- Blood glucose levels (N19 Q6, M25 Q1g)
- Water balance / osmoregulation (N20 Q2, M24 Q7a)
- Blood pH / CO₂ concentration (N23 Q2f)

### Negative feedback

- Negative feedback is the mechanism by which homeostasis works
- When a variable moves away from the set point, the body's response brings it back
- The response **opposes** the change (negative = opposing)
- Examples: if body temperature rises → body cools down; if blood glucose rises → insulin released to lower it

Diagram structure tested in N21 Q3:
- Normal body temperature (36.5–37.5°C) → temperature rises → body systems interact → temperature falls → back to normal (and the reverse cycle)

### Thermoregulation

**Systems involved** (N21 Q3a):
- Nervous system — monitors/detects temperature change
- Cardiovascular system OR muscular system — maintains temperature

**Response to cold temperature** (N21 Q3b, N23 Q2d):

Any pathway for 3 marks (N21 Q3b):
1. Nervous system detects reduction in temperature → vasoconstriction (blood vessels narrow / blood diverted away from extremities) → reduces heat loss
2. Nervous system detects reduction → hairs rise → reduces heat loss
3. Nervous system detects reduction → shivering (involuntary muscle contractions) → produces heat / increases cellular respiration in muscles

**Response to high temperature** (N23 Q2e — 6 marks):
- Identify sweating: sweat evaporates → cooling effect / heat lost to surroundings
- Identify vasodilation: blood vessels widen → increased blood flow to skin surface → more heat lost to surroundings
- Identify hair lying flat: no trapped air layer → insulation reduced → more heat lost
- Only award explanation mark if identification mark awarded first

**Why body temperature must stay 36.5–37.5°C** (N21 Q3c):
- Enzymes have an optimum temperature at this range
- Too cold → reactions do not occur fast enough
- Too hot → enzymes change shape (denature) → no longer function (cannot bind substrate)
- Terminology bonus mark (Criterion D): using active site / denature / catalyze / lock and key / substrate

Mark scheme note: Do NOT say enzymes "die" — say they denature or change shape.

**N23 Q2d** — additional responses to cold:
- Shivering OR vasoconstriction (accept a description of either)

### Blood glucose regulation (insulin and glucagon)

**Insulin** (N19 Q6, M25 Q1g):
- Produced by the pancreas
- Lowers blood glucose levels
- People with diabetes do not produce insulin or cannot respond to it
- Fast-acting insulin: taken before a meal because eating raises blood glucose; insulin lowers it before it rises too high (N19 Q6c)

**Glucagon** (M25 Q1g):
- Produced by the pancreas
- Raises blood glucose levels (the opposite of insulin)

**Key mark scheme rule (M25 Q1g)**: Only award mark for action if the correct hormone has been named. Glucagon raises; insulin lowers.

**M25 Q1h**: "Blood sugar levels, body temperature, and water balance are maintained within a narrow range. State the name of this regulating process." → Homeostasis

**M25 Q5c**: Temperature and plant growth — same enzyme denaturation logic applies:
- Increase in temperature → increased photosynthesis (growth increases)
- Beyond ~45°C → enzymes denature OR stomata close → photosynthesis stops → growth stops

**N21 Q3c** is also Criterion D for using scientific terminology correctly.

### Osmoregulation and the kidney

**Dialysis** (N20 Q2):
- Kidneys filter blood: remove excess water, toxins, waste products; regulate minerals; maintain pH
- If kidneys fail, dialysis replaces this function
- Blood passes through a dialyzer containing fine fibres with pores
- Processes used: diffusion and osmosis

**Diffusion** (N20 Q2c): movement of solute/salt/particles from high to low concentration
**Osmosis** (N20 Q2c): movement of water from low salt concentration (high water potential) to high salt concentration (low water potential) through a semi-permeable membrane — until equilibrium
- Do NOT say "water concentration" — say "water potential" (N20 MS)

**Why dialysis fluid is continuously removed** (N20 Q2d):
- To maintain a concentration gradient between blood and dialysis fluid
- If not removed: toxin/salt removal from blood will stop; toxins build up

**Why many fine tubes rather than one large tube** (N20 Q2e):
- Greater surface area for diffusion/osmosis
- Rate of salt/toxin removal is faster and more efficient

**Dehydration effects** (M24 Q7a — 4 marks):
- Excretion: waste builds up / more water reabsorbed → urine volume decreases / urine becomes more concentrated
- Temperature control: sweat production decreases / less evaporative cooling → harder to regulate body temperature / harder to cool down

**Osmosis arrows in a dehydrated cell** (M24 Q7b):
- More solute outside the cell → water moves OUT of the cell by osmosis (net movement outward)
- Shows: large arrow pointing outward + small arrow pointing inward

**M24 Q7a** dehydration and excretion: constipation / harder stools accepted for excretion impact

### Homeostasis as negative feedback (general)

Other examples of negative feedback (N23 Q2f — accept any one):
- Blood glucose levels
- Water balance
- Certain hormone levels
- CO₂ concentration / blood pH

## Mark scheme insights

1. **Homeostasis definition**: "Maintenance of a constant internal environment." Do NOT accept a single named example as a complete definition.
2. **Thermoregulation 3-mark chain (N21 Q3b)**: Must show all three steps — detection by nervous system → physiological response → outcome (heat retained or produced). Missing the third step loses a mark.
3. **Enzyme denaturation (N21 Q3c)**: "Change shape" is accepted; "die" is NOT. Must link shape change to loss of function.
4. **Criterion D terminology bonus (N21 Q3c)**: One extra mark for correctly using any of: active site, denature, catalyze, lock and key, substrate.
5. **Osmosis direction (M24 Q7b)**: Must draw BOTH arrows (net outward = large arrow out; small arrow in). Net movement only is insufficient for 2 marks.
6. **Dialysis (N20 Q2d)**: Two-part answer — WHY fluid is removed (maintain concentration gradient) AND what happens if it is not removed (toxins build up).
7. **ORA (own response accepted) in N21 Q3b**: If student named different systems in Q3a, the mark scheme accepts their own systems' response.
8. **Dehydration (M24 Q7a)**: Each effect has TWO marks — the initial change AND its consequence. E.g., "sweat production decreases" (1 mark) AND "harder to regulate body temperature" (1 mark).

## Common mistakes

- Defining homeostasis with only an example ("blood glucose is kept at 5 mmol/L") rather than the general principle
- Saying enzymes "die" instead of "denature" when temperatures are too high
- Forgetting the third step in the thermoregulation chain (what the physiological response achieves)
- Mixing up vasoconstriction (cold response) and vasodilation (hot response)
- In osmosis questions: writing "water concentration" instead of "water potential" (N20 MS rejects "water concentration")
- Not specifying the direction of osmosis (which way is more concentrated?)
- Forgetting to mention the concentration gradient when explaining dialysis
- Confusing insulin (lowers blood glucose) with glucagon (raises blood glucose)

## Revision priorities

1. Definition of homeostasis — short, precise, general (not example-specific)
2. Three mechanisms of thermoregulation in cold: vasoconstriction, shivering, hairs rising — and for heat: sweating, vasodilation, hairs lying flat
3. Enzyme logic: optimum temperature → denaturation above it → "change shape" NOT "die"
4. Insulin vs glucagon: which hormone does what; why fast-acting insulin is needed before meals
5. Dialysis: diffusion + osmosis; fine tubes (surface area); continuous fluid removal (maintain gradient)
6. Dehydration: effects on excretion (less/concentrated urine) AND temperature control (less sweating → overheating)
7. Negative feedback concept — be able to give examples beyond the one in the question
