# Forces and Motion

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Speed, Velocity, and Acceleration

Speed is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude only and is measured in m s⁻¹ or km h⁻¹. Velocity is the vector equivalent: it has both magnitude and direction. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity and is also a vector, measured in m s⁻².

| Quantity | Type | Formula | Unit |
|----------|------|---------|------|
| Speed | Scalar | distance ÷ time | m s⁻¹ |
| Velocity | Vector | displacement ÷ time | m s⁻¹ |
| Acceleration | Vector | change in velocity ÷ time | m s⁻² |

**N16 Q1a (1 mark — Calculate):** A bar-tailed godwit flies 1200 km in a day. Calculate its speed in km h⁻¹. Answer: 1200 ÷ 24 = **50 km h⁻¹**. The mark scheme requires the unit — "50" alone is not credited.

**Do NOT say** "speed is the same as velocity" — say "speed is a scalar (magnitude only); velocity is a vector (magnitude and direction)."

---

### Velocity-Time Graphs

A velocity-time graph is the single most tested representation in this topic. The two key rules are:

- **Gradient = acceleration** (units: m s⁻² or cm s⁻²)
- **Area under the graph = displacement** (distance travelled)

A horizontal line means constant velocity — the resultant force is zero (Newton's first law). A positive gradient means acceleration; a negative gradient means deceleration. A curved line means changing acceleration.

**M22 Q1d (3 marks — Calculate):** A speed-time graph for a bullet train through a tunnel is trapezoidal. To find the length of the tunnel, calculate the area under the graph (area of trapezium = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height, or split into rectangles and triangles). The MS accepted any answer in the range 3534–3627 m. The mark scheme rewarded: evidence of speed × time calculation (seen or implied); use of two data points from the graph; correct numerical answer. Common error: reading one speed value and multiplying by total time — this ignores the shape of the graph.

**M25 Q4c (3 marks — Calculate/State):** Students drew a line of best fit on a velocity-time graph for a cotton reel car (0.05–0.35 s) and calculated the gradient. MS answer: **gradient = 105 ± 5 cm s⁻²; unit = cm s⁻²; quantity = acceleration**. All three components (numerical value, unit, quantity name) are each awarded a separate mark — omitting the unit or the word "acceleration" each costs one mark.

**Do NOT say** the gradient represents "speed" or "distance" — say "the gradient of a velocity-time graph represents acceleration."

**Do NOT say** the area represents "velocity" — say "the area under a velocity-time graph represents displacement (distance travelled)."

### Newton's Three Laws of Motion

**Newton's First Law:** An object remains at rest, or continues moving at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a resultant (net) force. This is the law of inertia.

**Newton's Second Law:** The resultant force acting on an object is directly proportional to its acceleration: **F = ma** (force in newtons, mass in kg, acceleration in m s⁻²). The bigger the resultant force, the greater the acceleration; the bigger the mass, the smaller the acceleration for the same force.

**Newton's Third Law:** When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. These forces act on different objects — they are never a "balanced pair" on the same object.

**N16 Q1d (4 marks — Explain):** A velocity-time graph showed a bird decelerating from C to D. Students had to explain force, velocity, acceleration, and Newton's law at this stage. MS answers: Force — the retarding force is increasing, or forces are no longer balanced; Speed — reducing to zero; Acceleration — deceleration / negative acceleration; Newton's law — **second law, or quoting F = ma**. The MS awarded ECF from the first marking point, so if the force description was incorrect but the rest followed logically, subsequent marks could still be earned.

**M25 Q6h (2 marks — Suggest/Justify):** Students had to suggest how to increase a propeller car's acceleration and justify using scientific principles. The MS accepted "reduce the mass of the car" with the justification "same force on lower mass means greater acceleration — Newton's second law." The justification must name Newton's second law or state F = ma to earn the mark.

**Do NOT say** Newton's third law pairs act on the same object — say "the action and reaction forces act on different objects."

### Free-Body Diagrams and Balanced Forces

A free-body diagram shows all forces acting on a single object as labelled arrows. At constant velocity, the resultant force is zero — all forces are balanced (Newton's first law).

For a bullet train at constant speed, four forces act: weight (downward), normal/magnetic force (upward), driving force (forward), drag force (backward). At constant speed, upward = downward and driving force = drag force.

**M22 Q1b (2 marks — Label):** Students dragged four labels onto a bullet train diagram. The MS accepted either "normal force" or "magnetic force" for the vertical upward component since the train uses magnetic levitation. Two correct labels awarded 1 mark; all correct awarded 2 marks. A common error is applying only two forces or forgetting that the train still has weight despite levitating.

**M22 Q1c (3 marks — Explain):** The redesigned train is more efficient because the drag force is reduced (streamlined nose), so less driving force is needed to maintain the same speed. MS awarded marks for: drag/air resistance is reduced; forces are balanced at constant speed; resultant force is zero. "More efficient" must be linked to reduced drag — a vague reference to "streamlining" alone is not credited.

**M24 Q4b (3 marks — Draw):** Forces on a ball rolling toward a magnet. Three marks awarded: (1) magnetic force drawn horizontally toward the magnet; (2) normal force upward and weight downward, equal in magnitude; (3) no extra forces added. The third mark was conditional — only awarded if mark 2 was also awarded.

### Newton's Second Law — F = ma Calculations

The formula **F = ma** connects resultant force (N), mass (kg), and acceleration (m s⁻²). In calculation questions, always show the equation, substitute values, and include the unit.

**M17 Q10a (2 marks — Calculate):** An elephant weighing 30 000 N stands on three feet, each with area 0.2 m². Calculate pressure: P = F/A = 30 000 / (3 × 0.2) = **50 000 Pa**. The MS awarded one mark for using P = F/A (seen or implied) and one mark for the correct answer with unit. A common error is forgetting to multiply the area by 3 feet.

**M16 Q2a (3 marks — Calculate):** Students calculated acceleration due to gravity on Mercury and Mars using free-fall data (s = ½at²). MS answers: Mercury = 3.7 m s⁻², Mars = 3.8 m s⁻². Maximum 2 marks if more than two significant figures were given — a rare example where the precision rule actively costs marks.

**M24 Q5b (3 marks — Calculate):** Using s = ½at² to find acceleration of a current-carrying rod: s = 16 cm, t = 1.70 s. The MS awarded marks for (1) selecting s = ½at²; (2) correct numerical answer 0.11 m s⁻²; (3) correct unit m s⁻² or cm s⁻². The unit was a separate marking point.

### Momentum and Impulse

Momentum is a vector quantity defined as **p = mv** (mass × velocity), measured in kg m s⁻¹. Impulse is the change in momentum: **impulse = F × t = Δp**.

The law of conservation of momentum states that in a closed system (no external forces), the total momentum before a collision equals the total momentum after.

Newton's second law can be written as F = Δp/Δt — force equals the rate of change of momentum. This is why a longer impact time (e.g. crumple zones, airbags) reduces the peak force for the same momentum change.

**Do NOT say** "momentum is the same as velocity" — say "momentum = mass × velocity, measured in kg m s⁻¹; it is a vector quantity."

**Do NOT say** the law of conservation of momentum applies to all situations — say "it applies only when the resultant external force on the system is zero."

### Stopping Distance

Total stopping distance = **thinking distance + braking distance**.

Thinking distance is the distance travelled during the driver's reaction time (before the brakes are applied). It is affected by: driver fatigue, alcohol/drugs, distractions, speed of the vehicle. Thinking distance is directly proportional to speed.

Braking distance is the distance travelled while the brakes are applied. It is affected by: vehicle speed (braking distance ∝ speed²), road conditions (wet/icy), tyre condition, brake condition, vehicle mass.

| Factor | Affects thinking distance? | Affects braking distance? |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Speed | Yes (proportional) | Yes (proportional to v²) |
| Tiredness / alcohol | Yes | No |
| Wet/icy road | No | Yes |
| Worn tyres or brakes | No | Yes |
| Vehicle mass | No | Yes |

**M17 Q7 (22 marks — Processing/Reflection):** The sail-trolley investigation showed that stopping distance decreases as sail area increases (more drag force). The key mark scheme insight: the relationship was inversely proportional to radius², not radius — the hypothesis was partially supported (direction correct) but the exact relationship was wrong. Evaluating a hypothesis means comparing the predicted relationship with the actual data, not just saying "supported" or "not supported."

**Do NOT say** "tiredness increases braking distance" — say "tiredness increases thinking distance (reaction time) but does not directly affect braking distance."

### Terminal Velocity

When an object falls through a fluid (air, water), two forces act: weight (downward, constant) and drag/air resistance (upward, increases with speed). Initially weight > drag, so the object accelerates. As speed increases, drag increases until drag = weight — at this point the resultant force is zero and the object reaches terminal velocity (constant speed, zero acceleration).

**M16 Q3 (11 marks — Inquiry/Terminal velocity):** Students designed a parachute investigation. The hypothesis mark required: a prediction linking terminal velocity and parachute area (e.g. "terminal velocity decreases as area increases"), plus a scientific explanation (increased area → greater air resistance → increased upward drag force → reduced acceleration). The MS awarded marks for: prediction; reference to increased air resistance/drag force; reference to how drag reduces acceleration. A common error was stating only the conclusion without the mechanism.

**M16 Q2d (3 marks — Explain):** A test object falls faster before hitting Io (g = 1.8 N kg⁻¹) than Ganymede (g = 1.4 N kg⁻¹). MS answer: lower g on Ganymede means lower acceleration; no other forces act; lower acceleration leads to lower final/terminal velocity. The MS explicitly required the phrase "no other forces acting" or equivalent to earn full marks.

**The sequence for terminal velocity (must know the order):**
1. Object released — weight > drag — object accelerates
2. Speed increases — drag increases — acceleration decreases
3. Eventually weight = drag — resultant force = zero — terminal velocity reached
4. If disturbed (e.g. parachute opens) — drag suddenly > weight — object decelerates until new terminal velocity

**Do NOT say** "the object stops accelerating because the forces cancel out" — say "the resultant force becomes zero, so by Newton's first law the object continues at constant (terminal) velocity."

---

### Drag and Air Resistance

Drag is the resistive force that opposes motion through a fluid. It depends on: the speed of the object (drag increases with speed), the cross-sectional area of the object, the shape (streamlined objects have lower drag), and the density of the fluid.

**M17 Q7f (3 marks — Explain):** Students explained why a larger sail area produced greater drag: "as radius of sail increases, drag/resistance increases, because there are more collisions with air particles; area is related to radius squared." The MS required the link from area to radius — not just saying "bigger area, more drag."

**N16 Q1b (2 marks — Outline):** Factors affecting average speed of migrating birds. MS accepted: headwind, drag, different weather systems, increasing fatigue, behavioural flight patterns (flying in groups). "Drag" alone without context earned a mark — but "air resistance" was also accepted.

**M22 Q1c (3 marks — Explain):** The redesigned bullet train is more efficient because its streamlined nose reduces drag, meaning less energy is needed to maintain the same speed. The MS required the word "drag" or "air resistance" and the phrase "forces are balanced" or "resultant force is zero."

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

Calculation questions reward **showing the equation first** (even if substitution is implied), then the numerical answer, then the unit — each is often a separate mark. In N16 Q1a, the numerical answer "50" without the unit "km h⁻¹" is only awarded 0 marks — the unit is the mark. In M24 Q5b, the unit m s⁻² was a separate marking point from the numerical answer.

Explanation questions reward **causal chains**: state the cause, then state the effect, then link to the consequence. In N16 Q1d, four separate marking points each required a different element (force, speed, acceleration, Newton's law). Writing a general paragraph without explicitly labelling each element loses marks.

Graph gradient questions reward three things independently: the **numerical value** (within a given tolerance), the **unit** (e.g. m s⁻² or cm s⁻²), and the **quantity name** (acceleration). In M25 Q4c, all three earned separate marks.

Free-body diagram questions reward arrows that **touch or are very close to the surface of the object**, are labelled correctly, and have no extra forces added. In M24 Q4b, the third mark was explicitly conditional on the second being awarded first.

Hypothesis questions reward: a clear prediction linking IV and DV, a direction (increases/decreases), and a scientific explanation (the "because"). Simply saying "the stopping distance will decrease" without explaining why earns only one mark.

The mark scheme regularly uses **ECF (error carried forward)**: if an incorrect value from an earlier part is used consistently and correctly in a later calculation, full marks for the method may still be awarded.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

Calculation questions: giving more significant figures than the data allows costs marks. In M16 Q2a, more than two significant figures in the answer reduced the maximum to 2 marks.

Velocity-time graphs: describing the gradient as "speed" or "distance" — not accepted. The gradient is acceleration. Describing the area as "velocity" — not accepted. The area is displacement.

Newton's laws: saying "the forces cancel" without explicitly connecting this to zero resultant force and Newton's first law earns no marks for Newton's law reference. The MS requires either naming "second law" or writing "F = ma" or equivalents.

Terminal velocity: saying "the object stops" instead of "the object reaches constant (terminal) velocity" — not accepted. The object does not stop; it continues at constant speed.

Free-body diagrams: including additional forces not present in the situation, or drawing force arrows that do not contact the object, or giving arrows unequal lengths when they should be equal (e.g. weight and normal force on a horizontal surface) — each can cost the conditional third mark.

Hypothesis evaluation: writing "the hypothesis is supported" without linking to specific data (e.g. a calculated gradient or a comparison of two data points) is generally not accepted alone. The MS note "do not award this mark alone" appears repeatedly.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Speed and velocity are the same thing" | Speed is scalar (magnitude only); velocity is vector (magnitude + direction) |
| "The gradient of a v-t graph is distance" | Gradient = acceleration; area = displacement |
| "At terminal velocity, all forces disappear" | Weight and drag are both present and equal; resultant force = zero |
| "Tiredness increases braking distance" | Tiredness increases thinking distance (reaction time); braking distance is unaffected |
| "Newton's 3rd law pairs act on the same object" | Action and reaction forces act on different objects |
| Omitting the unit in a calculation answer | Units are frequently a separate mark — always include them |
| Giving too many significant figures in an answer | Check the question — some MS cap marks if sig figs exceed the data |
| "The hypothesis is supported" without data comparison | Must reference at least one specific data point or calculation to earn the evaluation mark |
| "The object decelerates to a stop" when describing terminal velocity | The object reaches a constant, non-zero terminal velocity — it does not stop |
| Writing forces on a free-body diagram from outside the object | Arrows must originate from (or touch) the surface of the object |
| Confusing F = ma with momentum (p = mv) | F = ma is for resultant force and acceleration; p = mv is for momentum in kg m s⁻¹ |
| Saying "drag decreases with speed" | Drag increases with speed — this is what causes terminal velocity to be reached |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Practise reading velocity-time graphs: draw a graph, then state the gradient (with unit), identify constant velocity sections, and calculate the area for a given region.
2. Know all four forces on a moving object and be able to state when they are balanced and which Newton's law applies.
3. Memorise the terminal velocity sequence — object accelerates → drag increases → acceleration decreases → drag = weight → terminal velocity — and be able to describe it in five linked sentences.
4. Learn the stopping distance table: which factors affect thinking distance only, which affect braking distance only, and which affect both. This is a high-frequency two-mark question.
5. For every calculation: write the equation → substitute values → calculate → include unit. Practise F = ma, GPE = mgh, s = ½at², P = F/A, and v = s/t until equation selection is automatic.
6. When evaluating a hypothesis, always include: (a) what the hypothesis predicted, (b) what the data showed, and (c) an explicit conclusion. Do not write the conclusion alone — it cannot earn the mark by itself.
7. Know the difference between scalar and vector quantities: speed/distance are scalar; velocity/displacement/force/acceleration/momentum are vector.
8. For free-body diagram questions, draw arrows touching the object, label each arrow, check that balanced forces are equal in length, and do not add extra forces.
