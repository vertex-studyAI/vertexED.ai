# Scientific Inquiry (Criterion B)

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Research Question / Problem Statement

The research question is the single most important sentence in a Criterion B answer. It must name the independent variable (IV) and the dependent variable (DV) explicitly, and it must be phrased as a testable question — not a general topic or aim.

The accepted structure is: **"How does [IV] affect [DV]?"** Mark schemes accept reasonable variations but always require both variables to be named. A question that names only one variable, or that is phrased as "I will investigate…", does not receive the mark.

**M17 Q5c (1 mark — State):** The stimulus gives a ramp experiment where height varies and distance travelled is measured. The accepted answer is: *"How does the height of the ramp affect the distance travelled by the block?"* The mark scheme notes ORA (or reverse argument) and gives ECF from the student's own choice of IV if it was stated clearly in an earlier part.

**M24 Q3a (1 mark — State):** With a wire resistance investigation, the accepted answer is: *"How does the length of the wire affect the resistance of the wire?"* The mark scheme does not accept a question that names the wrong IV or that leaves the DV vague.

The research question also acts as the anchor for every other Criterion B decision. Control variables, hypothesis, and method must all be consistent with it. If the RQ is wrong, ECF is applied throughout — so getting it right first is critical.

---

### Hypothesis: If / Then / Because Structure

The hypothesis is a testable prediction supported by scientific reasoning. Physics mark schemes expect three elements: a directional prediction (if…then) and a scientific justification (because).

**If** states the change in the independent variable.
**Then** states the predicted change in the dependent variable, including direction (increases/decreases) and ideally a quantitative prediction (doubles, halves, is proportional to).
**Because** gives a scientific reason — Newton's Second Law, conservation of energy, electron theory, particle theory, etc.

A hypothesis that gives a direction but no scientific reasoning scores 1 out of the available marks. A hypothesis that gives both the direction and a quantitative prediction but still no reasoning also scores partially. Full marks require all three components.

**M24 Q3b (3 marks — Formulate):** For the wire resistance investigation, the mark scheme awards: (1) any hypothesis linking length and resistance regardless of direction; (2) scientifically correct explanatory points such as "electrons collide with more ions as they pass through a longer wire" or "as R = ρL/A, resistance is directly proportional to length". Two explanation points are needed for full marks.

**M17 Q5d (3 marks — Formulate, Explain):** For the ramp height experiment: (1) any simple prediction (e.g. "as the ramp gets higher the block travels further"); (2) a quantitative element ("as the height doubles, the distance doubles"); (3) a scientific explanation referring to gravitational potential energy or kinetic energy. The mark scheme explicitly awards a separate mark for the quantitative claim.

**N24 Q3a (2 marks — Formulate):** For the diameter and resistance investigation: *"If the diameter increases, then the resistance will decrease because there are more paths for the electrons to follow."* The mark scheme does NOT accept "there is more space or volume only" — it requires the explanation to reference electrons or current carriers specifically.

### Variables: Independent, Dependent, and Controlled

Identifying variables correctly is one of the most heavily tested Criterion B skills. Physics papers test all three types repeatedly, often in a table or drag-and-drop format.

**Independent variable (IV):** The one quantity the student deliberately changes. There is only ever one IV per investigation. If the student names two IVs, no mark is awarded for that entry.

**Dependent variable (DV):** The quantity measured as a result of changing the IV. It must be the thing actually recorded — not a processed quantity derived later. For example, in a motor efficiency investigation the DV is time (what is measured), not efficiency (what is calculated from the data).

**Control variables (CVs):** All other quantities kept constant to make the test fair. Physics mark schemes consistently require at least two CVs for full marks on a variable identification question, and they must be realistic and measurable. "Everything else" or "background conditions" are not accepted. The CVs must also be consistent with the IV — if the IV is length, then material and cross-sectional area are valid CVs; height of ramp is not.

How to control each CV matters and is often asked separately. The method for controlling must be specific: "use the same ball" is acceptable; "keep things the same" is not.

**M17 Q5e (4 marks — Identify):** IV = height of ramp (1); DV = distance travelled (1); CVs = material of sliding block, same distance to slide along the ramp, mass of the block (2 marks for two correct). The mark scheme explicitly states: "Do not accept temperature, colour or gravitational field strength."

**M21 Q4b (3 marks — Identify):** Galileo's ball-on-ramp investigation. IV = distance travelled by the ball; DV = time taken; CVs = mass of the ball, size of the ball, angle of the slope. The mark scheme is strict: "IV — distance only; DV — time only; CV — mass, size, angle of slope only."

**N18 Q4a (4 marks — Select):** Football bounce height vs pressure. IV = internal air pressure only; DV = bounce height only; CVs chosen from: drop height, size of ball, bouncing surface, material of ball. Full marks require all control variables correct.

**N22 Q5c (3 marks — Identify):** Thermistor temperature investigation. IV = temperature of the resistor; DV = current in the circuit; CVs = length and diameter of resistor, voltage across resistor. The mark scheme lists all three CVs explicitly and requires the student to separate them correctly.

### Method: Step-by-Step, Sufficient Data, Safety

A complete Physics method must do four things: list specific equipment, describe the exact steps so another student could follow them, explain how sufficient data will be collected, and address safety. Mark schemes apply a multi-strand rubric where each component scores separately.

**Equipment:** Must be specific. "Measuring device" or "something to measure distance" does not score. "Ruler / measuring tape" (M17 Q5f), "thermometer or temperature probe and voltmeter" (M25 Q6b), "stopwatch or timer" (M21 Q4g) are accepted.

**Steps:** The method must be reproducible. It must say what is changed (IV), what is measured (DV), and how CVs are maintained. An "attempt at a method" gets 1 mark; a "complete method that could easily be followed by another student" gets the top mark.

**Sufficient data:** The mark scheme consistently requires at least five different values of the IV and at least three trials (repeats) at each value to calculate a mean. Five increments alone scores 1–2 marks; five increments AND three trials AND plans to calculate an average scores maximum marks for this component.

**Safety:** Must name a specific hazard linked to the investigation. Generic safety statements ("tie back hair", "be careful") score 0. Investigation-specific hazards that appear in mark schemes include: electrical hazards from a live circuit (N24 Q5d), high temperatures from a bead bath or Bunsen burner (N18 Q4c), injury from a ball rolling off a table (M24 Q4f), and overpressure from inflating a ball beyond its rated pressure (N18 Q4c). The mark scheme for N18 Q4c explicitly states: safety factor "linked to the question" — the justification must say why the named hazard matters.

**N18 Q6b (14 marks — Design):** The squash ball / temperature investigation uses a four-strand rubric: Hypothesis / Method–Equipment / Data / Safety. For the data strand: "at least 5 values of DV OR at least 3 trials" scores 1 mark; "at least 5 values AND 3 trials AND plans to calculate average" scores 3 marks.

**M21 Q5b (13 marks — Design):** The parachute / surface area investigation uses variables (IV + DV + CVs with justification), equipment, method, data, and safety strands. Safety: "mentions a relevant precaution" scores 1. Variables strand: two CVs with justification required for top mark.

**N22 Q5 (14 marks — Design):** The motor efficiency / voltage investigation uses four strands: Variables, Method, Data, Processing. Full marks on the Method strand require "complete method with measurements of voltage and time AND could easily be followed by another student AND with details of m and ΔH as CV".

### Circuit Diagrams in Physics Inquiry

Several Physics Criterion B questions require drawing a circuit diagram as part of the method. This is tested more often in Physics than in any other MYP science. Key rules from mark schemes:

- The ammeter must always be in series with the component under test.
- The voltmeter must always be in parallel across the component under test (not across the battery).
- Only include components that are required for the measurement. Extra components (lamps, resistors not under test) lose marks if they change the circuit function.
- A switch is acceptable and does not lose marks, but is not required.
- Labels are required if the question asks you to label — drag-and-drop questions mark each symbol's position independently.

**N22 Q4d (3 marks — Draw):** Circuit to measure power output of a motor. Mark scheme awards: (1) motor AND ammeter AND voltmeter only present; (2) ammeter in series; (3) voltmeter in parallel across motor. "Ignore any switch if present."

**N24 Q5b (3 marks — Draw):** Circuit for thermistor investigation. Mark scheme: resistor connected to battery in complete circuit (1); ammeter in series (1); voltmeter in parallel with no other components added (1). "Allow addition of a switch only."

**M24 Q3e (1 mark — Select):** Drag-and-drop placement of ammeter and voltmeter in a wire resistance circuit. Full mark requires voltmeter in parallel with wire and ammeter in series. "Switch and ammeter acceptable in either position."

### Criterion B Markbands: 1–3 / 4–7 / 8–11 / 12–16

For extended Design questions (typically 13–16 marks), the mark scheme uses a strand-based rubric rather than point-by-point marking. Understanding the architecture of the rubric is as important as knowing the content.

Each strand (Variables, Hypothesis, Method, Data, Safety) is marked independently, typically on a 1–3 or 1–4 scale. Missing one strand entirely caps your mark for that component at 0 regardless of how strong the rest of the answer is.

The general progression across mark bands:

| Mark range | What it describes |
|---|---|
| 1 mark per strand | A statement or attempt — one variable named, or a method mentioned |
| 2 marks per strand | Two components present, or one with explanation |
| 3 marks per strand | Complete response for that strand — both items stated with justification or explanation |
| 4 marks per strand | As 3 marks, plus detail or additional support |

The overall design question mark is the sum of all strand marks. A student who writes a strong hypothesis and good method but forgets safety and gives only one CV will lose 3–5 marks even with otherwise excellent content.

**M25 Q5a (16 marks — Design):** Cotton reel car investigation. Five strands: IV/DV/CV (max 4), Hypothesis (max 3), Equipment (max 4), Method (max 4), Sufficient data (max 4). A testable hypothesis in "If…then" format scores 2; adding a scientific explanation scores 3. Five different values of the IV stated explicitly is required for the upper data marks.

### Number of Trials

The number of trials (repeats at each IV value) is tested explicitly in justify and outline questions. Mark schemes are consistent: three trials minimum for sufficient data; at least five different values of the IV for sufficient range. The reason for repeating trials must be stated precisely.

Accepted reasons: "to calculate a mean / average"; "to reduce the effect of random errors"; "to increase reliability". Vague phrases like "to make it more accurate" score 0 unless they include the specific mechanism.

**N18 Q4b (3 marks — Select; Justify):** Three options offered — 1 repetition at 5 kPa intervals; 3 repetitions at 10 kPa intervals; 8 repetitions at 30 kPa intervals. Correct answer: 3 repetitions at 10 kPa intervals. Justification requires: (1) allows a mean to be calculated OR reduces experimental uncertainty; (2) sufficient data across the full range.

**N24 Q3e (2 marks — Suggest):** "How would more trials improve the investigation?" Accepted: "average could be calculated"; "random errors are eliminated"; "to increase reliability" — two separate points required.

### Safety in Physics Investigations

Physics investigations introduce hazards not found in Biology or Chemistry: electrical shock, high-speed projectiles, heavy masses, extreme temperatures from heating circuits. Mark schemes penalise generic safety statements and require the hazard to be named and linked to the specific investigation.

Common Physics-specific safety points by investigation type:

| Investigation type | Accepted safety concern | Justification required |
|---|---|---|
| Electric circuits | Electrical hazard from live circuit | Risk of electric shock |
| Heating with Bunsen / bead bath | High temperature of beads or hot water | Risk of burns |
| Ball / projectile / ramp | Ball rolling off table / bouncing into face | Risk of injury from falling object |
| Ball inflation (pressure) | Over-pressurising the ball | Ball might explode / burst |
| Magnets / heavy masses | Trapping fingers between colliding magnets | Risk of crush injury |

**N18 Q4c (2 marks — State; Justify):** For the football bounce investigation: safety factor stated (e.g. "avoid over pressurizing the ball") (1); justification (e.g. "the ball might explode") (1). Mark scheme notes: "Consider both response boxes when awarding marks."

**M24 Q4f (Safety strand):** For the magnetic launcher: "Taking care not to trap fingers between the colliding balls/magnets" and "precautions regarding heavy balls rolling off the table onto feet" are both accepted. "Handle magnets carefully" alone is NOT accepted — the mark scheme states "this is not linked to a hazard."

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- A research question that explicitly names both the IV and the DV using the structure "How does [IV] affect [DV]?" — vague questions that omit one variable score 0.
- A hypothesis in the If/Then/Because structure where "Because" contains a named scientific principle: Newton's Second Law, conservation of energy, electron collision theory, particle theory. A direction alone ("it will increase") scores 1 at most.
- A quantitative prediction in the hypothesis scores an additional mark in most sessions (M17, M24, M25): "doubles", "is directly proportional to", "inversely proportional to".
- Control variables that are specific, realistic, and measurable. Generic CVs ("everything else", "background conditions") score 0. CVs must be consistent with the IV chosen.
- Five different values of the IV stated explicitly, plus at least three trials at each, plus a plan to calculate the mean — this combination scores maximum marks on the Data strand in all observed sessions.
- Safety statements that name a specific hazard AND give a justification linked to that hazard. Two separate marks are typically available (N18 Q4c, M21 Q5b, M24 Q4f).
- Circuit diagrams with ammeter in series and voltmeter in parallel. Both must be correct for full marks; each is awarded independently.
- ECF (error carried forward) is applied generously throughout Criterion B. If the RQ is non-standard but internally consistent, CVs and hypothesis that match it still receive marks.

### What the mark scheme penalises

- A hypothesis with no scientific reasoning — even a perfectly correct directional prediction scores at most 1 mark without the "Because" component.
- Naming two independent variables. Only one IV is allowed; naming two means neither scores.
- Control variables that are not measurable, not linked to the investigation, or are the same as the IV or DV. Examples explicitly excluded in past papers: temperature and colour as CVs in a ramp experiment (M17); height as a CV when length is the IV (N22 Q6c); voltage, current, or power as variables in an investigation where they are not manipulated directly (M25 Q5a).
- Safety statements with no justification, or generic statements: "tie hair back", "be careful", "handle magnets carefully" without naming the hazard. M24 Q4f explicitly penalises this.
- "Accurate" or "precise" used without a specific explanation of how accuracy or precision is improved. "Repeat trials to make it more accurate" scores 0; "repeat trials to calculate a mean and reduce random errors" scores the mark.
- Circuit diagrams with the voltmeter in series or the ammeter in parallel — both are incorrect and score 0 for the relevant mark point.
- Data collection plans that specify fewer than five IV values or fewer than three trials, even if the rest of the method is excellent. The data strand is marked independently.
- Mentioning a CV without saying how to control it, when the question specifically asks for justification. N18 Q6b and M21 Q5b both award justification as a separate mark.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Writing "I will investigate the effect of height on distance" as the RQ | Write it as a question: "How does the height of the ramp affect the distance travelled?" |
| Hypothesis says "the distance will increase" with no reason | Add "Because the block gains more gravitational potential energy at greater heights, which is converted to kinetic energy" |
| Listing "temperature" or "colour" as a CV for a ramp experiment | Choose only measurable quantities that can realistically affect the DV: mass of block, surface material, ramp length |
| Naming the same variable as both IV and DV | IV is what you change; DV is what you measure as a result — they must be different quantities |
| Saying "repeat trials to make it more accurate" | Say "repeat trials to calculate a mean, which reduces the effect of random errors" |
| Drawing the voltmeter in series with the component | The voltmeter is always in parallel — connected across the component only |
| Saying "handle the magnets carefully" as the safety point | State the specific hazard: "fingers may be trapped between magnets during collision, causing a crush injury" |
| Giving only two or three different IV values in the data plan | Five different values of the IV are required for sufficient range |
| Giving only one trial at each IV value | Three trials at each value are required to calculate a reliable mean |
| Writing a hypothesis that changes direction depending on wording | Decide on the direction first, then commit: "If length increases, then resistance increases" not "If length increases, resistance may increase or decrease" |
| Listing "efficiency" as the DV when you measure time | The DV is what is directly measured; efficiency is a processed/calculated quantity — time is the DV |
| Omitting the "Because" clause entirely in the hypothesis | "Because" is where the marks are — it must name a relevant Physics principle |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the three-part hypothesis structure (If / Then / Because) and practise writing one for five different Physics investigation types: electric circuits, motion/ramps, waves/optics, pressure, and thermal experiments.
2. Learn what counts as a valid IV, DV, and CV for each major Physics context — and practise naming two specific, measurable CVs with a method for controlling each.
3. Practise drawing circuit diagrams from memory: ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel. This appears in almost every recent session and is an easy source of lost marks.
4. Memorise the sufficient data formula: five different IV values + three trials at each + plan to calculate the mean. This combination scores maximum marks on the Data strand regardless of the investigation context.
5. Learn the investigation-specific safety hazards for Physics: electrical shock, burns from hot beads/Bunsen, crush injury from magnets, falling objects from a ramp or table. Always state the specific hazard and its consequence.
6. Study the full Design markband rubric from at least two sessions (N18 Q6b and M21 Q5b recommended) to understand how the strand structure works and how missing one strand caps your score for that component.
7. For equipment questions, know the standard Physics measuring instruments by name: ruler / measuring tape, stopwatch or stopclock, ammeter, voltmeter, ohmmeter, thermometer / temperature probe, newton meter / force meter. Vague names like "measuring device" do not score.
8. Review the ECF rule: if you write a non-standard RQ in part (a), make sure every subsequent answer (CVs, hypothesis, method) is consistent with it — ECF protects you as long as internal logic holds.
