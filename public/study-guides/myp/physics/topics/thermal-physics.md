# Thermal Physics

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Particle Model and States of Matter

All matter is made of particles in constant motion. The three states of matter differ in how those particles are arranged and how much energy they have.

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|----------|-------|--------|-----|
| Particle arrangement | Regular, closely packed | Random, close together | Random, far apart |
| Particle movement | Vibrate about fixed positions | Flow and slide past each other | Move freely at high speed |
| Shape | Fixed | Takes shape of container | Fills container |
| Volume | Fixed | Fixed | Not fixed |
| Compressibility | Very low | Very low | High |

**Mark scheme key phrase:** Do not say particles are "stationary" in a solid — they vibrate. Do not say gases have "no" arrangement — accept "random."

**N21 Q1a (states of matter drag-drop):** Three-mark question matching state to properties — one mark per correct state. Full marks require all three correct.

---

### Changes of State

When a substance changes state, energy is transferred to or from its particles without changing temperature (during the change itself).

| Change of state | Direction | Heat energy |
|-----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Melting | Solid → Liquid | Added |
| Boiling / Vaporization | Liquid → Gas | Added |
| Condensation | Gas → Liquid | Removed |
| Freezing | Liquid → Solid | Removed |
| Sublimation | Solid → Gas | Added |

**N21 Q1b (change-of-state table):** Condensation: Gas → Liquid. Vaporization: Liquid → Gas. Both marks awarded only when both pairs are entirely correct.

**N22 Q2a (drag-drop state changes):** Heat added = Boiling and Melting. Heat removed = Condensing and Freezing. One correct = 1 mark; all correct = 2 marks.

### Evaporation vs Boiling

Evaporation and boiling are both processes that turn liquid into gas, but they differ in several important ways.

| Feature | Evaporation | Boiling |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Temperature | Occurs at any temperature | Only at boiling point |
| Location | Surface of the liquid only | Throughout the liquid (bubbles form) |
| Speed | Slow | Fast |
| Cooling effect | Yes — cools the liquid | No net cooling (temperature stays constant) |
| Bubbles formed | No | Yes |

**How evaporation causes cooling:** The highest-energy (fastest-moving) particles at the surface escape the liquid, leaving behind lower-energy particles. The average kinetic energy of the remaining liquid falls, so its temperature drops.

**Mark scheme key phrase (M18 Q3c / N20 Q1d / N22 Q2b):** Three-mark model answer for evaporation cooling:
1. High-energy particles in the liquid escape from the surface.
2. Leaving lower-energy particles behind.
3. Link between kinetic energy and temperature (average KE falls → temperature falls).

> Accept "fast moving" in place of "high energy." Do not accept "the cold air makes the liquid cold" — the explanation must come from particle-level reasoning.

**N22 Q2b (compare and contrast evaporation and boiling):** Similarity = both are types of vaporization / both are liquid → gas changes. Difference = boiling only at boiling point / evaporation cools the liquid / evaporation is at the surface only. One mark for a valid similarity, one mark for a valid difference.

**N22 Q2e (which student is correct about energy for evaporation?):** Cooking oil did not evaporate (no temperature drop); ethanol evaporated most. Student B (cooking oil requires most energy to evaporate) is correct because it requires enough energy to even begin evaporating — and it never did under lab conditions. Mark requires linking the graph evidence to the claim.

### Heat Transfer: Conduction, Convection, and Radiation

There are three mechanisms by which thermal energy moves from one place to another.

**Conduction** is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by particle-to-particle collision. It requires a medium (matter) and is most effective in solids where particles are close together. Metals are good conductors because free electrons carry energy. Non-metals, air, and vacuum are poor conductors (insulators).

**Mark scheme (N23 Q7b — three layers of glass):** "Conduction is heat transfer through solids" [1]. "Air or air gaps are a poor conductor / good insulator" [1]. The second mark requires linking to the air gap — it is not enough to say glass is an insulator.

**Convection** is the transfer of thermal energy through fluids (liquids and gases) by the bulk movement of particles. Hot fluid is less dense and rises; cooler fluid sinks to replace it, creating a convection current.

**Worked example — M18 Q3a–b (cooling units in indoor ski slope):**
The air cooling units are placed at the top of the building. The correct heat transfer process is convection (M18 Q3a, 1 mark). The explanation (M18 Q3b, 3 marks): cold air goes down / hot air goes up; hot air is less dense than cold air so it rises; cooling is more effective at the top. Do NOT say "heat rises" — the mark scheme requires "hot air is less dense."

**Radiation** is the transfer of thermal energy as infrared (electromagnetic) waves. It does not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum. Dark, matt surfaces are the best absorbers and emitters of infrared radiation. Light, shiny surfaces are the best reflectors and poorest emitters.

**Mark scheme (M18 Q3d — colour to reduce solar heating):** Silver / white / shiny / metallic AND "best at reflecting sunlight." Must also make a correct reference to infrared radiation / waves. Do NOT accept incorrectly named EM radiation types.

### Thermal Equilibrium

Two objects are in thermal equilibrium when they are at the same temperature and there is no net transfer of thermal energy between them. Heat always flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature until equilibrium is reached.

**Laboratory relevance (N22 Q2c–d):** In the evaporation cooling experiment, the temperature probes eventually stabilise at room temperature — this is thermal equilibrium. The graph shows each liquid reaching a plateau at lab temperature (approximately 20.8 °C, read from the graph ± 0.1 °C).

### The Dewar Flask and Insulation Applications

The Dewar flask (vacuum flask / thermos) is a key exam example of how all three heat transfer mechanisms are minimised.

| Feature | Heat transfer blocked | How |
|---------|----------------------|-----|
| Vacuum between walls | Conduction and convection | No medium for particles to travel through |
| Silvered surfaces | Radiation | Reflect infrared away from or back toward the contents |
| Insulated support / stopper | Conduction | Poor conducting materials reduce contact transfer |

**Worked example — M19 Q1c (4 marks):** A student places ice and water in a Dewar flask and leaves it in sunshine for four hours. The ice has not melted. Explain why.

Mark scheme model answer:
- Silvered surfaces reflect infrared / radiation — the sun's rays are reflected away from the ice [1].
- Vacuum prevents conduction — conduction needs a medium and there is no medium for heat energy to transfer from the surroundings to the ice [1].
- Insulated support prevents conduction — heat cannot transfer through insulating materials [1].
- Any third valid feature of the flask correctly linked to a heat transfer mechanism [1].

> Do not accept reference to the stopper alone as a full-mark answer. Each feature must be correctly named AND linked to a specific type of heat transfer.

**N23 Q7a–b (passive houses):** Passive houses apply the same principles at architectural scale. Three layers of glass trap air gaps that block conduction (N23 Q7b). Insulated walls and airtight seals reduce conduction and convection. Windows face toward sunlight to maximise solar radiation gain (N23 Q7a).

### Specific Heat Capacity: E = mcΔT

Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C (or 1 K). The equation is:

> **E = mcΔT**
>
> where E = energy transferred (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹), ΔT = change in temperature (°C or K).

A high specific heat capacity means a substance needs a lot of energy to heat up — and releases a lot when cooling. Water has a very high specific heat capacity (4200 J kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹), which is why it is used in radiators and as a coolant.

**Mark scheme guidance:** Show all steps — state the formula, substitute values, calculate. In a 2-mark calculation, the correct final answer alone usually awards both marks (seen or implied rule), but showing the formula secures the first mark even if arithmetic is wrong.

### Kinetic Theory and Gas Pressure

According to kinetic theory, gas particles move randomly at high speed and exert pressure by colliding with the walls of their container. Pressure is caused by the force of these collisions per unit area.

**Increasing temperature increases pressure** (fixed volume): particles move faster → more frequent collisions → greater force per collision → higher pressure.

**Worked example — N21 Q1c (3 marks — balloon expanding when heated):**
- Particles have greater kinetic energy / move faster [1].
- Number of collisions per second increases / particles collide with greater force [1].
- Resulting in greater pressure [1].

**Mark scheme note:** Do not accept "particles expand" or "particles get bigger" — it is the increased speed and collision rate that matters. Accept WTTE.

**N21 Q1d (Brownian motion, 2 marks):** Air particles are moving but too small to be seen [1]. They collide with larger smoke particles that can be seen, causing random movement [1].

**N23 Q7c (kinetic theory — locating air leaks in passive house, 3 marks):**
- Air particles move from higher pressure to lower pressure [1].
- If there is a gap, air will escape through it [1].
- Enabling the location of the leak to be found [1].

---

### Boyle's Law and Gas Laws

Boyle's Law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional:

> **P₁V₁ = P₂V₂** (at constant temperature)

As volume decreases, gas particles are confined to a smaller space, so collisions with the walls become more frequent → pressure increases.

**Practical connection:** Syringes, bicycle pumps, and sealed containers all demonstrate Boyle's Law. If you halve the volume of a gas at constant temperature, the pressure doubles.

**Mark scheme phrasing for gas pressure questions:** Link the change (volume/temperature) → particle speed or collision frequency → pressure. A chain of reasoning is expected for full marks.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- Naming the specific heat transfer type and correctly linking it to a physical feature or context (e.g., "vacuum prevents conduction because conduction requires a medium").
- The three-step evaporation cooling chain: high-energy particles escape → lower-energy particles remain → average kinetic energy / temperature falls.
- For kinetic theory and pressure: particles move faster → more/harder collisions → greater pressure. The chain must be complete for full marks.
- For state changes: correct identification of initial and final states, not just the name of the change.
- Using the graph to identify laboratory temperature (thermal equilibrium plateau) — read to the precision asked (±0.1).
- For radiation questions: mentioning infrared waves by name, not just "heat rays" or "light."
- For passive house questions (N23): linking each design feature to a specific heat transfer mechanism with a reason.
- For Boyle's Law / gas pressure: demonstrating the cause-and-effect chain from volume change → particle collision frequency → pressure change.

### What the mark scheme penalises

- Saying "heat rises" instead of "hot air rises" or "hot air is less dense and rises" — the mark scheme explicitly excludes "heat alone" (M18 Q3b).
- Saying "particles expand when heated" — particles move faster, they do not expand.
- Describing evaporation cooling without the particle-level mechanism — "the liquid gets cold" earns zero.
- Naming the wrong type of electromagnetic radiation when discussing radiation heat transfer (e.g., saying "UV" instead of "infrared").
- Saying a Dewar flask uses "thick glass" to prevent conduction without mentioning the vacuum.
- Stating only the name of a state change without correctly identifying initial and final states.
- Accepting "sec" or "sec(s)" for the unit of time — always write "s" or "second(s)."
- For evaporation vs boiling comparison: giving only a difference without a similarity (or vice versa) when both are asked for.
- "Cooking oil requires least energy to evaporate" — the correct answer is cooking oil requires the most energy (it barely evaporated at all in the experiment).

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Heat rises" | Hot air rises because it is less dense than cool air. Heat (energy) does not itself rise. |
| "Particles expand when heated" | Particles move faster / have more kinetic energy when heated — their size does not change. |
| "The vacuum in a Dewar flask keeps it cold" | The vacuum prevents conduction and convection by removing the medium — the contents stay at their original temperature. |
| "Evaporation is the same as boiling" | Evaporation occurs at the surface at any temperature and cools the liquid; boiling occurs throughout the liquid at the boiling point only. |
| "The cooling is caused by the cold air outside" | Evaporation cooling is caused by the escape of high-energy particles, reducing average kinetic energy — not by the surrounding air. |
| Omitting the particle step in kinetic theory pressure questions | Must link temperature → particle speed → collision frequency → pressure. Skipping one step loses marks. |
| "Conduction, convection, radiation happen in a vacuum" | Only radiation travels through a vacuum. Conduction and convection both need a medium (matter). |
| Writing "the flask is insulated" without specifying which heat transfer it blocks | Name the feature (silvered surface, vacuum, insulated support) and the specific type of heat transfer it prevents. |
| Reading room temperature from a cooling curve too early | The lab temperature is the plateau the curve settles to — read from where all curves converge and level off. |
| Forgetting to halve the distance in ultrasound / echo calculations | Ultrasound travels to the reflector and back — the distance to the object is half the total path length. (Not strictly thermal, but pattern seen in N22 Q8a.) |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the three-step evaporation cooling explanation (high-energy particles escape → lower-energy remain → temperature falls) — this appears almost every session (M18 Q3c, N20 Q1d, N22 Q2b).
2. Know all six changes of state (melting, freezing, boiling/vaporization, condensation, sublimation, deposition) with correct initial and final states and whether heat is added or removed.
3. For each of the three heat transfer types, have one example where it is the dominant mechanism and one where it is blocked — the Dewar flask covers all three in a single question.
4. Practise the kinetic theory pressure chain for both temperature increase (N21 Q1c style) and Boyle's Law (volume decrease) — exam questions ask for the full chain, not just the conclusion.
5. For insulation / passive house questions (N23 Q7), be ready to name design features and explicitly link each to conduction, convection, or radiation — one mark is always for naming the mechanism.
6. Know how to compare and contrast evaporation and boiling with at least one clear similarity and two differences — the mark scheme awards separate marks for each (N22 Q2b).
7. Practise reading and interpreting cooling curves: identifying laboratory temperature (plateau), ranking cooling effect of liquids, and explaining why some liquids show no cooling effect.
8. Review the E = mcΔT formula — know all four variables, their units, and how to rearrange for any unknown. Show working step by step in calculations.
