# Waves

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

All waves transfer energy without transferring matter. The key distinction is the direction of oscillation relative to the direction of energy transfer.

| Feature | Transverse | Longitudinal |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Particle oscillation | Perpendicular to direction of travel | Parallel to direction of travel |
| Wave regions | Crests and troughs | Compressions and rarefactions |
| Examples | Light, all EM waves, water waves | Sound, ultrasound, infrasound |
| Can travel in vacuum? | Yes (EM waves) | No — needs a medium |

**N16 Q2a (2 marks — Label):** A diagram of a transverse (sinusoidal) wave required labelling of wavelength and amplitude; a longitudinal spring diagram required compression and rarefaction. Labels in order: wavelength — amplitude — compression — rarefaction. Any two correct = 1 mark; all four = 2 marks.

Do NOT confuse "compression" (region of high pressure, particles close together) with "crest." Compression and rarefaction apply only to longitudinal waves.

---

### Wave Properties: Amplitude, Wavelength, Frequency, Period, Wave Speed

**Amplitude (A):** Maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position. Larger amplitude = more energy carried. Do NOT confuse amplitude with wavelength — amplitude is the height of the wave from rest, not peak to trough.

**Wavelength (λ):** Distance between two successive identical points on a wave (e.g., crest to crest, or compression to compression). Unit: metres (m).

**Frequency (f):** Number of complete waves passing a point per second. Unit: hertz (Hz). Read from a displacement-time graph as cycles per second.

**Period (T):** Time for one complete wave. T = 1/f. Unit: seconds (s).

**Wave speed (v):** Speed at which the wave pattern moves through the medium.

**N16 Q2b (1 mark — Calculate):** Wave A completed 12 cycles in 1 second → frequency = 12 Hz. Reading frequency from a displacement-time graph: count full cycles in the time shown, then divide by the total time.

**M23 Q7a (3 marks — Calculate):** Period read from position-time graph as approximately 1.6–1.8 s; frequency = 1/T = 0.56–0.63 Hz. Answer must be to 2 significant figures and include unit (Hz or s⁻¹). Do NOT omit the unit — the unit mark is awarded independently.

Do NOT say "frequency is how often the wave moves" — say "frequency is the number of complete waves per second."

---

### The Wave Equation: v = fλ

The wave equation links all three key wave quantities:

> **v = fλ**   (wave speed = frequency × wavelength)

Rearrangements: f = v/λ and λ = v/f

**N16 Q2d (2 marks — Calculate):** Wave C had 6 cycles per second (f = 6 Hz); speed of infrasound = 330 m s⁻¹. Wavelength = 330/6 = **55 m**. The unit (m) was required for the second mark. "Award one mark only for correct wavelength without unit."

**M23 Q2d (2 marks — Calculate):** Red light wavelength = 750 nm = 750 × 10⁻⁹ m; speed = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. f = v/λ = 3.00 × 10⁸ / 750 × 10⁻⁹ = **4.00 × 10¹⁴ Hz**. Answer must be in scientific notation. Formula step "seen or implied."

**M25 Q2a (3 marks — Calculate):** Yellow light wavelength = 550 nm = 550 × 10⁻⁹ m; speed = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. f = 3.0 × 10⁸ / 550 × 10⁻⁹ = 5.454… × 10¹⁴ Hz → rounded to **5.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz** (2 sig figs). "No ECF from mp2 — a wrong frequency cannot be ECF'd from wrong method."

Always convert nm to m before substituting into v = fλ. 1 nm = 1 × 10⁻⁹ m.

### The Electromagnetic Spectrum

All electromagnetic (EM) waves:
- are transverse waves
- travel at the speed of light in a vacuum: **3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹**
- can travel through a vacuum
- transfer energy

The spectrum ordered from longest wavelength / lowest frequency to shortest wavelength / highest frequency:

| Type | Typical wavelength | Uses / properties |
|------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Radio waves | km–m | Broadcasting, communications |
| Microwaves | cm | Satellite communication, cooking |
| Infrared (IR) | µm | Heat lamps, TV remotes, thermal imaging |
| Visible light | 400–700 nm | Sight, photography |
| Ultraviolet (UV) | nm | Sterilisation, fluorescence, causes sunburn |
| X-rays | 0.01–10 nm | Medical imaging (bones) |
| Gamma rays | <0.01 nm | Cancer treatment, sterilising medical equipment |

**M25 Q2b (1 mark — State):** Name one colour of visible light with a longer wavelength than yellow. Answer: red or orange. (Red has the longest wavelength in the visible spectrum; violet/blue have the shortest.)

**M23 Q2c (1 mark — State):** One difference between infrared and red light: "IR has longer wavelength / lower frequency / is detectable as heat." WTTE.

**M25 Q2c (1 mark — Select):** When light is refracted, which quantity stays constant? Answer: **frequency**. Speed and wavelength both change at a boundary; frequency does not.

Do NOT say "all EM waves travel at the same speed" without specifying "in a vacuum" — they slow down in denser media such as glass.

### Dispersion: White Light Through a Prism

When white light enters a glass prism, each colour refracts by a different amount because each colour travels at a slightly different speed in glass (they have the same speed in a vacuum but different speeds in a medium). The result is dispersion — white light is split into the visible spectrum.

**M23 Q2a (1 mark — Select):** The term for white light being split into colours by a prism is **dispersion** (not reflection, not absorption).

**M23 Q2b (3 marks — Explain):** Why is red light at the top of the spectrum from a prism?
- Red light has the longest wavelength / lowest frequency.
- Red is refracted the least of all colours.
- Red light travels fastest in the prism / has the lowest refractive index in glass / is slowed down the least.

All three points are needed for full marks. Do NOT simply say "red bends less" without explaining why — the mark scheme requires the link to wavelength/frequency and speed in the medium.

---

### Refraction of Light

Refraction occurs when a wave changes speed as it crosses a boundary between two media. The wave bends towards the normal when entering a denser medium (slowing down) and away from the normal when entering a less dense medium (speeding up).

**N16 Q3a–3b (5 marks — Identify / Explain):** A ray passed from glass → water → air (top to bottom), bending at each boundary. Glass has the highest refractive index (1.50), so it slows light the most. The degree of refraction depends on the change in density / refractive index / speed at the boundary. "The ray bends towards the normal as it slows down." (ORA accepted.)

**M25 Q2d (3 marks — Explain):** Why does a person on a cliff see a fish in a different position?
1. Light travels from the fish to the person's eye (water → air).
2. Light refracts / bends away from the normal at the surface.
3. The eyes/brain expect light to travel in a straight line, so the fish appears to be in a different position to where it really is.

Do NOT say "light bends because the water is different" — say "light changes speed at the boundary between water and air, causing it to refract."

---

### Total Internal Reflection

When light travels from a denser to a less dense medium (e.g., glass to air) and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, all light is reflected back inside the medium — no refraction occurs. This is total internal reflection (TIR).

TIR is used in: optical fibres (telecommunications, endoscopes), prismatic periscopes, and diamond cutting (to maximise sparkle).

---

### Sound Waves: Speed, Pitch, and Volume

Sound is a longitudinal wave that requires a medium (cannot travel in a vacuum). Speed of sound varies with medium:

| Medium | Approximate speed |
|--------|------------------|
| Air (room temperature) | ~330–340 m s⁻¹ |
| Water | ~1500 m s⁻¹ |
| Steel | ~5000 m s⁻¹ |

Sound travels faster in solids than liquids than gases because particles are more closely packed and transmit vibrations more quickly.

**Pitch** is determined by frequency: higher frequency → higher pitch.
**Volume (loudness)** is determined by amplitude: larger amplitude → louder sound.

Do NOT mix up pitch and volume. Pitch = frequency; loudness = amplitude. A common error is to say "high volume = high frequency" — this is wrong.

**M22 Q4 (pan flute, 16 marks):** As tube length increases, frequency decreases. The relationship is f = v/4L (for a tube closed at one end), where v = speed of sound. The gradient of a frequency vs 1/4L graph equals v — the speed of sound in air. Calculated value: **343 ± 10 m s⁻¹** (M22 Q4h). Unit: m s⁻¹ (not Hz — the unit mark is awarded separately).

**M22 Q6b (2 marks):** Sound intensity level at 6 m from speaker = **113 dB** (average of three trials; do not accept additional decimal places such as 113.3).

### Infrasound and Ultrasound

**Infrasound:** frequency below 20 Hz (below human hearing range). Examples: elephants, whales, rhinoceros, alligators use it for long-distance communication. Low frequency means long wavelength, meaning less energy is lost over large distances and the wave diffracts more easily around obstacles.

**Ultrasound:** frequency above 20 000 Hz (above human hearing range). Used in: medical imaging (foetal scanning), sonar (depth-finding), industrial flaw detection, SODIS water treatment.

**N16 Q2f (3 marks — Explain):** Elephants communicate over a hill using infrasound because:
1. Identification: diffraction occurs.
2. General explanation: diffraction is the spreading of waves around obstacles / through gaps.
3. Specific link: infrasound has a long wavelength, comparable to the size of the hill, so it diffracts around the hill effectively, reaching elephants on the other side.

All three points required for 3 marks. Do NOT say "infrasound goes through the hill" — say "it diffracts around the hill."

**N16 Q2e (1 mark — Identify):** Of waves A (12 Hz), B (12 Hz), C (6 Hz), D (highest frequency, >20 Hz) — the one that does NOT show infrasound (f < 20 Hz) is wave D (frequency too high to be infrasound).

### Doppler Effect and Red-Shift

When a wave source moves relative to an observer, the observed frequency changes. This is the Doppler effect:
- Source moving towards observer → waves compressed → higher observed frequency (blue-shift).
- Source moving away from observer → waves stretched → lower observed frequency (red-shift).

In astrophysics, **red-shift** is the observation that light from distant galaxies is shifted towards the red (longer wavelength) end of the spectrum. This means galaxies are moving away from us. The more distant the galaxy, the greater its red-shift and the faster it is receding.

**N23 Q1d (3 marks — Describe):** Red-shift observations support the Big Bang theory because:
- Galaxies moving apart (with greater speeds at greater distances) imply the universe is expanding.
- If galaxies are expanding away from each other now, the universe was once in a hot, dense state (the Big Bang).
- The universe is finite in age (~13.7 billion years) and was smaller in the past.

Do NOT say "red-shift means stars are red" — red-shift means the spectral lines are shifted towards longer (redder) wavelengths compared to a stationary source.

---

### Reflection and Diffraction

**Reflection:** A wave bounces off a surface. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection (measured from the normal). Applies to all waves including sound and light.

**Diffraction:** Waves spread out when passing through a gap or around an obstacle. The effect is greatest when the wavelength is similar in size to the gap or obstacle. This is why low-frequency (long wavelength) waves like infrasound and radio waves diffract around hills and buildings, while high-frequency (short wavelength) waves diffract less.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Wave equation calculations:** Show the formula (v = fλ or f = v/λ) seen or implied, then a correct numerical answer, then the correct unit. All three steps can each earn a separate mark.
- **Units on every calculation:** Hertz (Hz) for frequency, metres (m) for wavelength, m s⁻¹ for wave speed. The unit mark is awarded independently — you can lose the numerical mark but still gain the unit mark.
- **Scientific notation:** Required when dealing with EM wave frequencies (~10¹⁴ Hz) or very small wavelengths (nm → m conversion). Must be expressed correctly to the number of sig figs stated.
- **Diffraction explanation:** Must name diffraction, give a general definition (waves spread around obstacles/gaps), then apply to the specific context (wavelength comparable to obstacle size).
- **Refraction explanation:** Must link bending to change in speed at the boundary; must state direction of bending relative to the normal.
- **Dispersion explanation:** Must reference that different colours travel at different speeds in a medium (not in vacuum), linking to wavelength/frequency differences.
- **Frequency vs loudness/pitch:** Pitch = frequency; amplitude = loudness. Must use the correct pairing.
- **Red-shift / Big Bang:** Must state that galaxies are moving apart, universe was once hot and dense, universe is expanding — three separate creditworthy points.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- Wavelength calculations without units — "one mark only for correct wavelength without unit" (N16 Q2d).
- Saying frequency changes during refraction — frequency stays constant; only speed and wavelength change (M25 Q2c).
- "Infrasound goes through the hill" — must say it diffracts around it.
- "Red-shift means stars are red" — red-shift means wavelength is shifted toward the red end compared to a stationary source.
- Frequency expressed to wrong significant figures — M23 Q7a requires 2 sig figs and the unit mark is separate.
- Omitting the conversion 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m before using v = fλ — a common error that loses all marks if the substituted number is wrong.
- "Sound travels through everything" — sound cannot travel in a vacuum; it requires a medium.
- Confusing pitch with volume: saying "louder = higher frequency" is explicitly penalised in sound questions.
- nm left unconverted in wavelength calculations (M23 Q2d, M25 Q2a).

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Red-shift means the star is red-coloured" | Red-shift means spectral lines are shifted to longer wavelengths, indicating the source is moving away |
| "Frequency changes when light is refracted" | Frequency stays constant during refraction — only speed and wavelength change |
| Leaving wavelength in nm in v = fλ | Always convert: 1 nm = 1 × 10⁻⁹ m before substituting |
| "Infrasound travels through the hill" | Infrasound diffracts around the hill because its wavelength is comparable to the hill's size |
| "Louder sound = higher frequency" | Louder = larger amplitude; higher pitch = higher frequency |
| "Sound travels in a vacuum" | Sound is a longitudinal wave requiring a medium; it cannot travel in a vacuum |
| Forgetting the unit in wave speed/frequency calculations | Always state the unit — the mark is awarded independently of the numerical answer |
| "All EM waves travel at the same speed" (without context) | All EM waves travel at 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum; they slow in denser media |
| Saying "compression" for a transverse wave | Compressions and rarefactions only apply to longitudinal waves |
| Confusing amplitude with wavelength on a diagram | Amplitude = height from rest to crest; wavelength = distance from crest to next crest |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the full EM spectrum in order (radio → microwave → IR → visible → UV → X-ray → gamma) with one use for each type.
2. Practise v = fλ calculations including unit conversions (nm → m) and expressing answers in scientific notation to 2 sig figs.
3. Know exactly which quantities change during refraction (speed, wavelength) and which stay constant (frequency) — this is tested directly as a select question.
4. Build the transverse/longitudinal table from memory; be able to label amplitude, wavelength, compression, rarefaction on diagrams.
5. Practise explaining diffraction in three steps: name it, define it generally, apply to the specific context with reference to wavelength and obstacle size.
6. Link red-shift to the Big Bang theory in three clear steps: galaxies moving apart → universe expanding → once hot and dense.
7. For dispersion through a prism: know that red refracts least (longest wavelength, travels fastest in glass), violet refracts most (shortest wavelength, travels slowest in glass).
8. For sound: always distinguish pitch (frequency) from loudness (amplitude) and state that speed increases through solids > liquids > gases.
