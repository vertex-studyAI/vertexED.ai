## 1. Key Concepts

### The Solar System: Structure and Objects

The solar system consists of the Sun at the centre, eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets (such as Pluto and Ceres), the asteroid belt (between Mars and Jupiter), and comets. In order from the Sun, the planets are: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

The **asteroid belt** is a region of rocky debris between Mars and Jupiter. **Comets** are icy bodies with highly elliptical orbits that develop a visible tail when close to the Sun. **Moons** (also called natural satellites) orbit planets rather than the Sun directly.

**Geocentric vs heliocentric models:**

| Model | Description | Disproved by |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Geocentric | Earth is at the centre; all bodies orbit Earth | Galileo's observations of Jupiter's moons (1610) — they orbited Jupiter, not Earth |
| Heliocentric | Sun is at the centre; planets orbit the Sun | Now accepted; explains retrograde motion of Mars and other planets |

**Mark scheme key phrase (N22 Q3b):** To explain how Galileo disproved the geocentric model, you must state that he saw moons orbiting Jupiter — and that if the geocentric model were true, those moons would orbit Earth. Simply saying "he used a telescope" scores 0.

**Retrograde motion of Mars (M22 Q7a):** Mars appears to move backwards in the sky as seen from Earth. This is inconsistent with the geocentric model because if Earth were the centre, Mars would follow a circular arc around Earth and never appear to reverse direction. The mark scheme accepts any two of: Mars seems to go backwards / changes direction; if Earth were the centre, Mars would follow a circular path; Mars's distance from Earth changes.

### Stars: Formation and Life Cycle

A **star** forms when a cloud of gas and dust (nebula) collapses under gravity. The core heats up until nuclear fusion begins — hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing enormous energy. This is a **main sequence star** (like our Sun).

The key difference between a star and a planet (N22 Q3a, N23 Q1c):

| Feature | Star | Planet |
|---------|------|--------|
| Light | Emits own light (luminous) | Reflects light from a star |
| Energy source | Nuclear fusion in the core | No nuclear fusion |
| Size/mass | Much larger and more massive | Smaller |
| Orbit | Does not orbit a planet | Orbits a star |

**Mark scheme note (N23 Q1c):** Do not accept "the Sun is a star" as a difference. Comparison must be implied or explicit.

**Stellar life cycle:**

A star's fate after the main sequence depends on its mass:

**Low/medium mass star (like the Sun):**
- Expands into a **red giant** (hydrogen in core exhausted; outer layers expand and cool)
- Outer layers shed to form a **planetary nebula**
- Core remains as a **white dwarf** (dense, slowly cooling remnant)

**High mass star:**
- Expands into a **red supergiant**
- Explodes in a **supernova** (massive energy release; heavy elements scattered into space)
- Core collapses to form either a **neutron star** (incredibly dense) or, if massive enough, a **black hole** (gravitational field so strong even light cannot escape)

### Galaxies and the Scale of the Universe

A **galaxy** is a vast collection of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity. Our galaxy is the **Milky Way**, a spiral galaxy containing approximately 200–400 billion stars. The solar system is located in one of its spiral arms.

Beyond the Milky Way are billions of other galaxies. The universe contains everything — all matter, energy, space, and time.

**Scale of the universe (approximate distances):**

| Object | Distance from Earth |
|--------|---------------------|
| Moon | ~384 000 km |
| Sun | ~150 million km (1 AU) |
| Proxima Centauri (nearest star) | ~4 × 10¹⁶ m / 4.2 light-years / 1.3 parsecs |
| Milky Way diameter | ~100 000 light-years |
| Andromeda galaxy | ~2.5 million light-years |

**N23 Q1e (non-SI units in astrophysics):**
- **Advantage of parsec/light-year:** simplifies very large numbers; avoids the need for scientific notation in everyday astronomical use.
- **Disadvantage:** unfamiliar to non-specialists; inconsistent with SI units used in the rest of physics; conversions are more difficult.

### Hubble's Law, Red-shift, and the Big Bang

**Red-shift** occurs when a light source is moving away from an observer. The wavelength of the emitted light is stretched (shifted towards the red end of the spectrum), meaning the observed frequency is lower than the emitted frequency. The more distant the galaxy, the greater its red-shift — meaning it is moving away faster.

**Hubble's law** states: the recession speed of a galaxy is proportional to its distance from us. Edwin Hubble established this by observing red-shift in distant galaxies using the Hooker telescope (M24 Q6d).

**Big Bang theory (N23 Q1d):** The observation that all distant galaxies are moving away, and the further they are the faster they recede, implies the universe is expanding. Running this expansion backwards leads to the conclusion that the universe began from a hot, dense point approximately 13.7 billion years ago. Accepted mark scheme points include:

- Galaxies moving apart implies the universe is expanding
- The universe was once in a hot, dense state
- The universe is finite in age (~13.7 billion years)
- The universe was smaller in the past

**M24 Q6d:** Hubble observed red-shift / galaxies moving away / increased wavelength of light from galaxies. The mark scheme also accepts "reference to stars instead of galaxies."

### Orbital Mechanics: Gravity and Orbits

**Gravitational force** acts between any two objects with mass. The factors that determine the force of gravitational attraction (M19 Q3b): **distance** and **mass** (either accepted for 1 mark). The force is always attractive and acts along the line joining the two objects.

**Newton's law of universal gravitation:**

$$F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:
- F = gravitational force (N)
- G = gravitational constant (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²)
- m₁, m₂ = masses of the two objects (kg)
- r = distance between the centres of the objects (m)

This is the **inverse square law**: if the distance doubles, the force decreases by a factor of 4.

**Drawing gravitational force arrows (M19 Q3c):** Arrows must point toward the Sun at both positions. The arrow at the position closer to the Sun must be longer (larger force). Both arrows must be drawn toward the Sun, not perpendicular to the orbit.

**Gravitational field strength (g):** The force per unit mass experienced at a point in a gravitational field. Used to calculate the weight of an object: W = mg.

**Types of orbit:**

| Orbit type | Altitude | Period | Use |
|------------|----------|--------|-----|
| Geostationary (GEO) | ~36 000 km | 24 hours (matches Earth's rotation) | Communications; weather; TV broadcasting; appears fixed above one point |
| Low Earth Orbit (LEO) | 200–2000 km | ~90 minutes | International Space Station; Earth observation; Hubble Space Telescope |

**M25 Q7a–7b (Newton's cannonball):** At orbital speed (~7800 m s⁻¹), the cannonball falls at the same rate as Earth's surface curves — it enters orbit. The only force on a satellite in circular orbit is **gravity** (weight, directed toward Earth's centre). Do not add thrust or any other force for an object in a stable orbit.

### Telescopes: Refracting and Reflecting

**Refracting telescope:** Uses a convex lens to focus light. A disadvantage is **chromatic aberration** — different wavelengths (colours) of light refract by different amounts through a lens, causing colour fringing (M24 Q6a). This is because white light is made of different frequencies, and different frequencies refract by different amounts.

**Reflecting telescope:** Uses a curved primary mirror and a flat secondary mirror to focus light. No chromatic aberration because reflection is independent of wavelength. Used in the Hooker telescope (used by Hubble) and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST).

| Feature | Refracting | Reflecting |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Focussing element | Convex lens | Curved mirror |
| Chromatic aberration | Yes — colours separate | No |
| Large apertures | Difficult (heavy lenses sag) | Easier (mirrors can be supported from behind) |
| Examples | Early Galileo telescope | Hooker, Hubble, JWST |

**Advantages of space telescopes (M24 Q6e):** A telescope in space can make clearer observations because it is above Earth's atmosphere. The atmosphere causes: scattering, refraction, absorption, light pollution, and weather disturbances. Do not accept "it is closer to space/stars" — this scores 0 in the mark scheme.

**JWST specifically:** Detects infrared radiation; positioned at L2 point ~1.5 million km from Earth; cannot be physically repaired; was designed to observe the formation of early galaxies and search for signs of life on exoplanets (M24 Q7a–7b).

### Light-Years and Speed of Light Calculations

A **light-year** is the distance light travels in one year in a vacuum. It is a unit of distance, not time.

Speed of light: c = 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹

**Formula:** distance = speed × time, i.e. d = c × t

**M22 Q7b (worked example):** Calculate the time for a radio signal to travel 4 × 10¹¹ m at 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹.

t = d / v = (4 × 10¹¹) / (3 × 10⁸) = 1333 s ≈ 22 minutes

The mark scheme awards: (1) correct time in seconds; (2) correct conversion and rounding to nearest minute. A correct final answer alone earns both marks.

**Proxima Centauri (N23):** 4 × 10¹⁶ m away = 1.3 parsecs. Questions may ask you to convert between metres and light-years, or to identify the advantage of non-SI units (smaller numbers).

### Gravitational Force Calculations (F = Gm₁m₂/r²)

MYP papers often provide g (gravitational field strength) rather than asking students to use the full Newton's law formula. The weight formula W = mg is used more frequently in calculations.

**M19 Q3d–3e (Oumuamua — worked example):**

Step 1 — Calculate mass (Q3d):
- density = mass / volume → mass = density × volume
- mass = 2000 kg m⁻³ × 281 000 m³ = 5.62 × 10⁸ kg

Step 2 — Calculate gravitational force (Q3e):
- W = mg = 5.62 × 10⁸ × 0.1 = 5.62 × 10⁷ N

Mark scheme note: ECF from Q3d applies — if an incorrect mass is used correctly with g = 0.1, the method mark is still awarded.

**Mark scheme key phrase:** In M19 Q3b, either "distance" or "mass" is accepted — do not need to give both for 1 mark.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Red-shift / expanding universe (M24 Q6d):** The mark scheme accepts "galaxies moving away," "red-shift," or "increased wavelength/decreased frequency of light from galaxies." Either "stars" or "galaxies" is accepted.
- **Space telescope advantage (M24 Q6e):** Award requires (1) a reference to the atmosphere or vacuum, AND (2) a specific effect — scattering, refraction, light pollution, weather, or absorption. Both parts needed for full marks.
- **Geocentric disproof (N22 Q3b):** Must state (1) moons orbit Jupiter, AND (2) if geocentric were true, moons would orbit Earth. WTTE (words to the effect) accepted.
- **Star vs planet (N22 Q3a, N23 Q1c):** Any two valid differences accepted. "Satellite" accepted as equivalent to "moon." The mark scheme gives a long list — any two correct differences score full marks.
- **Big Bang (N23 Q1d):** Three marks — any three from: universe expanding (galaxies moving apart); hot dense state at beginning; finite age (~13.7 billion years); universe was smaller in the past. All four are equivalent in mark scheme weight.
- **Force arrows (M19 Q3c):** Both arrows must point toward the Sun. Arrow closer to Sun must be shorter (greater distance = smaller force — arrow at position B which is closer should be longer). Judged by eye.
- **Gravitational force factor (M19 Q3b):** Only one of "distance" or "mass" required for 1 mark.
- **Calculation questions:** A correct final answer alone earns all marks ("seen or implied"). ECF applies throughout — using a wrong earlier answer correctly in a later step still earns the method mark.
- **Mars living conditions (M22 Q9):** Requires both effect and solution for each of temperature, radiation, and low gravity. Effect alone scores half. "Constant heating" as a solution to cold temperatures is NOT accepted unless insulation is also mentioned.
- **Non-SI units (N23 Q1e):** Any reasonable advantage (smaller numbers, less scientific notation) and any reasonable disadvantage (unfamiliarity, difficulty converting) each earn 1 mark.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Space telescope advantage (M24 Q6e):** Do NOT say "it is closer to space" or "closer to the stars" — this is explicitly rejected.
- **Chromatic aberration (M24 Q6a):** Must say white light is made of different frequencies/wavelengths, AND different frequencies refract by different amounts. Saying only "light bends" earns 0.
- **Retrograde motion (M22 Q7a):** Must link retrograde motion to the geocentric model specifically — general statements about Mars's orbit without reference to the geocentric prediction score 0.
- **Orbiting satellite forces (M25 Q7b):** Only gravity/weight directed toward Earth. Do NOT add thrust, centripetal force as an additional force, or any horizontal force. Centripetal is the role gravity plays — it is not a separate force.
- **Star vs planet (N23 Q1c):** Do NOT accept "the Sun is a star" as a difference between stars and planets.
- **Solar system objects (N23 Q1b):** Do NOT accept human-made objects such as rockets or satellites.
- **Mars launch window (M22 Q7c):** Answers must relate to the shortest journey time/distance or fuel minimisation. General statements about Mars being visible in July are not sufficient.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Saying a space telescope is better "because it is closer to stars" | The advantage is the absence of atmosphere — no scattering, refraction, absorption, or light pollution |
| Confusing light-year (distance) with a measure of time | A light-year is the distance light travels in one year; it is always a unit of distance |
| Drawing gravitational force arrows pointing away from the Sun, or perpendicular to orbit | Gravity always points toward the attracting body (the Sun); arrows must point toward the Sun |
| Saying "the force of gravity is zero" in orbit | Gravity still acts on satellites in orbit — it is what keeps them in orbit. Objects feel weightless but gravity is not zero |
| Listing "thrust" as a force on an orbiting satellite | A satellite in a stable circular orbit has only one force: gravity toward Earth. No thrust |
| Saying geocentric model is wrong "because Galileo used a telescope" | Must say he observed moons orbiting Jupiter — not Earth — which disproved the geocentric model |
| Saying Mars's retrograde motion is "because Mars moves backwards" | Must explain this is inconsistent with geocentric because Mars would follow a circular arc around Earth |
| Confusing red-shift with blue-shift | Objects moving away show red-shift (wavelength increases). Blue-shift occurs for approaching objects |
| Saying the Big Bang was an explosion in space | The Big Bang was an expansion of space itself from a hot, dense state — not an explosion through existing space |
| Giving only "distance" or only "mass" as a factor in gravity, when asked for both | For 1 mark, either is fine. If asked for two factors, both mass and distance are needed |
| Chromatic aberration: saying "colours split because of different speeds" without further detail | Must say different frequencies/wavelengths refract by different amounts (have different refractive indices) |
| Not converting seconds to minutes in signal travel time questions (M22 Q7b) | Read the question — M22 specifically asks for the answer "to the nearest minute." Always check units requested |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Memorise the planet order** and know where the asteroid belt sits (between Mars and Jupiter). Drag-and-drop questions on solar system diagrams appear in M19, N23, and implicitly in other sessions.

2. **Know the geocentric vs heliocentric argument cold** — especially Galileo's Jupiter moons observation. This is one of the most frequently tested conceptual points (N22, M22). You need both parts: what he saw, and why it disproves the geocentric model.

3. **Practise gravitational force calculations** using both W = mg (more common in MYP) and the full inverse-square law F = Gm₁m₂/r². For M19 Q3d–3e, the two-step calculation (density → mass → force) is a good worked example.

4. **Learn the stellar life cycle** with correct sequence and terminology: nebula → main sequence → red giant/red supergiant → white dwarf / supernova → neutron star/black hole. Know which path applies to which mass of star.

5. **Understand red-shift and the Big Bang** as a linked argument: red-shift of galaxies → universe expanding → Big Bang. Be ready to write 3–4 mark descriptions (N23 Q1d format). Accept all four Big Bang bullet points as equivalent.

6. **Distinguish refracting from reflecting telescopes** and know why chromatic aberration occurs in refracting only. Know the two advantages of space telescopes: atmosphere-free observation AND the specific effect (scattering, refraction, etc.).

7. **For Criterion D (extended response) space questions** — JWST (M24), Mars missions (M22), satellites (M25), space elevators (M25) — always include: scientific/technical challenge, social/economic/political implication, and a concluding appraisal. Missing any one of these drops you an entire mark band.

8. **Learn the key unit:** a light-year is a unit of distance. Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. Time = distance / speed. Always check whether the question asks for time in seconds or minutes (M22 Q7b required minutes).
