# Electricity and Magnetism

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Static Electricity — Charging by Friction and Induction

All materials contain equal numbers of positive and negative charges in their neutral state. Only electrons can move between materials — protons and neutrons cannot transfer.

**Charging by friction:** When two materials are rubbed together, electrons are transferred from one surface to the other. The material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged; the one that loses electrons becomes positively charged.

**Charging by induction:** A charged object brought near a neutral object causes electrons within the neutral object to redistribute. The side nearest the charged object acquires the opposite charge — this is induction. The objects are then attracted without touching.

| Feature | Conductors | Insulators |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Examples | Copper, aluminium, steel | Rubber, plastic, glass |
| Charge movement | Electrons move freely | Electrons cannot move freely |
| Behaviour when charged | Charge spreads across surface | Charge stays where it was deposited |
| Can be charged by friction? | Difficult (charge leaks away) | Yes |

**Mark scheme key phrase (N16 Q4b; N17 Q1b):** "Electrons are the only particle which can move / electrons are transferred." Do NOT say "charge is transferred" without specifying that it is electrons. Do NOT say "protons move."

**N16 Q4c (worked example — induction, 4 marks):**
Harry the cat rolls on the carpet and becomes charged. Polystyrene pellets stick to his fur.
- Cat is charged by friction with carpet
- Pellets are uncharged (neutral)
- The charge on the cat induces an opposite charge in the surface of the pellets
- The induced opposite charge is attracted to the cat's charge

**N17 Q1c–1d:** Two pieces of nylon charged identically by rubbing repel each other. If they touch, nothing happens — same charge means no net transfer of electrons.

### Current, Voltage and Resistance — Ohm's Law (V = IR)

**Current (I):** Flow of electric charge (electrons) around a circuit. Measured in amperes (A) using an ammeter connected in series.

**Voltage (V):** The "push" that drives current around the circuit — also called potential difference (p.d.). Measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter connected in parallel across a component.

**Resistance (R):** Opposition to the flow of current. Measured in ohms (Ω).

**Ohm's Law:** V = IR (or R = V/I)

> A component is an **ohmic conductor** if its current is directly proportional to voltage — the graph of I vs V is a straight line through the origin. The resistance is constant.
> A component is a **non-ohmic conductor** if the graph curves — resistance changes with voltage or current. A filament bulb is non-ohmic because its resistance increases as it heats up.

**M16 Q7c (worked example — calculating resistance from a graph, 4 marks):**
Two data points on a V–I graph are chosen; resistance is calculated using R = V/I for each:
- Low voltage point: e.g. V = 2 V, I = 0.05 A → R = 40 Ω
- High voltage point: e.g. V = 10 V, I = 0.12 A → R = 83 Ω
- Resistance increases at higher voltage — because the filament heats up and atoms vibrate more, causing more collisions with electrons.

**Mark scheme note (M16 Q7d):** To support or reject the hypothesis "current is directly proportional to voltage," you must state that a proportional relationship requires the I–V graph to be a straight line through the origin. Do NOT just say "the hypothesis is supported/rejected" alone — this mark is not awarded without a reference to the graph shape.

**N21 Q4b (ohmic conductor test):** To check if component X is ohmic, calculate the ratio V/I for at least two data points. If the ratio is constant (within experimental precision), it is ohmic.

**N21 Q4c (non-ohmic):** Component Y's graph is not straight — gradient is not constant — so it is a non-ohmic conductor. Do NOT award the second mark unless the first (graph is not straight) is awarded.

### Series vs Parallel Circuits — Current and Voltage Rules

| Property | Series circuit | Parallel circuit |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Current | Same throughout — I is the same past every component | Splits at junctions — each branch carries part of the total |
| Voltage | Splits across components — V₁ + V₂ + … = V_total | Same across each parallel branch — equals the supply voltage |
| Total resistance | Increases — R_total = R₁ + R₂ + … | Decreases — total resistance is less than the smallest branch |
| Effect of removing one component | Circuit breaks — all components stop | Other branches continue to work |
| Ammeter placement | In series with component being measured | In series within the branch being measured |
| Voltmeter placement | In parallel across the component | In parallel across the branch |

**M16 Q6a (worked example — ammeter placement, 2 marks):**
An ammeter placed in parallel with a bulb cannot measure the bulb's current because ammeters have negligible resistance — almost all current flows through the ammeter and bypasses the bulb. The ammeter must be in series.

**N21 Q6a (circuit diagram, 3 marks):**
Two bulbs in series: ammeter connected in series in the main loop to measure current through both bulbs. No additional components in series with the bulbs. Voltmeter (if added) must be in parallel.

**Mark scheme note:** Accept a voltmeter added in parallel — it does not invalidate the circuit. Do NOT connect ammeter in parallel.

### Power in Circuits: P = IV, P = I²R, P = V²/R

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred. All three formulae are equivalent and derived from P = IV and V = IR.

| Formula | Use when you know |
|---------|-----------------|
| P = IV | Current and voltage |
| P = I²R | Current and resistance |
| P = V²/R | Voltage and resistance |

**N17 Q3b (worked example — calculating power, 2 marks):**
Circuit has a 12 V battery and a bulb drawing 0.5 A.
P = IV = 0.5 × 12 = **6 W**
Unit must be given: watts (W). Not accepted without unit.

**N17 Q3c (charge, 1 mark):**
Charge Q = I × t = 0.5 × 100 = **50 coulombs**

**N17 Q3e (economics — LED bulbs, 4 marks):**
The gradient of a cost vs time graph represents running costs (cost per hour of use). LED bulbs have the shallowest gradient — lowest running cost. The y-intercept shows initial purchase cost. The intersection of two lines shows the point at which the total cost (purchase + running) is the same for both options. Beyond the intersection, LED becomes cheaper overall.

### Resistance — Factors Affecting It

Resistance of a wire depends on four factors:

| Factor | Effect on resistance | Reason |
|--------|---------------------|--------|
| Length (L) | Longer wire → higher resistance | Electrons collide with more ions as they travel further |
| Cross-sectional area (A) | Larger area → lower resistance | More pathways for electrons; fewer collisions per electron |
| Material (resistivity ρ) | Higher resistivity → higher resistance | Material property — number and mobility of charge carriers |
| Temperature | Higher temperature → higher resistance (for metals) | Atoms vibrate more, causing more collisions with electrons |

**Formula:** R = ρL/A (where ρ = resistivity of the material)

The gradient of a resistance vs length graph equals ρ/A.

**N21 Q5a–5f (worked example — resistance and length, systematic error):**
- A graph of resistance vs length should pass through the origin (zero length = zero resistance)
- If the graph does not intercept the origin, this indicates a systematic error (e.g. zero error in ammeter, resistance of connecting wires)
- The systematic error shifts all data points equally, so the gradient of the graph is still correct — the statement that gradient accuracy is affected is invalid

**M24 Q3b (hypothesis — resistance and length, 3 marks):**
If: the length of the wire increases
Then: the resistance increases
Because: electrons collide with more ions as they pass through a longer wire / electrons have to travel further with more obstacles / R = ρL/A shows resistance is directly proportional to length.

**M24 Q3c (selecting wires — control variables, 3 marks):**
To investigate resistance vs length, only the length must vary. Select wires of the same material and same cross-sectional area across different lengths. The correct selection is: 40, 60, 80, 100, 120 cm silver wires at 2.5 mm² cross-sectional area.

**Mark scheme note (M24 Q3d):** Justify by stating: length is the only variable that should change; material and cross-sectional area must be controlled; five data points are required for sufficient data.

**N21 Q5f (area and resistance — hypothesis, 3 marks):**
If the area of the wire increases, resistance decreases — inversely proportional. Reason: a larger area provides more pathways for electrons; fewer collisions per unit length.

**N17 Q3d (variable resistor — brightness, 5 marks):**
When a variable resistor is added in series and its resistance increases:
- Fixed voltage from the supply
- Total resistance in circuit increases
- Current in circuit decreases (same current through all series components)
- Voltage across bulb decreases
- Brightness decreases because brightness depends on current (or voltage or power)
All five steps are needed for full marks; each one follows logically from the last.

### Electromagnetic Induction — Generators and Transformers

**Electromagnetic induction:** When a conductor moves through a magnetic field (or when a magnetic field changes through a conductor), an electromotive force (EMF) is induced. This is the principle behind electrical generators.

**Generator:** A coil of wire rotated in a magnetic field. As the coil cuts through field lines, a current is induced. The output is alternating current (AC) because the direction of motion (and therefore the induced current) reverses each half-turn.

**Transformers** use electromagnetic induction to change the voltage of an AC supply. They do not work with DC.

**Transformer equation:**

> Vp / Vs = Np / Ns

where Vp = primary voltage, Vs = secondary voltage, Np = number of turns on primary coil, Ns = number of turns on secondary coil.

| Transformer type | Ns vs Np | Effect on voltage | Effect on current |
|-----------------|----------|------------------|------------------|
| Step-up | Ns > Np | Voltage increases | Current decreases |
| Step-down | Ns < Np | Voltage decreases | Current increases |

**For an ideal (100% efficient) transformer:** Vp × Ip = Vs × Is (power in = power out)

**N16 Q9c (worked example — transformer and current, 1 mark):**
If P = IV and the secondary voltage doubles, the secondary current halves (power is conserved in an ideal transformer).

**N16 Q9d (why high voltage reduces transmission losses, 3 marks):**
- Energy loss in cables is proportional to the size of the current (P_loss = I²R)
- Increasing the transmission voltage reduces the current for the same power
- Lower current means less heat generated in the cables — less energy lost

**Mark scheme note (N16 Q9d):** All three steps must be present. "Less energy is lost" alone is not enough — must explain the mechanism via current reduction and heat.

### Magnetic Fields — Field Lines and Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

**Magnetic field lines:**
- Show the direction a north pole would move
- Run from north pole to south pole outside the magnet
- Closer lines = stronger field
- Never cross

**M24 Q4d:** The field pattern of a bar magnet shows lines looping from the north pole, curving around, and entering the south pole — denser near the poles. Correct option is A (lines from N curving to S on the same bar).

**Force on a current-carrying conductor:** When a wire carrying a current is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. The direction of the force depends on:
- The direction of the current
- The direction of the magnetic field

This is the principle behind electric motors.

**M24 Q5 (current-carrying rod — force and current, 11 marks):**
A copper rod carrying current in a magnetic field accelerates. The force on the rod is related to the current — when current is doubled the force should double if the relationship is proportional. However, the graph of force vs current shows a straight line that does not pass through the origin (x-intercept at 4 A), which means the hypothesis is not supported:
- At currents below 4 A, the magnetic force cannot overcome friction — the rod does not move
- The frictional force can be estimated from the x-intercept of the force graph (~2.7 mN)

**Mark scheme note (M24 Q5e):** To outline why hypothesis is not supported, state that the line does not pass through the origin OR that doubling the current does not double the force — use specific data points to show this.

### Electrostatics Applications

**Lightning conductor:**
- A metal spike attached to the top of a tall building, connected by a thick copper wire to the earth
- Metal has lower resistance than the building structure
- Current from lightning flows through the conductor to earth rather than through the building
- Prevents structural damage and fire

**N18 Q2d (worked example — lightning conductor, 3 marks):**
- Metal is a better conductor / has a lower resistance than the building
- Current/charge from lightning will flow through the conductor
- Current passes to earth without passing through the building

**Charging by friction in thunderstorms (N18 Q2a):**
Water droplets and ice crystals collide in storm clouds. Electrons are transferred from crystals to water drops — water becomes negative at the base of the cloud, ice becomes positive at the top. The correct answer: electrons are transferred from crystals to water drops. Do NOT say protons move.

**Photocopier:** A drum is given a positive electrostatic charge. Light reflects off white areas of the page and discharges those parts of the drum. Toner (negatively charged ink) is attracted to the remaining charged (dark) areas and transferred to paper.

**Defibrillator:** Uses a large capacitor to store charge. A controlled electrical shock is delivered to the chest to restore the heart's normal rhythm. The current passes through the heart muscle.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Electron transfer, not charge transfer:** State explicitly that electrons move. "Charge is transferred" is vague; "electrons are transferred from X to Y" is credited.
- **Ohm's law hypothesis:** Must reference the graph — "a proportional relationship requires a straight line through the origin." The conclusion alone ("hypothesis is supported") is never awarded without the graphical reasoning.
- **Series circuit brightness:** Award full marks only for a complete causal chain — voltage fixed → resistance increases → current decreases → voltage across bulb decreases → brightness decreases. Missing one step loses a mark.
- **Transformer current:** When secondary voltage doubles, secondary current halves. State the reason: power is conserved (P = IV), so I must decrease when V increases.
- **High-voltage transmission:** Three-step answer — energy loss proportional to current; increasing voltage reduces current; reduced current means less heat lost. All three steps required.
- **Lightning conductor:** Three steps — metal is better conductor (lower resistance); current flows through conductor; passes to earth. Each step is a separate mark.
- **Resistance factors:** R = ρL/A is the linking formula. For a hypothesis, use If/Then/Because format and reference electron collisions with ions for the Because.
- **Non-ohmic conductor:** State that the graph is not straight (gradient not constant), then conclude it is non-ohmic. Second mark depends on first.
- **Power calculation:** P = IV; unit must be given (watts, W). Not awarded without unit in most mark schemes.
- **Circuit diagrams:** Ammeter in series; voltmeter in parallel. Any additional components (e.g. switch) are ignored — do not cost marks if circuit is otherwise correct.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **"Protons move" in charging questions** — protons cannot move between materials; only electrons transfer. Explicitly excluded.
- **"Charge is transferred" without specifying electrons** — insufficient for charging by friction questions.
- **"The hypothesis is supported" / "is not supported" alone** — never awarded without the graphical or mathematical justification.
- **"Less energy is wasted" alone in transmission questions** — must explain via current reduction and I²R heating.
- **Weight instead of mass (M24 Q5c)** — for calculating force from acceleration, you need mass. "Weight" is not accepted.
- **"Gravity" for the downward force (N17 Q2c)** — must say "weight" or "gravitational force."
- **Resistance of zero when length is zero ignored in graphs** — if a resistance–length graph does not intercept the origin, this is systematic error, not a problem with the gradient calculation.
- **"LED bulbs are more economical over time" alone (N17 Q3e)** — this restates the question. Must use gradient, y-intercept, and intersection to justify.
- **Voltmeter in series / ammeter in parallel** — these are penalised in circuit diagram questions.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Protons are transferred when objects are charged by friction" | Only electrons transfer; protons are fixed in the nucleus |
| "The hypothesis is supported because the results match" | Must state: proportional relationship requires straight line through origin; compare this to graph shape |
| "Adding resistance makes the bulb brighter" | Adding series resistance reduces current → reduces brightness |
| Connecting ammeter in parallel with a component | Ammeter must be in series; parallel placement bypasses the component (all current flows through ammeter) |
| "High voltage reduces energy loss because it is safer" | Must explain: high voltage → lower current → less heat dissipated in cables (P_loss = I²R) |
| "Resistance increases when wire is thicker" | Larger cross-sectional area → lower resistance (more pathways for electrons) |
| Confusing step-up and step-down transformers | Step-up: more turns on secondary → higher secondary voltage; step-down: fewer turns on secondary → lower secondary voltage |
| "The lightning conductor attracts lightning" | The conductor provides a low-resistance path to earth — current flows through it rather than the building |
| "LDR resistance increases in bright light" | LDR resistance decreases as light intensity increases |
| "Thermistor resistance increases when temperature rises" | Standard (NTC) thermistor resistance decreases as temperature increases |
| Forgetting to halve the distance in ultrasound calculations | Ultrasound travels to the object and back — divide total distance by 2 to find one-way distance |
| "Voltage is the same in a series circuit" | Voltage splits across series components; current is the same throughout |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Master the three core formulae — V = IR, P = IV (and P = I²R, P = V²/R) — and know which to use. Practice using them from a graph as well as from given values.

2. Learn the rules for series and parallel circuits cold: current is the same in series; voltage is the same across parallel branches. These rules appear in almost every circuit question.

3. Know the factors affecting resistance (length, area, material, temperature) and be able to write a full If/Then/Because hypothesis linking each factor to resistance via electron collisions.

4. Practise drawing and interpreting circuit diagrams: ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel, correct symbols for LDR, thermistor, LED. A wrong ammeter placement loses marks in both Criterion A and B questions.

5. Understand the ohmic vs non-ohmic distinction using graphs. A straight line through the origin = ohmic. A curve = non-ohmic. Always reference the graph shape, not just the conclusion.

6. For transformer and transmission questions, learn the three-step chain: high voltage → lower current → less heat lost in cables. Know Vp/Vs = Np/Ns and apply it to step-up and step-down scenarios.

7. For static electricity, always state that electrons (not protons, not "charge") are transferred. Name the direction of transfer (from X to Y) for full marks.

8. For electrostatics applications (lightning conductor, photocopier, defibrillator), learn the mechanism in three steps — each step is typically one mark.
