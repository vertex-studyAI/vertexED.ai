# Extended Writing (Criterion D)

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### How Criterion D Markbands Work

Criterion D (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science) is always assessed using a markband table, never a single right-or-wrong mark scheme. Each extended writing question is split into three to five named sub-criteria — for example, Technical / Economic / Political / Concluding appraisal — and each sub-criterion is scored on a 1–4 (or 1–2) scale independently. Your total mark is the sum of all sub-criterion scores. This means a weak paragraph on one sub-criterion does not drag down a strong paragraph on another. It also means you can score Band 4 on economic implications while scoring only Band 1 on the concluding appraisal — the marks are completely independent.

The general logic across all markbands is: **Band 1 = one point stated; Band 2 = one point supported with evidence or two points stated; Band 3 = two points, one with justification; Band 4 = two points, both with justification.** "Support" means naming a specific piece of evidence from the stimulus material or from scientific knowledge. "Justification" means explaining the mechanism or consequence, not just restating the claim.

**M18 Q8 (Three Gorges Dam, 14 marks):** The markband for River Impacts was: Band 1 — a positive or negative impact stated; Band 2 — an impact supported with information; Band 3 — both a positive and negative impact, each supported; Band 4 — both impacts supported and linked to scientific knowledge. The phrase "linked to science" is the gate to Band 4. It means citing a physical or biological mechanism — for example, not just "fish species declined" but "disruption of flow regime altered spawning migration routes, threatening the 177 unique fish species of the Yangtze."

---

### Discuss vs Evaluate vs Justify — Command Term Differences in Practice

These three command terms appear regularly in Criterion D questions and are not interchangeable.

**Discuss** means consider different viewpoints or arguments. A discuss question expects you to explore multiple sides — advantages and disadvantages, positive and negative implications — without necessarily reaching a verdict. In practice, every Criterion D extended question labelled "discuss" has a separate sub-criterion for a positive perspective and a negative perspective. Omitting one side entirely caps you at Band 1 or 2 for that sub-criterion.

**Evaluate** means weigh the evidence and reach a judgement. In the mark scheme, "evaluate" questions always include a concluding appraisal sub-criterion worth 1–2 marks. A Band 1 appraisal is a simple conclusion ("I think the JWST is worth the cost"). A Band 2 appraisal explicitly balances the arguments you raised and explains why the positive outweighs the negative or vice versa. Do not end with "in conclusion, there are advantages and disadvantages" — that scores Band 1. Write: "Although the $10 billion cost could fund healthcare, the long-term economic return from technological spin-offs and global scientific collaboration justifies the investment" — that scores Band 2.

**Justify** appears mainly within sub-criteria rather than as the main command term. In the economic sub-criterion of M22 Q8, scoring Band 3 required that at least one economic point was "supported with further discussion" — this means you explain the mechanism, not just name the point.

**M21 Q8 (Drones in Surveillance, 14 marks):** The social implication sub-criterion distinguished: Band 2 — a positive and negative social implication stated; Band 3 — the same, with one supported by further justification; Band 4 — both supported. Students who wrote "drones can be used to spy on people (negative) but also help find missing persons (positive)" scored Band 2. Students who added "privacy is a fundamental right, so unregulated drone use could undermine civil liberties in ways not easily reversed by legislation" scored Band 3.

### What "Multiple Perspectives" Means in Practice

The stimulus material and the bullet-point prompt in each Criterion D question define which perspectives are expected. You do not need to spontaneously generate them — but you do need to address each one named in the prompt to reach the top band for each sub-criterion. The common perspective categories across all sessions are:

| Perspective | What it means in a physics D essay |
|---|---|
| Scientific / Technical | Physical mechanisms, challenges of the technology, links to physics knowledge |
| Economic | Costs, funding, government budgets, return on investment, industry impact |
| Social | Effects on individuals, communities, quality of life, equity of access |
| Political | International relations, government policy, security, national prestige |
| Ethical | Rights, fairness, consent, unintended consequences, values |
| Environmental | Ecological impact, emissions, resource use |

Not every question asks for all six. M22 Q8 (Mars missions) asked for Technical, Economic, Political, Concluding. N22 Q8b (Ultrasound vs X-rays) asked for Waves mechanism, Health, Economic, Ethical, Concluding. N24 Q6c (Hand pump) asked for Health, Ethical, Concluding. Read the bullet-point prompt carefully — it tells you exactly which perspectives the markband table assesses.

A common mistake is writing only about a perspective you are confident in (usually economic or environmental) and ignoring the others. Because each perspective is a separate sub-criterion, skipping one means a guaranteed 0 for that row.

### How to Structure a High-Scoring Criterion D Essay

The structure that maps most cleanly onto the markband table is: one paragraph per sub-criterion, in the order the bullet-point prompt lists them, followed by a separate concluding appraisal paragraph.

**Suggested paragraph structure for each perspective:**

1. Open with a clear statement of the positive point (one sentence).
2. Support it with specific data or scientific mechanism from the stimulus (one to two sentences).
3. State the negative counterpoint (one sentence).
4. Support it with evidence or mechanism (one to two sentences).
5. Optional: add a third point for the "additional advantage or disadvantage" required for Band 4 in some question structures (see N20 Q9).

This gives you Band 3 minimum for that sub-criterion and positions you for Band 4 if the supporting detail is sufficiently scientific. The total essay should be around 400–600 words. Bullet points are not penalised but the mark scheme uses the phrase "supported by further justification," which is easier to demonstrate in continuous prose.

**N20 Q9 (Transport options, Amsterdam to Paris, 15 marks):** The economic sub-criterion had four levels: Band 4 required "an economic advantage and disadvantage and an additional advantage or disadvantage compared to driving." This means three economic points, not two. Students who wrote only one advantage and one disadvantage were capped at Band 3. The comfort and safety sub-criteria only went up to Band 3 and Band 2 respectively — so spending extra time on those relative to economic and environmental was a poor strategy.

### Appraisal: What It Is and How to Write One That Scores the Top Band

Every Criterion D extended question includes a "concluding appraisal" sub-criterion worth 1–2 marks. This is always the last bullet point in the question prompt.

**Band 1 appraisal:** A simple conclusion. "I think the Three Gorges Dam was a good idea." This scores Band 1.

**Band 2 appraisal:** A conclusion that explicitly references the arguments you raised and weighs them. The mark scheme for M18 Q8 describes Band 2 as "a concluding appraisal evaluating all arguments." For M24 Q7a (JWST), Band 2 is: "Candidate recognises that there are positive and negative aspects but makes a case for why they are in support/against by weighing up both sides."

The practical formula: **"Although [strongest counter-argument from your essay], [your position] because [reason that tips the balance]."**

Example for M22 Q8 (Mars missions): "Although the $100 billion cost could be redirected to address immediate humanitarian needs, the long-term political and scientific returns — including the development of new materials and the demonstration of international technological leadership — justify the investment for a government with the fiscal capacity to sustain it."

Do not introduce a new argument in the appraisal. Reference arguments already made in the body. Do not write "there are advantages and disadvantages to both sides" — this is a non-appraisal and scores Band 1.

### Linking to Scientific Knowledge — What It Means in D Essays

In Criterion D, "link to scientific knowledge" or "use scientific language correctly" appears as the gate to Band 4 in many sub-criteria. This does not mean the essay has to be about physics facts — it means the explanation of any perspective must invoke a scientific mechanism where one exists.

| Sub-criterion | Example of a non-scientific statement (Band 2–3) | Example with scientific link (Band 4) |
|---|---|---|
| Environmental impact of the dam | "The dam harmed wildlife in the river." | "Altered flow regime and sediment transport disrupted spawning migration of Yangtze fish species, including the extinction of the Chinese river dolphin." |
| Health impact of x-rays | "X-rays are dangerous for pregnant women." | "X-ray absorption increases ionisation in rapidly dividing foetal cells, raising the probability of DNA damage and subsequent cancer development." |
| Technical challenge of JWST | "The telescope was hard to launch." | "The 6.5 m primary mirror had to be folded into 18 hexagonal segments to fit the rocket fairing, requiring autonomous mechanical deployment at L2 without possibility of repair." |
| Climate effect of fossil fuels | "Burning coal causes climate change." | "Combustion of coal releases CO₂, which increases the concentration of greenhouse gases and enhances absorption and re-emission of infrared radiation, raising mean global temperature." |

In practice, "linked to science" can be achieved in one sentence appended to any point. It does not require a full physics explanation — it requires that you name the mechanism rather than only naming the effect.

**M21 Q8 (Drones, Criterion D):** The environmental monitoring sub-criterion at Band 4 required "a statement of at least two uses of drones in environmental monitoring and a statement of how these uses are a benefit to science" — the phrase "benefit to science" is the science-link requirement.

### How Partial Mark-Scheme Credit Works

Because Criterion D uses a markband table with independent sub-criteria, partial credit is the norm, not the exception. Each sub-criterion row is scored separately, and you take the band that best describes what is written for that specific sub-criterion — you do not need to reach the same band in every row.

This has two practical consequences:

**First:** If you run out of time, write at least a sentence for every sub-criterion before expanding any. A sentence for each sub-criterion gets Band 1 in each row. Five rows at Band 1 outscores three rows at Band 4 and two rows blank in most question structures.

**Second:** The sub-criteria are not equally weighted. In N22 Q8b (16 marks), the Waves sub-criterion and the Health sub-criterion each went up to Band 4 (4 marks each), but the Ethics sub-criterion only went to Band 2 (2 marks). Spending equal time on all five sub-criteria was inefficient — you should have allocated more time to the higher-weighted rows.

**N24 Q7c (SODIS, 11 marks):** Four sub-criteria — Climate (3 marks), Health (2 marks), Implementation (4 marks), Concluding appraisal (2 marks). Implementation was the highest-weighted row. Students who wrote one sentence about implementation challenges and three sentences about health were leaving marks on the table.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the Mark Scheme Rewards

- **Two-sided arguments with data support.** For every perspective sub-criterion, Band 3 always requires a positive and a negative point, each supported. The support can come from the stimulus data table or from scientific knowledge stated in the question.
- **Specific numbers and named facts from the stimulus.** Mark schemes say WTTE (Will Take The Equivalent) for wording, but they consistently reward responses that cite specific figures. In M18 Q8, citing "177 unique fish species" or "1.2 million people displaced" placed an answer above one that just said "many fish" or "many people."
- **Mechanism, not just effect.** The science-link gate to Band 4 is always a mechanism. "CO₂ causes warming" is an effect. "CO₂ absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation, increasing the energy retained in the atmosphere" is a mechanism.
- **A genuine appraisal that references earlier arguments.** The mark scheme for every session requires the concluding appraisal to "link to previous arguments" or "evaluate all arguments" for Band 2. Copy-pasting the question prompt as a conclusion is not an appraisal.
- **Addressing every named bullet point in the prompt.** The sub-criteria in the markband table correspond exactly to the bullet points in the question. Skipping a bullet point means a 0 for that sub-criterion's row.

### What the Mark Scheme Penalises

- **One-sided answers capped at Band 1 for that sub-criterion.** Every positive-and-negative sub-criterion explicitly requires both sides for Band 2 and above. Writing only advantages, even three well-supported ones, is still Band 1 or 2 for that row.
- **Vague environmental or social statements without justification.** "It pollutes the environment" scores Band 1. "It releases CO₂, contributing to the greenhouse effect and rising global temperatures" scores Band 2. The mark scheme notes for M21 Q3f explicitly reject "CO₂ emissions" or "pollution" without a mechanism.
- **Conclusions that restate the question.** "In conclusion, there are many advantages and disadvantages to drones in surveillance" is not an appraisal. It scores Band 1. The mark scheme for M21 Q8 describes the Band 2 appraisal as "a concluding appraisal linking to previous arguments."
- **Introducing new arguments only in the conclusion.** The appraisal must draw on the body of the essay. Raising a point only in the conclusion that was not developed in a paragraph is not credited for the appraisal sub-criterion.
- **Unscientific descriptions of wave-based or nuclear contexts.** In N22 Q8b, the Waves sub-criterion explicitly requires "identification of one wave phenomenon" (e.g. reflection, absorption, refraction). Describing ultrasound as "sending waves into the body to see things" without naming the phenomenon (reflection/echo) was Band 1.
- **Spending disproportionate time on a low-mark sub-criterion.** The Ethics sub-criterion in N22 Q8b was worth 2 marks maximum. Writing three detailed paragraphs on ethics while barely touching the Health sub-criterion (4 marks) was a time management error that the mark scheme cannot compensate for.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Writing only advantages or only disadvantages for a perspective | Every sub-criterion for a perspective requires both a positive and a negative point to reach Band 2 and above |
| Concluding with "there are advantages and disadvantages on both sides" | Name a specific argument and explain why it tips the balance; reference points from the body of the essay |
| Skipping a bullet point from the question prompt | Each bullet point is a separate sub-criterion row; skipping it is a guaranteed 0 for that row |
| Using everyday language instead of scientific terminology | Write "absorption of ionising radiation increases mutation risk in rapidly dividing cells" not "x-rays are bad for babies" |
| Writing only about the context given in the D question, ignoring earlier A/B/C questions | The physics content in earlier questions (energy calculations, wave properties, etc.) provides the scientific language for Band 4 |
| Repeating the same argument under multiple sub-criteria | The mark scheme awards each point once; a repeated point does not score twice even in a different section |
| Not using the stimulus data table or infographic | Many questions provide specific figures (costs, populations, distances). Citing these is the simplest way to "support with information" and reach Band 2–3 |
| Writing a long introduction before addressing sub-criteria | There are no marks for an introduction in the markband table; go directly to the first sub-criterion |
| Treating all sub-criteria as equally weighted | Check how many marks each sub-criterion is worth (usually 2–4) and allocate time accordingly |
| Forgetting the science link when writing about social or economic points | Appending one sentence explaining the physical mechanism ("because UV radiation at these wavelengths carries enough energy to break DNA bonds in bacteria") lifts a social/health point to a science-linked one |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise the markband logic: Band 1 = one point stated; Band 2 = one point supported or two stated; Band 3 = both sides, one supported; Band 4 = both sides, both supported with science link. Apply this to every sub-criterion before writing.
2. Practise reading the bullet-point prompt and mapping each bullet onto a paragraph — never skip a bullet point, and write at least one sentence per bullet before expanding any.
3. Learn the formula for a Band 2 concluding appraisal: "Although [counter-argument], [position] because [mechanism or evidence that tips the balance]." Write this last and ensure it references points from the body.
4. Build a bank of science-link phrases for the most common essay contexts: Mars (radiation exposure from cosmic rays, low atmospheric pressure, muscle wasting in low gravity), JWST (infrared detection of biomolecular signatures, mirror deployment at L2), drones (lift force from rotors, electromagnetic spectrum for remote control signals), energy and dams (gravitational potential energy, CO₂ greenhouse mechanism), medical imaging (wave reflection and absorption, ionising radiation and DNA mutation).
5. For each past paper essay, identify which sub-criterion was worth the most marks — allocate proportionally more time and detail to high-mark rows and write a minimum viable paragraph for low-mark rows (Ethics, Safety, Comfort-type sub-criteria are typically 2 marks maximum).
6. Practise converting a vague social or ethical point into a science-linked one by asking: "What is the physical or biological mechanism that explains this consequence?" One sentence answering that question is usually all that is needed to move from Band 3 to Band 4 for that sub-criterion.
