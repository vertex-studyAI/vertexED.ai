# Energy

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Energy Stores

Energy is always stored in one of several forms. The mark scheme uses the word "store" and expects you to name the store correctly.

| Energy store | Description | Mark scheme phrasing |
|---|---|---|
| Kinetic | Energy of a moving object | KE = ½mv² |
| Gravitational potential (GPE) | Energy due to height above a reference point | GPE = mgh |
| Thermal (internal) | Energy stored in the random motion of particles | Do NOT say "heat energy is stored"; say "thermal energy" |
| Chemical potential | Energy stored in chemical bonds | Fuel, food, batteries |
| Elastic potential | Energy stored in a stretched or compressed object | Springs, elastic bands |
| Nuclear | Energy stored in atomic nuclei | Released by fission or fusion |
| Electromagnetic | Energy carried by photons/EM waves | Light, radio waves |

**Mark scheme key phrase:** When describing energy transformations, always name the energy store before and after — do NOT say "energy changes from one form to another" without naming both forms.

---

### Energy Conservation

In a closed system, the total energy is always constant. Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it is only transferred between stores or dissipated (spread out) as thermal energy.

**M17 Q5a (1 mark — Select):** As a wooden block slides down a ramp, the correct answer is "gravitational potential energy is transferred to kinetic energy." The mark scheme does NOT accept "elastic energy is transferred to kinetic energy" or any answer that omits both named stores.

**M21 Q3d (1 mark — Select):** In regenerative braking, the energy chain is: Kinetic energy → Electrical energy → Chemical potential energy. The MS requires all three in the correct order for the mark.

**N21 Q2b (2 marks — Select):** Sankey diagram for a child on a slide. Input = gravitational potential energy. The MS awards marks independently — you can earn the label mark even if the numerical value is wrong.

**Do NOT say:** "Energy is lost" — say "energy is dissipated as thermal energy" or "transferred to the surroundings as thermal energy."

### Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv²

Kinetic energy depends on both mass and velocity. Because velocity is squared, doubling the speed quadruples the KE.

**Formula:** KE = ½mv²
- KE in joules (J)
- m in kilograms (kg)
- v in metres per second (m s⁻¹)

**M21 Q3a (2 marks — Calculate):** Calculate the kinetic energy of a 1500 kg car moving at 25 m s⁻¹.

Working:
- KE = ½ × 1500 × 25²
- KE = ½ × 1500 × 625
- KE = 468 750 J

**MS note:** Accept any correctly rounded answer to 2 or more significant figures. Accept answers in kJ. The mark scheme awards 1 mark for use of the equation (seen or implied) and 1 mark for the correct numerical answer.

**N21 Q2c (3 marks — Calculate):** Using the Sankey diagram (KE output = 570 J, child mass = 25 kg), find the child's velocity. Rearrange KE = ½mv²: v = √(2 × KE / m) = √(2 × 570 / 25) = 7.3 m s⁻¹ (to 2 significant figures). The MS awards 3 marks: formula (1), unsimplified correct answer (1), correctly rounded to 2 sf (1).

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT write KE = mv² (missing the ½). Do NOT leave answer as 7.26… when the question specifies 2 significant figures.

---

### Gravitational Potential Energy: GPE = mgh

GPE depends on mass, gravitational field strength, and height above the reference point.

**Formula:** GPE = mgh
- GPE in joules (J)
- m in kilograms (kg)
- g = 10 N kg⁻¹ (always use this value unless told otherwise in MYP)
- h in metres (m)

**M17 Q5b (3 marks — Calculate):** Calculate the GPE of a 0.25 kg block at a height of 60 cm. g = 10 N kg⁻¹.

Working:
- Convert height: 60 cm = 0.6 m
- GPE = mgh = 0.25 × 10 × 0.6
- GPE = 1.5 J

**MS note:** The mark scheme awards: substitution seen or implied (1), correct numerical answer (1), correct unit J or Nm (1). Accept joules written in full. "Accept Nm or joules."

**N20 Q2b (4 marks — Calculate):** GPE for an 880 kg car climbing from 2860 m to 4300 m (Pikes Peak). Height gain = 4300 − 2860 = 1440 m. GPE = 880 × 10 × 1440 = 12 672 000 J = 12.672 MJ. The MS awards: height gain seen or implied (1), correct formula (1), correct numerical answer (1), unit J/MJ (1). The unit mark is assessed under Criterion D in this session.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT use h = 4300 directly — you must calculate the *gain* in height. Do NOT accept j (lowercase); the unit must be J or joules.

---

### Work Done: W = Fd

Work is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.

**Formula:** W = Fd
- W in joules (J)
- F in newtons (N)
- d in metres (m)

Work done equals the energy transferred. If a block slides 1.5 m against a friction force of 2 N, the work done by friction = 2 × 1.5 = 3 J of energy dissipated as thermal energy.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT say "work is the same as energy" without context. Say "work done equals the energy transferred to/from the object."

---

### Power: P = W/t = E/t

Power is the rate of energy transfer. It is measured in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J s⁻¹.

**Formula:** P = W/t (or P = E/t)
- P in watts (W)
- W (or E) in joules (J)
- t in seconds (s)

**N20 Q2c (3 marks — Calculate):** A lighter car gained 8.8 MJ of GPE in 546 s. Calculate its power output.

Working:
- P = E/t = 8 800 000 / 546
- P = 16 117 W (≈ 16.1 kW)

**MS note:** The mark scheme accepts correctly rounded values to 2 or 3 significant figures. The unit must match the scale of the number — "Do not accept MJs⁻¹ or kJs⁻¹." Accept W, kW, or MW but the prefix must match the numerical answer.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT convert 8.8 MJ to 8.8 J — always convert MJ to J before dividing by seconds.

---

### Efficiency

Efficiency measures how much of the input energy is usefully transferred. It is expressed as a decimal or a percentage.

**Formulae:**
- Efficiency = useful energy output / total energy input
- Efficiency (%) = (useful energy output / total energy input) × 100

**M24 Q1e (2 marks — Determine):** A coal power station generates 26 700 MW at 28% efficiency. Determine the useful power output.

Working:
- Useful power = 0.28 × 26 700 MW
- Useful power = 7476 MW

**MS note:** The mark scheme awards: recognition of efficiency formula (1), correct numerical answer with unit (1). Accept answers in W, kW, or MW to 2 or more significant figures.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT confuse efficiency as a fraction (0.28) with percentage (28%). When using the formula, always decide which form you are using before substituting. Do NOT say "the power lost is 28%"; 28% efficiency means 28% is *useful* output — 72% is wasted.

### Sankey Diagrams

A Sankey diagram shows the flow of energy through a system. The width of each arrow is proportional to the amount of energy it represents.

Rules for reading a Sankey diagram:
- The input arrow enters from the left.
- Useful energy output arrows bend upward or continue to the right.
- Wasted energy arrows (usually thermal) bend downward.
- All arrow widths must add up: input = useful output + all wasted outputs.

**N20 Q2a (2 marks — Label):** Sankey diagram for a car engine. Labels required:
- Chemical energy (100%) = input
- Useful energy output (25%) = top-right arrow
- Heat losses (70%) = large downward arrow
- Frictional losses (5%) = small downward arrow

**MS note:** Two correct labels = 1 mark; all correct = 2 marks.

**N21 Q2b (2 marks — Select):** Sankey diagram for a child on a slide: input arrow = gravitational potential energy. The MS awards this mark independently from the numerical label mark.

**How to draw a Sankey diagram from scratch:**
1. Draw an input arrow on the left with width proportional to total input energy.
2. Split it: bend the useful output arrow to the right (width proportional to useful energy).
3. Bend the wasted (thermal) arrow downward (width proportional to wasted energy).
4. Label all arrows with the energy type and value.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT draw all arrows the same width — the whole point of a Sankey diagram is that width represents quantity of energy. Do NOT label the input arrow as "electrical energy" in a fuel-burning system — the input to a combustion engine is chemical energy.

### Energy Resources: Renewable vs Non-Renewable

| Resource | Type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coal, oil, gas (fossil fuels) | Non-renewable | Reliable, high energy density | CO₂ emissions; finite supply |
| Nuclear (uranium) | Non-renewable | High power output, no CO₂ during operation | Radioactive waste; long decay times; expensive decommissioning |
| Solar | Renewable | No emissions, widely available | Intermittent (weather/time of day); high initial cost |
| Wind | Renewable | No emissions, offshore available | Intermittent; visual/noise impact |
| Tidal | Renewable | Predictable, long lifetime | High upfront cost; disrupts coastal ecosystems |
| Geothermal | Renewable | Stable continuous supply | Limited to geologically suitable areas; earthquake risk |
| Biomass | Renewable (debated) | Carbon neutral if managed | Needs large land area; time delay in production |

**N21 Q8a (2 marks — Identify/Justify):** Nuclear is non-renewable because uranium is a finite resource. The MS requires both the identification (nuclear) AND the justification (uranium/nuclear fuel is finite). "Uranium is a finite resource" — WTTE accepted.

**N21 Q7a (3 marks — Select):** Matching disadvantages to energy types — one mark per correct match. The mark scheme tests whether you know specific disadvantages, not just general ones.

**N21 Q7b (2 marks — Label):** Energy flow in a biomass power station: Chemical → Thermal → Kinetic → Electrical. The MS awards 1 mark for the correct energy forms and 1 mark for correct order.

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT say nuclear energy is renewable — the fuel (uranium) is finite. Do NOT say "wind/solar produce no pollution" without specifying what type of pollution; the MS expects "no CO₂ emissions" or "no greenhouse gas emissions during operation."

### E = mc²: Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein's equation shows that mass and energy are interchangeable. In nuclear reactions, a tiny loss of mass produces an enormous quantity of energy.

**Formula:** E = mc²
- E in joules (J)
- m in kilograms (kg)
- c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ (speed of light)

**N21 Q2a (1 mark — Label):** SI units for E = mc²: E = joule (J), m = kilogram (kg), c² = (metre per second)². The MS awards 1 mark for all three correct.

**N21 Q2d (2 marks — Calculate):** When an electron (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg) and a positron (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg) annihilate, calculate the energy released.

Working:
- Total mass = 2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ = 1.822 × 10⁻³⁰ kg
- E = mc² = 1.822 × 10⁻³⁰ × (3.0 × 10⁸)²
- E = 1.822 × 10⁻³⁰ × 9.0 × 10¹⁶
- E = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J

**MS note:** The mark scheme awards 1 mark for correct substitution into E = mc² and 1 mark for the correct final answer (1.6398 × 10⁻¹³ J). If only one mass is used, the MS awards 1 mark (8.199 × 10⁻¹⁴ J).

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT use only one particle's mass — you must add both masses (electron + positron) before substituting. Do NOT confuse c with c²; the formula requires squaring the speed of light.

### Heat Transfer: Conduction, Convection, Radiation

Heat energy is transferred by three mechanisms.

| Mechanism | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Conduction | Particles vibrate and pass energy to neighbours by direct contact | Metal spoon in hot soup |
| Convection | Warm fluid rises (less dense), cool fluid sinks (more dense) — creates a current | Ice melting in water; central heating |
| Radiation | Energy carried by electromagnetic waves (infrared) — requires no medium | Heat from the Sun; thermal camera |

**M22 Q3a (1 mark — Match):** All three descriptions must be matched correctly: Radiation = "transfer of heat by electromagnetic radiation"; Conduction = "transfer of heat by direct contact"; Convection = "transfer of heat by moving fluids." Only all correct earns the mark.

**M22 Q3e (3 marks — Explain):** Ice melts more slowly in salt water because salt water is denser, so cold meltwater stays near the surface rather than sinking. This prevents convection currents from forming, so heat is transferred less quickly. The MS requires: density comparison (1), consequence for convection (1), conclusion about rate of heat transfer (1). "Do not award the final mark unless the first and second marks are awarded."

**N20 Q1b (2 marks — Outline):** Reindeer fur with hollow hairs keeps them warm because: hollow fibres trap air (1); air is a poor conductor / good insulator (1). The MS awards both marks independently.

**N20 Q1c (2 marks — Outline):** Arctic fox white fur reduces heat lost by radiation; fennec fox large ears have a large surface area to increase heat lost by conduction/convection/radiation. Both marks require: the structural feature AND the heat transfer mechanism named.

**N20 Q1d (3 marks — Explain):** Evaporation cools dogs because: heat is required for evaporation (1); high-energy particles escape the liquid surface (1); leaving lower-energy particles behind, so temperature drops (1). "Accept 'fast moving' for 'high energy'."

**Inline MS callout:** Do NOT say "convection happens in solids" — convection requires a fluid (liquid or gas). Do NOT say "radiation needs air" — radiation travels through a vacuum. Do NOT say "dark surfaces emit less radiation" — dark/matt surfaces are BETTER absorbers AND emitters of infrared radiation.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Named energy stores** — both the "before" and "after" store must be named in any energy transfer description (e.g. "gravitational potential energy is transferred to kinetic energy" — not just "energy changes").
- **Correct units stated explicitly** — J for energy, W for power, m s⁻¹ for velocity. Unit marks are often independent of the numerical answer; you can still earn them even if your calculation is wrong.
- **Formula use seen or implied** — writing "KE = ½mv²" before substituting earns a process mark even if arithmetic goes wrong. The mark scheme frequently says "seen or implied."
- **Height gain, not height** — GPE problems that involve a change in height (e.g. Pikes Peak) require you to calculate the difference in altitude, not use the final altitude directly.
- **Efficiency as a decimal** — when using efficiency = useful / total, ensure you are consistent: 28% means 0.28 as a decimal. The answer must include the correct unit.
- **Specific phrasing for wasted energy** — "cannot be used to do useful work" or "transferred to surroundings as thermal energy." Vague answers like "it is lost" are not accepted.
- **Correct order in energy chains** — for biomass: Chemical → Thermal → Kinetic → Electrical. All four in order earns full marks; wrong order loses the sequence mark.
- **Convection explanation structure** — for ice/salt water questions: (1) density difference → (2) convection suppressed → (3) slower heat transfer. The third mark is conditional on the first two.
- **Evaporation explanation structure** — for cooling questions: (1) heat is required for evaporation → (2) high-energy particles escape → (3) lower-energy particles remain, temperature drops.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- "Energy is lost" — the MS does not accept this; say "dissipated as thermal energy."
- "Heat energy is stored" — say "thermal energy store" or "internal energy."
- Using only one particle's mass in E = mc² annihilation questions — must add both masses.
- Forgetting to square velocity in KE = ½mv².
- Stating efficiency as useful output without dividing by total input.
- "Nuclear is renewable" — uranium is finite; nuclear is non-renewable.
- Watt (W) and joule (J) confused — power is W (J s⁻¹); energy is J.
- "Convection occurs in solids" — convection requires a fluid.
- "Radiation needs a medium" — radiation (infrared) travels through a vacuum.
- Lowercase j for joule — must write J or "joules."
- "MJs⁻¹" or "kJs⁻¹" as a power unit — the unit must be consistent with the scale of the numerical answer (W, kW, or MW).

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "Energy is lost during braking" | "Kinetic energy is dissipated as thermal energy" |
| Using h = 4300 m directly in GPE (Pikes Peak) | Calculate height gain: 4300 − 2860 = 1440 m |
| KE = mv² (missing the ½) | KE = ½mv² — the factor of ½ is always required |
| Forgetting to square v in KE = ½mv² | Square the velocity first, then multiply by ½m |
| Efficiency = useful output only | Efficiency = useful output ÷ total input (× 100 for %) |
| "Nuclear is renewable" | Nuclear is non-renewable — uranium is a finite resource |
| Sankey arrows all drawn the same width | Arrow width must be proportional to energy quantity |
| "Heat energy cannot escape" (insulation) | "Air trapped in hollow fur is a poor conductor / good insulator, reducing conduction" |
| "Dark surfaces absorb but don't emit radiation" | Dark/matt surfaces are better absorbers AND better emitters of infrared radiation |
| "Convection happens in solids" | Convection requires a fluid (liquid or gas) |
| E = mc² with only one particle's mass | Add both masses before substituting (electron + positron) |
| Power units not matching the number | If answer is ~16 000, use W; if ~16, use kW; match prefix to magnitude |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. Memorise all five core formulae with units: KE = ½mv², GPE = mgh, W = Fd, P = W/t, Efficiency = useful output / total input. Practice substituting into each and rearranging.
2. Practise GPE calculations that require a height *gain* (subtract start height from end height) — a common error is using the final height rather than the change.
3. Learn the Sankey diagram rules: input on left, useful output straight or upward, wasted energy downward, all widths proportional. Be able to label energy stores for fuel-burning systems, regenerative braking, and biomass power stations.
4. Learn the three heat transfer mechanisms (conduction, convection, radiation) with one real-world example each and be able to state which mechanism cannot occur in a solid (convection) or which requires no medium (radiation).
5. For E = mc², practise the electron-positron annihilation calculation: add both masses, then multiply by c² = (3.0 × 10⁸)² = 9.0 × 10¹⁶. Always express the answer in standard form.
6. Build a comparison table for renewable vs non-renewable energy sources, including one specific advantage and one specific disadvantage for each — the MS often asks for justification linked to a named country or context.
7. For evaporation and convection explanation questions, practise the three-step chain: (physical process) → (particle-level explanation) → (macroscopic consequence). The third mark is conditional on the first two in almost every session.
