# Nuclear and Atomic Physics

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### Atomic Structure

Every atom has a nucleus at its centre containing **protons** (positively charged) and **neutrons** (no charge). **Electrons** (negatively charged) orbit the nucleus. The electrostatic force of attraction between the positive nucleus and the negative electrons holds the atom together — this is the force keeping electrons in the atom, not gravity or magnetism.

| Particle | Charge | Mass (relative) | Location |
|----------|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Proton | +1 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | 0 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Electron | −1 | ~1/2000 | Orbits nucleus |

**Atomic number (Z):** number of protons in the nucleus. This defines which element the atom is.

**Mass number (A):** total number of protons + neutrons (nucleons) in the nucleus.

**Isotopes** are atoms of the same element (same atomic number / same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons, and therefore different mass numbers. Example: carbon-12 and carbon-14 both have 6 protons, but carbon-12 has 6 neutrons and carbon-14 has 8 neutrons.

**Mark scheme key phrase (M23 Q3b):** For the similarity between C-12 and C-14 — accept "same number of protons" or "same charge on the nucleus." Do NOT accept "same atomic number" without explanation, and do NOT accept "both are carbon nuclei." For the difference — accept "different number of neutrons" or "C-14 is unstable, C-12 is stable." Do NOT accept "different mass number" alone.

### Radioactive Decay: Alpha, Beta, and Gamma Radiation

Unstable nuclei decay spontaneously, emitting radiation. The three types differ fundamentally in nature, penetrating power, and ionising ability.

| Property | Alpha (α) | Beta (β) | Gamma (γ) |
|----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nature | Helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons) | Fast-moving electron | High-energy electromagnetic wave / photon |
| Charge | +2 | −1 | 0 |
| Ionising power | Highest | Medium | Lowest |
| Penetrating power | Lowest — stopped by paper or a few cm of air | Medium — stopped by a few mm of aluminium | Highest — only reduced (not stopped) by thick lead |
| Affected by electric/magnetic fields | Yes (deflected strongly) | Yes (deflected, opposite direction to α) | No |

**Alpha particles (α):** High ionising power because the +2 charge interacts strongly with surrounding atoms. Low penetrating power because they lose energy quickly. Am-241 in smoke detectors is an alpha emitter — the alpha particles ionise the air between two charged plates, creating a small current. Smoke entering disrupts this current and triggers the alarm (N24 Q2b, Q2g).

**Beta particles (β):** High-energy electrons emitted when a neutron in the nucleus converts to a proton. More penetrating than alpha, less ionising. Used to monitor aluminium foil thickness in industry — beta radiation is absorbed in proportion to the foil thickness, so changes in the count rate indicate changes in thickness (N16 Q7).

**Gamma rays (γ):** Electromagnetic radiation emitted from the nucleus. Not a particle — do NOT accept "particle" in a mark scheme. High energy, high frequency, short wavelength. Most penetrating; not fully blocked by bone or skin, so a gamma camera can detect them from outside the body. Least ionising — do not damage cells significantly at diagnostic doses (M19 Q7b, N18 Q3c).

**Key mark scheme phrase:** Gamma radiation is described as "high energy or high frequency or short wavelength" and must be identified as "electromagnetic waves or radiation or photon." Writing "particle" for gamma — NOT accepted (N18 Q3c).

### Nuclear Equations: Balancing Proton and Mass Numbers

In any nuclear reaction, the total mass number (A) and the total proton number (Z) are conserved on both sides of the equation.

**Alpha decay:** Mass number decreases by 4; atomic number decreases by 2.

Example: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂α (M23 Q3d)

**Beta decay:** Mass number is unchanged; atomic number increases by 1 (a neutron becomes a proton, emitting an electron).

Example: ²³²₉₀Th → ²³²₉₁Pa + ⁰₋₁β (M23 Q3d)

**Identifying the decay type from an equation:** Check mass number change and atomic number change. If mass number drops by 4 and atomic number by 2 → alpha. If mass number unchanged and atomic number increases by 1 → beta.

**Worked example — Meitnerium-274 alpha decay (N18 Q3b):**
²⁷⁴₁₀₉Mt → ²⁷⁰₁₀₇Bh + ⁴₂α
Atomic number: 109 − 2 = 107 (Bohrium); Mass number: 274 − 4 = 270.

**Worked example — fission equation balancing (N22 Q1b):**
²³⁶₉₂U → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + [?] + 3¹₀n
Missing nuclide: mass number = 236 − 141 − 3 = 92; proton number = 92 − 56 = 36 → ⁹²₃₆Kr

**Worked example — M25 Q3c:**
²³⁶₉₂U → ¹⁴⁰₅₄Xe + ⁹³₃₈Sr + [?]¹₀n
Neutrons needed: mass 236 − 140 − 93 = 3; proton 92 − 54 − 38 = 0 → answer is 3 neutrons.

**Mark scheme note (N24 Q2c):** For Am-241 alpha decay, the correct equation is ²⁴¹₉₅Am → ²³⁷₉₃Np + ⁴₂He. Check that mass number decreases by 4 and proton number by 2.

### Half-Life: Definition, Calculations, and Decay Graphs

**Half-life** is the time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei (or the activity) of a sample to halve. Radioactive decay is a random process — it is impossible to predict when any individual nucleus will decay, but the half-life of an isotope is a fixed, characteristic value.

**How to read a half-life from a graph:** Start at the initial value, find the point where it has halved, and read the time difference. Repeat from a different starting point and average the readings for reliability (N16 Q8c).

**Worked example — N16 Q8d:**
Caesium-134 has a half-life of 2 years. Starting with 1 600 000 atoms, how long to reach 100 000?
1 600 000 → 800 000 (1 half-life) → 400 000 (2) → 200 000 (3) → 100 000 (4)
Four half-lives × 2 years = **8 years.**
Mark scheme notes: Award 1 mark for "4 half-lives" and 1 mark for "8 years." Award only 1 mark for "4 years" (incorrect multiplication).

**Worked example — N24 Q2e–Q2f:**
Graph gives Am-241 half-life as 420 ± 10 years. Starting with 10 000 nuclei, time for 625 to remain:
10 000 → 5 000 → 2 500 → 1 250 → 625 (four half-lives) → 4 × 420 = 1680 years. ECF from Q2e accepted.

**Worked example — N22 Q1c:**
Half-life of barium-141 read from activity-time graph: 18.5 ± 0.5 minutes.

**Choosing an isotope for dating (N18 Q3d):** The isotope's half-life must be appropriate to the age being investigated. For a 10 000-year-old artefact, carbon-14 (half-life 5 700 years) is appropriate because the change in abundance over 10 000 years will be measurable. Potassium-40 (half-life 1.3 billion years) and Uranium-238 (half-life 4.5 billion years) would show virtually no change — not detectable for such a short time scale.

### Carbon-14 Dating

Carbon-14 is a naturally occurring radioactive isotope found alongside stable carbon-12 in all living organic material. While an organism is alive, it continuously exchanges carbon with its environment, maintaining a fixed ratio of C-14 to C-12. When the organism dies, it stops taking in new carbon, and the C-14 decays with a half-life of approximately 5 700 years.

By measuring the ratio of C-14 remaining to the expected level, scientists can calculate how many half-lives have elapsed and therefore the age of the sample.

**Mark scheme notes (N18 Q3d):** Must state two things: (1) carbon-14 is found in organic/biological materials; (2) the half-life is appropriate to the age being investigated (roughly comparable — typically within one order of magnitude).

### Nuclear Fission and Chain Reactions

**Nuclear fission** is the splitting of a heavy nucleus (typically uranium-235 or uranium-236) into two smaller nuclei (daughter nuclei), releasing two or three neutrons and a large amount of energy.

**The process:** A slow (thermal) neutron is absorbed by U-235, forming U-236 (unstable), which then splits. Example from M25:
²³⁶₉₂U → ¹⁴⁰₅₄Xe + ⁹³₃₈Sr + 3¹₀n + energy

The released neutrons can each trigger further fission reactions — this is a **chain reaction**: one reaction causes another to occur, with released neutrons causing the subsequent reactions (M24 Q2c).

**E = mc²:** The energy released in fission comes from a tiny loss of mass. E = mc², where c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. Even a very small mass difference corresponds to an enormous amount of energy (M25 Q3d).

**Worked example — M25 Q3d:**
Each fission of U-236 releases 2.8 × 10⁻¹¹ J.
Mass equivalent: m = E/c² = 2.8 × 10⁻¹¹ / (3.0 × 10⁸)² = 3.1 × 10⁻²⁸ kg

**Worked example — M25 Q3e:**
Family uses 9.7 GJ = 9.7 × 10⁹ J per year.
Number of fissions = 9.7 × 10⁹ / 2.8 × 10⁻¹¹ = 3.46 × 10²⁰ fissions

**Uncontrolled chain reaction danger (M24 Q2d):** Each fission releases more neutrons, which trigger more fissions, releasing more energy → the reaction accelerates rapidly → results in a nuclear explosion or meltdown → radioactive material is released into the surrounding environment, causing harm to living things.

**Nuclear power station efficiency (M25 Q3f):**
If 35% efficient and generates 1.5 × 10⁹ W electrical:
Total power from fission = 1.5 × 10⁹ / 0.35 = 4.3 × 10⁹ W

### Background Radiation and Its Sources

Background radiation is naturally occurring ionising radiation present in the environment at all times. It does not come from experimental sources and must be measured and subtracted from any experimental count rate before analysis (N16 Q7e, Q7f).

**Sources of background radiation include:**
- Cosmic rays from space
- Naturally occurring radioactive rocks (e.g. granite containing radon gas)
- Radioactive isotopes in food and the human body
- Medical procedures (X-rays)
- Fallout from past nuclear weapons testing

**How to correct for background (N16 Q7f):** Measure the count rate with no radioactive source present (background count). Subtract this value from all subsequent measurements taken with the source.

### Medical Uses: Technetium-99 and Gamma Imaging

Technetium-99 (Tc-99) is the most widely used radioactive isotope in medical diagnosis. It is a gamma emitter with a half-life of about 6 hours.

**Why 6 hours is useful (M19 Q7a):** Long enough to allow medical images to be recorded; short enough to prevent long-term radiation exposure to the patient.

**Why gamma is used rather than alpha or beta (M19 Q7b):**
- Gamma rays are not blocked by bone or skin, so they can be detected by a gamma camera outside the body.
- They do not cause significant ionisation damage to cells at diagnostic doses.
Mark scheme explicitly says: do NOT accept half-life as an advantage of gamma over alpha/beta.

**Production and ethical context (M19 Q7c, Q8):** Tc-99 is produced in nuclear reactors (most of which are ageing) or potentially by cyclotrons or linear accelerators inside hospitals. Key trade-offs: reactor has 8-day distribution radius vs. cyclotron's 1-day local radius; cyclotrons produce virtually no long-lived radioactive waste; costs and policy implications vary by country. The Goiânia accident (1987) illustrates the risk of uncontrolled radioactive sources — one stolen radiotherapy source led to 4 deaths and contamination of an entire neighbourhood.

### Industrial Use: Smoke Detectors and Thickness Gauges

**Smoke detectors — americium-241 (N24 Q2):**
Am-241 is an alpha emitter placed inside smoke detectors. Alpha particles ionise the air between two charged plates, producing a small current. When smoke enters, it absorbs the alpha particles, the ionisation current drops, and the alarm sounds.

Alpha is chosen because:
- High ionising power — efficiently creates a current through the air
- Low penetrating power — contained safely within the detector casing; the alpha cannot escape through the plastic housing
- A beta or gamma emitter would not work: beta/gamma are less ionising (no sufficient current without smoke), and their higher penetrating power means they would pass through smoke without being absorbed — smoke would not be detected (N24 Q2g)

**Thickness monitoring — beta and aluminium (N16 Q7):**
Beta radiation from strontium-90 is used to monitor the thickness of aluminium foil in factories. The amount of beta absorbed depends on the thickness of the aluminium. A Geiger-Müller tube detects the count rate after the foil: if the foil becomes too thick, fewer beta particles penetrate and the count rate falls; the system signals the rollers to adjust.

### Safety Precautions When Handling Radioactive Sources

**Accepted precautions (N16 Q7g):**

| Precaution | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Minimise time of exposure | Dose is proportional to time of exposure |
| Increase distance from source / use tongs | Dose falls with increasing distance (inverse square law) |
| Use shielding (lead, thick glass) | Radiation is absorbed by shielding material |
| Do not point source at the body | Beta radiation is directional |
| Pregnant women should not be present | Radiation increases risk of genetic abnormality to foetus |

**Not accepted (N16 Q7g):** Gloves or goggles — the mark scheme explicitly excludes these.

---

### Nuclear Waste Disposal: Long-Term Challenges

Used nuclear fuel rods still contain uranium-235 and decay products that emit radiation for millions of years. This poses a serious challenge for safe disposal.

**Key points (M24 Q2f):**
- Radiation from spent fuel is harmful to humans, living things, and the environment.
- Because U-235 has a half-life of ~700 million years, fuel rods remain significantly radioactive for geological timescales.
- They must be stored safely and securely for a very long time — practical storage solutions (e.g. deep geological repositories) are difficult and expensive to guarantee over such timescales.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Alpha particle definition:** "helium nucleus" or "made of 2 protons and 2 neutrons" — both accepted. Stating "positively charged" and "low penetrating power / stopped by paper" are accepted properties (N24 Q2b).
- **Beta particle definition:** "high-energy or fast-moving electron" — both components needed (N16 Q7b).
- **Gamma radiation:** Must say "high energy / high frequency / short wavelength" AND "electromagnetic waves / radiation / photon." Both parts needed for full marks (N18 Q3c).
- **Half-life from graph:** Must use at least two or three data points and average them for full marks (N16 Q8c).
- **Isotope similarity/difference:** Similarity = same proton number (same atomic number / same charge). Difference = different neutron number. These are the only accepted phrasings (M23 Q3b).
- **Chain reaction:** Two separate points needed — (1) one reaction causes another; (2) released neutrons cause the further reactions (M24 Q2c).
- **Danger of uncontrolled fission:** Three sequential points — energy builds rapidly → explosion/meltdown → release of radioactive material into environment (M24 Q2d).
- **Smoke detector explanation:** Must show understanding that alpha ionises air → creates current → smoke absorbs radiation → current drops → alarm. For the beta/gamma question, the chain must be: less ionising → no current (or too penetrating → passes through smoke → smoke not detected) → alarm either always sounds or never sounds (N24 Q2g). Only award mark 3 if marks 1 and 2 are both awarded.
- **Half-life calculation:** Always show the decay steps explicitly: 10 000 → 5 000 → 2 500 → 1 250 → 625 = four half-lives. Then multiply by the half-life value. ECF applies if the wrong half-life was used from the previous part (N24 Q2f).
- **Background radiation correction (N16 Q7f):** Two points — (1) measure background count rate with no source present; (2) subtract this from all subsequent readings.
- **Safety precautions (N16 Q7g):** Each precaution must be paired with a correctly linked reason explaining the physics (e.g. "increase distance because dose ∝ 1/distance²"). Precaution alone without reason — only 1 mark of 2.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Gamma radiation:** Never write "gamma particle" — gamma is an electromagnetic wave/photon (N18 Q3c).
- **Isotopes:** Do NOT accept "different mass number" as the stated difference between isotopes. Do NOT accept "same atomic number" as the similarity without further explanation (M23 Q3b).
- **U-235 vs U-236 difference:** Do NOT accept "different mass numbers" or "they are isotopes" — must say "different number of neutrons" (M25 Q3b).
- **Half-life N16 Q8d:** Writing "4 years" instead of "8 years" (i.e., confusing number of half-lives with elapsed time) — only 1 mark awarded.
- **Safety precautions:** Gloves and goggles are explicitly NOT accepted (N16 Q7g).
- **Medical gamma advantage:** Do NOT reference half-life as an advantage of gamma over alpha/beta — half-life is a property of the isotope, not of the radiation type (M19 Q7b).
- **Isotope identification:** An isotope must have the same atomic number (same protons). If the proton number differs, it is a different element, not an isotope.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| Saying gamma is a particle | Gamma is an electromagnetic wave / photon — never a particle |
| Confusing atomic number and mass number | Atomic number = number of protons; mass number = protons + neutrons |
| Defining isotopes as "same mass number, different atomic number" | Isotopes have the SAME atomic number (same protons) and DIFFERENT mass numbers (different neutrons) |
| Saying "different mass numbers" is the key difference between isotopes | The key difference is different numbers of neutrons |
| Calculating half-life time as "number of half-lives" (not multiplying by t½) | Always multiply the number of half-lives by the value of one half-life: time = n × t½ |
| Forgetting to subtract background radiation | Measure background first; subtract from all readings with source present |
| Saying alpha is "stopped by lead" | Alpha is stopped by paper or a few cm of air; lead is for gamma |
| Writing safety precaution without a linked reason | The mark scheme requires both the precaution AND a physics-based justification |
| Saying "gloves or goggles" as radiation safety | Explicitly not accepted; use tongs, shielding, distance, minimise time |
| Stating "half-life" as the advantage of gamma for medical imaging | Half-life is a property of the isotope (Tc-99), not of gamma radiation itself |
| Describing fission as burning or chemical reaction | Fission is a nuclear reaction — the nucleus splits; mass is converted to energy via E = mc² |
| Confusing chain reaction with explosion | A controlled chain reaction (moderated) powers a nuclear plant; an uncontrolled one can cause a meltdown or explosion |
| Missing the second point in smoke detector explanation | Must explain both why alpha works (ionises air → current) and why smoke stops it (absorbs alpha → current drops) |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Memorise all three radiation types** in full — nature, charge, penetrating power, ionising power, and what stops each. This comes up in nearly every session (N16, N18, M19, N24).

2. **Practise balancing nuclear equations** for both alpha and beta decay. Know the rules: alpha → mass number −4, proton number −2; beta → mass number unchanged, proton number +1. Use conservation to find missing nuclides (M23 Q3d, N22 Q1b, M25 Q3c).

3. **Half-life calculations from scratch and from graphs.** Know how to: read from a graph using multiple points, calculate elapsed time using n × t½, work backwards from a final count to find the number of half-lives (N16 Q8d, N24 Q2f).

4. **Isotopes:** Learn the exact accepted phrasing — same number of protons (atomic number), different number of neutrons. Be able to identify isotopes from nuclear symbols (M23 Q3b, N22 Q1a, N24 Q2d).

5. **Nuclear fission and chain reactions:** Know the steps of fission, the role of neutrons, the meaning of a chain reaction, and the dangers of an uncontrolled one. Be able to use E = mc² to calculate mass-energy equivalence (M24 Q2, M25 Q3d).

6. **Applications:** Be able to explain fully how a smoke detector uses Am-241 alpha radiation, and why beta/gamma would not work (N24 Q2g). Be able to explain medical imaging with Tc-99 gamma radiation and why gamma is preferred over alpha/beta (M19 Q7b).

7. **Safety and ethics (Criterion D):** Know at least four accepted safety precautions with linked reasons. Be ready to discuss the implications of nuclear technology — medical benefits, waste disposal, environmental risk, and the Goiânia case study (M19 Q8, M24 Q2f).

8. **Background radiation:** Know its sources and how to subtract it from experimental data. This is a standard method question in any radioactivity experiment (N16 Q7e, Q7f).
