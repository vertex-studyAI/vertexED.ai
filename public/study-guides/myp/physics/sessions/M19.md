### Q1c (4 marks) — Explain

A Dewar flask maintains the temperature of the liquid placed in it for many hours. A diagram of a Dewar flask is shown below.

A student places a mix of ice and water into a Dewar flask and leaves it in the sunshine. Four hours later she returns and finds the ice has not melted. Explain why the ice has not melted. In your answer, you should refer to two features of the flask and use your knowledge and understanding of heat transfer.

**MS answers:**
- Features of Dewar flask named and type of heat transfer correctly linked scientific explanation [2 max]:
  - silvered surfaces reflect infra-red/radiation so the sun's rays are reflected away from the ice
  - vacuum prevents conduction because conduction needs a medium and there is no medium for heat (thermal) energy to transfer (from the surroundings) to the ice
- insulated support prevents conduction
- heat (thermal) energy cannot transfer through insulating materials and (so) cannot transfer (from the surroundings) to the ice

## Key Mark Scheme Patterns

| Pattern | Explanation |
|---|---|
| WTTE | Will To The Effect — accept any answer with the same meaning |
| ECF | Error Carried Forward — award marks even if based on an incorrect earlier answer |
| Accept overlapping | For atomic structure diagrams, protons and neutrons may overlap |
| Judge by eye | For diagram/arrow questions, reasonable placement is accepted |
| Seen or implied | The step does not need to be explicitly written if it is clear from the answer |
| Do not accept references to half-life | Q7b: half-life is not an advantage of gamma over alpha/beta |
| Do not accept gravity | For electrostatic force questions, only accept electrostatic force (not gravity) |
| Two marks for correct answer | In calculation questions, a correct final answer awards all marks even without working shown |
| Award separately | Sub-parts of a mark can be awarded independently |
| No ECF from part b | Specified in Q4d: selection marks are independent |
| Accept displaced | Q6a: accept "displaced" as alternative to "volume of boat below water" |
