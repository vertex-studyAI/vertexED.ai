## Question 1 — Atomic Structure and Static Electricity (7 marks)

**Stimulus:** Introductory text: "Atoms are made up of particles called protons, neutrons and electrons, which are responsible for the mass and charge of atoms."

### Q1a (3 marks) — Select

Select the correct options to complete the statements below.

A proton is a particle found in the [Select] of an atom. It has a relative mass of [Select] and a charge of +1.

A neutron is a particle found in the [Select] of an atom. It has a relative mass of 1 and a charge of [Select].

An electron is a particle found in the orbitals of an atom. It has a relative mass of [Select] and a charge of [Select].

**MS answers:**
- Proton: found in the **nucleus**; relative mass **1**; charge **+1**
- Neutron: found in the **nucleus**; relative mass **1**; charge **0**
- Electron: found in the **orbitals**; relative mass **0** (or ~0 / negligible); charge **−1**
- Award 1 mark for each correct row (3 rows total)

**MS note:** Award (1 mark) for each correct row.

---

### Q1b (1 mark) — State

One model for explaining static electricity states that electrons can move from one atom to another because of friction. This can lead to an uneven distribution of charge.

Two identical pieces of nylon are charged with static electricity by rubbing with the same type of cloth and are suspended from a thread so that they can swing freely.

State why the nylon is charged after it is rubbed.

**MS answers:**
- The electrons were transferred or moved (from the cloth to the nylon)

**MS note:** Accept electrons transferred from nylon to the cloth.

---

### Q1c (2 marks) — Describe

Describe what will happen to one piece of charged nylon suspended by a thread if the other charged piece of nylon is brought very close to it but without touching.

**MS answers:**
- There will be repulsion
- Because the two pieces of nylon must be the same charge

**MS note:** Accept either positive or negative charge if given to the nylon, but they must be the same.

---

### Q1d (1 mark) — Justify

Justify what would happen if the nylon pieces were to touch.

**MS answers:**
- Nothing would happen because the nylon pieces have the same charge
- OR no particles would exchange because the nylon pieces have the same charge

## Question 2 — Raindrop: Kinematics, Forces, and Momentum (12 marks)

**Stimulus:** "A raindrop falls from a cloud that is 500 m above the ground."

### Q2a (3 marks) — Calculate

Calculate the theoretical maximum velocity of the raindrop before it hits the ground. Assume that the acceleration due to gravity, g, is equal to 10 m s⁻².

**MS answers:**
- Selecting the correct equation: v² = u² + 2as OR mgh = ½mv²
- Rearrange equation: v = √(2gh)
- Substitute correctly and calculate: **100 (m s⁻¹)**

**MS note:** Award (1 mark) only if only 100 is seen (without working).

---

### Q2b (1 mark) — Select

The raindrop does not reach this theoretical maximum speed: instead it reaches the terminal velocity.

Select the free body diagram that shows the forces acting on the raindrop when it reaches its terminal velocity.

**MS answers:**
- Diagram **1** (equal upward and downward arrows — forces balanced)

---

### Q2c (1 mark) — Label

Label the forces A and B.

**MS answers:**
- Force A: **air resistance / drag**
- Force B: **weight / gravitational force**

**MS note:** Do not accept "gravity".

---

### Q2d (1 mark) — State

A typical raindrop has a mass of 3.0 × 10⁻⁵ kg.

State the mass of the raindrop in grams (g).

**MS answers:**
- **0.03 / 3.0 × 10⁻² / 3 × 10⁻² (g)**

---

### Q2e (2 marks) — Calculate

Use your answers to part (a) and part (d) to calculate the maximum final theoretical momentum of the raindrop. You should include the unit in your answer.

**MS answers:**
- Uses correct equation (seen or implied)
- p = 3 × 10⁻⁵ kg × 100 m s⁻¹ = **3 × 10⁻³ kg m s⁻¹** or **0.003 kg m s⁻¹**
- OR p = 0.03 g × 100 m s⁻¹ = **3 g m s⁻¹**
- Unit must be included and must agree with the value

**MS note:** ECF from parts (a) and (d). Unit must be included and must agree with the value.

---

### Q2f (2 marks) — Discuss

A student reads that the terminal velocity of a raindrop is determined by its radius.

The student makes the following prediction: "The terminal velocity of a raindrop is proportional to the radius of the raindrop because the weight will be larger."

The student measures the terminal velocity of different raindrops and produces the following graph (terminal velocity / m s⁻¹ on y-axis vs radius of raindrop / m on x-axis, showing a curve not a straight line through origin).

Use the graph to discuss the validity of the hypothesis.

**MS answers:**
- The graph should be a straight line and go through the origin (for proportionality)
- As a straight line could not be drawn, the hypothesis is incorrect

---

### Q2g (2 marks) — Explain

A second student completes a similar investigation plotting terminal velocity squared / m² s⁻² against radius of hailstones / m, producing a straight line through the origin.

Explain what these results show about the relationship between the radius of hailstones and terminal velocity.

**MS answers:**
- The graph shows a straight line through the origin

## Question 3 — Circuits and Economics of Lighting (14 marks)

### Q3a (1 mark) — Select/Organise

Select appropriate terms and organize them into a sequence to show the energy changes taking place in the circuit.

**MS answers:**

---

### Q3b (2 marks) — Calculate

Calculate the power transferred to the bulb in the circuit diagram above. You should include the unit in your answer.

**MS answers:**
- 12 × 0.5 = **6**
- **Watts / W**

---

### Q3c (1 mark) — Calculate

Calculate the charge which flows through the bulb in 100 seconds.

**MS answers:**
- **50 (coulombs)**

**MS note:** Ignore all units (just the numerical answer is required).

---

### Q3d (5 marks) — Explain

The circuit now has a variable resistor added in series. Changing the resistance of the variable resistor changes the brightness of the bulb.

Explain why changing the resistance will affect the brightness of the bulb.

**MS answers (any complete pathway for full marks):**

*Pathway 1 (voltage):*
- A fixed voltage is supplied by the cell/battery
- The voltage drops across the resistor
- As the resistance increases, so does the voltage drop
- (Hence) the voltage across the bulb decreases
- (Bulb's brightness decreases because) brightness is related to voltage

*Pathway 2 (current):*
- A fixed voltage is supplied by the cell/battery
- The total resistance in the circuit is now greater
- The current flowing will now be smaller
- (As all circuit components are connected in series) the bulb draws smaller current
- (Because bulb's brightness decreases because) brightness is related to current

*Pathway 3 (energy):*
- A fixed voltage is supplied by the cell/battery
- Energy is lost as the current travels through the resistor
- As resistance increases the energy loss increases
- (Hence) the energy available for the bulb decreases
- (Brightness decreases because) brightness is related to available energy

---

### Q3e (4 marks) — Justify

The graph below shows the costs of different types of light bulbs (incandescent, CFL, LED). Each bulb produces the same level of brightness. The y-axis shows cost in GBP, x-axis shows time in hours.

Use information from the table and the graph to justify why LED bulbs are more economical over the long term even though they are more expensive to buy. You should use data to support your answer and you should refer to:
- the gradients of the lines
- the significance of the y intercept
- the significance of the intersections of lines.

**MS answers (any four reasonable points):**
- The gradients of the lines are related to running costs
- Identification of the bulb with the greatest running cost has the steepest gradient
- The y intercept indicates the initial cost
- Recognition that when the lines cross, the total prices of cost and use are the same
- Even though the brightness is the same, the energy used is different

**MS note:** Do not accept "LED bulbs are more economical over time even though they are more expensive to buy" alone, as this is given in the question.

**MS answers:**

## Question 4 — Evaporation Investigation: Inquiry and Processing (21 marks)

**Stimulus:** "The video shows how the particles in a liquid leave the surface and become gas particles when the liquid evaporates." Students are asked to investigate the effect that surface area has on the mass of liquid evaporated in an hour.

### Q4a (1 mark) — State

State the question that could be answered in this scientific investigation.

**MS answers:**
- How does the surface area affect the mass of water evaporated or remaining?

**MS note:** WTTE (Words To That Effect).

---

### Q4b (3 marks) — Calculate

If 0.25 g of water evaporates from a container in 12 minutes, calculate the rate of evaporation. Give your answer in g h⁻¹.

**MS answers:**
- mass / time (seen or implied)
- rate = 0.25 / 0.20
- **1.25 (g h⁻¹)**

---

### Q4c (3 marks) — Formulate, Explain

Formulate and explain the hypothesis that this question would test.

**MS answers:**
- Any simple prediction, for example: as the surface area increases the rate of evaporation increases (1 mark)
- Explanation contains relevant scientific knowledge: (because) the water molecules/particles have a greater area from which to escape (1 mark)
- Quantitative element to prediction, for example: as the area doubles the rate of evaporation doubles / as double the area means double the opportunities for the particles to escape (1 mark)

---

### Q4d (3 marks) — Identify

Below is a list of variables that are important in this experiment. Select the most appropriate description for each variable.

Variables listed: Mass of water, Water temperature, Air temperature, Time, Surface area, Type of liquid, Movement of air above the liquid

**MS answers:**
- Only one each of independent and dependent variable identified (the variables do not need to be correct for this mark) — 1 mark
- Independent variable identified as **surface area only** — 1 mark
- Dependent variable identified as **mass (of water evaporated) only** — 1 mark

Control variables (for reference): Water temperature, Air temperature, Time, Type of liquid, Movement of air above the liquid

---

### Q4e (3 marks) — Describe

Describe how you will ensure that you collect sufficient data.

**MS answers:**
- At least five increments (in surface area)
- Measurements of both mass and diameter/radius
- At least three repeated trials

---

### Q4f (2 marks) — Describe

The whole class collected their data and presented it in a graph (mass of water evaporated after one hour / g on y-axis vs area of dish / cm² on x-axis, showing a straight line through origin).

Describe the relationship between the two variables shown in the graph.

**MS answers:**
- As area increases, mass of water evaporated increases
- (Mass evaporated is) proportional (to area) / there is a linear relationship between area and mass of water evaporated that goes through zero

**MS note:** "The mass of water evaporated after one hour is proportional to area" scores both marks.

---

### Q4g (3 marks) — Determine

Determine the constant of proportionality from the graph. Give your answer in g cm⁻².

**MS answers:**
- Indication of calculation of the ratio of mass and area (seen or implied)
- Gradient calculated from two sufficiently spaced points
- **0.0284 (g cm⁻²)**

**MS note:** Accept the use of data points. Units not needed for the method marks.

---

### Q4h (3 marks) — Calculate

A pond has a surface area of 4 m² (40 000 cm²). Using your answer from part (g), calculate the mass of water lost from this pond in an hour.

**MS answers:**
- 0.0284 × 4 × 10⁴ (calculation seen or implied; ECF from part g)
- Either **1.1 (kg)** or **1136 (g)**
- Unit agrees with value

**MS note:** Accept answer in the range 1.0 to 1.2 kg.

## Question 6 — Altitude and Boiling Point of Water (16 marks)

**Stimulus:** "A student living in Tanzania decides to investigate if there is a relationship between the altitude (height above sea level) and the boiling point of water. She climbs Mount Kilimanjaro recording the temperature at which water boils at different altitudes." Results table provided with altitudes and boiling temperatures; at 5200 m the student took a photo of a thermometer instead of recording.

### Q6a (3 marks) — Read/Label/Complete

The student forgot to label the table correctly. Write down labels for heading 1 and heading 2. At 5200 m the student did not record a temperature, but took a photo of the thermometer. Measure the temperature shown in the photograph and add it to the results table.

**MS answers:**
- Thermometer reading: **83.5** (must be stated to this precision)
- Heading 1: **altitude or height** and **m** (ignore incorrect use of brackets)
- Heading 2: **temperature** and **°C**

---

### Q6b (2 marks) — Explain

Explain why a bar chart is not appropriate to display these results.

**MS answers (any two):**
- A bar chart is used to represent discrete data
- A line of best fit cannot be drawn on a bar chart
- A bar chart cannot be used to determine a relationship between the independent and dependent variables

**MS note:** WTTE.

---

### Q6c (2 marks) — Explain

Air pressure reduces as altitude increases. The higher we are above sea level, the fewer particles there are to be found in the air.

Explain (using particle theory) why air pressure reduces as altitude increases.

**MS answers:**
- (At higher altitude) there are fewer air particles
- So there are fewer collisions (with water particles / with surfaces)

---

### Q6d (2 marks) — Outline

Outline what this graph shows about the relationship between altitude and boiling temperature of water.

**MS answers:**
- As the altitude increases the boiling temperature decreases
- This is a linear relationship

---

### Q6e (4 marks) — Explain

Explain the results of the investigation using particle theory.

**MS answers:**
- Water particles need sufficient energy to escape the surface of the water
- There are fewer air particles at higher altitude
- So there are fewer collisions with water particles which prevent their escape
- Any additional reasonable suggestion, for example:
  - Energy of particles is related to temperature
  - At lower temperatures a greater proportion of water particles will have enough energy to escape the surface
  - So the boiling point is lower, justified with reference to particles

---

### Q6f (2 marks) — Comment on validity

Student's hypothesis: "As the altitude increases the boiling point will increase. The water molecules will find it harder to escape from the liquid because the pressure is dropping as you go up the mountain."

Use the results shown on the graph to comment on the validity of the hypothesis.

**MS answers:**
- The hypothesis is incorrect / not supported
- (Because) as the altitude increases the boiling point **decreases** (not increases)

---

### Q6g (1 mark) — Suggest

Suggest an extension to this investigation.

**MS answers:**
- Accept any reasonable extension with the same independent variable (e.g., investigate a different liquid, investigate boiling point at even higher altitudes, etc.)

## Key Mark Scheme Patterns

| Pattern | Explanation |
|---|---|
| WTTE | Words To That Effect — accept any answer with equivalent meaning |
| ECF | Error Carried Forward — award marks for correct method even if based on a wrong earlier answer |
| ORA | Or Reverse Argument — accept the opposite logical direction if correctly reasoned |
| "seen or implied" | The step does not need to be written explicitly if it is evident from the calculation |
| Do not accept "gravity" | Must say "weight" or "gravitational force" for force B in Q2c |
| Markband questions | Q7, Q8b, Q9 are assessed against multi-row markbands — each row is independent |
| Precision required | Q6a thermometer reading must be stated to 83.5 (not 83 or 84) |
| Unit must agree | In momentum (Q2e) the unit must be consistent with the numerical value given |
