## Best-approach command-term glossary (from MS p. 4)

| Term | Definition (verbatim from MS) |
|---|---|
| **State** | Give a specific name, value or other brief answer without explanation or calculation. |
| **Outline** | Give a brief account or summary. |
| **Describe** | Give a detailed account or picture of a situation, event, pattern or process. |
| **Explain** | Give a detailed account including reasons or causes. |
| **Compare and contrast** | Give an account of similarities and differences between two (or more) items or situations, referring to both (all) of them throughout. |
| **Construct** | Display information in a diagrammatic or logical form. |
| **Estimate** | Obtain an approximate value. |
| **Identify** | Provide an answer from a number of possibilities. Recognize and state briefly a distinguishing fact or feature. |
| **Justify** | Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion. |
| **Suggest** | Propose a solution, hypothesis or other possible answer. |
| **To what extent** | Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument or concept. Opinions and conclusions should be presented clearly and supported with appropriate evidence and sound argument. |

## Detailed Question Sections

### Question 1 — Freedom of Speech: Source-Sorting & Source Evaluation (10 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** A diagram showing two arrow-shaped destination boxes — "Limited freedom of speech" and "Unlimited freedom of speech" — and four draggable source items labelled **Source A, Source B, Source C, Source D**. Source B is shown placed inside "Limited freedom of speech" and Source D inside "Unlimited freedom of speech". Source A appears as a person/portrait icon and Source C as a CCTV-camera icon (these are the two draggables to be moved by the student).

The pre-release material referenced in this question includes:
- **Source A** — *Paradox of Tolerance* article by Barbara Ramirez, MYP year 5 student, school website 2016 (or *International Baccalaureate Organization 2019, Tree Top High*).
- **Source C** — Statistical study from https://thenetmonitor.org on internet-filtering across several countries.

#### 1a — Identify limited vs unlimited freedom-of-speech sources (2 marks)
**Question (verbatim):** *"The diagram represents different perspectives on the issue of freedom of speech. **Identify** **one** source making a case in favour of limited freedom of speech and **one** source in favour of unlimited freedom of speech. Drag and drop the source into the appropriate arrow in the diagram."*

**Mark scheme:** Award 1 mark for each correctly placed source (one in each Perspective).

**Note (verbatim):** *"Note: students who put more than **one source** in the Perspective should not be awarded the mark for that Perspective."*

**Key MS notes:** Drag-and-drop; over-selection is penalised independently per Perspective.

#### 1b — State origin and purpose of Source A and Source C (4 marks)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**State** the origin and the purpose of Source A and Source C."*

**Mark scheme:** Award 1 mark each for Origin and 1 mark each for Purpose.

**Note (verbatim):** *"Note: answers in students' own words or directly cut from the pre-release material are acceptable."*

| | **Source A** | **Source C** |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | MYP year 5 student from a school in Argentina, published in the school website in 2016. **Or** Barbara Ramirez. **Or** Tree Top High. **Or** International Baccalaureate Organization 2019. Tree Top High. | https://thenetmonitor.org |
| **Purpose** | To show understanding of the *Paradox of Tolerance*. | To provide statistical data from an empirical study on internet filtering in several countries. **Or** To describe important trends in the political, social, technological spheres that shape the current state of global internet freedom. **Or** OWTTE. |

**Key MS notes:** OWTTE accepted on Source C purpose; both literal lifts and paraphrases earn marks.

#### 1c — Justify one limitation of Source C (4 marks)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**Justify** **one** limitation of Source C."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student: **describes** one limitation of source C; attempts to use evidence from the source to support the explanation. |
| 3–4 | The student: **explains** one limitation of source C; uses evidence from the source to directly support the explanation. |

**Key MS notes:** 1–2 vs 3–4 hinges on (i) describe vs explain, (ii) "attempts to use evidence" vs "uses evidence … to directly support". Limitation must be anchored in Source C's own evidence (coverage, sampling, recency) — generic limitations don't reach the top band.

### Question 2 — Statistical Reasoning on Crowdfunding Donations (10 marks)

**Stimulus (verbatim from MS p. 8):**

| Percentage of target achieved | Average donation in $ |
|---|---|
| 10 | 15 |
| 33 | 26 |
| 67 | 40 |

#### 2a — Construct a scatter diagram (4 marks)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**Construct** a scatter diagram to represent the percentage of monetary target achieved against the average size of donation."*

**Mark scheme:** *"Award (1 mark) each for correct element on the graph: axes label: Percentage (%) of target achieved; axes label: Average donation in $ (or dollars); just the three data points plotted correctly; equal intervals from (0,0)."*

**Note (verbatim):** *"Note: axes can be labelled either way around."*

**MS exemplars (p. 9):** Two accepted plots — *"For example: ACCEPT y axis from 0 to 40"* and *"ACCEPT the axes in the opposite direction as shown below."*

**Key MS notes:** Each of the four scoring elements is independent. "(0,0)" requirement is about equal-interval scaling, not data plotting.

#### 2b — State the relationship (1 mark)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**State** the relationship between the percentage of monetary target achieved and the average size of donation."*

**Mark scheme answer:** *"As one increases the other increases (positive correlation or directly proportional)."*

#### 2c — Estimate average donation at 50 % target (1 mark)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**Estimate** the average size of the donation for 50 % of the target achieved."*

**Markscheme note (verbatim):** *"The answer must include the $ sign or have the written down dollars."*

**Mark scheme answer:** *"Accept answers in the range $30 < Donation < $35 i.e. between and not including $30 and $35."*

**Key MS notes:** Strict open interval; unit required.

#### 2d — Suggest why prediction at 80 % may not be valid (1 mark)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**Suggest** a reason why you would not predict the size of the donation for 80 % of the target achieved."*

**Mark scheme answer (verbatim):** *"Answers will be words to the effect of: You do not know if the data points will continue in the same pattern. Extrapolation is unreliable."*

#### 2e — Explain key concept of Relationships (3 marks)
**Question (verbatim):** *"**Key concept: Relationships** — Relationships in MYP mathematics refers to the connections between quantities, properties or concepts and these connections may be expressed as models, rules or statements. Relationships provide explicit opportunities for students to explore patterns in the world around them. Connections between the student and mathematics in the real world are important in developing deeper understanding. Source: International Baccalaureate Organization 2019. **Explain** why the key concept of relationships is important in nudge theory. In your answer, you must use **two** examples to support your answer."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1 | The student makes a general **statement** about mathematical relationships and nudge theory **or** provides an unexplained mathematical example. |
| 2 | The student **explains** why mathematical relationships are important to nudge theory with **one** mathematical example. |
| 3 | The student **explains** why mathematical relationships are important to nudge theory with **two** mathematical examples. |

**Key MS notes:** Two **mathematical** (not generic) examples required for full marks.

### Question 6 — To what extent can you know the truth from one source? (12 marks)

**Question (verbatim, MS p. 18):** *"**To what extent** can someone know the truth if they only receive their information from one source? In your answer, you must include: a justification of when it is appropriate to only use one source; a justification of when it is not appropriate to only use one source; evidence from the pre-release material to support your arguments; a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: **states** when it is appropriate to only use one source; **states** when it is not appropriate to only use one source; does not provide a conclusion. |
| 4–6 | The student: **outlines** when it is appropriate to only use one source; **outlines** when it is not appropriate to only use one source; attempts a conclusion. |
| 7–9 | The student: **describes** when it is appropriate to only use one source using evidence; **describes** when it is not appropriate to only use one source using evidence; provides a conclusion. |
| 10–12 | The student: **explains** when it is appropriate to only use one source using **explicitly linked** evidence from the pre-release material; **explains** when it is not appropriate to only use one source using **explicitly linked** evidence from the pre-release material; provides a **justified** conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:** Verb ladder *state → outline → describe → explain*. Top band requires evidence **explicitly linked** to pre-release material on both sides; conclusion must be **justified** (not merely present).

### Question 7 — Evaluate freedom of speech for social change (8 marks)

**Question (verbatim, MS p. 19):** *"**Evaluate** how freedom of speech is important to facilitate social change from your perspective. In your answer, you must include: strengths of freedom of speech; limitations of freedom of speech; an example from your "service as action" experience; a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student **makes a statement about at least one** of the points: strengths; limitations; service-as-action example; a conclusion. |
| 3–4 | The student **attempts to evaluate** by **outlining at least two** of: strengths; limitations; an appropriate service-as-action example; a conclusion. |
| 5–6 | The student **partially evaluates** by **describing at least three** of: strengths; limitations; an appropriate service-as-action example; an evaluative conclusion. |
| 7–8 | The student **fully evaluates** by **explaining all four** of: strengths; limitations; an appropriate service-as-action example; an evaluative conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:**
- Coverage-based banding (one→two→three→four) plus verb climb (statement→outline→describe→explain).
- "Service as action" hook required from 3–4 upward; generic example doesn't satisfy bullet.
- Conclusion specification climbs from "a conclusion" (1–4) to "evaluative conclusion" (5–8).
