### Question 1 — Monetizing Disinformation: Discuss (12 marks) | Criteria A & B (Disciplinary grounding + Synthesis)

**Stimulus screen header:** "Question 1 — References 1, 2, 6, 8 visible in the right-hand pre-release tray." The student sees the GDI disinformation article (Ref 1), the brand-funding pie chart and table (Ref 2), and the two related-concepts grids (Ref 8) at the foot of the screen.

**Glossary on screen:** *"Disinformation: False information deliberately spread to deceive people."*

**Question (verbatim):**
> **Discuss** how mathematics and language and literature can be used to monetize disinformation.
>
> In your answer you must:
> - explain how mathematics can be used to monetize disinformation
> - explain how language and literature can be used to monetize disinformation
> - explain how the synthesis of both subjects impacts the monetization of disinformation
> - use the concepts from both mathematics and language and literature to support your answer.
>
> *(12 marks)*

**Mark scheme — Markband rubric (12 marks):**

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: <br>• states that mathematics can be used in the monetization of disinformation <br>• states that language and literature can be used in the monetization of disinformation <br>• uses one example to show synthesis of the monetization of disinformation. |
| 4–6 | The student: <br>• outlines how mathematics contributes to the monetization of disinformation <br>• outlines how language and literature contributes to the monetization of disinformation <br>• outlines possible synthesis examples in monetization of disinformation, with a reference to a Mathematics or Language & Literature concept. |
| 7–9 | The student: <br>• describes how mathematics is used in the monetization of disinformation, including reference to subject concepts <br>• describes how language and literature is used in the monetization of disinformation, including reference to subject concepts <br>• describes how the synthesis of both subjects impacts the monetization of disinformation, with a reference to subject concepts. |
| 10–12 | The student: <br>• explains how mathematics is used in the monetization of disinformation, with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts <br>• explains how language and literature is used in the monetization of disinformation, with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts <br>• explains how the synthesis of both subjects impacts the monetization of disinformation, with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts. |

**Key MS notes:**
- Best-fit applied: examiner picks the markband that overall best matches the response.
- Three strands within each band: (1) maths contribution, (2) language & literature contribution, (3) synthesis. A response strong on two strands but lacking on the third sits at the lower end of the band that fits the strong strands.
- Top band (10–12) requires *explicit and accurate* naming of related concepts (e.g. *Quantity*, *Representation*, *Models*, *Validity* from maths; *Audience imperatives*, *Purpose*, *Style*, *Point of view* from language and literature) — listing them is not enough; they must be used to drive the explanation.
- Marks rewarded for using the GDI funding figures (US$236.4 million; the named advertiser-to-disinformation-site mappings) as evidence of how *Quantity* / *Models* in mathematics and *Audience imperatives* / *Purpose* in language and literature combine to make disinformation profitable.

### Question 2 — Spread of Disinformation: Explain (12 marks) | Criteria A & B

**Stimulus:** Question 2 screen reuses the right-hand reference tray. Active references for this question are Refs 3 (digital-footprint video script), 4 (idioms map — UK / Russia / China), 5 (MIT Twitter fake-news study with the unique-users line chart, the seven-category shares bar chart, and the politics-vs-natural-disasters scatter plot), and Ref 2 (funding pie chart).

**Question (verbatim):**
> Throughout this examination you have developed new interdisciplinary understanding that could influence your interaction with the online world.
>
> **Explain** how funding, alongside changes in technology and changes in language, can contribute to the spread of disinformation.
>
> In your answer you must:
> - explain the role of funding in the spread of disinformation, using mathematical evidence from the references
> - explain how changes in technology contribute to the spread of disinformation
> - explain how changes in language (idioms, framing, point of view) contribute to the spread of disinformation
> - use related concepts from both mathematics and language and literature to support your answer.
>
> *(12 marks)*

**Mark scheme — Markband rubric (12 marks):**

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: <br>• states that funding contributes to the spread of disinformation <br>• states that changes in technology contribute to the spread of disinformation <br>• states that changes in language contribute to the spread of disinformation <br>• refers to the pre-release material. |
| 4–6 | The student: <br>• outlines the role of funding in the spread of disinformation <br>• outlines how changes in technology contribute to the spread of disinformation <br>• outlines how changes in language contribute to the spread of disinformation <br>• outlines the synthesis between funding, technology and language, with a reference to subject concepts. |
| 7–9 | The student: <br>• describes the role of funding in the spread of disinformation, with reference to subject concepts <br>• describes how changes in technology contribute to the spread of disinformation, with reference to subject concepts <br>• describes how changes in language contribute to the spread of disinformation, with reference to subject concepts <br>• uses appropriate information from the pre-release material. |
| 10–12 | The student: <br>• explains the role of funding in the spread of disinformation, with explicit use of subject concepts <br>• explains how changes in technology contribute to the spread of disinformation, with explicit use of subject concepts <br>• explains how changes in language contribute to the spread of disinformation, with explicit use of subject concepts <br>• uses extensive information from the pre-release material. |

**Key MS notes:**
- Three contributory factors must each be addressed (funding / technology / language) — a response that handles only two will be capped in the lower part of the relevant band.
- "Mathematical evidence" is rewarded when the student quotes the actual figures (US$236.4 million; ~126 000 stories; ~3 million people; ~4.5 million; the 95–98 % fact-checker agreement; the 1 000 vs 1 000–100 000 reach gap; the seven-category share counts) and links them to a maths concept (Quantity, Models, Patterns, Representation, Validity).
- Top band requires *extensive* use of the pre-release material — not just one reference. The MIT Twitter study (Ref 5), the digital-footprint script (Ref 3) and the idioms map (Ref 4) all need to surface together for full marks.
- Idioms (Ref 4) reward analysis of *style*, *point of view*, *audience imperatives*, or *intertextuality* — i.e. how culturally specific framings ("economical with the truth", "hang noodles on someone's ears", "half the truth is to give life to a new lie") shape what counts as a lie and travel between communities.

### Question 3 — Article on the Spread of News: Create (12 marks) | Criterion C (Communication / creative synthesis)

**Stimulus:** Article statement displayed on screen:
> *"There is no such thing as freedom of thought: opinions are paid for by the rich."*

**Question (verbatim):**
> Using the statement for inspiration, **create** an article expressing your point of view on the spread of news on social media. In your answer you must:
> - synthesize mathematics and language and literature to explain how news spreads on social media
> - explain your point of view about the spread of news on social media using both mathematics and language and literature
> - use relevant information from the pre-release material to support your answer.
>
> *(12 marks)*

**Mark scheme — Markband rubric (12 marks):**

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: <br>• uses mathematics to state how news spreads on social media <br>• uses language and literature to state how news spreads on social media <br>• states their point of view about the spread of news on social media using mathematics **or** language and literature. |
| 4–6 | The student: <br>• outlines how news spreads on social media using mathematics and language and literature <br>• outlines their point of view about the spread of news on social media using both mathematics **and** language and literature <br>• attempts to use appropriate information from the pre-release material. |
| 7–9 | The student: <br>• describes how news spreads on social media using mathematics and language and literature <br>• describes their point of view about the spread of news on social media using both mathematics **and** language and literature <br>• uses appropriate information from the pre-release material. |
| 10–12 | The student: <br>• explains how news spreads on social media using mathematics and language and literature <br>• explains their point of view about the spread of news on social media using both mathematics **and** language and literature <br>• uses extensive information from the pre-release material. |

**Examiner notes (in red on MS):**
- *"Note: The point of view needs to be explicit."*
- *"Note: The structure of the article is not relevant to awarding credit."*

**Key MS notes:**
- The product is an *article* but examiners do not credit article-form features (headline, byline, columns, etc.) — credit is for the synthesis of disciplines and the explicit point of view.
- "Both mathematics **and** language and literature" — a response that builds its point of view from only one discipline is capped at band 1–3 even if otherwise rich.
- "Extensive information from the pre-release material" (band 10–12) is read as *multiple* references woven into the argument, not just a single source.
- The provocation ("opinions are paid for by the rich") invites use of Ref 1 / Ref 2 (funding flows) and Ref 5 (MIT data on speed and reach of false news) together with language concepts (*audience imperatives*, *purpose*, *point of view*, *style*).

**Question (verbatim):**
> Throughout this examination you have developed new interdisciplinary understanding that could influence your interaction with the online world.
>
> **Demonstrate** the new understanding in an informative speech for younger students on how they can act responsibly online.
>
> In your answer you must:
> - discuss what you have learned as a result of this examination about interacting with the online world
> - suggest actions for younger students to act responsibly online based on this new understanding
> - explain personal positive and negative consequences of these actions
> - use relevant information from the pre-release material to support your answer.
>
> *(6 marks)*

**Mark scheme — Markband rubric (6 marks):**

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student: <br>• states one thing they have learned from the examination about interacting with the online world <br>• suggests one action for younger students to act responsibly online <br>• states one consequence of that action <br>• refers to the pre-release material. |
| 3–4 | The student: <br>• describes things they have learned, especially linked to their new understanding <br>• outlines actions for younger students to act responsibly online, based on this new understanding <br>• outlines positive and negative consequences of these actions <br>• uses appropriate information from the pre-release material. |
| 5–6 | The student: <br>• explains things they have learned, especially linked to their new understanding <br>• explains actions for younger students to act responsibly online, based on this new understanding <br>• explains positive and negative consequences of these actions <br>• uses extensive information from the pre-release material. |

**Key MS notes:**
- The reflection must be *interdisciplinary* — i.e. the new understanding has to be the kind that could not have been gained from either Maths or Lang & Lit alone.
- Consequences must be **both positive and negative** — one-sided answers (e.g. only listing dangers of social media) are capped at the middle band.
- "Younger students" is the audience — reward responses that adjust *audience imperatives* / *style* accordingly (clarity, examples, no jargon).
- The video-script (Ref 3) "own your footprint" framing is the natural anchor for the action-and-consequence pairs (deleting can fail; future employers will look you up; etc.).

## Key Mark Scheme Patterns (IDL-specific)

| Pattern | Details |
|---|---|
| **Best-fit, four-strand markbands** | Every task in this paper uses a multi-strand best-fit rubric (12 / 12 / 12 / 6). No point-by-point answer keys. The examiner picks the band that best matches *overall* performance and then locates within the band based on the higher-order strands. |
| **Discipline parity is mandatory** | Q1, Q2 and Q3 each require evidence from **both** Maths *and* Language & Literature. A response that handles only one discipline is capped in the bottom band, regardless of how strong it is in that discipline. |
| **Concepts must be named, not just used** | Top-band descriptors require "explicit and accurate use of subject concepts". Implicit reasoning (e.g. discussing "trends" without using *Patterns*, or "tone" without using *Style* / *Audience imperatives*) is read down a band. The Reference 8 grids define the in-bounds vocabulary. |
| **Pre-release material — gradient of use** | Bands progress: *refers to* (1–3) → *attempts to use appropriate information* (4–6) → *uses appropriate information* (7–9) → *uses extensive information* (10–12). "Extensive" = multiple references woven through the response. |
| **Quantitative evidence for maths credit** | Reach for the actual numbers from Ref 1 (US$236.4 million), Ref 2 (named advertiser shares) and Ref 5 (~126 000 stories, ~3 million people, ~4.5 million, 95–98 %, the seven-category bar chart values, the log-scaled time-to-reach data). Without numerical evidence, maths content is treated as "stated" rather than "explained". |
| **Article structure is NOT credited (Q3)** | Despite the prompt asking for an *article*, the MS explicitly says "the structure of the article is not relevant to awarding credit". Marks are entirely for synthesis + explicit point of view + use of references. |
| **Point of view must be explicit (Q3)** | An article that catalogues evidence without taking a side is capped — a stated position is required. |
| **Reflection requires both upsides and downsides (Q4)** | "Personal positive **and** negative consequences" — single-sided reflections cap at the middle band. |
| **Audience awareness (Q4)** | The speech is for *younger students*; credit is given for adapting style and audience imperatives accordingly. |
| **Spelling errors are not penalised** | MS opening guidance: *"Please note that spelling errors should not impact the marks awarded in any part of the examination."* |
| **Command-term hierarchy** | The MS prints a command-term table at the start: **State** (specific name/value, no explanation), **Outline** (brief account), **Describe** (detailed account/picture), **Explain** (detailed account *with reasons or causes*), **Discuss** (considered and balanced review with arguments and evidence), **Create** (evolve from one's own thought as a work or invention), **Demonstrate** (make clear by reasoning or evidence, with examples or practical application). The four band labels in every rubric (state / outline / describe / explain) mirror this hierarchy. |

## Cross-reference: Concepts available for synthesis

Drawn from Reference 8, in case students need a concept-to-evidence map when revising:

**Mathematics concepts most often rewarded in this paper:**
- *Quantity* — the dollar figures, story counts, user counts in Refs 1, 2, 5.
- *Representation* — pie chart (Ref 2), line chart, bar chart and scatter plot (Ref 5).
- *Models* — the GDI's run-rate model; the MIT classification model with 95–98 % fact-checker agreement.
- *Patterns* — politics consistently outpacing natural disasters in Ref 5; the seven-category share ranking.
- *Validity* — the methodological framing of the MIT study; the brand-by-site mapping in Ref 2.
- *Approximation* — "around 126 000 stories", "approximately 3 million people".
- *Change* — the time-to-reach lines diverging between true and false information.

**Language and literature concepts most often rewarded in this paper:**
- *Audience imperatives* — who the disinformation is targeted at; how the speech (Q4) addresses younger students.
- *Purpose* — disinformation written to monetize attention; idioms used to soften lies.
- *Style* / *Genre* — article (Q3), report (Ref 1), video script (Ref 3), idiom (Ref 4).
- *Point of view* — required explicitly in Q3; the first-person video script in Ref 3.
- *Intertextuality* — idioms that travel between cultures (Ref 4) carrying different valence.
- *Context* — the cultural specificity of the UK / Russia / China idioms; the 2018 *Science* publication context for Ref 5.
- *Theme* — truth, deception, ownership of one's footprint, freedom of thought.
