## Best-approach command-term glossary (from MS p. 3)

| Term | Definition (verbatim from MS) |
|---|---|
| **State** | Give a specific name, value or other brief answer without explanation or calculation. |
| **Outline** | Give a brief account or summary. |
| **Describe** | Give a detailed account or picture of a situation, event, pattern or process. |
| **Explain** | Give a detailed account including reasons or causes. |
| **Compare and contrast** | Give an account of similarities and differences between two (or more) items or situations, referring to both (all) of them throughout. |
| **Construct** | Display information in a diagrammatic or logical form. |
| **Evaluate** | Make an appraisal by weighing up the strengths and limitations. |
| **Identify** | Provide an answer from a number of possibilities. |
| **Justify** | Give valid reasons or evidence to support an answer or conclusion. |
| **Provide** | Give something needed or wanted. |
| **To what extent** | Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument or concept. Opinions and conclusions should be presented clearly and supported with appropriate evidence and sound argument. |
| **Analyse** | Break down in order to bring out the essential elements or structure. To identify parts and relationships, and to interpret information to reach conclusions. |

## Detailed Question Sections

### Question 1 — Mathematical Modelling of 100 m Winning Times (10 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** A pre-release reference (data table and graph) showing winning times in the men's and women's Olympic 100 m sprint across multiple Games. The reference is used to support a claim about whether male and female times will converge.

#### 1a — Justify the claim that males and females will eventually run the same time for the 100 m (2 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"\"Males and females will eventually run the same time for the 100 m.\" Mathematically **justify** an argument for **or** against the statement."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1 | The student **outlines** an argument for **or** against the statement by using general evidence from the data. |
| 2 | The student **justifies** an argument for **or** against the statement by providing explicit evidence from the data. |

**Key MS notes:** The discriminator between 1 and 2 is "general evidence" vs "explicit evidence from the data" — students must cite specific values, trends or model outputs to reach 2. Either side of the argument (for or against) can score full marks. Mathematical (not narrative) justification is required.

---

#### 1b — To what extent can mathematical models be used to predict the future winning times for this event? (8 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"**To what extent** can mathematical models be used to predict the future winning times for this event? You must include:*
- *a justification of how models can be used to predict the future winning times*
- *a justification of how models cannot be used to predict the future winning times*
- *explicit evidence from both the data table and graph*
- *a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student: **states** how models can be used to predict the future; **states** how models cannot be used to predict the future; does not provide evidence; does not provide a conclusion. |
| 3–4 | The student: **describes** how models can be used to predict the future; **describes** how models cannot be used to predict the future; provides a general piece of evidence; attempts a conclusion. |
| 5–6 | The student: **explains** how models can be used to predict the future; **explains** how models cannot be used to predict the future; uses evidence from the data table **or** graph; provides a conclusion. |
| 7–8 | The student: **explains** how models can be used to predict the future; **explains** how models cannot be used to predict the future; explicitly links their **explanation** to evidence from both the data table **and** graph; provides a **justified** conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:** All four bullet-point requirements must be addressed for the top band. Distinguishing 5–6 from 7–8 hinges on (i) "states/describes/explains" verb level, (ii) "general piece of evidence" vs "evidence from the data table **or** graph" vs "evidence from both the data table **and** graph", and (iii) "attempts a conclusion" vs "conclusion" vs "**justified** conclusion". Both supporting and limiting cases for mathematical modelling must be present.

### Question 2 — Modes of Persuasion in Olympic Protest Posters (10 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** Two protest posters drawn from the pre-release material, each using a different mode of classical rhetorical persuasion. The MS exemplifies Poster 1 as appealing primarily to **logos** (logical/data-based persuasion) and Poster 2 as appealing primarily to **pathos** (emotional persuasion).

#### 2a — Identify the mode of persuasion most evident in each poster (2 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"Below are two protest posters. **Identify** the mode of persuasion that is most evident in each poster. Drag your choice into the boxes provided below."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not match any of the modes of persuasion with the correct poster. |
| 1 | The student matches one of the modes of persuasion with the correct poster. |
| 2 | The student matches both modes of persuasion with the correct poster. |

**Exemplification for examiners:**

| | Protest poster 1 | Protest poster 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Mode of persuasion** | Logos | Pathos |

**Key MS notes:** Drag-and-drop; one mark per correctly placed poster. The exemplification table sits inside the MS as the canonical answer key.

---

#### 2b — Describe how the mode of persuasion in protest poster 2 affects the audience (4 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"Using the answer to part (a), **describe** how the mode of persuasion in protest poster 2 affects the audience."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not provide any appropriate analysis of the chosen mode of persuasion in poster 2 with the references to the audience. |
| 1 | The student **states** how the mode of persuasion in poster 2 affects the audience without explicit links to the references. |
| 2 | The student **outlines** how the mode of persuasion in poster 2 affects the audience using **one** of the references with explicit links to the references and providing effects of the mode of persuasion to the audience. |
| 3 | The student **describes** how the mode of persuasion in poster 2 affects the audience using **two** of the references with explicit links to the references and providing effects of the mode of persuasion to the audience. |
| 4 | The student **describes** how the mode of persuasion in poster 2 affects the audience using **three or more** of the references with explicit links to the references and providing effects of the mode of persuasion to the audience. |

**Exemplification for examiners — Explanation of the effect on the audience:**

| Mode | Effect (verbatim from MS) |
|---|---|
| **logos** | An appropriate explanation of the effect related to the use of logic, structure or persuasion. |
| **pathos** | An appropriate explanation of the effect related to the use of pathos, with an emotional, sympathetic or empathetic response from the audience. |
| **ethos** | An appropriate explanation of the effect related to the use of credibility, ethics, principles or morals of the source/author. |

**Key MS notes:** The marks scale on (i) verb level (states → outlines → describes), and (ii) the *number* of references explicitly linked (zero → one → two → three or more). Each step up requires both more references **and** explicit linkage to audience effect. Generic descriptions of "pathos" with no audience effect cannot exceed mark 1.

---

#### 2c — Explain how the mode of persuasion *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest (4 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"**Explain** how the mode of persuasion **kairos** is relevant to Olympic protest."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not provide any appropriate explanation of how *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest. |
| 1 | The student **states** how the mode of persuasion *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest. |
| 2 | The student **outlines** how the mode of persuasion *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest. |
| 3 | The student **describes** how the mode of persuasion *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest. |
| 4 | The student **explains** how the mode of persuasion *kairos* is relevant to Olympic protest. |

**Exemplification for examiners — Explanation of the effect on the audience:**

| Mode | Effect (verbatim from MS) |
|---|---|
| **kairos** | An appropriate explanation of the effect related to the timing of the Olympics, the world stage, the media presence, the visibility *etc.* |

**Key MS notes:** A single verb-level rubric (states → outlines → describes → explains). The exemplification points to *kairos*-specific anchors: timing, world stage, media presence, visibility. Answers must be tied to the Olympic context, not given as abstract rhetorical theory.

### Question 3 — Designing & Evaluating a Synthesising Protest Poster (16 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** A drawing canvas / response area in which the student composes a protest poster against the country's application to host the Olympics. Students must synthesise mathematics (statistical/quantitative evidence) and language & literature (modes of persuasion) into a single composition, then evaluate that synthesis in writing.

#### 3a — Design a protest poster (8 marks)

**Question (reconstructed from MS p. 9):** *"**Design** a poster to protest the country's application to host the Olympics. In your poster, you must include the use of:*
- *the modes of persuasion*
- *language and literature*
- *mathematics*
- *evidence from the pre-release material*
- *a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student creates a poster **using at least two** of the four areas (modes of persuasion / language and literature / mathematics / evidence from the pre-release material) **correctly**. |
| 3–4 | The student creates a poster **using at least three** of the four areas **correctly**. |
| 5–6 | The student creates a poster **using all four** areas **effectively** in the protest of the country hosting the Olympics. |
| 7–8 | The student creates a poster **using all four** areas **effectively** in the protest of the country hosting the Olympics, **including a justified conclusion**. |

**Key MS notes:** Banding is keyed to count of areas covered (two → three → four), and from "correctly" to "effectively" between bands. Top band 7–8 requires both effective use of all four areas **and** a justified conclusion. The synthesis must explicitly protest the host application — generic Olympic posters are scored down.

---

#### 3b — Evaluate how the poster synthesises mathematics and language & literature (8 marks)

**Question (verbatim):** *"**Evaluate** how your poster synthesizes mathematics and language and literature to protest the country's application to host the Olympics. In your answer, you must include:*
- *benefits of the synthesis*
- *limitations of the synthesis*
- *evidence from the poster*
- *an evaluative conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student **makes a statement about** the poster by addressing **one** of the following points: states the benefits of the synthesis; states the limitations of the synthesis; states appropriate evidence from the poster; states a conclusion. |
| 3–4 | The student **attempts to critique** the poster by addressing **at least two** of the following points: outlines the benefits of the synthesis; outlines the limitations of the synthesis; outlines appropriate evidence from the poster; outlines a conclusion. |
| 5–6 | The student **partially critiques** the poster by addressing **at least three** of the following points: describes the benefits of the synthesis; describes the limitations of the synthesis; describes appropriate evidence from the poster; describes a conclusion. |
| 7–8 | The student **fully critiques** the poster by addressing **all four** of the following points: explains the benefits of the synthesis; explains the limitations of the synthesis; explains appropriate evidence from the poster; explains an evaluative conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:** Marks scale on **count of bullets covered** (one → two → three → four) and **verb level** (states → outlines → describes → explains). Evidence must come from the student's *own* poster from 3a. Without an evaluative conclusion the response cannot reach 7–8.

### Question 4 — Compare and Contrast Two References on Olympic Protest (12 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** Two pre-release references on the Olympics — the MS text describes them as a video reference and an infographic reference. Students must compare and contrast the views, ideologies and synthesis between the two.

**Question (reconstructed from MS p. 11):** *"**Compare and contrast** the differences in views and ideologies, the difference in the synthesis, between the video and the infographic, using explicit evidence from the references."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: **states** the differences in views and ideologies; **states** the difference in the synthesis between the video and infographic; refers to evidence from the references. |
| 4–6 | The student: **outlines** the differences in views and ideologies; **outlines** the difference in the synthesis between the video and infographic; refers to evidence from the references. |
| 7–9 | The student: **describes** the differences in views and ideologies; **describes** the difference in the synthesis between the video and infographic; uses evidence from the references. |
| 10–12 | The student: **compares and contrasts** the differences in views and ideologies; **compares and contrasts** the difference in the synthesis between the video and infographic; uses evidence from the references. |

**Key MS notes:** Verb-progression banding (states → outlines → describes → compares and contrasts). The top band requires the *command term itself* to be enacted — a side-by-side comparison referring to both sources throughout — not just sequential descriptions. Both *views/ideologies* **and** *synthesis* axes must be addressed; missing one caps the response.

### Question 5 — Choosing the Best Olympic-Host Applicant City (12 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** Data tables for two candidate host cities, covering revenue and expenditure (financial), legacy implications, and inclusivity implications.

**Question (verbatim):** *"Review the data for both cities and choose the best applicant. **Justify** your choice referring to both data sets. You must include information regarding:*
- *revenue and expenditure*
- *legacy*
- *inclusivity."*

**Note (verbatim from MS):** *"Award a maximum of (6 marks) if the student only addresses financial, legacy, or inclusivity implications from one city. Award a maximum of (3 marks) if the student does not make a choice of city."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student attempts to address at least **one** of the following: states the financial implications of their choice; states the legacy implications of their choice; states the inclusivity implications of their choice. *Within this markband, answers that communicate the ideas clearly and coherently will score higher.* |
| 4–6 | The student addresses at least **two** of the following: outlines the financial implications of their choice; outlines the legacy implications of their choice; outlines the inclusivity implications of their choice. *Within this markband, answers that communicate the ideas clearly and coherently will score higher.* |
| 7–9 | The student addresses **all** of the following: describes the financial implications of their choice; describes the legacy implications of their choice; describes the inclusivity implications of their choice. *Within this markband, answers that communicate the ideas clearly and coherently will score higher.* |
| 10–12 | The student addresses **all** of the following: explains the financial implications of their choice; explains the legacy implications of their choice; explains the inclusivity implications of their choice. *Within this markband, answers that communicate the ideas clearly and coherently will score higher.* |

**Key MS notes:** Two cap rules apply *before* the markband is evaluated:
- **6-mark cap** if only one city is referenced (i.e. no comparison drawn).
- **3-mark cap** if no city is chosen at all.

Within bands, communication quality discriminates upward placement. All three implication axes (financial, legacy, inclusivity) are required at 7+ — partial coverage caps in 4–6.

### Question 6 — "All Sports Must Be Popular" (10 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** Pre-release material on the role of popularity in selecting sports for the Olympic programme.

**Question (verbatim):** *"Taking into account your experience with sports and the **pre-release material**, **discuss** the following:*
- *reasons why you agree with the statement Anna applied for*
- *a difference of the synthesis between the four areas given in the pre-release material*
- *a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–3 | The student: **states** reasons why they agree with the statement Anna applied for; **states** a difference of the synthesis between the four areas given in the pre-release material; **states** a conclusion. |
| 4–6 | The student: **outlines** reasons why they agree with the statement Anna applied for; **outlines** a difference of the synthesis between the four areas given in the pre-release material; **outlines** a conclusion. |
| 7–9 | The student: **describes** reasons why they agree with the statement Anna applied for; **describes** a difference of the synthesis between the four areas given in the pre-release material; **describes** a conclusion. |
| 10–12 | The student: **explains** reasons why they agree with the statement Anna applied for; **explains** a difference of the synthesis between the four areas given in the pre-release material; provides a **justified** conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:** The MS rubric carries four bands up to 10–12, but the printed mark allocation for Q6 on MS p. 14 is **10 marks**. Treat the top band as 10/10 in practice. All three bullet-points (reasons, synthesis difference, conclusion) must be addressed; verb-level escalates with band; only the top band requires a *justified* (not merely stated) conclusion.

### Question 7 — To What Extent Can an Olympic Legacy Endure? (8 marks)

**Stimulus (described, not reproduced):** Pre-release material on the long-term legacy of past Olympic Games (infrastructure, social, economic and cultural after-effects).

**Question (verbatim):** *"**To what extent** can an Olympic legacy endure? In your answer, you must provide:*
- *a justification of when an Olympic legacy is sustainable*
- *a justification of when an Olympic legacy is not sustainable*
- *evidence from the pre-release material to support your arguments*
- *a conclusion."*

| Marks | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| 0 | The student does not achieve a standard described by any of the descriptors given below. |
| 1–2 | The student: **states** when an Olympic legacy is sustainable; **states** when an Olympic legacy is not sustainable; does not provide a conclusion. |
| 3–4 | The student: **outlines** when an Olympic legacy is sustainable; **outlines** when an Olympic legacy is not sustainable; attempts a conclusion. |
| 5–6 | The student: **describes** when an Olympic legacy is sustainable using evidence from the pre-release material; **describes** when an Olympic legacy is not sustainable using evidence from the pre-release material; provides a conclusion. |
| 7–8 | The student: **explains** when an Olympic legacy is sustainable using explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material; **explains** when an Olympic legacy is not sustainable using explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material; provides a **justified** conclusion. |

**Key MS notes:** Same shape as Q1b (8-mark "to what extent" reflective question). Discriminators between bands:
- 1–2 → 3–4: appearance of *any* attempt at a conclusion
- 3–4 → 5–6: integration of evidence from the pre-release material
- 5–6 → 7–8: evidence is **explicitly linked** to the explanations, and conclusion is **justified** (not merely stated)

Both the *sustainable* and *not sustainable* sides are required throughout — single-sided answers cannot reach the upper bands.

## Key Mark Scheme Patterns (N20)

| Pattern | Details |
|---|---|
| **Banded markschemes throughout** | Every question on this paper uses a banded rubric (no point-by-point marking). Bands typically scale on (i) verb level — states → outlines → describes → explains/compares/explains-with-evidence — and (ii) coverage count (one → two → three → all required bullets). |
| **"To what extent" structure** | Q1b and Q7 share the same 8-mark scaffold: justify the supportive case + justify the limiting case + evidence + conclusion. Top band 7–8 requires *explicit linkage* of evidence to explanations and a *justified* (not just stated) conclusion. |
| **Cap rules on Q5** | Hard caps applied **before** banding: 6-mark cap if only one city is addressed; 3-mark cap if no city is chosen. These caps override otherwise-eligible band placement. |
| **Modes of persuasion canon** | Q2 uses the four classical modes — **logos** (logic/structure), **pathos** (emotion/empathy), **ethos** (credibility/morals), **kairos** (timing/world stage/media visibility). The MS exemplification gives the canonical effect-on-audience for each. |
| **Synthesis-of-disciplines as the criterion** | Q3a explicitly requires four areas: modes of persuasion, language & literature, mathematics, and evidence from the pre-release material. Banding scales on count: at least 2 (1–2) → at least 3 (3–4) → all 4 correctly (5–6) → all 4 effectively + justified conclusion (7–8). |
| **Evaluation rubric on Q3b** | Critique depth banding: makes a statement → attempts to critique → partially critiques → fully critiques. Tied to the count of bullets addressed (one → two → three → all four). |
| **Compare-and-contrast = command-term-as-rubric** | Q4 top band 10–12 requires the student to actually *compare and contrast* (referring to both sources throughout), not merely describe each in turn. The verb-progression (states → outlines → describes → compares and contrasts) is the explicit ladder. |
| **"Justified conclusion" as top-band gatekeeper** | Q1b, Q3a, Q3b, Q6, Q7 each reserve the top band for responses that include a *justified* conclusion (Q1b/Q7 verbatim; Q3a "justified conclusion"; Q3b "evaluative conclusion"; Q6 "justified conclusion"). Stated/attempted conclusions cap below the top band. |
| **"Within this markband, answers that communicate the ideas clearly and coherently will score higher."** | Q5 (and parallel rubrics) explicitly include an internal-band tiebreaker on communication quality — clarity and coherence move responses up *within* a band. |
| **Pre-release evidence requirement** | Q1b, Q3a, Q3b, Q6, Q7 (and implicitly Q2b/Q4) all require explicit evidence from the pre-release pack. "General piece of evidence" caps at the lower middle band; "explicit evidence" is the gate to the top band. |
| **Visual-reference handling** | The MS describes visual stimuli (two protest posters in Q2; a video and infographic in Q4; data tables in Q5/Q6) but does not reproduce them. Students must reference these by content; the MS does not permit "the poster" or "the infographic" alone — explicit linkage is required. |
