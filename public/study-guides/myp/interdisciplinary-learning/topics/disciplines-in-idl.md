# Disciplines in IDL

IDL pairs two (sometimes three) disciplines per paper. The pair varies by session; the *skill* of working across them is constant. This page covers what each discipline contributes when it appears.

> Source: synthesised from `IDL_wiki/overview.md` (frequency stats and discipline-pair patterns) and the four representative session pages M19 (Maths-heavy), N16 (Sciences-heavy), N21 (L&L-heavy), N20 (triple-discipline). Quoted MS rules are verbatim from those pages.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mathematics | 7 / 10 (M17, M19, N20, M22, M23, M24; implicit in N16's experimental design) | Statistics, modelling, percentages, scale, correlation, scatter plots, key concept *Relationships* | Construct, Estimate, Justify, Explain, To what extent (modelling) | Data tables, scatter plots, percentages, infographics with stats |
| Sciences | 5 / 10 (N16, N19, M21, M23, M22 ecology) | Experimental method, sustainability, biological/ecological systems, control variables, hypothesis, key concept *Systems* | Evaluate (method), Suggest (improvement), Analyse, Compare graphs | Experimental write-ups, ecology data, biology/chemistry/physics text |

## Mathematics in IDL

**Frequency:** 7 / 10 sessions
**When it pairs with:** I&S (M17, M19, M22), Sciences (M23), L&L (M24), L&L + I&S triple (N20)

**Recurring concepts** (named in mark schemes):
- Statistics and correlation — scatter diagrams, positive/negative correlation, interpolation vs extrapolation (M19 Q2a–d).
- Mathematical modelling — using a model to predict future values; recognising that models break down outside the data range (N20 Q1a, Q1b on the men's/women's 100 m convergence).
- The MYP key concept *Relationships* — "the connections between quantities, properties or concepts and these connections may be expressed as models, rules or statements" (M19 Q2e quoted from the IB MYP guide).
- Percentages and quantitative reasoning applied to a non-mathematical topic (M19 crowdfunding %; N20 host-city revenue & expenditure).
- Diagrammatic display of information — axes, equal intervals, units, scale (M19 Q2a "axes label … just the three data points plotted correctly; equal intervals from (0,0)").
- M24-specific concept grid: *Quantity / Models / Patterns / Representation / Validity* must be named at top band.

**Typical question types:**
- *Construct a scatter diagram* (M19 Q2a, 4 marks) — point-marked: axes labels, three points plotted, equal intervals; "axes can be labelled either way around".
- *Estimate from the graph* (M19 Q2c, 1 mark) — strict open interval, e.g. "$30 < Donation < $35 i.e. between and not including".
- *Suggest why a prediction may not be valid* (M19 Q2d, 1 mark) — extrapolation reasoning.
- *Justify mathematically* (N20 Q1a, 2 marks) — outlines an argument with general evidence (1 mark) → justifies with explicit evidence from the data (2 marks).
- *To what extent can mathematical models be used to predict …?* (N20 Q1b, 8 marks) — both supporting and limiting cases; explicit linkage to data table **and** graph at top band.
- *Explain why the key concept of Relationships matters* (M19 Q2e, 3 marks) — full marks need *two mathematical (not generic) examples*.

**Key technique demands:**
- Show working / cite specific values when justifying — "general evidence" caps at 1 mark, "explicit evidence" earns 2 (N20 Q1a).
- Label axes and include units. M19 Q2c: "The answer must include the $ sign or have the written down dollars".
- Distinguish interpolation from extrapolation — M19 Q2d expects students to flag that extrapolation is unreliable.
- Use mathematical (not narrative) examples when illustrating a concept. M19 Q2e top band needs two *mathematical* examples; one mathematical + one narrative caps at 2 / 3.
- For modelling: state both what the model captures and where it breaks down (N20 Q1b).

**MS rules specific to this discipline:**
- M19 Q2a: "axes can be labelled either way around" — orientation does not lose marks.
- M19 Q2c: "$30 < Donation < $35 i.e. between and not including $30 and $35" — boundary values excluded.
- M19 Q2c: "answer must include the $ sign or have the written down dollars" — units mandatory.
- M19 Q2e: full marks require "**two** mathematical examples"; one example caps at 2 / 3.
- N20 Q1a: 2-mark gate is "explicit evidence from the data" — citing actual times/years from the table.
- N20 Q1b: 7–8 band requires "explicitly links their **explanation** to evidence from both the data table **and** graph" — using only one of the two source forms caps at 5–6.
- M24 (per overview): top band requires *named* maths concepts (Quantity / Models / Patterns / Representation / Validity), not implicit reasoning.

**Common student traps:**
- Plotting points but forgetting to label axes — M19 Q2a awards each scoring element independently, so unlabelled axes lose 2 of 4 marks.
- Giving "30" or "35" as the answer to M19 Q2c — boundary values are excluded.
- Quoting "$32" without the $ sign — unit gate fails the mark.
- Extrapolating into the 80 % region without flagging it as unreliable (M19 Q2d).
- Justifying "for or against" with a vague trend statement — N20 Q1a needs specific values, not "the times are getting closer".
- For modelling questions, listing only the strengths of mathematical models — both sides required (N20 Q1b's "can be used" + "cannot be used" structure).
- Using a non-mathematical example in M19 Q2e (e.g. "people respond to peer pressure") instead of a mathematical one (e.g. "the % of target achieved correlates linearly with average donation").

**Revision drill (1 paragraph):** Practise constructing a scatter diagram from a 3-row data table with full labelling, units, and an equal-interval scale — then immediately answer (a) state the relationship, (b) estimate at an in-range value with a strict inequality and a unit, (c) suggest a reason the prediction at an out-of-range value is invalid. After that, take a "to what extent can a model predict X" prompt and write a 4-bullet plan — supportive case with one specific data citation, limiting case with one specific citation, evidence linking explicitly to both the table and the graph, justified conclusion. Practise with M19 Q2 for the short ladder and N20 Q1 for the extended modelling response.

## Sciences in IDL

**Frequency:** 5 / 10 sessions
**When it pairs with:** I&S (N16, N19), Maths (M23), L&L + I&S triple (M21), I&S ecology (M22)

**Recurring concepts** (named in mark schemes):
- Experimental method — control variables, independent/dependent variables, hypothesis, number of trials, position of light source, numerical values (N16 Q1b lists eight specific issues to consider).
- Sustainability and ecological systems — closed systems, inputs/outputs, energy flow, waste-to-resource cycles (N16 Q4a Sciences exemplars).
- The MYP key concept *Systems* — "environmental and human systems interconnectedness" (N16 Q3b, Q5).
- Adaptation of method to ethical constraints — animal welfare, food, water, space, temperature (N16 Q1c).
- Comparison of graphs / trial data (N16 Q1a — three trials of bean-plant growth at four CO2 levels).
- Renewable energy, organic food production, vertical farming, water management (N16 Q4a sustainable-living video).

**Typical question types:**
- *Compare the graphs generated by the three trials* (N16 Q1a, 4 marks) — bullet-counted at 1–3, with the 4th mark gated on an *explicit comparative statement* (atmospheric vs above-atmospheric CO2).
- *Evaluate the experimental method* (N16 Q1b, 8 marks) — strengths + limitations + improvements; "to achieve more than (4 marks) both strengths **and** limitations need to be included" and improvements must be given.
- *Suggest two changes to the investigation* (N16 Q1c, 2 marks) — point-marked, six interchangeable bullets.
- *Analyse the impact of fishing on sustainability* (N16 Q2b, 12 marks) — SWOT-style, source-evidence-counted (1 → 2 → ≥3 quotations across bands).
- *Academic response evaluating a scientist's claim* (N16 Q3a, 15 marks) — four sub-bullets, all required for top band, source-evidence gate.
- *Identify disciplinary knowledge from a video transcript* (N16 Q4a, 4 marks) — capped at 2 marks per discipline to force balance.

**Key technique demands:**
- Name control variables specifically — "temperature, watering, soil type" rather than "things that should be kept the same" (N16 Q1b).
- Pair every issue identified with an improvement — bands explicitly couple the two: 1–2 = one issue, no improvement; 3–4 = two issues + 1 improvement; 5–6 = three + 2; 7–8 = four + 2.
- For ecological / sustainability arguments, structure as SWOT (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats) and quote the source (N16 Q2b).
- Make the comparative statement *explicit* — burying it in the wording of two bullets is acceptable, but missing it caps at 3 / 4 (N16 Q1a).
- For sustainability: name closed systems, "outputs of one system are the inputs of another", energy positivity, waste-to-resource (N16 Q4a Science exemplars).

**MS rules specific to this discipline:**
- N16 Q1a: "Therefore plants exposed to above atmospheric CO2 levels grow faster than plants exposed to atmospheric level of CO2 (this comparison may be implicit in their wording of the 2nd and 3rd bullet)" — comparison can be implicit but must be present.
- N16 Q1b: "To achieve more than (4 marks) both strengths **and** limitations need to be included in the answer. To achieve more than (4 marks) suggestions for improvement must be given."
- N16 Q2b: source-quotation count gate — "no examples or quotations" (1–2), "at least one example/quotation" (3–5), "two examples/quotations" (6–8), "at least three examples/quotations" (9–12).
- N16 Q3a: "Band 1–3 is explicitly for responses with no source examples"; bands 2–4 require source examples; "Responses that make implicit references to the sources are acceptable as examples".
- N16 Q3b: "A maximum of (5 marks) can be given if only one of the disciplines from science and individuals and society has been mentioned."
- N16 Q4a: "(1 mark) for each response and a maximum of (2 marks) for each individual discipline" — must give 2 from Sciences and 2 from I&S.
- N16 Q5: "To receive (8 marks) and above, the context of the script for the social guidelines must be followed."

**Common student traps:**
- Listing two limitations and stopping — caps at 4 / 8 in N16 Q1b (no strengths, no improvements).
- Suggesting an improvement that doesn't link to an identified issue — improvements must address the issues raised.
- Comparing graphs in pure description ("trial 1 had no growth, trial 2 had slow growth …") without the comparative line — caps at 3 / 4 in N16 Q1a.
- For animal-ethics adaptation (N16 Q1c), giving a chemistry-style change ("use 4× CO2 instead of 3×") instead of welfare adaptations.
- Quoting only one or two sources in a 12-mark SWOT — N16 Q2b top band needs ≥ 3 quotations.
- Single-discipline reflection on the key concept *systems* — caps at 5 / 10 in N16 Q3b.
- Generic "renewable energy is good" instead of the specific phrasing from the source ("energy positive homes", "vertical farming aquaponics", "waste-to-resource systems") in N16 Q4a.

**Revision drill (1 paragraph):** Take an experimental method (CO2/bean-plant style) and write a 4-issue list of weaknesses, each paired with an improvement, and at least two strengths. Practise the comparison sentence ("Therefore X grow faster than Y under …") explicitly. Then take a SWOT analysis question on a sustainability source set — write four bullets (strength, weakness, opportunity, threat) each anchored by a direct quotation from the source. Finish with a 10-mark reflection on the *systems* key concept that names *both* science and I&S contributions to the synthesis (the discipline-balance cap is the single biggest mark-loss point on Sciences IDL papers).

## Individuals & Societies in IDL

**Frequency:** 9 / 10 sessions (every session except M23)
**When it pairs with:** Sciences (N16, N19), Maths (M17, M19, M22), L&L (N21), Maths + L&L triple (N20), Sciences + L&L triple (M21)

**Recurring concepts** (named in mark schemes):
- The MYP key concept *Global interactions* — "the interdependence of the larger human community, including the many ways that people come into conflict with and cooperate with each other, and live together in a highly interconnected world to share finite resources" (N21 Q1d quoted verbatim from Reference 1).
- Stakeholders and perspectives — consumer, fisher, corporate fishing company, environmentalist, policy maker (N16 Q2a); Allie's vs Richard's POV on aid distribution (N21 Q3a).
- Source analysis — origin, purpose, primary vs secondary, limitations (M19 Q1a–c "Identify… State origin and purpose… Justify a limitation"; N21 Q1a primary/secondary).
- Implications across categories — "economic, social, political, environmental, ethical, legal or technological factors" (N16 Q2a).
- Research questions — clear and focused, considering "relevance, manageability, originality, ability to be assessed, availability of resources, level of student interest, connection with the discipline" (N21 Q1c standardisation note).
- Justified opinion grounded in a global issue — N21 Q7 on local nuclear-plant siting; M19 Q6 on knowing the truth from one source; N20 Q5 host-city choice.

**Typical question types:**
- *Identify which source supports limited / unlimited freedom of speech* (M19 Q1a, 2 marks) — drag-and-drop, overselection penalised independently per perspective.
- *State origin and purpose of two sources* (M19 Q1b, 4 marks) — point-marked, 1 mark each.
- *Justify a limitation of a source* (M19 Q1c, 4 marks) — verb ladder describes (1–2) → explains (3–4); evidence from the source itself, not generic.
- *Outline two stakeholder perspectives and implications* (N16 Q2a, 4 marks) — sample-answer table; "other sensible perspectives and implications are acceptable providing they link to the stakeholder".
- *Explain how Global interactions is demonstrated* (N21 Q1d, 3 marks) — outlines (1) → describes (2) → explains (3) ladder.
- *Justify a choice between two stakeholder positions / cities* (N20 Q5, 12 marks) — financial / legacy / inclusivity all required at top band.
- *To what extent do you support …?* (N21 Q7, 12 marks) — opinion + disagreement with opposing + explicitly linked evidence + justified conclusion.

**Key technique demands:**
- Name the stakeholder and pair them with both a perspective *and* an implication (N16 Q2a 1 mark per cell, 2 stakeholders × 2 cells = 4 marks).
- Anchor every "limitation of a source" claim in the source's own evidence — coverage, sampling, recency. Generic limitations don't reach the top band (M19 Q1c).
- For *Global interactions* / key-concept questions, explicitly cite the concept and link it to the stimulus (N21 Q1d; this was tightened in M24 to *named* concept use).
- When asked for "an opinion" in a "to what extent" question, also write the *disagreement with opposing opinions* — N21 Q7 explicitly has both as bullet requirements.
- Refer to *both* sides / data sets when justifying a choice — N20 Q5 caps at 6 if only one city is addressed.
- Format-follow when the question sets a context (script, social guidelines, role-play) — N16 Q5, M21 Q5a.

**MS rules specific to this discipline:**
- M19 Q1a: "students who put more than **one source** in the Perspective should not be awarded the mark for that Perspective" — overselection penalty per perspective, not globally.
- M19 Q1b: "answers in students' own words or directly cut from the pre-release material are acceptable" — paraphrase is fine.
- N16 Q2a: "other sensible perspectives and implications are acceptable providing they link to the stakeholder" — sample answers are illustrative, not exhaustive.
- N20 Q5: "Award a maximum of (6 marks) if the student only addresses financial, legacy, or inclusivity implications from one city. Award a maximum of (3 marks) if the student does not make a choice of city."
- N21 Q1c: "consider relevance, manageability, originality, ability to be assessed, availability of resources, level of student interest, and connection with the discipline" — examiner standardisation list.
- N21 Q3a: "Points of view must be in the context of aid distribution. If not, max 3 marks." Plus "Maximum number of posts from each person is 2."
- N21 Q7: top band 10–12 requires "**explicitly** linked evidence from the examination" and "**justified** conclusion" (not merely a stated one).
- M22 Q1a (per overview): "Wrong stakeholder card / wrong dropdown = 0 for that mark" — drag-and-drop precision matters.

**Common student traps:**
- Giving a stakeholder's perspective without an implication (or vice versa) — N16 Q2a needs both per stakeholder.
- Listing five stakeholders to be safe — M19 Q1a's overselection penalty applies; N16 Q2a caps at 4 marks (2 stakeholders).
- Stating a generic limitation ("the source is biased") in M19 Q1c — top band needs the limitation anchored in *Source C*'s evidence (it's a study from a website with limited sample).
- Choosing a host city in N20 Q5 but only discussing finance — caps at the 4–6 band; all three of finance, legacy, inclusivity required at 7+.
- Forgetting to choose a city at all — 3-mark cap.
- Writing a research question that's interesting but not on the I&S content of the poster (N21 Q1c) — cap.
- Writing a "to what extent" answer with only the supporting case — N21 Q7 explicitly requires *disagreement with opposing opinions*.

**Revision drill (1 paragraph):** Pick a source (poster, article, study) and run the I&S source-work ladder: primary or secondary (1 mark) → describe two reasons (4 marks) → state origin and purpose (4 marks) → justify a limitation anchored in the source's own evidence (4 marks). Then practise a stakeholder grid for any global issue: name 2 stakeholders, give each a perspective and an implication (across economic/social/political/environmental/ethical/legal/technological). Finish with a "to what extent" answer of 12 marks, structured as: justified opinion + disagreement with opposing opinion + ≥ 3 explicitly linked evidence pieces + justified conclusion. Use M19 Q1, N16 Q2a, N21 Q7 as templates.

## Language & Literature in IDL

**Frequency:** 4 / 10 sessions (N20, M21, N21, M24 — all from N20 onwards)
**When it pairs with:** I&S (N21), Maths + I&S triple (N20), Sciences + I&S triple (M21), Maths (M24)

**Recurring concepts** (named in mark schemes):
- Modes of persuasion — *logos* (logic/structure), *pathos* (emotion/empathy), *ethos* (credibility/morals), *kairos* (timing/world stage/media visibility) (N20 Q2a–c).
- Style elements — use of second person, icons, adjectives to appeal to emotion (N21 Q2a).
- Related concepts (named in N21 Reference 3): *Audience imperatives, Character, Context, Genres, Intertextuality, Point of view, Purpose, Self expression, Setting, Stylistic choices, Structure, Theme*.
- Point of view and culture as paired analytical lenses (N21 Q3a–b on the Allie/Richard social-media conversation).
- Origin, purpose, language, style of an article — synonyms agreed in standardisation: "Purpose = theme, Style = format, origin = source, origin = time" (N21 Q4 standardisation note).
- M24 introduced explicit naming of *Audience imperatives / Purpose / Style / Point of view* at top band.
- Idioms and culturally-encoded language (M24 Reference 4 "Around the world in idioms").

**Typical question types:**
- *Identify the mode of persuasion most evident in each poster* (N20 Q2a, 2 marks) — drag-and-drop; logos vs pathos.
- *Describe how the mode of persuasion affects the audience* (N20 Q2b, 4 marks) — banded by number of references explicitly linked: zero (1) → one (2) → two (3) → three or more (4).
- *Explain how kairos is relevant to Olympic protest* (N20 Q2c, 4 marks) — verb ladder states → outlines → describes → explains; effect must be tied to the Olympic context, not given as abstract rhetorical theory.
- *Select three elements of style* (N21 Q2a, 2 marks) — point-marked.
- *Evaluate the impact of this poster on the audience* (N21 Q2b, 8 marks) — strengths + limitations + ≥ 3 evidence pieces + evaluative conclusion.
- *Create a social-media conversation* (N21 Q3a, 12 marks) — point of view from each character explained, all 4 posts linked to generate coherent dialogue.
- *Compare and contrast two articles* (N21 Q4, 12 marks) — language, style, origin, purpose; both comparisons *and* contrasts; linked to both articles.
- *Analyse how you have synthesised point of view and culture* (N21 Q3b, 8 marks) — reflective, conclusion explicitly referring to the related concepts at top band.

**Key technique demands:**
- Name the persuasion mode then explain its *effect on the audience* — generic descriptions of "pathos" with no audience effect cap at 1 mark (N20 Q2b).
- Tie the mode to the *specific context* (Olympic timing/media/world stage for kairos) rather than reciting Aristotelian theory (N20 Q2c).
- Quote / cite the source — N20 Q2b scales literally on the count of references linked (1 → 2 → ≥ 3).
- For compare-and-contrast: enact the command term — refer to *both* texts throughout, not in two separate paragraphs (N21 Q4 top band requires the verb itself to be enacted, not just sequential descriptions).
- For evaluation of a poster's impact: give *both* strengths and limitations; one-sided answers cap at 2 / 8 (N21 Q2b).
- For role-play / dialogue creation: stay in role *and* produce a coherent, linked dialogue (N21 Q3a's "all 4 posts linked" requirement at 10–12).

**MS rules specific to this discipline:**
- N20 Q2b: number-of-references gate explicit — "states without explicit links" (1) → "one of the references" (2) → "two of the references" (3) → "three or more" (4).
- N20 Q2c kairos exemplification: "An appropriate explanation of the effect related to the timing of the Olympics, the world stage, the media presence, the visibility *etc.*" — abstract definitions of kairos do not earn marks.
- N21 Q2a: must select *exactly* three style elements; selecting only two = 1 mark, selecting three = 2 marks.
- N21 Q2b: "if only strengths or only limitations are given, a maximum of (2 marks) can be awarded" — caps at 2 / 8.
- N21 Q3a: "Maximum number of posts from each person is 2"; "Points of view must be in the context of aid distribution. If not, max 3 marks."
- N21 Q4: "if only comparisons or only contrasts are given, a maximum of (3 marks) can be awarded"; "if comparisons and contrasts are not linked to both articles, a maximum of (2 marks) can be awarded (for example two separate lists)."
- N21 Q4 standardisation synonyms: *Purpose = theme; Style = format; origin = source; origin = time*.
- M24 (per overview): top band requires *named* L&L concepts (Audience imperatives / Purpose / Style / Point of view), not implicit reasoning.

**Common student traps:**
- Defining pathos correctly but giving no audience effect — caps at 1 / 4 in N20 Q2b.
- Reciting kairos as "the rhetorical use of timing" with no Olympic anchor — caps at 2 / 4 in N20 Q2c.
- Selecting 4+ style elements in N21 Q2a "to be safe" — wording is "select three", overselection is not rewarded.
- Writing two separate paragraphs (one per article) for compare-and-contrast — caps at 2 / 12 in N21 Q4.
- Listing only similarities (or only differences) — caps at 3 / 12 in N21 Q4.
- Letting the dialogue drift into general humanitarian discussion instead of "where the UN funding should be directed" — caps at 3 / 12 in N21 Q3a.
- Failing to name the related concept in the conclusion — N21 Q3b top band requires "a conclusion explicitly referring to the related concepts".

**Revision drill (1 paragraph):** Memorise the four modes of persuasion with a one-line audience effect each (logos = logic/structure → reasoned acceptance; pathos = emotion → sympathetic/empathetic response; ethos = credibility/morals → trust; kairos = timing/world stage/media presence → urgency now). Then practise the N20 Q2 ladder: identify the mode in two posters → describe the effect by linking ≥ 3 references → explain how kairos fits a *specific event*. For N21 Q4-style compare-and-contrast, write a four-element grid (language, style, origin, purpose) with rows for both articles, then translate the grid into prose that refers to *both* throughout. Practise a 12-mark poster evaluation using strengths + limitations + 3 evidence pieces + evaluative conclusion (N21 Q2b shape).

## Triple-discipline papers

Two of the ten sessions bundle three disciplines: **N20** (Maths + L&L + I&S, Olympics) and **M21** (Sciences + L&L + I&S, Healthcare).

**Why this happens:** The integrating context naturally demands three lenses. N20's Olympics frame requires (i) a mathematical model of athletic performance over time, (ii) rhetorical analysis of protest posters, and (iii) evaluative comparison of host cities — no two of these alone capture the topic. M21's healthcare frame requires (i) the science of randomised controlled trials, (ii) the narrative voice of patients and drug-company representatives, and (iii) the global-issue debate over healthcare as a right.

**Mark distribution observed (N20 example):**

| Discipline focus | Questions | Marks | Approx. share |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mathematics-led | Q1a, Q1b | 10 | 12.5 % |
| Language & Literature-led | Q2a, Q2b, Q2c | 10 | 12.5 % |
| Synthesis (Maths + L&L) | Q3a, Q3b, Q4 | 28 | 35 % |
| I&S-led | Q5, Q6 | 22 | 27.5 % |
| Reflective / metacognitive | Q7 | 8 | 10 % |
| **Total** | | **78–80** | |

The pattern: each individual discipline gets a 10-mark "grounding" block; then 35 %+ of the paper is *synthesis* across two of the three; finally I&S typically owns the application + reflection ladder.

**How candidates should pace responses:**
1. Allocate roughly one-third of the time to disciplinary-grounding short questions (e.g. M19 Q1, Q2 style) — these are point-or-low-band marks where speed beats elaboration.
2. Reserve the largest single block of time for the synthesis questions (Q3 in N20, Q5 in M19) — these are 12–20-mark items where missing a bullet costs 2–3 marks each.
3. Triage I&S extended responses (justify a host-city choice, debate a global issue) for about a quarter of the paper — these reward structure (financial / legacy / inclusivity grid; opinion / counter-opinion / evidence / conclusion grid).
4. Always leave 10 minutes for the reflective "to what extent" question at the end — top-band entry requires a *justified* conclusion, and writing a justified conclusion needs a few minutes of explicit weighing.

**Trap:** in triple-discipline papers, candidates over-invest in the discipline they feel strongest in and skim the third. The discipline-balance caps still apply at the synthesis level — N20 Q3a explicitly counts "all four areas" (modes of persuasion, L&L, mathematics, evidence from PRM); covering only three caps at 5 / 8.

## How to identify which disciplines a session pairs

Practical pre-exam guide.

### Step 1 — Read the PRM cover and Reference 8

If the pre-release pack contains an explicit "related concepts" reference, it names the disciplines in the paper. The May 2024 pack is the cleanest example: Reference 8 is titled *"Related concepts in mathematics and language and literature"* — a static image listing the related-concepts grid for each discipline. This was the first session where the disciplines were named *up-front in the PRM* (per `IDL_MYP/pre-release/may-2024/README.md`). Always check whether such a reference exists; if it does, the discipline pair is given.

### Step 2 — Inspect reference *types*

The mix of reference types in the PRM signals the disciplines.

| Reference type | Likely discipline |
|---|---|
| Video / video transcript with rhetoric, narration, interviews | Language & Literature |
| Poster, infographic with persuasive language, slogans | Language & Literature |
| News articles, speeches, idioms | Language & Literature |
| Scatter plots, data tables, percentages, modelling graphs | Mathematics |
| Statistical studies (e.g. M19 Source C from thenetmonitor.org) | Mathematics + I&S overlap |
| Interactive widgets with quantitative data (e.g. M24 Reference 5 on true/false news spread) | Mathematics |
| Experimental method write-ups, ecology/biology/chemistry data | Sciences |
| Sustainability infographics, ecosystem flow diagrams | Sciences (often + I&S) |
| Stakeholder lists, perspective grids, role descriptions | Individuals & Societies |
| Key-concept boxes (e.g. "Global interactions" in N21 Reference 1) | Individuals & Societies |
| Government / policy documents, news articles on social issues | Individuals & Societies |

If two distinct reference clusters appear (e.g. a scatter plot *and* a stakeholder grid), the paper is almost certainly a Maths + I&S pair (M17, M19, M22 pattern).

### Step 3 — Look at the question stems on exam day

Command terms cluster by discipline. As soon as the paper opens, scan Q1–Q3 stems and tally:

| Command term spotted | Likely discipline being tested |
|---|---|
| Construct, Estimate, Justify mathematically | Mathematics |
| Evaluate (the experimental method), Suggest (improvements), Compare (graphs), Analyse (impact on sustainability) | Sciences |
| Identify (stakeholder), Outline (perspective), Justify (choice), To what extent (do you support) | Individuals & Societies |
| Compare and contrast (two articles/posters), Analyse (the language), Describe (the effect on the audience), Explain (the mode of persuasion) | Language & Literature |

**Cross-check rule:** if Q1 is "Construct a scatter diagram" and Q2 is "Identify two stakeholder perspectives", you're in Maths + I&S (M19 pattern). If Q1 is "Compare three trial graphs" and Q2 is "Outline two stakeholder perspectives", you're in Sciences + I&S (N16 pattern). If a poster question with "modes of persuasion" appears, L&L is in the paper.

### Step 4 — Watch for the synthesis questions

The synthesis question (often Q3 or Q4) names the disciplines explicitly: e.g. N16 Q3b "synthesized your knowledge of both the sciences and individuals and societies"; N20 Q3b "your poster synthesizes mathematics and language and literature"; N21 Q5/Q6 "skills from language and literature and individuals and societies could be synthesised". If you reach this question and the disciplines named differ from your pre-exam expectation, recalibrate the discipline-balance gate immediately — single-discipline answers will be capped at half-marks (N16 Q3b, M17 Q4b, M23 Q2 all enforce this).

## Cross-paper checklist (before you leave the exam room)

- Have I covered *both* (or all three) disciplines in every banded question? Single-discipline answers are capped at half-marks across the entire IDL series.
- Have I cited the PRM by reference number / title where the rubric requires it (M19 Q5a–b, N20 Q1b/Q3a/Q3b/Q6/Q7, N21 Q7, M24 Q1–3)?
- Have I named the *key concept* (Systems / Global interactions / Relationships) where it's been flagged in the PRM (N16 Q3b, N21 Q1d, M19 Q2e, M22 Q1b)?
- For "to what extent" questions, have I given (i) the supportive case, (ii) the limiting case, (iii) explicitly linked PRM evidence, (iv) a *justified* conclusion?
- For compare-and-contrast questions, have I referred to *both* texts throughout (not in two separate paragraphs)?
- For role-play / format questions, have I sustained the requested format (script, social-media post, canvas + text, role)?
