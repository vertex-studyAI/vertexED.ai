# Source and Evidence Work

> **Why this topic matters:** Across the 10 IDL sessions analysed, every top-band rubric for an extended response includes a source / evidence gate. Mid-band candidates name a source. Top-band candidates *quote* it, *attribute* it by reference number, and *link* the quotation explicitly to the question's demands. This page distils the rules, examples and templates the candidate needs to lock in those marks.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### What "source" means in IDL

Every IDL paper bundles a **pre-release material (PRM) pack** issued before the exam, plus in-paper references. References are numbered (Reference 1, Reference 2…) and are the *only* legitimate evidence base for top-band answers. Unlike a single-subject exam where stimuli are novel, IDL students have weeks to study the references and must walk into the exam knowing them cold.

References come in many forms — videos and transcripts, infographics, interactive widgets, articles, statistical studies, idiom maps, perspectives lists, and concept grids. The candidate must cite by **reference number** (or, in older sessions, by Source letter) and use specific content from that reference, not gestural acknowledgement.

The May 2024 PRM, for example, contains 8 numbered references spanning a propaganda video transcript, a tech-timeline interactive widget, a digital-footprint prose article, an idioms interactive map, an MIT fake-news study with multiple charts, a GDI funding interactive, a regulation interactive, and a static concepts grid. Every Q1–Q4 task on the M24 paper draws on that same reference pool.

### Reference types catalogue

| Type | What it looks like | How to cite | Example session |
|---|---|---|---|

| Infographic / static image | Chart, pie, table with labels | Cite a specific data point or label | M24 Ref 2 (funding pie chart with named brands & %) |
| Interactive widget | Click-through with tabs / drag-drop / hover-reveal | Cite a specific tab or revealed state and its content | M24 Refs 2, 4, 5, 6, 7 |

| Perspectives list | Paragraphs from named stakeholders | Cite the named role + viewpoint | N16 Q2 (marine biologist, fisher, consumer, environmentalist, policy maker) |

| Idiom / cultural artefact | Country-tagged short phrases | Cite the country and the phrase | M24 Ref 4 (UK / Russia / China idioms) |
| Concept grid | Two-column reference table | Name the concept used, drawn from this reference | M24 Ref 8 (12 maths + 12 L&L concepts) |

### Three levels of source linkage

The verb ladder for source use across sessions is consistent:

1. **No reference** — caps the answer at the lowest band. N16 Q3a 1–3 band rubric: *"Contains no examples from the sources provided"*. M24 Q2 1–3 band: *"refers to the pre-release material"* (a mention only).
2. **Implicit reference** — accepted on some questions but not the top band. N16 Q3a notes: *"Responses that make implicit references to the sources are acceptable as examples"* (mid-band only).
3. **Explicit / direct linkage** — required for top band on every extended response. M19 Q6 10–12 band: *"explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material"*. N20 Q7 7–8 band: *"explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material"*. M22 / M23 / M24 use *"uses extensive information from the pre-release material"* at top band.

The **gradient** in M24 is the cleanest illustration of how examiners read source use:

| Band | Source-use verb (M24) |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | refers to the pre-release material |
| 4–6 | attempts to use appropriate information |
| 7–9 | uses appropriate information |
| 10–12 | uses **extensive** information from the pre-release material |

"Extensive" in M24 means **multiple references woven through the response**, not just one source quoted repeatedly.

### Worked example: N16 Q2b source-count ladder

The 12-mark "Analyse the impact of fishing on the sustainability of marine life" question maps the source-count gate cleanly onto a SWOT structure:

| Band | Source rule | SWOT coverage |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | "No examples or quotations are used" | 1 of strengths / weaknesses / opportunities / threats |
| 3–5 | "Using at least one example/quotation" | 2 of the 4 SWOT axes |
| 6–8 | "Using two examples/quotations" | 3 of the 4 SWOT axes |
| 9–12 | "Using at least three examples/quotations" | All 4 SWOT axes |

A candidate who covers all four SWOT axes brilliantly but quotes only one source caps at 5/12. A candidate who quotes three sources but only addresses two SWOT axes also caps. The two ladders multiply — both are required for top band.

### Source-count gates

Several rubrics ladder the band by raw count of references / quotations used:

| Session / Q | Source-count rule |
|---|---|
| N16 Q2b | 1 / 2 / >=3 examples or quotations across bands |
| N16 Q3a | Band 1–3 = no source; bands 2–4 require source examples |
| M17 Q1c | 0 / >=1 / >=2 / >=3 sources, with outside material at top |
| M17 Q3b | 0 / 1 / >=2 / >=3 evidence pieces |
| N20 Q2b | states (no link) / outlines using **one** ref / describes using **two** refs / describes using **three or more** refs |
| N21 Q2b | >=1 / >=2 / >=3 pieces of evidence per band |

### Hard zero rules

Some questions auto-zero off-topic responses regardless of evidence quality:

- **M19 Q5a** — hard zero if response doesn't address one of the six "reference six" legal-support areas (lawyer, interpreter, accommodation, mediation, food, water). Canvas-only or text-only responses capped at 3.
- **M21 Q4** — *"If only one speaker is discussed, then 0 should be awarded"*; cap at 6 if no examples.
- **M22 Q1a** — wrong stakeholder card / wrong dropdown earns 0 for that mark.
- **M23 Q1** — *"If a response only compares the cars [instead of how the brochures synthesise maths + science], then students score 0 for the first two strands"*.
- **M23 Q2** — *"Do not credit any scientific information that is not from the examination or PRM"*.
- **M24 Q1, Q2, Q3** — discipline parity required; off-topic content not credited.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Quoting verbatim with the reference number cited.** N16 Q2b's 9–12 band requires *"at least three examples / quotations"*. M24 Q2's 10–12 band rewards quoting actual figures (the US$236.4 million from Ref 1, the ~126 000 stories / ~3 million people / 95–98% from Ref 5).
- **Multiple references used.** Band climbs explicitly with count: N16 Q2b ladder 1 → 2 → >=3; N20 Q2b ladder zero → one → two → three or more; M17 Q1c ladder 0 → >=1 → >=2 → >=3 + outside material.
- **Explicit linkage** to the question's demands. M19 Q6 and N20 Q7 both reserve the top band for *"explicitly linked"* evidence on both sides of the argument. Generic source-mention without a then-clause does not score top.
- **Mixing reference types.** M24 Q2 and Q3 require the candidate to weave the GDI article (Ref 1/6), the funding chart (Ref 2), the digital-footprint script (Ref 3), the idioms map (Ref 4) and the MIT study (Ref 5) into one synthesis. A response that quotes three from the same reference does not reach "extensive".
- **Naming the concept the source supports.** M24 introduced an explicit "concepts must be named" gate at top band — students must name *Quantity / Models / Patterns / Representation / Validity* from maths or *Audience imperatives / Purpose / Style / Point of view* from L&L. The reference is the evidence, the concept is the lens, and both must be present.
- **Citing the PRM rather than the persona.** M23 Q3: naming the delivery driver alone does not earn the PRM strand — the candidate must cite the actual PRM content about the driver.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Generic statements with no source anchor.** N16 Q3a band 1–3 is explicitly reserved for responses *"contains no examples from the sources provided"*. The top of the bottom band is the ceiling for unevidenced answers, no matter how articulate.
- **Citing the source but not using it.** Naming "Reference 5" without quoting or describing its content is treated as "refers to" (M24 1–3 band) rather than "uses".
- **Misattribution.** A wrong stakeholder card in M22 Q1a earns 0 for that mark — examiners do not give benefit of the doubt where the reference structure is itself part of the question.
- **Ignoring named sources the question explicitly asks for.** M19 Q5a hard-zeros responses outside the six "reference six" legal-support areas. M22 Q1b / Q3 / Q5 / Q6 require an example from Reference 1 *at every band* — without it, the response cannot enter even the bottom band on that strand.
- **One-sided source use.** N16 Q1b: *"To achieve more than 4 marks both strengths and limitations need to be included"*. M21 Q6: *"If a candidate only discusses one side, the maximum allowable score is 6 marks"*. M24 Q4: positive + negative consequences both required; one-sided capped at middle band.
- **Off-task source use.** M23 Q1 hard-zeros responses that compare the cars themselves instead of how the brochures synthesise maths + science. The source is the brochure, not the car.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Naming a reference but not quoting from it ("Reference 5 talks about fake news") | Quote a specific phrase or figure: *"Reference 5 reports that ~126 000 stories were spread by ~3 million people, showing the scale of the false-news problem"* |
| Using only one reference even on a 12-mark synthesis question | At top band, examiners expect **multiple references woven together**. Plan to cite at least 3 different references on any 10+ mark question |
| Quoting without attribution ("the article says…") | Always cite the reference number: *"Reference 1 states '…'"*. Bare quotation reads as memorised content, not source use |
| Citing the persona / case study instead of the PRM content (M23 Q3 trap) | The persona is the *vehicle*; cite the actual PRM detail attached to that persona |
| Generic limitations of Source C ("it might be biased") | Anchor the limitation in the source's *own evidence* — sampling, recency, coverage. M19 Q1c top band requires evidence from the source itself |
| One-sided evidence (only strengths, only "for" arguments) | Both-sides gate is universal: strengths + limitations, for + against, similarities + differences. Single-sided responses cap mid-band |
| Listing concepts without driving them ("uses Patterns and Quantity") | M24 top-band gate: concepts must be *used*, not listed. Show the concept doing work in the explanation |
| Ignoring the format gate (canvas-only, text-only, role-play) | M19 Q5a: canvas-only or text-only capped at 3. M21 Q5a: role-play frame must be sustained. Read the format requirement before writing |
| Using only PRM, no outside material when asked | M17 Q1c top band requires PRM **plus** outside-source evidence. Read the rubric — sometimes PRM alone caps the answer |
| Stating a conclusion instead of justifying one | M19 Q6, N20 Q1b/Q7, N21 Q7 all reserve top band for *justified* (not stated) conclusions. The evidence must support the conclusion explicitly |
| Implicit reference where explicit is required | Implicit references are accepted in N16 Q3a mid bands but **never at top band**. From band 8+ on most extended questions, the linkage must be visibly stated |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Drill the references in your PRM until you can cite by number cold.** For M24 that is 8 references; for older sessions usually 4–7. Build a one-line summary card per reference (title, type, key fact, key quote, concept it supports). On exam day you should not be searching the tray — you should know which reference answers which question.
2. **Practise the "verbatim quote + interpretation" sentence pattern.** The fastest band-jump is replacing *"Reference 5 talks about false news"* with *"Reference 5 reports that ~126 000 stories were spread by ~3 million people, which demonstrates Quantity at a scale that traditional verification cannot match."* Same content, two extra moves: quote and concept-name.
3. **Memorise key statistics from each reference.** Examiners reward numerical evidence. M24 winners: US$236.4 million (Ref 1), 95–98% fact-checker agreement (Ref 5), ~126 000 stories / ~3 million people / ~4.5 million reach (Ref 5), the seven-category share counts. Without a number, maths content reads as "stated" not "explained".
4. **Build a reference-to-concept map** for your session. M24 example: Ref 5 → Quantity, Models, Patterns, Validity. Ref 4 → Style, Point of view, Audience imperatives, Intertextuality. Ref 2 → Representation, Quantity. The map turns a vague "use the references" prompt into a deterministic sentence-builder.
5. **Practise three-reference paragraphs.** A typical top-band paragraph weaves three references: a *figure* from one (Quantity), a *quote* from another (Style or Purpose), and a *pattern* from a third (Models or Patterns). Drill writing 150-word paragraphs that cite three references explicitly.
6. **Rehearse hard-zero and format gates.** Before writing, scan the question for: required PRM areas (M19 Q5a six legal areas; M22 Q1a stakeholder card), required format (canvas + text box; script form; article point of view), required both-sides coverage. Missing a gate caps the entire question regardless of evidence quality.

## 6. Citation templates

Three sentence frames the candidate can adapt to any reference type. Each names the reference, uses the source content, and links to the question's demand.

**Direct quote:**
> *"Reference X states that '[verbatim phrase]', which shows that [interpretation tied to the question's command term]."*
>
> Example (M24 Q1): *"Reference 1 states that 'an estimated US$236.4 million per year flows to disinformation outlets via online advertising', which shows how the related concept of Quantity in mathematics gives advertisers and platforms a measurable incentive to monetize disinformation."*

**Statistic:**
> *"Reference X reports [number / percentage / range], which means [interpretation tied to the question's command term and a named concept]."*
>
> Example (M24 Q2): *"Reference 5 reports that the top 1% of false news reached between 1 000 and 100 000 people while the truth rarely reached more than 1 000, which means the related concept of Patterns identifies an asymmetric reach that funding alone cannot explain — language and framing in Reference 4 (idioms) shape what travels."*

**Perspective:**
> *"From the [stakeholder / role] perspective in Reference X, we see [viewpoint], which links to [question demand] because [reasoning]."*
>
> Example (N16 Q2b): *"From the marine-biologist perspective in Source 3, we see that scientific data must drive policy, which links to sustainability of marine life because without this evidence base policy makers cannot regulate fishing effort. The fisher perspective in Source 5, by contrast, foregrounds livelihood, surfacing a strength-vs-weakness tension in the SWOT analysis."*

Each template ends with a *because* / *which means* / *which shows* clause — that clause is the *explicit linkage* examiners reward at the top band. Drop the clause and the response slides to the mid band.

### Building paragraphs from these templates

The strongest top-band paragraphs **chain** the templates rather than using one in isolation. A typical 150-word top-band paragraph for M24 Q2 looks like this:

> *"Funding drives the spread of disinformation through the related concept of Quantity. Reference 1 states that an estimated 'US$236.4 million per year' flows to disinformation outlets via online advertising, which shows the financial scale of the ecosystem. Reference 2 visualises this through Representation: the pie chart maps named brands (Boots, Nike, Coca-Cola) onto disinformation URLs, making advertiser complicity legible. Technology amplifies this through the digital-footprint framing in Reference 3 — 'You've got to own your footprint because it's with you for life' — which through the L&L concept of Audience imperatives positions every user as a potential target. Language compounds the effect: from the Russian idiom in Reference 4, 'hang noodles on someone's ears', we see Style and Point of view normalising deception across cultures."*

That paragraph cites four references (1, 2, 3, 4), names four concepts (Quantity, Representation, Audience imperatives, Style/Point of view), uses one direct quote and one statistic, and links each move to the question's three contributory factors (funding / technology / language). It is the explicit shape of a 10–12 band response.
