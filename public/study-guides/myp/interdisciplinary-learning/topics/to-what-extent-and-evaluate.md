# "To What Extent" and "Evaluate" — the high-mark template

The most demanding cluster on every IDL paper. These verbs gate the upper bands across N16, M17, M19, N19, N20, M21 and N21 — a single template, with predictable rubric language, governs them all. Master it once and you have a reusable scaffold for the highest-leverage questions on the paper.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### What "to what extent" demands

This is the most demanding command on IDL papers. The MS definition is identical across every glossary that prints it (M17, M19, N19, N20, M21, N21):

> *"Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument or concept. Opinions and conclusions should be presented clearly and supported with appropriate evidence and sound argument."*

It requires the candidate to **take a position** and **justify it** — not merely list pros and cons. Top-band rubrics gate on three things together: (a) coverage of two opposing sides, (b) source-based evidence explicitly linked to each side, (c) a justified / evaluative conclusion. Drop any one and the band drops.

The "off-task" warning attached to M21 Q6 is decisive: *"Responses should relate to whether it should be a right, and not about the pros and cons of it being a right."* — i.e. the question demands a *verdict on the spectrum*, not a balanced report on advantages and disadvantages.

### What "evaluate" demands

The MS definition is identical across M17, N20, M21, N21:

> *"Make an appraisal by weighing up the strengths and limitations."*

Same shape as TWE but the framing is a thing (a method, a perspective, a synthesis, a claim) rather than a proposition. The output is the same: both sides + evidence + an evaluative conclusion.

### The TWO-step structure (both verbs)

1. **Both sides covered** — strengths/limitations, benefits/weaknesses, sustainable/not-sustainable, appropriate/not-appropriate, can/cannot, is/is-not.
2. **Justified or evaluative conclusion** — a *stance*, not a fence-sit, supported by the evidence weighed in step 1.

If a response collapses to one side, or arrives at a conclusion that doesn't follow from its body, the rubric punishes it heavily — see caps below.

### The "both-sides cap" rule

Many sessions cap one-sided answers below the top band. Specific instances:

- **M21 Q6** (healthcare as a right, 12 marks) — explicit examiner instruction: *"If a candidate only discusses one side, the maximum allowable score is (6 marks)."*
- **M19 Q6** (truth from one source, 12 marks) — top band 10–12 requires *both* "appropriate" and "not appropriate" sides covered with explicitly linked evidence; missing either drops the response to the 7–9 band at best.
- **N20 Q1b** (mathematical models, 8 marks) — top band 7–8 requires *both* "can" and "cannot" cases explained with evidence from data table AND graph.
- **N20 Q7** (Olympic legacy, 8 marks) — top band 7–8 requires *both* "sustainable" and "not sustainable" cases with explicitly linked evidence from PRM.
- **N16 Q4b** (video synthesis, 16 marks) — top band 13–16 requires benefits AND limitations of the synthesis (one-sided answers can't enter the band).
- **N21 Q2b** (Evaluate the cyclone poster, 8 marks) — *"Max 2 marks if only strengths OR only limitations."*

### The conclusion ladder

A common rubric ladder runs through every TWE question on the 12-mark scale. The cleanest verbatim version is M19 Q6:

| Band | Conclusion descriptor (M19 Q6 verbatim) |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | "**does not provide a conclusion**" |
| 4–6 | "**attempts a conclusion**" |
| 7–9 | "**provides a conclusion**" |
| 10–12 | "**provides a justified conclusion**" |

N20 Q1b and Q7 use the same 8-mark version: *no conclusion → attempts a conclusion → conclusion → justified conclusion*. M21 Q6 substitutes "evaluative conclusion" for the top-band wording.

The discriminator between the top two bands is therefore not the *presence* of a conclusion but its quality — does it *justify* / *evaluate*, or merely state.

### Evidence-linkage gradient (TWE / Evaluate specific)

| Band | Evidence phrasing in MS |
|---|---|
| Lowest | "**does not provide evidence**" (N20 Q1b) |
| Lower-mid | "**provides a general piece of evidence**" (N20 Q1b) / "attempts evidence" |
| Upper-mid | "**uses evidence**" / "uses evidence from the data table OR graph" (N20 Q1b) |
| Top | "**explicitly linked evidence**" (M19 Q6, N20 Q1b, N20 Q7) — both sides, both sources |

"Explicitly linked" is the single most-repeated top-band phrase across these questions. It means: the evidence sentence physically attaches to the claim sentence — not "Reference 4 says X" alone, but "Reference 4 says X, *which shows that* the position [supports / contradicts] my claim."

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Explicit acknowledgment of both sides** — using clear signposts ("On the one hand... on the other hand...", "Conversely...", "However..."). Implicit balance is read down.
- **Source / PRM evidence on both sides** — not just one. M19 Q6 and N20 Q1b/Q7 both reserve the top band for *"explicitly linked evidence ... on both sides"*.
- **A stance taken in the conclusion** — wording like "On balance, X tips the case because..." or "Taken together, the evidence shows that, although Y, [position] holds." This is the "justified / evaluative" conclusion the rubric demands.
- **Evaluative connectives throughout the body** — *however, on the other hand, nevertheless, despite, although, conversely, whereas, while, by contrast, in spite of, even so, granted that, on balance.* These are the textual markers examiners look for to award "evaluates" rather than "describes".
- **Subject-concept naming** when the question is interdisciplinary — *Patterns*, *Models*, *Quantity*, *Audience imperatives*, *Style*, *Point of view*, *Systems*, *Global interactions*. M22 / M24 explicitly require these in the top band.
- **Quantified evidence** where available — specific values, dates, percentages, named references — not generic gestures ("the data shows..." with no numbers).

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **One-sided answers** — capped at the lower band or below half-marks (M21 Q6 max 6/12; N21 Q2b max 2/8).
- **Fence-sitting** ("both have merits, it depends...") with no judgement — caps at the "provides a conclusion" band, not "justified". The rubric specifically rewards a *verdict*.
- **Conclusion that contradicts the body** — read down within the band even if both sides are otherwise well covered. The conclusion must follow from the weighing.
- **"To what extent" treated as "describe"** — listing without weighing. Top band requires *evaluation* (M19 Q6, N20 Q1b explicit verb climb: *state → outline → describe → explain*; the band-shift to *explain with explicit linkage* is what unlocks the top band).
- **Off-task framing** — M21 Q6 explicit: discussing pros/cons of healthcare as a right is off-task. The question asks whether it *should be* a right, not what's good or bad about it being one.
- **General rather than explicit evidence** — N20 Q1b discriminator between bands 3–4 ("general piece of evidence") and 7–8 ("explicitly linked"). Generic "the data supports this" is a 3–4 phrasing.
- **Conclusion absent at low bands** — 1–3 / 1–2 bands are explicitly reserved for responses with no conclusion (M19 Q6, N20 Q1b, N20 Q7).
- **Single-discipline framing** when the TWE is interdisciplinary — N16 Q4b cap mid-band; recurring across synthesis-style TWEs.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Listing pros and cons without weighing them | Add an explicit weighing paragraph: "While both arguments have merit, [side X] tips the balance because [reason linked to evidence]." |
| Conclusion = restatement of question | Conclusion = *verdict* + *justification*. Use "On balance, therefore...", "The weight of evidence shows...", "To a [large / limited / partial] extent..." |
| One side covered in detail, the other in a single sentence | Match the depth across both sides. Both must be "explained" with "explicitly linked evidence" for the top band. |
| Generic evidence ("the source supports this") | Cite the specific Reference / data point / quotation: "Reference 3 reports that 76% of...". The MS rewards "explicit linkage", not gestures. |
| TWE treated as Discuss | Discuss is balanced review; TWE demands a committed verdict on the spectrum (fully / partly / not at all / to a limited extent). Use spectrum language. |
| Pros/cons of the proposition instead of whether it should hold (M21 Q6 trap) | Re-read the stem. "TWE *should* X" = verdict question; "TWE *can* X" = capacity question. Neither is "what are the advantages and disadvantages of X". |
| No evaluative connectives in the body | Salt the body with: however, nevertheless, although, despite, on the other hand, conversely, by contrast, while. These signal evaluation to the examiner. |
| Conclusion that contradicts the body | Read your body before drafting the conclusion. The verdict must follow from where the weighing landed. |
| "Both have merits" as the conclusion | Not a stance. Specify which weighs more, and why — "Although [Y] is a meaningful counterweight, [X] is more decisive because [reason]." |
| Single-discipline framing on interdisciplinary TWE | Both disciplines must do real explanatory work on both sides. Name them (e.g. mathematical models AND historical context) and use both in the weighing. |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Practise the 5-paragraph TWE template** (intro framing the spectrum → side A with evidence → side B with evidence → evaluative weighing → justified conclusion). Drill on M19 Q6, N20 Q1b, N20 Q7, M21 Q6 first — they share an identical structure with different content.
2. **Memorise 10 evaluative connectives** and use at least 4 in any TWE answer: *however, on the other hand, nevertheless, despite, although, conversely, whereas, while, by contrast, on balance.*
3. **Memorise the conclusion ladder verbatim** — *no conclusion → attempts a conclusion → provides a conclusion → provides a justified / evaluative conclusion.* Diagnose your own conclusion against this ladder before submitting.
4. **Practise the "explicit linkage" sentence** — every evidence citation should be followed by "...which shows that [claim]" or "...which means [claim]" or "...this supports the position that [claim]." Make this a reflex.
5. **Drill the spectrum verdict** — close every TWE answer with one of: *to a large extent, to a limited extent, only partly, almost entirely, not at all.* Pair with a justification clause.
6. **Build a both-sides checklist** — before drafting, write the two sides as parallel headings ("Yes because..." / "No because...") and force at least two PRM references under each. If one side has no evidence, you'll cap below 7–9.
7. **Mark your own past TWE answers against the 4-band rubric** for M19 Q6 / N20 Q1b. The verb climb (states → outlines → describes → explains-with-linkage) is the band's spine — diagnose which verb your own writing is performing.
8. **Eliminate the "discuss" reflex** — TWE / Evaluate require *verdict*. If your answer reads as a balanced report with no judgement, rewrite the conclusion.
9. **Pre-load 3 PRM facts per side** for the May 2026 release once it lands. The fastest route through a TWE is having pre-paired evidence ready to deploy on either side of the likely framings.
10. **Match the response length to the marks** — 8-mark TWE/Evaluate ≈ 4–5 short paragraphs; 12-mark ≈ 5–6 paragraphs with two PRM references per side. Disproportionate length wastes time and rarely lifts the band.

## 6. Sentence frames

Ready-to-adapt frames keyed to the 5-paragraph structure.

### Opening (frame the spectrum)

- *"This question hinges on whether [X] outweighs [Y] when [context / condition]."*
- *"The extent to which [proposition] holds depends on the weight given to [factor A] versus [factor B]."*
- *"Two opposing positions are defensible, but they are not equally well supported by the pre-release material."*

### Side A — supporting the proposition

- *"On the one hand, Reference [N] shows that [evidence], **which means** [position] is supported because [mechanism]."*
- *"There is a clear case that [proposition] holds: as Reference [N] reports, [data], a finding that directly underpins [position]."*

### Side B — against the proposition

- *"However, Reference [N] suggests the opposite: [counter-evidence], **which undermines** the case that [proposition] because [mechanism]."*
- *"By contrast, [Reference / data] indicates that [counter-claim] — a meaningful caveat to the supportive case above."*

### Weighing

- *"While both arguments have merit, the [scale of evidence / disciplinary weight / quantitative force] of [side X] tips the balance because [reason]."*
- *"Although [side Y] cannot be dismissed, its support rests on [narrower / weaker / single-source] evidence, whereas [side X] draws on [multiple / quantitative / cross-disciplinary] evidence."*
- *"The decisive consideration is [factor]: where this is present, [position] holds; where it is absent, [counter-position] gains traction."*

### Justified conclusion

- *"On balance, therefore, [proposition holds / does not hold] **to a [large / limited / partial] extent**, although [opposing side] remains a meaningful caveat in [specific scenario]."*
- *"Taken together, the evidence shows that [verdict] — not because [side Y] has no force, but because [side X] is more directly supported by [PRM evidence] and [disciplinary reasoning]."*
- *"The strongest reading of the pre-release material is that [verdict]; this conclusion is provisional only insofar as [named limitation] could shift the weighing."*

## 7. Five-paragraph TWE template

A reusable skeleton candidates can adapt to any TWE / Evaluate question on a 10–12 mark scale. For 8-mark questions, compress paragraphs 2 and 3 into shorter blocks.

**¶1 — Spectrum framing (2–3 sentences)**
> *"The question of [proposition] sits on a spectrum: it can hold fully, partly, or not at all depending on [named condition]. This response argues that [provisional verdict — large / limited / partial extent], a position established by weighing [side A] against [side B] using evidence from the pre-release material."*

**¶2 — Side A: the supportive case (1 paragraph)**
> *"There is a clear case that [proposition] holds. Reference [N] reports that [specific evidence], which means [mechanism]. Furthermore, Reference [M] adds that [second specific evidence], reinforcing the position because [second mechanism]. From a disciplinary perspective, [discipline] frames this as [named concept — e.g. Patterns, Models, Audience imperatives], strengthening the case."*

**¶3 — Side B: the limiting case (1 paragraph)**
> *"However, the opposing case is also supported by the PRM. Reference [P] shows that [counter-evidence], which directly undermines [side A] because [counter-mechanism]. In addition, [discipline / second reference] indicates [second counter-claim] — a position that holds when [condition]. Taken alone, this side is significant because [reason]."*

**¶4 — Evaluative weighing (1 paragraph)**
> *"Weighing the two sides, [side X] is the more decisive case. This is because [reason 1: scale or specificity of evidence], [reason 2: disciplinary support], and [reason 3: PRM coverage]. Although [side Y] cannot be dismissed, its support rests on [narrower / single-reference / qualitative-only] evidence, whereas [side X] is supported by [quantitative / multi-reference / cross-disciplinary] evidence. The decisive factor is [named factor]."*

**¶5 — Justified conclusion (2–3 sentences)**
> *"On balance, therefore, [proposition] holds **to a [large / limited / partial / very limited] extent**. The pre-release material weighs more strongly on the side of [verdict] because [one-sentence summary of weighing]. The opposing case retains force in [named scenario], which qualifies but does not overturn the verdict."*

**Self-check before submitting.** Run the response against this checklist:

- [ ] Both sides present, with at least one PRM reference each
- [ ] Evaluative connectives appear in body (however / although / nevertheless / on the other hand)
- [ ] Evidence is *explicitly linked* to claims ("...which means...", "...this supports...")
- [ ] Conclusion takes a *position* on the spectrum, not "both have merits"
- [ ] Conclusion follows from the weighing in ¶4
- [ ] No off-task drift (verdict question vs pros/cons report)
- [ ] Both disciplines named and doing work, if interdisciplinary

If every box is ticked, the response is operating at the top-band verb level (*explains with explicitly linked evidence + justified / evaluative conclusion*) — the precise phrasing the MS reserves for 10–12 / 7–8.
