## 1. Theme summary

> **Identity — nature vs. nurture**, with sub-strands on **diet/health** and **somatotyping (body composition for sport)**.

The PRM opens by asking *"who am I? Who are we?"* (Reference 1) and answers with the classical
psychology framework: **nature** (genes you inherit, e.g. eye colour, potential height) versus
**nurture** (environment — relationships, schools, culture, food, friends). The video resolves the
"either/or" question with a both/and: *"They work together to make you, you. Your genes might give
you a starting point, but your environment helps shape how these traits develop."*

The remaining seven references then stress-test that thesis from four angles:

| Angle | References |
|---|---|
| Names & life paths (a sociological identity claim) | Ref 2 — nominative determinism |
| Genes alone (a controlled comparison) | Ref 3 — twin-study heritability data; Ref 4 — twins separated at birth |
| Environment alone (genes held constant) | Ref 5 — UPF graphs; Ref 6 — Aimee Kingston / Twins UK / ZOE Predict article |
| Body, sport & training (gene-environment interaction made visible) | Ref 7 — somatotypes; Ref 8 — aspiring-athlete profiles |

**Core inquiry questions you should expect questions to revolve around:**

1. To what extent is identity (or a specific trait — health, athletic ability, name-driven success)
   shaped by **nature** vs **nurture**?
2. How can **two disciplines** — typically a science (Sciences / PHE) plus a humanities or
   language-based discipline (Lang & Lit / I&S) — together explain something that neither could
   explain alone?
3. How do quantitative tools (heritability percentages, NOVA energy-intake graphs, somatotype
   ratings) and qualitative tools (autobiography, video script, interactive profiles) **synthesise**
   to give a fuller picture of identity?

## 3. Disciplines likely to be paired

> **This is inference**, not confirmed by the IB. The related-concepts grid (typically a final
> reference) is the explicit confirmation. If it is in image-only form for M26, you must look at
> Ref 8 (interactive) and any image references yourself before sitting.

### 3.1 Strongest inference: Sciences + Language & Literature

Evidence:

- **Sciences** — the PRM is saturated with biological / physiological content: DNA and inheritance
  (Ref 1), heritability data (Ref 3), nutrition + metabolism + blood markers (Refs 5, 6),
  anatomy / body composition + biomechanics (Ref 7), athletic profile / training science (Ref 8).
- **Language & Literature** — the PRM is **explicitly text-rich**: a video script asking *"who am
  I?"* (Ref 1), an etymology-driven video about names and Latin roots (Ref 2), a narrative blog
  about twins (Ref 4), a 1 200-word Newsweek autobiographical article with explicit point of view
  (Ref 6), and an interactive widget of athlete profiles (Ref 8 — profile is a recognisable Lang &
  Lit genre).

This pairing matches the **most recent precedent (M24 — Maths + Lang & Lit)** in shape: both a
quantitative discipline and a textual discipline are clearly co-present.

### 3.2 Plausible alternative: Sciences + Mathematics

Evidence:

- The 50-year **meta-analysis** of twin studies (Ref 3) is a flagship maths-of-statistics object:
  combining data, percentages, validity of pooled conclusions.
- The three NOVA graphs (Ref 5) are explicit data-representation objects with named sample sizes
  (n = 13 608; n = 20 080).
- Somatotyping (Ref 7) uses a **three-digit numerical rating on a 1–7 scale** — a quantitative
  identity descriptor.

This matches **M23 (Maths + Sciences)** in shape — and that was also a 3 × 12 = 36-mark paper.

### 3.3 Less likely but possible: Sciences + I&S

Evidence: identity is an I&S construct; nominative determinism (Ref 2) and the autobiographical
article (Ref 6) both raise sociological / cultural framings of selfhood. Only Lang & Lit has been
in 4 of the last 5 sessions, so I&S is the *historic* default but is being displaced.

### 3.4 Triple-discipline (rare): Sciences + Maths + Lang & Lit

The PRM has enough material to support this, and N20 + M21 have used three disciplines before.
Treat as a low-probability but possible scenario; if the question forces three disciplines,
Sciences-as-anchor + Maths-on-graphs + L&L-on-articles is the natural split.

> **Bottom line**: prepare hardest for **Sciences + L&L**, but go in fluent in the maths of Refs 3
> and 5 and the somatotyping numbers of Ref 7. If a Maths question lands, it lands on these three.

## 4. Likely question shapes (drawn from past-paper patterns)

Recent IDL papers (M23, M24) are **3–4 questions, 36–42 marks**, almost entirely banded
extended-response. Use that as your format anchor.

### 4.1 Q1 — 12-mark "Discuss" / "Compare and contrast" — synthesis across two disciplines

Sample stems applied to identity:

- *"**Discuss** how Sciences and Language & Literature can be used to explore the influence of
  nature and nurture on identity."*
- *"**Discuss** how the synthesis of Sciences and Language & Literature helps us understand the
  impact of ultra-processed foods on twins with shared genetics."*
- *"**Compare and contrast** how Reference 3 (twin-study data) and Reference 6 (Newsweek twin
  article) use Sciences and Language & Literature to argue about identity."*

What top mark band requires (10–12, from M24 Q1 rubric pattern):

- Both disciplines named *and concepts named explicitly* (e.g. *Patterns*, *Validity* in Sciences
  / Maths; *Audience imperatives*, *Point of view*, *Style*, *Purpose* in L&L).
- Three strands covered: (1) discipline A contribution; (2) discipline B contribution;
  (3) **synthesis** of the two.
- **Specific evidence** drawn from the PRM (heritability percentages from Ref 3; named
  participants / institutions from Ref 6; somatotype scale and sport mappings from Ref 7).

Common traps (M23 Q1, M24 Q1):

- Two-list structure (paragraph on Sciences, paragraph on L&L, no synthesis paragraph) — capped
  at the lower band for the synthesis strand.
- Discussing **the cars / the twins / the food** rather than how the **disciplines** treat them
  (auto-zero risk on the disciplinary strands — see M23 Q1 MS note).
- Naming concepts but not using them to drive the explanation — read down a band.

### 4.2 Q2 — 12-mark "Explain" / "Justify" — disciplinary reasoning anchored in PRM

Sample stems:

- *"**Explain** how genetics, environment, and lifestyle choices interact to shape an
  individual's identity, using evidence from the pre-release material."*
- *"**Justify** which has more influence on health outcomes — nature or nurture — using
  mathematical and scientific reasoning from the pre-release material."*
- *"**Explain** how a person's somatotype and their training environment together shape their
  potential as an athlete."*

Top-band requirements (M23 Q2, M24 Q2 pattern):

- **Both disciplines** must be present (the *and* gate, not the *or* gate).
- **Quantitative evidence quoted directly**: n = 13 608, n = 20 080 (Ref 5 sample sizes); the
  somatotype scale 1–7 (Ref 7); heritability percentages from Ref 3.
- A **counter-argument** addressed (when the stem includes "justify").
- **Explicit linkage** to the PRM — generic references ("the article shows…") capped; specific
  ones ("Aimee gained weight while Nancy lost weight in just 2 weeks despite identical
  calories…") rewarded.

Common traps:

- One-sided answers when both sides required (M23 Q2 cap).
- Outside knowledge not in the PRM (M23 Q2 MS: *"Do not credit any scientific information that
  is not from the examination or PRM"*).
- Mathematical *calculations* — explicitly **ignored** in M23 Q2; only **understanding of
  mathematical concepts** is credited.

### 4.3 Q3 — 12-mark "Create" / "Discuss" — applied or creative response

Sample stems:

- *"**Create** an article (or speech, or blog post, or monologue) expressing your point of view
  on whether identity is more shaped by genes or by environment, drawing on the pre-release
  material."*
- *"**Discuss** the challenges and solutions facing an aspiring athlete (Reference 8) whose
  somatotype does not match the somatoplot for their preferred sport."*
- *"**Create** a profile / fitness plan for an aspiring athlete in Reference 8 that synthesises
  their somatotype data with a personal narrative about their identity."*
- *"**Create** a magazine article responding to the statement *'You are what you eat'*, using
  Refs 5 and 6."*

Top-band requirements (M24 Q3 pattern):

- **Explicit point of view** — examiners will not credit an article that catalogues evidence
  without taking a side.
- **Both disciplines** must drive the point of view (one-sided in discipline → cap at 1–3).
- **Extensive** PRM use — multiple references woven together, not just one.
- Article / speech / monologue **structure is NOT credited** (M24 Q3 MS: *"the structure of the
  article is not relevant to awarding credit"*) — synthesis takes precedence over format.

Common traps:

- Over-investing in headline / byline / formatting at the expense of synthesis.
- A neutral "balanced essay" without a stated position — capped.
- Citing a single PRM reference repeatedly instead of weaving multiple together.

### 4.4 Q4 — 6-mark "Demonstrate" / "Reflect" — metacognition about interdisciplinary understanding

Sample stems:

- *"**Demonstrate** the new understanding you have gained in this examination in an informative
  speech for younger students about how to make healthier food choices."*
- *"**Reflect** on how studying nature and nurture in two disciplines has changed your view of
  identity. Describe positive and negative consequences of your new understanding."*
- *"**Demonstrate** how you would advise a younger student deciding whether to pursue a sport
  for which they are not the typical somatotype."*

Top-band requirements (M24 Q4 pattern):

- The understanding must be **interdisciplinary** — i.e. could not have come from either
  discipline alone.
- **Positive and negative consequences** both required — one-sided capped at middle band.
- **Younger-student audience** rewards adjusted *audience imperatives* / *style* (clarity,
  concrete examples, avoidance of jargon).
- **Extensive** PRM use at top band.

Common traps:

- Reflecting on the content (food, sport, twins) rather than on the *interdisciplinary
  understanding* itself.
- Listing only dangers / only benefits instead of both.
- Forgetting the audience.

### Reference 3 — Twin study summary: genetic similarities for specific human traits (image)

**What is in it (image — open `ref3.png` to confirm):**

- A meta-analysis of **50 years of twin studies**.
- Heritability percentages for various human traits (intelligence / personality / disease risk
  / etc.) — typically expressed as concordance rates between **monozygotic (MZ)** and
  **dizygotic (DZ)** twins, or as a heritability statistic.

**Glossary (verbatim):** *"Meta-analysis: a statistical process that combines data from multiple
studies to draw a single conclusion about a topic."*

**Key facts to memorise:**

- "**50-year time scale**" — emphasises the validity claim (large temporal sample).
- Twin studies separate **shared environment** (DZ + MZ both have it) from **shared genes** (MZ
  only) — the **statistical mechanism** for isolating heritability.
- Define the terms in your own words: **MZ = monozygotic (identical, ~100% shared DNA); DZ =
  dizygotic (fraternal, ~50% shared DNA on average like normal siblings)**.

**Concept hooks:**

| Discipline | Concept | Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Sciences | Genetics / heritability | the percentages themselves |
| Maths | Validity / Models / Approximation | meta-analysis combines multiple studies → one conclusion |
| Maths | Quantity / Representation | percentages, table format |
| Sciences | Variability of human traits | the table's *variation* across traits |

**Worked-example question stem this could power:**

> *"**Explain** how the meta-analysis in Reference 3 uses mathematical and scientific concepts
> to estimate the heritability of human traits."*

### Reference 7 — Somatotyping (images)

**Verbatim key extracts:**

- *"Somatotyping is a technique that **assesses the shape and composition of the human body**."*
- *"It is quantified using a **three-digit rating on a scale of 1 to 7**."*
- *"Identifying a person's somatotype is often used to **design training programmes and identify
  potential athletes**."*
- *"The following diagram shows a **somatoplot**. A somatoplot is a representation of somatotype
  characteristics and associated sports."*

**Background you must own (high school PHE / Sciences canon):**

The three components of a somatotype rating are:

| Component | Description | Stereotype sport |
|---|---|---|
| Endomorphy | softer, rounder, higher body fat | weightlifting (heavyweight), strongman |
| Mesomorphy | muscular, athletic, lower body fat | sprinting, boxing, gymnastics |
| Ectomorphy | slim, lean, lower muscle mass | distance running, high jump, basketball |

A rating like **2-7-1** = low endomorphy / very high mesomorphy / very low ectomorphy → likely
a power athlete. **Open `ref7.png` and `ref7table.png`** and write down the actual sample
ratings the IB has chosen and which sports they map to on the somatoplot.

**Key facts to memorise:**

- **Three-digit rating, scale 1–7** for each component.
- Three components: **endomorphy, mesomorphy, ectomorphy**.
- Used for: (a) **designing training programmes**, (b) **identifying potential athletes**.

## 7. Sample answer skeletons

> One paragraph-skeleton per likely command term. Each skeleton is generic — fill in the
> specific PRM evidence based on the actual question on the day.

### 7.1 "Discuss" — *Discuss how Sciences and Language & Literature can be used to explore the influence of nature and nurture on identity.*

> **Sciences** illuminates the *nature* side of identity through quantitative, controlled
> evidence. The 50-year **meta-analysis** of twin studies (Reference 3) uses the concept of
> **validity** to combine multiple studies into a single estimate of **heritability**, isolating
> the genetic contribution to traits by comparing **monozygotic** with **dizygotic** twins.
> Reference 6 extends this with a **controlled experiment** at n = 1 pair: identical twins Aimee
> and Nancy Kingston ate identical calories, nutrients, fat, sugar and fiber for two weeks,
> differing only in **NOVA group 4** (UPF) vs whole-food intake — yet Aimee gained weight and
> Nancy did not, isolating the *environmental / nurture* contribution.
>
> **Language & Literature** illuminates *nurture* and the lived experience of identity through
> **autobiography, point of view and genre**. Reference 6's first-person Newsweek article uses
> Aimee's voice (*"I felt really tired … friends and family commented on how exhausted I
> looked"*) and the **audience-imperative** of public-interest health journalism to make the
> metabolic harm felt — not just measured. Reference 2's nominative-determinism video uses
> **etymology** (*Victoria = winner*) and the **Loser/Winner** Chicago Tribune anecdote to
> argue that even something as cultural as a name can shape the life path.
>
> The **synthesis** of the two disciplines is what makes the argument complete. Reference 3
> tells us *that* genes matter; Reference 4 (twins separated at birth) and Reference 6 tell us
> *what it feels like and what it does to a body*. Without the L&L story we have only
> percentages; without the Sciences we have only anecdote. Identity, the PRM argues, must be
> understood through both lenses at once.

### 7.2 "Explain" — *Explain how nurture (environment) can change health outcomes even when nature is held constant.*

> Sciences provides the **mechanism**: the NOVA classification (Reference 5) groups foods by
> the *nature, extent and purpose of processing* and identifies UPFs (group 4 — chips, biscuits,
> burgers, instant soups, cereals) as the metabolically problematic category. The Statistics
> Canada Survey 2015 graphs (n = 13 608 and n = 20 080) **represent** the population-level
> intake patterns. Reference 6 then provides the controlled within-pair test: Aimee and Nancy,
> monozygotic twins (genes constant), consumed identical calories, nutrients, fat, sugar and
> fiber, varying only the *processing* of the food. After two weeks, Aimee had gained weight
> and her blood sugar and blood-fat levels had worsened, while Nancy was slimmer with better
> blood results — a clear causal arrow from nurture to phenotype.
>
> Language & Literature provides the **point of view** and **audience imperatives** that allow
> this finding to do public work: Aimee's autobiographical voice (*"I was hungry all the time
> … noticeably lower in mood"*) and the article's headline framing (*"the results were
> shocking"*) push the science into general readers' food choices.

### 7.3 "Create" — *Create an article expressing your point of view on whether identity is shaped more by genes or by environment.*

> [Take an explicit position in the opening line. Examiner note from M24 Q3: "the point of view
> needs to be explicit".]
>
> *"Genes load the gun; environment pulls the trigger."* That is my view, and the pre-release
> material proves it.
>
> Reference 3's 50-year **meta-analysis** of twin studies tells us that genes set a **starting
> point** — heritability is real and measurable. But Reference 6 — the Aimee Kingston Newsweek
> article — shows that a *2-week* change in nurture (UPF vs whole foods) was enough to push two
> genetically identical twins onto opposite metabolic trajectories. Reference 1 frames this
> using basketball: natural height is the gun, hours of practice and a good coach are the
> trigger.
>
> Even the cultural side of nurture — names — can bend a path. Reference 2 tells us that
> brothers Loser and Winner, raised in the same household, walked opposite roads. The
> *language* of identity (the audience imperatives of a name, the etymology of a word) acts
> on the same lever as food and training.
>
> [Conclude with an explicit point of view + how multiple references together support it.]

### 7.4 "Demonstrate" — *Demonstrate the new understanding you have gained, in an informative speech for younger students about how to make healthy food choices.*

> [Audience: younger students. Adjust style accordingly — concrete examples, no jargon.]
>
> Hi. The biggest thing I learned in this exam is that **what you eat changes your body even
> in two weeks** — and even if you and your twin eat the same number of calories. In Reference 6
> a writer called Aimee Kingston ate only ultra-processed foods (UPFs — things like chicken
> nuggets, biscuits, instant soups) while her twin sister Nancy ate whole foods. Two weeks
> later, Aimee was heavier with worse blood sugar; Nancy was slimmer with better blood results.
>
> What I didn't know before is that things marketed as "whole foods" — fruity yogurts, sliced
> brown bread, cereal bars — can be UPFs in disguise because they contain emulsifiers,
> preservatives and sweeteners. So the action is: **read the ingredients list**, not just the
> calories on the front.
>
> The positive consequence is feeling more energy, better mood and better concentration — Aimee
> said she felt tired and hungry all the time on UPFs. The negative consequence is that whole
> foods take more time to cook and aren't as convenient, and Aimee said she still craved UPFs
> at the end. So you cannot cut them out completely — and you don't need to. Just eat them
> **mindfully**, like she now does.

### 7.5 "Reflect" — *Reflect on how the interdisciplinary approach changed your understanding of identity.*

> Before this examination, I would have answered "who am I?" using one discipline at a time —
> Sciences would tell me my genes, Lang & Lit would tell me my story. The interdisciplinary
> approach forced me to put the two together and what I now see is that **identity sits in the
> gap between data and narrative**. Reference 3 tells me MZ twins share ~100 % of their DNA;
> Reference 6 tells me Aimee and Nancy still went in opposite directions in two weeks. Neither
> reference *alone* could have told me what I now know: that the size of the genetic starting
> point and the size of the environmental nudge are both real, and they only become legible
> when you read the percentages and the personal essay together.
>
> The positive consequence is that I make food and training decisions with more agency — I
> don't think "my genes decide". The negative consequence is that I now over-attribute every
> health outcome to "lifestyle choice" — which can become a moralising trap (Aimee herself
> said cutting UPFs out completely is not sustainable).

### 7.6 "Evaluate" / "To what extent" — *To what extent is identity shaped more by nurture than by nature?*

> [Both sides required. Conclusion must be evaluative.]
>
> **For nurture:** Reference 6's controlled twin experiment is the strongest single piece of
> evidence in the PRM — identical genes, identical macros, opposite blood markers in 2 weeks.
> Reference 5's NOVA graphs (n = 13 608 and n = 20 080) confirm that the population is exposed
> to a UPF environment regardless of genotype. Reference 2's Loser/Winner anecdote suggests
> even *names* (a cultural / nurture variable) bend a life path against the same family
> environment.
>
> **For nature:** Reference 3's 50-year meta-analysis tells us heritability is real and
> measurable for many traits. Reference 4 (twins separated at birth) shows shared traits
> emerging despite different environments. Reference 7 anchors body composition in components
> (endomorphy, mesomorphy, ectomorphy) that are at least partly genetically determined.
>
> **Evaluative conclusion:** Nature sets the *range* (Reference 3's percentages, Reference 7's
> somatotypes), but nurture sets the *position within the range* over short timescales (the
> Kingston twins' 2-week divergence in Reference 6). The PRM as a whole rejects the either/or
> framing — Reference 1 explicitly says *"they work together to make you, you"*. So *to a
> very large extent* identity is shaped by both, and the question of which matters more is
> trait-specific: heritability of intelligence is high, but heritability of next-week's blood
> sugar is low.

### 7.7 "Compare and contrast" — *Compare and contrast Reference 3 and Reference 6.*

> **Similarities:** Both references use **monozygotic twins** as the methodological anchor for
> isolating the genetic contribution to identity. Both make a **scientific** claim about
> nature/nurture. Both rely on a *quantitative* core — Reference 3 on heritability percentages
> from a 50-year meta-analysis, Reference 6 on weight and blood markers across a 2-week
> controlled feed.
>
> **Differences:** Reference 3 is a **meta-analysis** (Maths concept: validity, large temporal
> sample, n in the thousands at minimum); Reference 6 is an **n = 1 pair, single autobiography**
> (Lang & Lit concept: point of view, audience imperatives, narrative voice). Reference 3
> isolates **genes** by holding environment variable across twins; Reference 6 isolates
> **environment** by holding genes constant within a single twin pair. Reference 3 gives us
> *generalisable percentages*; Reference 6 gives us *one couple's lived experience*.
>
> **Evaluative conclusion:** the two references are complementary — together they cover both
> sides of the nature-nurture lever, which neither could do alone.
