# Reflection & Metacognition (the Q6/Q7 reflective journal)

> **Why this topic matters.** Nine of the ten public IDL sessions close with one or two metacognitive questions — N16 Q6/Q7, M17 Q7, M19 Q6/Q7, N19 Q6/Q7, N20 Q7, M21 Q7, N21 Q7, M22 Q6, M24 Q4. Together they typically carry 8–30 marks, and they have the most predictable rubric in the entire paper. If you memorise the four-bullet pattern, the verb ladder, and the "specifically directed to interdisciplinary understanding" gate, you can bank the highest band on a question whose form you have already seen.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### What "reflective journal" means in IDL

Most IDL papers end with one or two questions that ask the candidate to step back and reflect on the **development of their interdisciplinary understanding** — not the disciplines themselves, but how using both (or all three) together has changed their thinking. This is **metacognition**, not content recall. The student is being asked: *What did you not know before this exam that you know now? Which moment of synthesis caused the shift? How will it change the way you approach the next problem?*

Two structural cues tell you a question is reflective rather than synthetic:

1. The stem includes one of the verbs **Reflect / Demonstrate / Evaluate (your own work) / To what extent (have you developed your understanding)** — see N16 Q7, M22 Q6, M21 Q7, M24 Q4 (M24 uses *Demonstrate* but the rubric is unmistakably Criterion D reflection).
2. The bullet list inside the stem points at **the candidate's own development**, not at the source material's claims. Compare M19 Q6 ("To what extent can someone know the truth if they only receive their information from one source?") which is a synthesis question, to N16 Q7 ("Reflect on the **development of your interdisciplinary understanding**") which is a reflection question. The pronoun *your* is the giveaway.

### The four reflection bullets

The clearest example is N16 Q7. The four bullets are *verbatim*:

> - refer to at least one source provided in this examination
> - consider perspectives / arguments / evidence related to sustainability
> - refer to questions from the examination
> - refer to discussions from your classroom preparation

The same four-bullet skeleton recurs across sessions, with the second bullet tuned to the integrating context (sustainability in N16; freedom of speech / refugees in M19; legacy / nuclear power in later papers). M19 Q7 swaps the third bullet for a **service-as-action example** and keeps the rest. M24 Q4 rephrases the bullets as: things learned this exam, suggested actions, positive + negative consequences, references to pre-release material — but the underlying logic (one source / one perspective lens / one exam question / one prep memory) is the same.

### The coverage-band ladder

Bands are gated on **two axes that climb together**:

| Axis | Band 1–3 (lowest) | Band 4–7 | Band 8–11 | Band 12–14 (top) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Number of bullets addressed** | at least one | at least two | at least three | all four |
| **Depth of treatment** | limited | adequate | significant | thorough and nuanced |

This is the verbatim structure of N16 Q7. M22 Q6 mirrors it in a 12-mark frame; M24 Q4 compresses the same idea into a 6-mark / three-band rubric (state → describe → explain). One axis without the other does not cross the gate: addressing four bullets superficially still caps in the adequate band; addressing two bullets brilliantly still caps at "significant".

The practical implication is *coverage first, depth second*. A candidate who is short of time should always touch all four bullets — even briefly — before going back to deepen any single one. A response that hits 4 bullets at "adequate" depth scores band 4–7 (up to 7 / 14 on N16 Q7); a response that nails 2 bullets at "thorough" depth caps at "significant" — and the band ceiling for "significant" is 11 / 14, only if all three of the first three bullets are present. Two bullets at "thorough" is structurally a band 4–7 answer.

### The verb ladder inside the reflective rubric

The reflective rubric uses the same four-rung verb ladder that runs through every IDL banded question. In the reflective context the verbs are:

| Band | Verb in the rubric | What it looks like in your prose |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | states | "I learned that…" / "The source said…" |
| 4–6 | outlines / describes | "The source described…, which made me think about…" |
| 7–9 | describes / explains | "The source's claim about X led me to reconsider Y because…" |
| 10–12 / 13–16 | explains / justifies / evaluates | "Synthesising X with Y revealed Z, which I justify by reference to [named evidence]; this changes how I will approach W" |

Top band = top verb. Writing "I learned" anywhere in your reflection is a tell that you are still in band 1–3 register; writing "synthesising both lenses revealed" is the register the top band rewards.

### "Specifically directed to" rule

The rubric line that hard-gates the whole question, verbatim from N16 Q7:

> *"For top marks in this band, **all of the points** below must be addressed in a thorough and nuanced manner."*

…and earlier in the same descriptor:

> *"…specifically directed to **their development of interdisciplinary understanding**."*

Generic reflection on the exam content alone — "the sources were interesting, I learned a lot about sustainability" — is capped in the bottom band even if it ticks every bullet. The reflection has to be about *how using both disciplines together changed the candidate's own thinking*. The same logic surfaces in M22 Q6 ("strengths and weaknesses of **your own response** in Q3/Q4/Q5"), M21 Q7 (own perception, own example), and M24 Q4 ("the new understanding **you have developed**").

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Coverage of all four bullets** — the most predictive single factor. The 12–14 / 5–6 / 7–8 / 10–12 top bands of N16 Q7, M24 Q4, M19 Q7, M22 Q6 all require addressing every bullet in the stem.
- **Depth wording** — reflective rubrics deliberately use the chain *limited → adequate → significant → thorough and nuanced* (N16 Q7) or *states → outlines → describes → explains* (M24 Q4). Aim for the top-band wording in your own response: write "I came to see…" / "I now recognise…" / "synthesising both lenses revealed…" rather than "I learned that…".
- **Specific examples drawn from the candidate's own preparation** — M19 Q7 demands a *named* service-as-action example; M22 Q6 demands a named example from the candidate's own Q3/Q4/Q5 response; M24 Q4 demands named actions (delete, verify, source-check) plus their consequences.
- **Explicit linkage to *interdisciplinary* understanding** — N16 Q7 caps responses that reflect on a single discipline; M21 Q7 wants strengths + weaknesses of the candidate's *own perception* with both lenses; M24 Q4's reflection has to be the kind that "could not have been gained from either Maths or Lang & Lit alone".
- **Metacognitive language** — "this changed how I…", "before the examination I would have…", "looking back, the moment I synthesised both lenses was…", "I now approach X by…". Rubrics never say *use these phrases*, but they are the linguistic signature of the top band.
- **Justified / evaluative conclusion** — M19 Q6 ("provides a **justified** conclusion"), N21 Q7, M19 Q7, N19 Q6/Q7 all gate top band on conclusion quality.
- **Evidence explicitly linked to the PRM or to the examination experience** — M19 Q6 top band uses the verbatim phrase *"explicitly linked"*; N21 Q7 uses the same; M24 Q4 uses *"extensive information from the pre-release material"*.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Generic reflection on the disciplines individually.** N16 Q6 explicitly rewards interdisciplinary balance over single-discipline depth; the rubric distinguishes "benefits and limitations of science" / "of individuals and societies" / "of using both disciplines in an interdisciplinary manner" — and gates the top band on the third strand.
- **Reflection that ignores the four bullets.** Even rich, eloquent reflection that omits "classroom preparation" or "questions from the examination" is structurally capped — the bullet count is a hard gate.
- **Single-discipline reflection.** M21 Q7 caps single-side responses; M24 Q4 caps reflection that could have come from one subject alone. N16 Q3b's "max 5 marks if only one discipline mentioned" is the template.
- **"I learned a lot" with no specifics.** M19 Q7 explicitly demands a service-as-action example from band 3–4 up; M22 Q6 demands an example at every band including band 1–3. Generic personal reflection without a named anchor is read down.
- **Failing to refer to any source / question / classroom prep.** N16 Q7 band 1–3 is reserved for responses with a single bullet treated in a "limited manner"; zero bullets is zero marks.
- **One-sided reflections.** M24 Q4 caps single-sided answers (only positive consequences, or only negative) at the middle band; M21 Q7 needs both strengths and weaknesses; N20 Q7 needs sustainable and not-sustainable.
- **Reflection on the *content* rather than the *learning experience*.** "Sustainability is important" is content; "I now understand sustainability differently because the science source forced me to quantify what the I&S source described qualitatively" is reflection. The rubric phrase "specifically directed to their development of interdisciplinary understanding" exists to enforce this distinction.

### Rubric phrases worth memorising verbatim

These exact phrases recur across reflective rubrics and signal what the examiner is scanning for:

- *"specifically directed to their development of interdisciplinary understanding"* — N16 Q7. Echo this phrase in your own conclusion: "this development was specifically directed by…"
- *"thorough and nuanced reflection"* — N16 Q7 top band. Use *nuanced* once in your final paragraph.
- *"explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material"* — M19 Q6, N21 Q7 top bands. Cite a source by number and quote a fragment of it.
- *"justified conclusion"* / *"evaluative conclusion"* — M19 Q6/Q7, N19 Q6/Q7, N21 Q7, N20 Q7. Conclusions that merely restate are read down a band.
- *"extensive information from the pre-release material"* — M24 Q4 top band. *Extensive* = multiple references woven through the response, not just one cited.
- *"significant reflection"* — N16 Q7 band 8–11. Use *significant* explicitly in your prose to signal this register.
- *"positive and negative consequences"* — M24 Q4. Both required; one-sided caps at the middle band.

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Reflecting on the topic (e.g. sustainability, freedom of speech) instead of on your own development | Use *your* and *I*. Anchor every paragraph in something you now think differently about because of the synthesis. |
| Omitting the "classroom preparation" bullet because it feels off-topic | The classroom-prep bullet appears in N16 Q7, M22 Q6 (implicit), M19 Q7 (via SAA hook). It is the easiest bullet to score and the one most often dropped. Always include one specific classroom moment. |
| Listing four bullets as four separate paragraphs with no connective synthesis | The top band wants *thorough and nuanced* — one well-woven response that touches all four points beats four mechanical paragraphs. Use connective phrases ("this echoed our class discussion of…", "the same source resurfaced in question 4 where I…"). |
| Using only one discipline in the reflection | Every reflective rubric implicitly or explicitly requires both lenses. Frame at least one paragraph around "what each discipline alone could not have shown me, but the two together did". |
| Giving a generic conclusion ("so reflection is important") | The top band requires a *justified* or *evaluative* conclusion (M19 Q6 verbatim). Restate your strongest piece of evidence and turn it into a forward-looking claim ("this is why I will approach X differently next time"). |
| Quoting the source without linking it to your thinking | M19 Q6 top band wording: *"explicitly linked evidence"*. Do not just cite Source 4 — say what Source 4 made you reconsider. |
| Treating the reflection as a content summary of the exam | Examiners read hundreds of these. A summary of the exam content reads as filler and caps the response in the bottom band. |
| Missing both-sides on M24 Q4–style consequence questions | "Personal positive **and** negative consequences" is the verbatim rule. One-sided answers cap at the middle band. |
| Using vague depth words ("a lot", "really", "very") | Replace with the rubric's own depth lexicon: *significant*, *thorough*, *nuanced*, *evaluative*, *justified*. The examiner is scanning for these. |
| Forgetting the named example | M22 Q6 requires an example at every band; M19 Q7 from band 3–4. Always name a Q number, a source number, or a specific classroom activity. |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Memorise the four reflection bullets** — write them on a sticky note: *source / perspectives / exam questions / classroom prep*. These are the spine of N16 Q7, the template every other reflective question varies on.
2. **Practise a 5-paragraph reflective response template** (see Section 6 below) until you can produce one in 10 minutes from any prompt.
3. **Build a mental list of three classroom-prep moments** before the exam — a class debate, a teacher's framing question, a peer's argument that changed your mind. The classroom-prep bullet is the most-dropped and most-portable.
4. **Pre-load metacognitive sentence frames** — "I came to see…", "before the examination I would have argued…", "synthesising both lenses revealed that…", "this changed how I will approach…". The rubric verbs (*explain*, *justify*, *evaluate*) reward this register.
5. **Practise naming the disciplines explicitly in the reflection** — write "the mathematical lens of *Quantity* combined with the language and literature lens of *Audience imperatives* showed me that…" rather than "both subjects helped me…". M24's concept-naming gate makes this explicit.
6. **Write at least one full reflection on a past session you have studied** — pick M22 Q6 or M19 Q7 and write a 12-mark answer end-to-end. Self-mark against the four bullets and the verb ladder.
7. **Drill the both-sides reflex** — for any reflective question, ask yourself "what is the strength and what is the weakness?" before writing. M21 Q7, M24 Q4, M22 Q6 and M19 Q7 all have hard both-sides gates.
8. **Plan the conclusion before writing the body** — one sentence in the rough box: *"My interdisciplinary understanding has shifted from X to Y, which I will apply by Z."* Write the body to land that conclusion.
9. **Quote one source verbatim and one classroom moment specifically** in every reflective response. Two named anchors is the minimum for the top band.
10. **Use the rubric's own depth words in your final paragraph** — *thorough*, *nuanced*, *evaluative*, *justified*. The examiner is matching your prose against the descriptor.

## 6. Template paragraphs

Sentence frames you can adapt to any reflective question. Each line maps to a bullet or rubric strand the markscheme rewards.

**Opening sentence (frames the metacognitive shift):**
> *"Working across [discipline A] and [discipline B] in this examination has changed the way I think about [integrating context] — before, I would have argued that [prior view], but the synthesis of the two lenses has led me to [revised view]."*

**Source-reference sentence (covers bullet 1 — refer to at least one source):**
> *"Source [N] was the turning point: its [data / image / claim — quote a specific element] forced me to weigh the [discipline-A concept, e.g. Quantity / Models] against the [discipline-B concept, e.g. Audience imperatives / Point of view], which I had previously treated as separate questions."*

**Discipline-synthesis sentence (covers the interdisciplinary gate):**
> *"Neither discipline alone would have surfaced this: [discipline A] gave me the [evidence type — numbers / mechanism / structure] but could not explain [the human / cultural / ethical dimension]; [discipline B] supplied that, and only the combined view made [specific insight] visible."*

**Exam-question reference sentence (covers bullet 3 — refer to questions from the examination):**
> *"This was reinforced when I worked on Question [N], where I had to [specific task]; the act of doing that question made me see that [insight], which I would not have reached from reading the sources alone."*

**Classroom-prep sentence (covers bullet 4 — refer to discussions from classroom preparation):**
> *"In our classroom preparation, we had debated [specific topic / framing]; my position then was [prior view], but applying the same question across both disciplines in this examination has shifted my position to [revised view] for the reason that [evidence-anchored claim]."*

**Justified-conclusion sentence (covers the top-band conclusion gate):**
> *"My interdisciplinary understanding of [integrating context] has therefore moved from [prior framing] to [revised framing]; the most significant change is that I will now [forward-looking action / approach], because the synthesis of [discipline A] and [discipline B] makes clear that [single most important evaluative claim, anchored to a quoted source or named question]."*

**Both-sides booster (drop in for M21 Q7 / M24 Q4 / M19 Q7 style questions):**
> *"This new understanding carries a clear strength — [positive consequence, named] — but also a limitation: [negative consequence, named]. Recognising both is itself part of what the interdisciplinary lens has taught me."*

**Service-as-action hook (M19 Q7-style, where the rubric demands an SAA example):**
> *"In my service-as-action work on [named project], I had encountered [specific situation]; the examination's framing of [exam concept] now lets me see that experience as [reframed insight], which links the classroom understanding to action in a way I could not have articulated before."*

## 7. A worked exemplar (in skeleton)

To show how the template paragraphs combine into a top-band response, here is a 5-paragraph skeleton for a hypothetical N16 Q7 answer (sustainability, sciences + I&S):

1. **Opening — frame the shift.** "Working across sciences and individuals & societies in this examination has changed how I understand sustainability — before, I would have framed it as primarily an environmental/scientific problem, but the synthesis of the two lenses has shown me it is irreducibly a social-systems problem."
2. **Source-reference paragraph.** Quote one specific element of Source 4 (the features-of-sustainability source). Show how it forced a recalibration: the science lens (Quantity, closed-loop systems) and the I&S lens (stakeholders, governance) had to be applied to the same feature.
3. **Discipline-synthesis paragraph.** Argue that neither lens alone surfaces the insight. Sciences gave the mechanism (CO2 / overfishing / closed-loop); I&S gave the human stake (livelihoods / governance / cultural value). Cite Q3a (the academic response on bluefin tuna) as the moment where the synthesis became visible to you.
4. **Classroom-prep paragraph.** Name a specific class debate or activity — a stakeholder-perspective exercise, a teacher's framing question — and show how this examination has changed your prior position on it.
5. **Justified-conclusion paragraph.** Restate the shift, name the most decisive piece of evidence, and project forward: "this is why I will now approach environmental questions by asking who is excluded from the system before asking how the system works."

Each paragraph touches a different one of the four bullets; collectively they cover all four. Each paragraph names a specific anchor (source, exam question, class moment). The conclusion is justified, not just present. The register uses the rubric's own depth lexicon. This is structurally a 12–14 / 14 response.
