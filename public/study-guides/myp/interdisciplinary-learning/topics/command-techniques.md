# Command-Term Techniques (Cheat Sheet)

A scannable companion to `command-terms.md`. Each entry: 1-line definition, 1-line technique, top-band trigger, common trap. For full mark-scheme detail, see the master `command-terms.md`.

---

## The verb ladder (once)

The universal IDL band-spine. Every banded rubric climbs through these verbs; depth and coverage decide where you sit *within* a band.

```
state  →  outline  →  describe  →  explain  →  integrate / evaluate / justify
 ↑          ↑           ↑            ↑                       ↑
1–3        4–6         7–9         10–12               top-band trigger
(bare)   (because…)  (detailed   (cause-and-           (justified verdict
         + example)  + evidence)  effect + concepts)    + both sides + extensive PRM)
```

| Band (12 mk) | Verb | What the writing does |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | States | Bare claim. No reasoning. |
| 4–6 | Outlines | Brief account + a "because…" clause. |
| 7–9 | Describes | Detailed, named features, evidence cited. |
| 10–12 | Explains / Justifies / Evaluates / Integrates | Cause-and-effect, named subject concepts, explicit PRM linkage, justified conclusion. |

## Per-term cheat cards

### **Discuss** (high-mark, M22 Q3, M23 Q3, M24 Q1)
- **Means**: balanced review of multiple sides supported by evidence
- **Do**: Open with the question. Give 2 perspectives. Use a synthesis-hinge sentence ("Taken together, X and Y mean…"). End with a position.
- **Top band**: both sides argued; PRM evidence on each; conclusion that weighs (not restates)
- **Trap**: listing without weighing; one-sided write-up
- **Example**: M24 Q1 — *Discuss how mathematics and language and literature can be used to monetize disinformation*

### **Explain** (every session)
- **Means**: detailed account *with reasons or causes*
- **Do**: Use cause-and-effect language — *because, therefore, so that, which leads to*. Name a subject concept (*Quantity*, *Audience imperatives*) per claim. Anchor to a specific PRM number/quote.
- **Top band**: cause-and-effect + named concepts + explicit PRM linkage
- **Trap**: describing what happens instead of why; single-discipline answers (M17 Q4b cap = 2/10; N16 Q3b cap = 5/10)
- **Example**: M24 Q2 — *Explain how funding, technology and language contribute to the spread of disinformation*

### **Justify** (M17 Q4a, N20 Q5, M21 Q3, M23 Q2)
- **Means**: give valid reasons / evidence to support an answer or conclusion — and **commit** to a position
- **Do**: State your choice up front. Back it with explicit data ("US$236.4M…", "60 charges vs 20"). Acknowledge a counter-argument and rebut it.
- **Top band**: explicit (not general) evidence + counter-argument addressed + evaluative conclusion
- **Trap**: no commitment to a side (N20 Q5 max 3/12 with no city chosen); "general" rather than specific evidence
- **Example**: M23 Q2 — *Justify which scenario (40%, 70%, 100% electrification) is best, with mathematical AND scientific reasoning for both choice and counter-argument*

### **Evaluate** (N16 Q1b, N20 Q3b, N21 Q2b)
- **Means**: appraise by weighing strengths AND limitations, then judge
- **Do**: Strengths paragraph + Limitations paragraph + evaluative conclusion. For "evaluate the method", append improvements (N16 Q1b caps at 4 if no improvements).
- **Top band**: both sides covered, specific evidence each side, evaluative (not stated) conclusion
- **Trap**: only strengths OR only limitations (N21 Q2b max 2/8); restating evidence instead of weighing it
- **Example**: N21 Q2b — *Evaluate the impact of the cyclone poster on the audience*

### **To what extent** (M19 Q6, N19 Q6, N20 Q1b, M21 Q6, N21 Q7)
- **Means**: argue along a spectrum — fully / partly / not at all — and commit
- **Do**: Frame answer as *"To a large/limited extent…"*. Run both sides. Land on a justified verdict.
- **Top band**: evidence **explicitly linked** to PRM on both sides; conclusion **justified** (not merely stated)
- **Trap**: pros/cons list without a verdict; off-task framing (M21 Q6: "is healthcare a right" ≠ "pros/cons of healthcare being a right")
- **Example**: M19 Q6 — *To what extent can someone know the truth from one source*

### **Compare and contrast** (M17 Q3a, N20 Q4, N21 Q4, M23 Q1)
- **Means**: similarities AND differences, referring to both items throughout
- **Do**: Paragraph 1 = a similarity referring to both. Paragraph 2 = a difference referring to both. Use comparator language: *whereas, more than, by contrast, similarly*.
- **Top band**: integrated treatment (NOT two separate lists)
- **Trap**: paragraph on A then paragraph on B with no comparators (N21 Q4 max 2/12; M23 Q1 cap 1–3); only similarities OR only differences (N21 Q4 max 3/12); off-task — comparing the things rather than how the sources treat them (M23 Q1 auto-zero on first two strands)
- **Example**: M23 Q1 — *Compare and contrast the synthesis of mathematics and science in the two sales brochures* (NOT compare the cars)

### **Analyse** (N16 Q2b, N21 Q3b, M21 Q5b)
- **Means**: break into parts, interpret relationships, reach a conclusion
- **Do**: Name the parts (SWOT, components, mechanism). Show how they relate. Pull together in a conclusion.
- **Top band**: all named elements covered + interpretation + conclusion referring to related concepts
- **Trap**: describe-with-extra-words; for reflective Analyse (M21 Q5b), drifting into general theory instead of analysing your *own* prior work
- **Example**: N16 Q2b — *Analyse the impact of fishing on sustainability* (SWOT-style; ≥3 quotations needed for top band)

### **Create** (M19 Q3/Q5a, N20 Q3a, N21 Q3a, M22 Q5, M24 Q3)
- **Means**: produce a defined creative product that integrates content and form per the brief
- **Do**: Hit every named component in the brief. Make the synthesis between disciplines do the work — not the layout.
- **Top band**: all components present and integrated; multiple PRM references woven in; both disciplines doing real work
- **Trap**: form-without-substance (M24 Q3 — article structure NOT credited; only synthesis + explicit POV + PRM use); single-mode (M19 Q5a canvas-only or text-only capped at 3/12); off-brief context (N21 Q3a max 3 if not in aid-distribution context)
- **Example**: M24 Q3 — *Create an article expressing your point of view on the spread of news on social media*

### **Identify / State** (low-mark openers)
- **Means**: name briefly, no explanation
- **Do**: One sentence, specific term, units if numerical. Respect any caps (e.g. N16 Q4a = 2 from each discipline).
- **Top band**: every item correctly named or placed
- **Trap**: paragraphs for 1-mark Q (wastes time, risks contradictions); over-selection (M19 Q1a forfeits the mark for that slot); missing units ($ for M19 Q2c)
- **Example**: M21 Q2a — *Identify the author's purpose for two text selections* (Persuade / Inform)

### **Outline / Describe**
- **Outline = brief account + a clause of reasoning**. **Describe = detailed account with named features and ≥1 piece of evidence** — but no causal "why".
- **Do (Outline)**: One sentence + "because…" + a worked example. **Do (Describe)**: short paragraph naming features and citing source.
- **Top band**: Outline = brief account WITH a valid example (N19 Q2a band 2). Describe = ≥3 references for multi-reference questions (N20 Q2b).
- **Trap**: reciting source content as "your" outline (M22 Q2a explicit zero); describing without source linkage; under-referencing on describe-with-references questions
- **Example**: N20 Q2b — *Describe how the mode of persuasion in poster 2 affects the audience* (banded by reference count)

### **Demonstrate** (M24 Q4)
- **Means**: make clear by reasoning or evidence, with examples / practical application
- **Do**: Show new understanding through reasoned examples. Adapt to the named audience. Cover both positive and negative consequences.
- **Top band**: explains learning + actions + both consequences + extensive PRM use, audience-adapted
- **Trap**: restating taught content instead of new understanding; single-sided consequences; ignoring audience (M24 Q4 = younger students → adapt *Audience imperatives* / *Style*)
- **Example**: M24 Q4 — *Demonstrate the new understanding in an informative speech for younger students on how they can act responsibly online*

### **Reflect** (N16 Q7, M22 Q6, M24 Q4)
- **Means**: look back at *your own* development, not the topic
- **Do**: Cite a specific PRM source. Reference specific exam questions. Anchor to your own prior answer. Show why the insight needed both disciplines.
- **Top band**: all required bullets covered + own-work example + interdisciplinary frame (insight not gainable from one discipline alone)
- **Trap**: generic reflection on the topic ("I learned X is important"); single-discipline reflection; no own-work anchor (M22 Q6 — example from candidate's own Q3/Q4/Q5 mandatory at every band)
- **Example**: M22 Q6 — *Reflect on strengths and weaknesses of your own response in Q3 / Q4 / Q5*

### **Formulate** (M17 Q5, M21 Q5a, N21 Q1c)
- **Means**: express precisely and systematically the relevant concept(s) or argument(s)
- **Do**: Research questions = clear AND focused, with methods. Arguments = sustain the assigned voice; back BOTH arguments with PRM; link opening/closing to the substantive arguments.
- **Top band**: all structural elements present (questions + methods, or opening + 2 args + evidence + closing); both arguments evidenced
- **Trap**: blacklisted generic Q ("To what extent is it viable to settle on Mars?" — M17 Q5 explicit); questions without methods (max 1–2 band); breaking the role-play frame (M21 Q5a)
- **Example**: M21 Q5a — *Formulate the drug company's opposition to Reference 6*

### **Compare** (standalone — N16 Q1a)
- **Means**: similarities (and implicit differences); make the comparative statement explicit
- **Do**: Use *more than / compared with / whereas* — not parallel descriptions
- **Top band**: explicit comparative statement (mark 4 in N16 Q1a is gated on this)
- **Trap**: two parallel feature lists with no comparator
- **Example**: N16 Q1a — *Compare the graphs from three CO2 trials*

### **Suggest** (N16 Q1c, M19 Q2d)
- **Means**: propose a reasonable possibility
- **Do**: Transfer logic from the source to the new context. Be specific.
- **Top band**: disciplinarily sound, novel proposal
- **Trap**: vague "it would help"; restating the prompt
- **Example**: N16 Q1c — *Suggest two changes to study animal life ethically*

### **Construct / Estimate**
- **Construct**: display in diagrammatic / logical form. Label axes WITH units; equal intervals from origin; plot accurately. Missing units = lost mark.
- **Estimate**: approximate value within an open interval. **Always include the unit** (M19 Q2c — `$30 < x < $35`, boundaries excluded; missing `$` = no mark).

### **Develop** (M21 Q2b)
- **Means**: improve incrementally, elaborate, evolve
- **Do**: Treat the given element as fixed (do NOT rewrite it). Develop the surrounding elements in the assigned voice, linking each back to the given element.
- **Top band**: all four narrative elements developed in voice + linked to climax
- **Trap**: rewriting the climax (it's given); drifting out of perspective

### **Use** (M19 Q4, M19 Q5b)
- **Means**: actively *deploy* PRM info to do the explanatory/persuasive work — not cite it
- **Do**: For each PRM reference, name it AND show how it supports the specific claim
- **Top band**: clear demonstration of the link between selected example and response
- **Trap**: PRM as a checklist (one tick per source) instead of woven evidence

## Top-band triggers (cross-cutting)

Phrases drawn from rubrics that switch on the top band. If your answer can claim these, you're at 10–12 / 7–8.

- **"explicitly linked evidence from the pre-release material"** (M19 Q6, N20 Q1b, N20 Q7, N21 Q7)
- **"uses extensive information from the pre-release material"** (M24 Q1, Q2, Q3 — multiple references woven, not one)
- **"explicit and accurate use of subject concepts"** (M24 Q1 — name *Quantity*, *Models*, *Audience imperatives*, *Purpose*; not "trends" / "tone")
- **"directly compares and contrasts"** (M23 Q1 — refer to both items throughout, not in turn)
- **"mathematical AND scientific reasoning"** (M23 Q2 — top band requires AND for both choice and counter-argument)
- **"both [strengths] AND [limitations]"** (N21 Q2b, N16 Q1b — one-sidedness caps mid-band)
- **"evaluative conclusion"** (M23 Q1, M23 Q2, M19 Q6, N19 Q6 — weighs the case, not restates it)
- **"justified conclusion"** (M19 Q6, N20 Q1b — committed verdict supported by evidence)
- **"a conclusion explicitly referring to related concepts"** (N21 Q5, N21 Q3b)
- **"explicit point of view"** (M24 Q3 — article without a stated position is capped)
- **"audience-adapted"** (M24 Q4 — younger-student speech adapts *Style* / *Audience imperatives*)

## Anti-patterns (cross-cutting)

Patterns that auto-cap or auto-zero the response.

- **Single-discipline content** when synthesis is required<br>
  Caps: N16 Q3b max 5/10; M17 Q4b max 2/10; M22 Q3 mid-band; M24 Q3 band 1–3
- **One-sided arguments** on "to what extent" / "discuss" / "evaluate"<br>
  Caps: M17 Q3b max 8/16; M21 Q6 max 6/12; N21 Q2b max 2/8; N20 Q5 max 6/12
- **Two separate lists** instead of integrated compare-and-contrast<br>
  Caps: N21 Q4 max 2/12; M23 Q1 cap 1–3 for first two strands
- **Off-task content** — answering the wrong question<br>
  M22 Q1a (off-topic Baobab); M24 Q1 blacklist; M21 Q6 ("pros/cons" instead of "should be"); M17 Q5 blacklisted generic Mars question; M23 Q1 (comparing cars not brochures → auto-zero on first two strands)
- **PRM cited but not used** — name-drop without deployment<br>
  Anything below "explicitly linked" caps below top band
- **Generic reference to the in-question stimulus** where PRM citation required<br>
  M23 Q3 explicit: "Reference to the delivery driver does not gain credit for the third strand"
- **No commitment to a position** on Justify / To what extent<br>
  N20 Q5 max 3/12 (no city chosen); M21 Q6 (no verdict)
- **Concepts implicit, not named**<br>
  Saying "trends" instead of *Patterns*, "tone" instead of *Style* / *Audience imperatives* — read down a band in M24
- **Reciting source as "your" outline**<br>
  M22 Q2a explicit zero — outlines must be your own production
- **Form-without-substance on Create**<br>
  M24 Q3 — headlines / bylines / columns NOT credited; M19 Q5a — canvas-only or text-only capped at 3/12
- **Generic reflection** that could be written without sitting the exam<br>
  N16 Q7, M22 Q6 — caps at lowest band
- **Bare lists for higher-verb tasks**<br>
  No causal language on "explain"; no comparator language on "compare and contrast"

## 30-minute drill plan

A pre-exam command-term drill. Run the night before, or the morning of.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| **0–5 min** | Read this cheat sheet end-to-end. Star the 4 verbs you're weakest on. |
| **5–15 min** | Drill 4 command terms. For each, write a 4-sentence answer to a sample stem from the per-term examples above (Discuss / Explain / Justify / Compare-and-contrast are highest-yield). Time-box 2.5 min each. |
| **15–25 min** | Practise the synthesis-hinge sentence. Take any two PRM references from M24 (e.g. Ref 2 funding chart + Ref 4 idioms map). Write 3 sentences of the form: *"While Ref X shows [maths concept = X], Ref Y shows [L&L concept = Y]; together this means [synthesis claim]."* Repeat with a different concept pair. |
| **25–30 min** | Re-read top-band triggers and anti-patterns. Mentally rehearse the verdict line you'll close every extended response with: *"On balance, [position] because [strongest evidence], although [counter] mitigates this."* |

**Drill rule**: every practice answer must end with a justified one-sentence verdict. Even on Describe questions — habituate the closing.

## Quick-reference one-liners (the night before)

- **Read the bullets in the question stem.** They are the band gates. Address all of them.
- **Name both disciplines explicitly.** Single-discipline = mid-band cap, every session.
- **Cite specific evidence.** Numbers, quotations, named references. "General" caps mid-band.
- **Name subject concepts.** *Quantity*, *Patterns*, *Models*, *Audience imperatives*, *Purpose*, *Style*, *Point of view*.
- **Always end with a justified verdict.** Even on Discuss. Especially on To what extent.
- **Match your verb to the rubric verb.** Use *because* for explain; use *whereas* for compare; use *strengths and limitations* for evaluate.
- **Don't write a paragraph for a 1-mark State.** Time saved is time for the 12-mark question.
