# Synthesising Across Disciplines

The cross-cutting IDL skill that gates the top band on almost every extended-response question. This page consolidates the rubric-level evidence on what counts as synthesis, how it is assessed across the verb ladder, and where the discipline-balance caps fall.

---

## 1. Key Concepts

### What synthesis means in IDL

Synthesis in MYP IDL is more than addition — it is the explicit demonstration that **insights from two (or more) disciplines together produce understanding that neither alone could**. The mark schemes test this through three coupled gates:

1. **Coverage** — both disciplines must be named and used.
2. **Balance** — one discipline cannot dominate; many rubrics cap single-discipline answers at half-marks or below.
3. **Linkage** — the disciplines must operate together inside the same argument, not appear in side-by-side paragraphs.

The IDL papers explicitly distinguish synthesis from "knowing two disciplines". M22 Q3 uses the verb *Synthesise* directly; M23 Q1 caps responses that compare the cars themselves rather than how the brochures *synthesise* maths and science; M24 Q1 dedicates a full strand to "the synthesis of both subjects". Single-subject MYP papers do not test this skill — it is the defining IDL competency.

> **Single most important difference between IDL and a single-subject MYP paper:** in IDL, *covering both disciplines and synthesising them explicitly* is the strongest predictor of moving from mid-band to top-band. In a single-subject paper, depth in one discipline alone can carry a top-band response.

### The synthesis ladder (state → outline → describe → explain → integrate)

Every banded IDL rubric uses the same vertical ladder. The verb in the band descriptor is the verb your answer must perform.

| Band (12-mark Q) | Verb | What synthesis looks like at this rung |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | **States** | "Mathematics is used. Language and literature is used." Two parallel claims, no link. |
| 4–6 | **Outlines** | "Mathematics is used because brands pay per click. Language and literature is used because idioms shape what counts as a lie." Brief account per discipline + a "because" clause. Mention of one subject concept. |
| 7–9 | **Describes** | A short paragraph per discipline with named features and at least one piece of evidence; subject concepts referenced (e.g. *Quantity*, *Audience imperatives*); attempt at a synthesis sentence linking the two. |
| 10–12 | **Explains / Integrates** | Cause-and-effect reasoning; both disciplines doing explanatory work in the same argument; explicit and accurate naming of subject concepts; multiple references woven through; justified / evaluative conclusion. |

The discriminator between *describe* (band 7–9) and *explain* (band 10–12) on synthesis questions is whether the response moves from "what each discipline says" to "why the disciplines together produce an insight neither could alone".

### Discipline-balance gates

Single-discipline content is capped in nearly every paper. The cap is the most common path to losing the top band. Rules drawn verbatim from mark schemes:

| Session | Q | Cap rule | What triggers the cap |
|---|---|---|---|
| N16 | Q3b | Max 5 / 10 | Only one of Sciences and I&S mentioned |
| N16 | Q4a | Max 2 marks per discipline | Listing 4 examples from one discipline (forces 2 + 2 split) |
| M17 | Q4b | Max 2 / 10 | Only one discipline addressed |
| M17 | Q5 | Top band requires "explicit and balanced" maths + I&S | Single-discipline research-question coverage |
| M21 | Q4 | Hard 0 | Only one speaker discussed (i.e. one discipline's perspective) |
| M22 | Q3 | Single-discipline cap mid-band | Only Sciences or only I&S used to explain cultural impact |
| M23 | Q2 | Bands 1–6 accept maths **or** sciences; bands 7–9 require **and** on one strand; band 10–12 requires **and** on both | Conjunction gate — top band needs both disciplines on choice **and** counter-argument |
| M23 | Q1 | Auto-zero on first two strands | Comparing the cars themselves instead of how the brochures synthesise maths + science |
| M24 | Q1, Q2, Q3 | Top band requires both maths and L&L concepts named explicitly | Single-discipline content drops a band |
| M24 | Q3 | Band 1–3 cap | Point of view built from only one discipline |

> **The "AND vs OR" rule (M23 Q2 most explicit).** Lower bands accept mathematical *or* scientific reasoning. The top band requires *and* — both disciplines doing real explanatory work on both your choice **and** the counter-argument.

### "Linking phrases" the rubric rewards

The rubric rewards explicit hinge language that shows the disciplines are operating together, not just listed. Phrases that signal synthesis:

- "This combination shows that..." / "Together, these two perspectives reveal..."
- "The mathematical view of X (using *Quantity* / *Patterns*) only becomes meaningful when read alongside the L&L view of Y (using *Audience imperatives* / *Style*) because..."
- "Synthesising both lenses, we can see that..."
- "Neither discipline alone could explain this — the maths shows the scale, but the L&L shows why it spreads."
- "The science explains the mechanism; the I&S explains the human cost; only together do they explain the policy choice."

What the rubric does **not** reward:
- "Both disciplines are important." (Generic; no specific evidence.)
- "In conclusion, mathematics and L&L both contribute." (Re-states without integration.)
- A maths paragraph followed by an L&L paragraph with no transition. (Two parallel one-sided arguments.)

### Subject-concept naming (the M24 tightening)

From M24 onward, the top band requires concepts to be **named, not just used**. Implicit reasoning is read down a band. The Reference 8 grids in M24 define the in-bounds vocabulary:

| Discipline | Named concepts (M24 Reference 8) |
|---|---|
| Mathematics | Approximation, Change, Equivalence, Generalization, Models, Patterns, Quantity, Representation, Simplification, Space, Systems, Validity |
| Language & Literature | Audience imperatives, Character, Context, Genre, Intertextuality, Point of view, Purpose, Self-expression, Setting, Structure, Style, Theme |

This concept-naming gate appears earlier in softer form: N16 Q3b (key concept *systems*), N19 Q2a (*global interactions*), N21 Q1d (*global interactions*), M22 Q1b (*global interactions*). M24 hardens it into an explicit top-band requirement.

## 3. Mark Scheme Insights

### What the mark scheme rewards

- **Explicit naming of both disciplines.** "Mathematics shows..." and "Language and literature shows..." — both labels visible in the response.
- **Specific evidence from each discipline.** Quantitative figures, named references, direct quotations. M24 rewards US$236.4 million, ~126 000 stories, ~3 million people on the maths side; idioms ("hang noodles on someone's ears"), the GDI quote, the digital-footprint script on the L&L side. M23 Q2 rewards the budget figures + the carbon-footprint curves + the power-outage line chart.
- **Linking sentences showing synthesis.** Hinge language ("only together...", "the maths shows scale, the L&L shows spread") that demonstrates the disciplines are doing combined explanatory work.
- **Subject concepts named, not just used.** M24 top band requires concepts from the Reference 8 grids — *Quantity*, *Patterns*, *Models*, *Representation*, *Validity* (maths); *Audience imperatives*, *Purpose*, *Style*, *Point of view* (L&L). Earlier sessions reward key concepts (*systems* in N16 Q3b; *global interactions* in N19 Q2a, N21 Q1d, M22 Q1b).
- **A justified conclusion that depends on both disciplines.** The discriminator between bands 7–9 and 10–12 is whether the conclusion is *evaluative* / *justified* (weighs the case using evidence from both lenses) rather than merely stated.
- **Extensive PRM use at the top band.** Multiple references woven through the response, not a single citation. M24 Q1, Q2, Q3 all use the ladder *refers to → attempts → uses appropriate → uses extensive*.

### What the mark scheme penalises / does not accept

- **Two parallel one-sided arguments without linkage.** N21 Q4 caps at 2/12 if comparisons/contrasts are not linked to both articles (i.e. two separate lists). M23 Q1 caps the comparison strands at 1–3 if the response is two paragraphs about each brochure separately.
- **Single-discipline content.** Capped at half-marks or lower in nearly every paper. See the discipline-balance table in §1. Most punishing: M21 Q4 (hard 0 if only one speaker), M17 Q4b (max 2/10), N16 Q3b (max 5/10).
- **Generic linking ("both disciplines are important") without specific evidence.** Caps in the bottom two bands across N16 Q3b, M17 Q4b, M22 Q3.
- **Missing balance — heavy on one discipline, thin on the other.** Best-fit marking allows compensation but the band sits at the lower end. The MS guidance (M17, M23 verbatim): "if student work matches two of three requirements within a markband but one is seriously lacking, the student should be awarded for the strands that have been met well, but the mark awarded should be at the lower end of the markband to compensate."
- **Off-task synthesis.** M23 Q1 explicit: "Responses must refer to the brochures use of mathematics and science, not the cars themselves. If a response only compares the cars, then students score 0 for the first two strands." M21 Q6 off-task: pros/cons of healthcare-as-a-right rather than whether it *should be* a right.
- **Subject concepts used implicitly.** From M24: "tone" without naming *Style* / *Audience imperatives*; "trends" without naming *Patterns*; "numbers" without naming *Quantity* — read down a band.
- **Single-mode response when both required.** M19 Q5a canvas-only or text-only capped at 3 / 12.
- **Article form prioritised over content.** M24 Q3 explicit: "the structure of the article is not relevant to awarding credit". Headlines, bylines, columns earn nothing — synthesis + explicit point of view + PRM use are the only credited strands.

### Evidence-linkage gradient (the most common top-band discriminator)

| Band | Phrasing |
|---|---|
| Lowest | "refers to" / "no evidence" |
| Lower-mid | "attempts to use evidence" |
| Upper-mid | "uses evidence" / "uses appropriate information" |
| Top | "explicitly linked evidence" / "uses extensive information" |

The single discriminator between mid and top bands across N20 Q1b, N20 Q7, M19 Q6, N21 Q7, M24 Q1–Q3 is whether the evidence is *explicitly linked* — the student must show how the evidence supports the specific synthesis claim, not just that the evidence exists.

### Conclusion ladder (the second most common top-band discriminator)

| Conclusion type | Typical band |
|---|---|
| No conclusion | 1–3 / 1–4 |
| Attempts a conclusion | 4–6 |
| Provides a conclusion | 7–9 |
| Justified / evaluative conclusion | 10–12 (top band only) |

Confirmed across M17 Q3b, Q6b, Q7; M19 Q6, Q7; N19 Q6, Q7; N20 Q1b, Q3b, Q6, Q7; M21 Q4, Q6, Q7; N21 Q2b, Q6, Q7; M23 Q1, Q2; M24. A "stated" conclusion repeats the case; an "evaluative" conclusion weighs both disciplines' evidence and commits to a position.

### Worked example: M24 Q1 (12 marks, Maths + L&L)

The three strands inside the rubric show how synthesis is graded line-by-line:

| Strand | Band 1–3 | Band 10–12 |
|---|---|---|
| Maths contribution | "states that mathematics can be used" | "explains how mathematics is used... with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts" |
| L&L contribution | "states that L&L can be used" | "explains how L&L is used... with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts" |
| Synthesis impact | "uses one example to show synthesis" | "explains how the synthesis of both subjects impacts the monetization... with explicit and accurate use of subject concepts" |

A response strong on the first two strands but lacking on the third sits at the lower end of whichever band the first two reach. **The synthesis strand cannot be skipped** — it carries equal weight to the discipline-grounding strands.

### Worked example: M23 Q2 (12 marks, Maths + Sciences) — the AND/OR gate

| Band | Choice strand | Counter-argument strand |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | states using maths **or** science | states using maths **or** science |
| 4–6 | outlines using maths **or** science | outlines using maths **or** science |
| 7–9 | describes using maths **and** science | describes using maths **or** science (or vice versa — one strand at AND) |
| 10–12 | explains using maths **and** science | explains using maths **and** science |

The conjunction is the gate. To enter band 10–12, both disciplines must be doing real work on *both* the choice and the counter-argument. This is the most explicit single-paper articulation of the synthesis-balance principle.

### Worked example: N16 Q3b (10 marks) — the half-marks cap

> "A maximum of (5 marks) can be given if only one of the disciplines from science and individuals and society has been mentioned."

This is the canonical single-discipline cap. A student writing brilliantly on Sciences but never naming I&S — or vice versa — cannot exceed 5/10 regardless of depth. The same logic is enforced softer in M22 Q3 (mid-band cap), harder in M21 Q4 (hard 0 if only one speaker / one discipline-lens discussed).

## 4. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Writing a maths paragraph then an L&L paragraph with no linkage | Use a third paragraph (or explicit hinge sentences within each paragraph) showing how the two interact: "the maths reveals X; the L&L explains why X matters / how X spreads" |
| Single-discipline rigour (deep maths but no L&L, or vice versa) | Run a coverage check after drafting: have I named both disciplines explicitly? Have I cited evidence from each? Have I named at least one concept from each? |
| Comparing the items themselves rather than how the items synthesise the disciplines (M23 Q1 trap) | Re-read the question stem. If it says "compare the synthesis", the units of comparison are the *brochures' use of disciplines*, not the cars |
| Subject concepts used implicitly ("trends", "tone", "the data shows") | Name the concept directly: *Patterns*, *Style*, *Quantity*, *Representation*. The M24 rubric reads implicit reasoning down a band |
| Using only one piece of PRM evidence at the top band | "Extensive use" requires multiple references woven through the same argument. Pull from at least 2–3 references (e.g. M24: GDI figures + MIT data + idioms map together) |
| Stated rather than evaluative / justified conclusion | The conclusion must take a position and weigh the case using evidence from both disciplines: "The synthesis is effective because... however, it is limited by... on balance, the case is..." |
| Generic linking ("both disciplines are important") | Replace with a specific synthesis claim: "The mathematical scale data (US$236.4 million) only becomes a moral problem when paired with the L&L analysis of how *Audience imperatives* shape what readers click" |
| Ignoring the role-play / format gate (M21 Q5a, N16 Q5, M22 Q1a) | Sustain the assigned voice or document format throughout. Drift = lose strand even if content is strong |
| Off-task framing ("pros/cons of X" when stem asks "should X be the case") | Re-read the question stem before writing. "To what extent" = take a position on the spectrum, not list both sides without judgement |
| Discipline ratio heavily skewed (e.g. 90% maths, 10% L&L) | Aim for rough parity. Best-fit marking allows compensation but places you at the lower end of the band. A balanced 60/40 split scores higher than 90/10 even with the same total content |
| Missing the "synthesis impact" strand entirely | M24 Q1 has three strands: maths contribution, L&L contribution, AND the synthesis impact. Many candidates do the first two and forget the third — capping at the lower end of whichever band the first two reach |
| Reflection drifts into general topic commentary | Reflective synthesis tasks (N16 Q7, M22 Q6, M24 Q4) require the reflection to be *interdisciplinary* and *self-directed* — show what you learned that needed both disciplines together |

## 5. Revision Priorities

1. **Memorise the discipline-balance cap rules.** N16 Q3b max 5; M17 Q4b max 2; M21 Q4 hard 0; M23 Q2 AND-vs-OR per band; M24 Q3 band 1–3 cap if point of view from one discipline only. Knowing these means you self-audit your draft against the cap before submitting.

2. **Practise the "synthesis hinge" sentence template.** Three usable templates:
   - "The mathematical view of X (using *Quantity* / *Patterns*) reveals Y; the L&L view of X (using *Audience imperatives* / *Style*) explains why Y matters; together they show that Z."
   - "The science explains the mechanism; the I&S explains the human cost; only the synthesis explains the policy choice."
   - "Neither discipline alone could account for this — [discipline 1] shows [scale / mechanism]; [discipline 2] shows [meaning / consequence]; combined, they show [the synthesis insight]."

3. **Build a discipline-concept vocabulary checklist.** From the M24 Reference 8 grids:
   - **Maths concepts to name:** Quantity, Patterns, Models, Representation, Validity, Approximation, Change.
   - **L&L concepts to name:** Audience imperatives, Purpose, Style, Point of view, Intertextuality, Context, Theme.
   - **Sciences key concepts (N16, N19):** systems, change, evidence, function.
   - **I&S key concepts (recurring):** global interactions, perspectives, sustainability, time-place-space.

4. **Train the verb match.** Match your answer's verb to the rubric's band-descriptor verb. "Explains" needs cause-and-effect language (because, therefore, so that, which leads to). "Describes" needs detailed features without necessarily reasoning about why. "Compares and contrasts" needs sustained side-by-side language throughout, not sequential paragraphs.

5. **Drill the both-sides / both-disciplines coverage check.** Before submitting any 12-mark response, run this 30-second audit:
   - Have I named both disciplines explicitly?
   - Have I cited at least one piece of evidence per discipline?
   - Have I named at least one subject concept per discipline?
   - Have I written a hinge sentence linking them?
   - Have I given a conclusion that depends on both?

6. **Practise PRM-weaving.** Do not cite each reference in its own paragraph. Train weaving multiple references into one argument: "The GDI run-rate model (Ref 1) projects US$236.4 m / year — and the MIT data (Ref 5) shows why that is profitable: false news reaches 4.5 m users versus 1 000 for truth, because *Audience imperatives* favour the surprising over the dull (Ref 4 idioms reinforce the point that 'spread like wildfire' is culturally encoded)."

7. **Memorise the "extensive" threshold.** "Extensive use of pre-release material" (top band of M24 Q1, Q2, Q3) means *multiple* references actively deployed, not a single citation. Plan to weave at least 3 references into the longest synthesis question.

8. **Practise auto-correcting from auto-zero traps.** M23 Q1: comparing cars instead of brochures = 0 for first two strands. M19 Q5a: missing one of six legal-support areas = hard zero. N21 Q3a: points of view not in context of aid distribution = max 3. The first sentence of any synthesis answer should anchor itself in the *synthesis target* the question names, not the underlying topic.

9. **Drill the evaluative conclusion.** The discriminator between band 7–9 and band 10–12 across most synthesis questions is whether the conclusion is *evaluative* (weighs both sides using evidence and takes a justified position) rather than merely stated. Practice 2-sentence evaluative conclusions: "On balance, [position], because the [discipline 1 evidence] outweighs the [discipline 2 counter-evidence] when [synthesis criterion] is the priority. However, this judgement depends on [named limitation]."

10. **Read the rubric, not just the question.** Bullet points in the question stem are the band gates. Top-band entry requires *all* bullets addressed. Run through the bullets and tick each one before submitting — uncovered bullets cap you a full band lower regardless of content depth.
