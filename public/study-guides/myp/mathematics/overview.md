# MYP Standard Mathematics — Exam Overview

This overview synthesises all 19 sessions from May 2016 (M16) to November 2025 (N25). Use it to understand the structure of the exam, which topics are tested most heavily, and how to allocate revision time.

---

## 1. Exam Format

The MYP Standard Mathematics eAssessment is sat **on-screen** (no printed paper). Key features:

- **Duration:** approximately 2 hours
- **Total marks:** 56–102. Early sessions were smaller (M19: 56 marks, N18: 70 marks, N19: 75 marks). From N20 onward the paper settled at **80–102 marks**, with most sessions at 80 or 100.
- **Number of questions:** 7–9. Every paper ends with a multi-part Criterion B investigation question.
- **Criteria assessed:** four criteria appear in every session:
  - **A — Knowledge and understanding:** short-to-medium questions testing mathematical technique
  - **B — Investigating patterns:** one large open-ended investigation (always the final or penultimate question)
  - **C — Communicating:** usually embedded inside B and D tasks; rarely awarded as standalone marks
  - **D — Applying mathematics in real-life contexts:** 1–3 questions set inside a real-world scenario, including one extended response worth 10 marks

- **Interactive elements:** the on-screen format includes drag-and-drop labelling (N24 Q1), sliders to animate sequences (M24 Q8, N24 Q8), animated 3D models (N24 Q7), image galleries (M24 Q7), and simulators. These are described in the question text — treat them as diagrams with extra context.

## 2. Criteria Distribution

Approximate mark allocation per criterion, based on all 19 sessions. These vary by session but the pattern is consistent.

| Criterion | Typical marks | Range seen | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| A — Knowledge and understanding | 35–55 | 28–60 | 3–6 questions; straightforward technique questions |
| B — Investigating patterns | 20–35 | 19–35 | Always 1 question; always the last question; most complex |
| C — Communicating | 0–5 standalone | 0–10 | Embedded in B and D rubrics; awarded for notation, clarity, coherence |
| D — Applying real-life contexts | 15–35 | 10–40 | 1–3 questions; one always includes a 10-mark extended response |

**The Criterion B question is always the highest-scoring single question in the paper.** In 17 of 19 sessions it is worth 28–35 marks. The 20-mark extended sub-part (labelled "Investigate") uses an analytic rubric with fixed strands: Predictions (P), Description (D), Notation/Rule (N or R), Testing (T), Verifying (V), Justify/Proof (J), and Communication (L or Co). These strands are the same across every session — learning what each strand requires is more valuable than memorising topic content.

## 5. Exam Trends

**Total marks increased then stabilised.** Papers grew from 56–98 marks (2016–2019) to a consistent 80–102 marks from 2020 onward. Budget your time assuming roughly 1 minute per mark.

**Criterion B rubric became more elaborate.** Early investigations (M16–N19) asked for a general rule, verification, and a written description. From M21 onward the rubric has consistently included seven named strands: Predictions, Description, Notation, Testing, Verifying, Justify/Proof, and Communication. The Justify strand now requires a geometric or algebraic derivation of the rule, not just substitution.

**The 10-mark extended response in Criterion D has become a fixture.** Every paper from M18 onward includes one Criterion D sub-part worth exactly 10 marks, always requiring candidates to "Analyse", "Design", or "Evaluate" with multiple bullet-point requirements. Examples: M24 Q7d (life expectancy headline analysis), N24 Q7f (rooftop garden design), M25 Q7e (robot purchase analysis), N25 Q7d (pigeon vs internet fastest delivery).

**Real-life contexts have become more elaborate.** Early D questions used straightforward applied calculations. From 2022 onward D questions introduce unfamiliar systems (metric time in M24, energy formulas in M25, Archimedes principle in N25) and require interpreting given formulas before calculating.

**Surds and index laws have become more prominent.** These appeared rarely before 2020. From M22 onward at least one question tests surd simplification or index law manipulation directly (M22 Q2, M24 Q1c, M25 Q1, N25 Q1).

**Probability questions increasingly involve "without replacement" over three selections.** M24 Q2d, N24 Q2d, and N25 Q2d all require multiplying three sequential probabilities with changing denominators (e.g., 4/24 × 3/23 × 2/22).

## 6. High-Value Topics

These are the topics most worth thorough revision, ranked by combination of frequency and mark yield.

### Criterion B investigation (30 marks — every session)

The pattern investigation always follows the same structure. The key skills are:

1. **Extending a table correctly** using the pattern, not arithmetic guessing.
2. **Describing in words** using precise terminology (e.g., "common difference of 4", "second difference is 2", "denominator increases by 1") — imprecise language (e.g., "it goes up") does not score.
3. **Writing the general rule in correct notation** (e.g., `T = n² + 3n`, not `T = n^2 + 3n` or a description in words).
4. **Testing** by substituting `n ≤ 4` and matching a value from the table.
5. **Verifying** by substituting `n ≥ 5` and matching a predicted value.
6. **Justifying geometrically or algebraically** — this is the hardest strand; it requires deriving the rule (e.g., using Pythagoras for a length rule, or showing that T = S + L and substituting the rules for each).

Investigation topics by session (general rule in brackets):

| Session | Topic | General rule |
|---|---|---|
| M16 | Mean of sequences | L = (a + 2b) / 3 |
| N16 | Cable-bridge coordinate geometry | m = 1 / (n + 1) |
| M17 | Parabola tangent intersections | y = −1 / (4k) |
| N17 | Counting greetings arrangements | G = 3m² − m |
| M18 | Perimeter of nested semicircles | P = 2r + 60π |
| N18 | Triangular number patterns | T = n² + 2n |
| M19 | Trapezium area (linear) | T = n(n + 1) |
| N19 | Trapezium perimeter | P = 6n + 8 |
| N20 | Square arrangement areas | A = (2n + 1)² |
| M21 | Trapezium areas (quadratic) | A = (2n + 1)² |
| N21 | Ring tiles arc length | L = π(1 + 2n) / n |
| M22 | Equilateral triangle perimeters | P = 3 × 2^(n − 1) (exponential) |
| N22 | Squares between parabolas | T = 4n² |
| M23 | Shrinking rectangle areas | A = 2^(3 − 2n) (indices) |
| N23 | Growing cross shape | A = (2n − 1)² + 4 |
| M24 | Midpoint lengths on diagonal lines | L = √(n² + 4) / 2 |
| N24 | Stick staircase patterns | S = 4n; T = n² + 3n |
| M25 | 3D cube cross patterns | C = 4n + 1; V = n² + 4n + 1 |
| N25 | Shaded/unshaded areas in nested squares | S = 6n; U = n² + 2n + 7 (approx) |

Most rules are **linear or quadratic**. Exponential rules appeared in M22 and M23 — an unusual case worth noting.

### Statistics + 10-mark analysis (17–22 marks per session)

Statistics appears in almost every paper across two phases: (a) Criterion A technique questions on box plots, cumulative frequency, quartiles, mean from grouped data (5–10 marks), followed by (b) a Criterion D extended analysis worth 10 marks. The 10-mark task always asks candidates to:
- identify relevant factors or elements in a bullet list
- compare two distributions using named measures (mean/median and IQR/range — generic terms like "average" or "spread" do not score)
- comment on a statement or headline, citing data
- justify the degree of accuracy or limitations of the data

Sessions with prominent statistics analysis: M16, M17, N17, M18, N18, M19, N19, N20, M21, M22, N22, M23, N23, M24, N24, M25, N25.

### Probability (6–12 marks per session)

Every session includes probability. The most common formats are:
- **Venn diagram** — find missing values, then calculate P(event) (M24, N24, N25, M21, M22, N22, N23)
- **Without replacement over 3 selections** — multiply three sequential fractions (M24 Q2d: 30/175 × 29/174 × 28/173; N24 Q2d: 4/24 × 3/23 × 2/22)
- **Sample space / combined events** — grid or tree diagram (M25 Q3)
- **Conditional from a tree** — less common but appeared in N17, N18

### Quadratic functions (8–15 marks per session)

Quadratics appear in virtually every paper. Common question types:
- Solve f(x) = g(x) where one is quadratic and one linear (M24 Q4)
- Vertex form h(x) = a(x − h)² + k — find a by substituting a known point (M24 Q5, N24 Q5, N25 Q5)
- Expand vertex form to standard form: "show that" (N24 Q5b, M24 Q5b)
- Find x-intercepts using factorisation or quadratic formula (N24 Q5c, M25 Q5a)

### Geometry and Trigonometry (8–15 marks per session)

Every paper tests geometry. High-value sub-topics:
- **Circle theorems** — inscribed angle = half central angle; angle in semicircle = 90°; specific theorems cited in "write down the reason" questions (M24 Q1, N25 Q3, N16, N17, M22)
- **Similar triangles** — ratio of sides or areas (M24 Q3b, N22 Q5, M18, N17)
- **3D mensuration** — volume of cone, cylinder, truncated cone, composite shapes (N24 Q7a, N25 Q6c, M25 Q6, N21, M22)
- **Trigonometry (right-angled)** — tan, sin, cos to find missing side or angle; always at least 3 marks (M24 Q3, N24 Q4e, M25 Q6a, N25 Q6d)
