# 2-Week MYP Standard Mathematics Revision Plan

Assumes roughly **1 hour of Mathematics per day**, with other subjects filling the rest. Adjust the daily slot to fit your schedule.

The exam is on-screen, approximately 2 hours, 80–102 marks. Budget roughly 1 minute per mark. Every paper ends with a Criterion B investigation worth ~30 marks — this is the single highest-value task in the paper and deserves the most preparation time.

---

## Week 1 — Foundation Topics

| Day | Focus | What to do |
|-----|-------|------------|
| **Day 1** | Criterion B investigation technique | Read [overview.md](overview.md) section 6 (Criterion B) in full. Study the seven strands: Predictions, Description, Notation, Testing, Verifying, Justify/Proof, Communication. Write one full "Investigate" response cold — pick any session from N23, M24, or N24 (stick-staircase or midpoint-lengths are good first attempts). Self-mark against each strand: did you use correct algebraic notation? Did you substitute n ≥ 5 for verification (not n ≤ 4)? Did you derive the rule geometrically or algebraically, not just confirm it? |
| **Day 2** | Trigonometry | Practise right-angled trigonometry (sin/cos/tan for missing side or angle), bearings, arc length, and circle theorems (inscribed angle = half central angle; angle in semicircle = 90°). Pull specific questions: M24 Q3, N24 Q4e, M25 Q6a, N25 Q6d, and the circle theorem questions from M24 Q1 and N25 Q3. These appear in 16 of 19 sessions at 6–10 marks each. |
| **Day 3** | Quadratic functions & graphs | Cover all four quadratic question types: (a) solve f(x) = g(x) where one equation is quadratic — M24 Q4; (b) vertex form h(x) = a(x − h)² + k, find a by substituting a known point — M24 Q5, N24 Q5, N25 Q5; (c) "show that" expansion from vertex to standard form — N24 Q5b, M24 Q5b; (d) find x-intercepts by factorisation or the quadratic formula — N24 Q5c, M25 Q5a. Quadratics appear in 17 of 19 sessions. |
| **Day 4** | Probability & statistics (Criterion A) | Probability: practise Venn diagram questions (find missing values, then calculate P(event) — M24, N24, N25, M22, N23), and "without replacement over 3 selections" (multiply three sequential fractions with decreasing denominators — M24 Q2d, N24 Q2d, N25 Q2d). Statistics: practise reading cumulative frequency graphs, finding quartiles, IQR, and median from box plots — these appear in almost every paper as Criterion A technique questions before the 10-mark analysis. |
| **Day 5** | Geometry (circles, similar triangles, 3D shapes) | Work through: circle theorems with written reasons (M24 Q1, N25 Q3, N16, N17, M22); similar triangles and ratio of sides or areas (M24 Q3b, N22 Q5, M18, N17); 3D mensuration — volume of cylinder, cone, truncated cone, composite solids (N24 Q7a, N25 Q6c, M25 Q6, N21, M22). Geometry appears in 18 of 19 sessions. |
| **Day 6** | Criterion D extended analysis (10-mark tasks) | Read overview.md section 5 (exam trends) on the 10-mark Criterion D task. Study the four recent examples: M24 Q7d (life expectancy headline), N24 Q7f (rooftop garden design), M25 Q7e (robot purchase analysis), N25 Q7d (pigeon vs internet delivery). Each requires: a bullet-point identification of relevant factors, a comparison using named measures (say "median" and "IQR", not "average" and "spread"), a comment citing specific data, and a justified conclusion. Write one practice response without looking at the mark scheme, then check. |
| **Day 7** | Past paper timed practice — N23 or M24 | Sit N23 (80 marks) or M24 (100 marks) under timed conditions — no notes, no calculator unless the question permits. Mark strictly using the mark scheme. Note every question where you lost marks. Do not re-do questions; just annotate what went wrong. Use this list to steer Week 2. |

## Week 2 — Consolidation

| Day | Focus | What to do |
|-----|-------|------------|
| **Day 8** | Algebra — sequences, simultaneous equations, inequalities | Sequences: practise identifying arithmetic (common difference) and geometric (common ratio) sequences outside Criterion B — e.g., M16 piano frequencies. Simultaneous equations: both substitution and elimination, including "form two equations from a real-life scenario and solve" — these appear in 13 of 19 sessions. Linear inequalities and linear programming (6/19 sessions): practise writing and shading feasible regions. |
| **Day 9** | Statistics — cumulative frequency, box plots, scatter graphs | Focus on the Criterion A statistics technique questions that precede the 10-mark D analysis. Practice: drawing and reading cumulative frequency curves, constructing box plots from quartile values, comparing two distributions using median and IQR (never "average" or "spread"), and identifying outliers. Sessions with prominent statistics Criterion A: M16, N17, M21, N22, M23, N23, M24, M25. |
| **Day 10** | Number — indices, surds, standard form, sets/Venn | Surds and index laws have grown more prominent since 2022 — at least one question in M22, N22, M24 Q1c, M25 Q1, N25 Q1. Practise: simplifying surds (rationalising denominator), index law manipulation (negative and fractional exponents), standard form calculations (M24 metric time, M18 population). Sets: set notation (∪, ∩, A'), Venn diagram shading, and calculating probabilities from a Venn diagram — appears in 13 of 19 sessions. |
| **Day 11** | Coordinate geometry & transformations | Coordinate geometry (10/19 sessions): gradient, equation of a line (y = mx + c and ax + by = c), midpoint, distance formula, parallel and perpendicular lines. Transformations and functions (11/19 sessions): matching graphs to equations, identifying reflections and translations, using function notation f(x). Spend extra time on circle geometry with coordinates — this was tested in N16 (cable-bridge investigation) and M24 Q8. |
| **Day 12** | Real-life modelling (Criterion D contexts) | Work through recent Criterion D questions that introduce unfamiliar formulas: M25 Q6 (energy formula), N25 Q6 (Archimedes/density), M24 Q6 (metric time). The skill is not knowing the formula — it is reading it carefully, identifying which variable to find, and substituting correctly. Also practise the "Show that" sub-parts that precede these calculations (N24 Q6a, M25 Q6a, N25 Q7a) — these are "answer given" questions where full algebraic working must be shown. |
| **Day 13** | Full timed paper — M25 or N24 | Sit M25 (102 marks, most recent) or N24 (100 marks) in full timed conditions. Mark with the mark scheme. Compare your score and weak areas to Day 7. Are the same topics causing drops, or different ones? |
| **Day 14** | Review weak areas + command term drill | Return to the specific question types you dropped marks on in Day 13. Then drill command terms from overview.md section 7: "Show that" requires complete working even when the answer is given; "Write down" requires no working; "Verify" requires substituting n ≥ 5; "Describe" a pattern requires named terminology (not "it goes up"). Finish with a light skim of the Criterion B investigation table in overview.md section 6 — remind yourself of the range of rule types (linear, quadratic, exponential, fractional). |

## Priority if time is tight

If Mathematics time gets squeezed by other subjects, protect these in order:

1. **Criterion B investigation strands** — 28–35 marks, follows an identical structure every session; learning the seven strands (P, D, N, T, V, J, L) and what each requires is more valuable than any other single topic.
2. **The 10-mark Criterion D extended response** — appears in every paper since M18; one practised template (identify, compare with named measures, comment on data, justify conclusion) covers any topic.
3. **Probability — Venn diagrams and "without replacement over 3 selections"** — both formats appear in almost every recent paper (M24, N24, N25) and are straightforward once drilled.
4. **Quadratic functions** — vertex form substitution and the "show that" expansion appear in 17 of 19 sessions and follow a predictable pattern.
5. **Trigonometry and 3D mensuration** — right-angled trig and volume of composite solids appear in nearly every paper; the question types are narrow and repeatable.
6. **Surds and index laws** — a new fixture since M22; typically 3–5 marks in the first question of the paper and straightforward if practised.
7. **Statistics comparison language** — "median and IQR", never "average and spread"; this single habit protects marks across multiple Criterion A and D questions every session.

## Mark scheme habits

These five patterns appear consistently across all 19 sessions. Getting these right protects marks you have already earned.

1. **"Show that" questions require complete, unambiguous working.** The answer is printed in the question — the mark is awarded for the steps, not the result. In M24 Q5b, N24 Q4a, and N25 Q5a, candidates who wrote the answer without showing the expansion or substitution scored zero. Show every algebraic line.
2. **Criterion B verification must use n ≥ 5, not n ≤ 4.** The mark scheme defines verification as substituting a value not used when finding the rule. Using n = 3 from your own table does not score. Use n = 6 or higher.
3. **Describe a pattern with precise terminology, not vague words.** "It goes up by 4" does not score. "The first difference is constant at 4, so the sequence is arithmetic with common difference 4" does. The mark scheme always quotes exact phrasing like "second difference is 2" or "denominator increases by 1."
4. **Comparison questions require named measures.** Any question asking you to compare two data sets (Criterion A statistics or the 10-mark Criterion D analysis) must name "median" and "IQR" or "range". Generic terms like "average" and "spread" are rejected in every mark scheme across all 19 sessions.
5. **The Justify strand in Criterion B is the hardest and most often missed.** Substituting your rule back into the pattern does not count as justification — that is verification. Justification requires deriving the rule: using Pythagoras to derive a length formula (M24 Q8), showing that T = S + L and substituting (N24 Q8), or proving a factored form algebraically. If you are short on time, practise at least one derivation per investigation type (linear, quadratic, geometric).
